# Respiratory, Allergy, Eye & ENT Medications

<p class="mh-subtitle">Inhalers, glucocorticoids, bronchodilators, decongestants, antihistamines, anticholinergics, and eye-drop safety</p>

ATI commonly tests these medications through **rescue versus maintenance therapy, inhaler technique, oral thrush prevention, anticholinergic effects, rebound congestion, tachycardia, and prevention of systemic absorption from eye drops.**

### Bronchodilators

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Albuterol</strong>

* Short-acting beta₂ agonist
* Rescue medication
* Opens airways quickly
* Used during acute bronchospasm

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Albuterol Effects</strong>

* Tachycardia
* Tremor
* Nervousness
* Palpitations
* Headache

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Theophylline</strong>

* Bronchodilator
* Relaxes airway smooth muscle
* Has a narrow therapeutic range
* Requires toxicity monitoring

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Theophylline Toxicity</strong>

* Nausea or vomiting
* Insomnia
* Tremor
* Tachycardia
* Dysrhythmias
* Seizures

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>ATI Cue</th>
        <th>Meaning</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Acute wheezing or bronchospasm</strong></td>
        <td>Use the prescribed rescue bronchodilator.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Using rescue inhaler frequently</strong></td>
        <td>Asthma may be poorly controlled; provider follow-up is needed.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Seizure or dysrhythmia with theophylline</strong></td>
        <td>Possible severe toxicity; hold and notify immediately.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Caffeine use with theophylline</strong></td>
        <td>Can increase CNS stimulation and adverse effects.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Albuterol is a rescue medication. Inhaled glucocorticoids and long-acting bronchodilators are not used to stop an acute attack.
</div>

</div>

### Inhaled Glucocorticoids

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Examples</strong>

* Fluticasone
* Budesonide
* Beclomethasone

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>What They Do</strong>

* Decrease airway inflammation.
* Prevent asthma symptoms.
* Used for maintenance.
* Do not provide immediate rescue.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Teaching</strong>

* Rinse mouth after each use.
* Spit out the rinse water.
* Use consistently as prescribed.
* Report white oral patches or soreness.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Long-Term Risks</strong>

* Oral candidiasis
* Hoarseness
* Possible bone loss
* Possible systemic steroid effects at high doses

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Situation</th>
        <th>Correct Action</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Bronchodilator and inhaled steroid both prescribed</strong></td>
        <td>Use the bronchodilator first to open the airways.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Multiple puffs prescribed</strong></td>
        <td>Wait about 1–2 minutes between inhalations when instructed.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>White patches in the mouth</strong></td>
        <td>Possible oral thrush; notify the provider.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Acute asthma attack</strong></td>
        <td>Use the rescue medication, not the inhaled steroid alone.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Rinsing the mouth after inhaled glucocorticoids reduces oral candidiasis.
</div>

</div>

### Anticholinergic Inhalers

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Example</strong>

* Ipratropium
* Bronchodilator
* Often used for COPD
* Reduces bronchoconstriction

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Anticholinergic Effects</strong>

* Dry mouth
* Blurred vision
* Tachycardia
* Constipation
* Urinary retention
* Photophobia

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Use Caution With</strong>

* Benign prostatic hyperplasia
* Glaucoma
* Older adults
* Other anticholinergic medications

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Teaching</strong>

* Increase fluids if allowed.
* Use sugar-free gum for dry mouth.
* Avoid spraying medication into the eyes.
* Report urinary retention.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Interaction Cue:</strong> Combining ipratropium with oxybutynin or other anticholinergics can intensify dry mouth, blurred vision, tachycardia, constipation, and urinary retention.
</div>

</div>

### Nasal Medications

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Fluticasone Nasal Spray</strong>

* Decreases nasal inflammation.
* Used for allergic rhinitis.
* Saline spray may be used first to clear crusting.
* Use consistently for best effect.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Fluticasone Teaching</strong>

* Blow or clear the nose first.
* Aim away from the nasal septum.
* Use the prescribed number of sprays.
* Do not expect immediate full relief.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Phenylephrine / Oxymetazoline</strong>

* Topical nasal decongestants
* Cause vasoconstriction
* Can increase blood pressure
* Can cause insomnia or tachycardia

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Rebound Congestion</strong>

* Occurs with prolonged topical use.
* Congestion worsens when medication wears off.
* Medication usually needs to be discontinued.
* Tapering one nostril at a time may be used.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Finding</th>
        <th>ATI Meaning</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Worsening congestion after repeated use</strong></td>
        <td>Think rebound congestion.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Hypertension or tachycardia</strong></td>
        <td>Can occur from sympathomimetic effects.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Insomnia</strong></td>
        <td>Can result from CNS stimulation.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Continuing the spray longer</strong></td>
        <td>Will not correct rebound congestion.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Topical decongestants should only be used for a short period because prolonged use can cause rebound congestion.
</div>

</div>

### Antihistamines and Motion-Sickness Medications

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Dimenhydrinate</strong>

* Used for motion sickness and nausea.
* Can cause sedation.
* Has anticholinergic effects.
* May help with Ménière’s symptoms.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Scopolamine</strong>

* Transdermal motion-sickness medication
* Apply behind the ear
* Replace about every 3 days
* Remove the old patch first

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Adverse Effects</strong>

* Drowsiness
* Dry mouth
* Blurred vision
* Constipation
* Urinary retention

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Contraindication Cue</strong>

* BPH increases urinary-retention risk.
* Older adults are more sensitive to confusion and sedation.
* Avoid alcohol and other CNS depressants.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> A client with BPH is at increased risk for acute urinary retention from antihistamine and anticholinergic medications.
</div>

</div>

### Eye Medications

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Timolol Eye Drops</strong>

* Beta blocker used for glaucoma.
* Decreases intraocular pressure.
* Can be absorbed systemically.
* May lower heart rate and blood pressure.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Correct Administration</strong>

* Wash hands.
* Pull down the lower eyelid.
* Instill into the conjunctival sac.
* Do not touch the dropper to the eye.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>After Instillation</strong>

* Close the eyes gently.
* Do not blink repeatedly.
* Press the nasolacrimal duct.
* Apply pressure for about 60 seconds.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Why Press the Duct?</strong>

* Reduces systemic absorption
* Decreases hypotension risk
* Decreases bradycardia risk
* Keeps more medication in the eye

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Incorrect Action</th>
        <th>Why It Is Incorrect</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Blinking repeatedly</strong></td>
        <td>Can move medication out of the conjunctival sac.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Closing eyes tightly</strong></td>
        <td>Can force medication out.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Pressing the upper eyelid</strong></td>
        <td>Can displace the medication.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Skipping nasolacrimal pressure</strong></td>
        <td>Increases systemic absorption.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Timolol eye drops can still cause systemic beta-blocker effects. Monitor for bradycardia, hypotension, or breathing difficulty.
</div>

</div>

<div class="mh-columns wide-left" markdown="1">

<div class="key-findings" markdown="1">
<h3>📋 Respiratory, Allergy, Eye & ENT Big Picture</h3>

* Albuterol is a rescue bronchodilator.
* Inhaled glucocorticoids are maintenance medications.
* Use a bronchodilator before an inhaled steroid when both are prescribed.
* Rinse the mouth after inhaled glucocorticoids.
* Theophylline toxicity can cause dysrhythmias and seizures.
* Ipratropium causes anticholinergic effects.
* Topical nasal decongestants can cause rebound congestion.
* Scopolamine and dimenhydrinate can cause sedation and urinary retention.
* Nasolacrimal pressure reduces systemic absorption of eye drops.

</div>

<div class="mh-col" markdown="1">

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

* Severe tachycardia or dysrhythmia
* Seizure while taking theophylline
* White patches or severe mouth soreness
* Acute urinary retention
* Severe eye pain or sudden vision loss
* Bradycardia or hypotension after timolol
* Wheezing or breathing difficulty after eye drops
* Severe hypertension or chest pain after a decongestant

</div>

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">
<h3>ATI Tip</h3>

When inhalers are prescribed together, think: **open the airway first, then deliver the anti-inflammatory medication.**

</div>

</div>
</div>

<div class="mh-columns" markdown="1">

<div class="quick-review" markdown="1">
<h3>Quick Review</h3>

1. Use albuterol for acute bronchospasm.
2. Use inhaled steroids consistently for prevention.
3. Rinse the mouth after inhaled steroids.
4. Monitor theophylline for toxicity.
5. Watch anticholinergics for urinary retention.
6. Limit topical decongestants to short-term use.
7. Remove the old scopolamine patch before applying a new one.
8. Apply nasolacrimal pressure after eye drops.

</div>

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">
<h3>Do Not Miss</h3>

* Do not use an inhaled steroid alone as a rescue medication.
* Do not ignore frequent rescue-inhaler use.
* Do not combine multiple anticholinergics without watching for additive effects.
* Do not continue topical nasal decongestants when rebound congestion develops.
* Do not give sedating antihistamines without considering fall risk.
* Do not allow eye-drop tips to touch the eye.
* Do not skip nasolacrimal pressure with timolol.

</div>
