### Client Identification

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Use at Least Two</strong>

* Full name
* Date of birth
* Facility identification number
* Telephone number
* Other client-specific identifiers

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Compare With</strong>

* Identification wristband
* Medication administration record
* Electronic health record
* Client verbal response when able

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Do Not Use Alone</strong>

* Room number
* Bed number
* Diagnosis
* Provider name
* Physical appearance

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>When to Verify</strong>

* Before medication administration
* Before blood products
* Before procedures
* Before specimen collection
* Before treatments

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Room and bed numbers are locations, not client identifiers.
</div>

</div>

### Safe Dosage Notation

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Unsafe</th>
        <th>Safe</th>
        <th>Why</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>.5 mg</strong></td>
        <td><strong>0.5 mg</strong></td>
        <td>A leading zero prevents the decimal from being missed.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>10.0 mg</strong></td>
        <td><strong>10 mg</strong></td>
        <td>A trailing zero can be misread as 100 mg.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Unclear abbreviation</strong></td>
        <td><strong>Write the term fully</strong></td>
        <td>Unsafe abbreviations can be misinterpreted.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Leading Zero</strong>

* Use before a decimal less than 1.
* Example: 0.25 mg

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Trailing Zero</strong>

* Never use after a whole number.
* Write 5 mg, not 5.0 mg.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Clarify the Order</strong>

* Illegible dose
* Missing route
* Missing frequency
* Unusual or unsafe amount

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Nursing Responsibility</strong>

* Do not guess.
* Hold the dose when safety is uncertain.
* Contact the prescriber or pharmacist.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Cue:</strong> A trailing zero is dangerous because the decimal point can be missed.
</div>

</div>

### Routes and Dosage Forms

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Do Not Crush</strong>

* Extended-release medications
* Sustained-release medications
* Enteric-coated medications
* Delayed-release forms

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Why</strong>

* Can release the full dose at once
* Can increase toxicity
* Can destroy protective coating
* Can irritate the GI tract

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patch Safety</strong>

* Remove old patch first.
* Rotate sites.
* Date and time the patch if required.
* Wear gloves when handling high-risk patches.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Route Restriction</strong>

* Penicillin G benzathine is IM only.
* Never administer it intravenously.
* Verify route-specific formulations.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Dosage Form</th>
        <th>Safety Rule</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Delayed-release PPI</strong></td>
        <td>Swallow whole; do not chew or crush.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Nitroglycerin patch</strong></td>
        <td>Remove for the ordered medication-free interval.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Scopolamine patch</strong></td>
        <td>Remove the old patch before applying a new one.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Extended-release antibiotic</strong></td>
        <td>Do not crush unless specifically approved.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Never alter a dosage form unless it is verified as safe to crush, split, or open.
</div>

</div>

### Controlled Substances

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Secure Handling</strong>

* Do not leave at the bedside.
* Keep medication attended.
* Follow facility storage policy.
* Verify counts as required.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Waste</strong>

* A witness is required.
* Document disposal.
* Follow facility procedure.
* Do not discard without verification.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Client Refusal</strong>

* Respect the right to refuse.
* Assess the reason.
* Provide teaching.
* Document refusal.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Documentation</strong>

* Record medication given.
* Record amount wasted.
* Record witness as required.
* Document client response.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Controlled substances must never be left unattended at the client’s bedside.
</div>

</div>

### Enteral Medication Safety

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Aspiration Cues</strong>

* Cough
* Gurgled speech
* Diminished lung sounds
* Low oxygen saturation
* Respiratory distress

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Feeding Concerns</strong>

* Large gastric residual
* Abnormal tube placement
* Vomiting
* Abdominal distention
* Reduced level of consciousness

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Action</strong>

* Stop the feeding.
* Hold scheduled enteral medications.
* Position the client safely.
* Notify the provider.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Medication Technique</strong>

* Give medications separately.
* Flush before and after medications.
* Do not mix incompatible drugs.
* Do not crush unsafe dosage forms.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Signs of aspiration require the feeding and scheduled enteral medications to be stopped before routine administration continues.
</div>

</div>

### Medication Errors

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>First Priority</strong>

* Assess the client.
* Check vital signs.
* Determine what medication was given.
* Identify immediate risk.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Then</strong>

* Notify the provider.
* Perform additional monitoring.
* Give reversal treatment if ordered.
* Notify the charge nurse per policy.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Medical Record</strong>

* Document objective facts.
* Document client assessment.
* Document provider notification.
* Document treatment and response.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Incident Report</strong>

* Complete according to facility policy.
* Submit to risk management.
* Do not place it in the medical record.
* Do not document that one was completed.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Incorrect Action</th>
        <th>Why It Is Incorrect</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Document “incident report completed”</strong></td>
        <td>Incident reports are confidential and separate from the medical record.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Complete paperwork before assessing</strong></td>
        <td>Client safety and assessment come first.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Hide or alter the record</strong></td>
        <td>Documentation must remain accurate and objective.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Assume no harm occurred</strong></td>
        <td>Delayed adverse effects may still develop.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> After a medication error, assess the client before completing the incident report.
</div>

</div>

### Transfusion Reactions

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Possible Findings</strong>

* Fever or chills
* Back or chest pain
* Dyspnea
* Wheezing
* Hypotension
* Hives
* Dark urine

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>First Action</strong>

* Stop the transfusion immediately.
* Keep the IV line open with normal saline using new tubing as directed.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Next Actions</strong>

* Assess airway and vital signs.
* Notify provider and blood bank.
* Recheck client and blood identifiers.
* Follow facility protocol.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Additional Steps</strong>

* Send blood bag and tubing if required.
* Obtain urine or blood specimens as ordered.
* Document the reaction and actions taken.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> The first action for a suspected transfusion reaction is always to stop the transfusion.
</div>

</div>

### Timing and Priority

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Stat Orders</strong>

* Administer immediately.
* Verify the order.
* Assess for contraindications.
* Do not delay for routine tasks.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Pain Before Procedures</strong>

* Give prescribed analgesic before painful care.
* Allow time for it to take effect.
* Reassess pain afterward.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Assess Before Medicating</strong>

* Obtain pain rating.
* Check relevant vital signs.
* Review allergies.
* Check labs or hold parameters.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Framework</strong>

* Emergency before routine
* Acute before chronic
* Unstable before stable
* Time-sensitive before nonurgent

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Situation</th>
        <th>Priority</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Postoperative client going to physical therapy</strong></td>
        <td>Give prescribed pain medication before therapy.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Stat pain medication and wound culture ordered</strong></td>
        <td>Give the stat medication, then culture before wound care or antibiotics.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Client requests pain medication</strong></td>
        <td>Ask the client to rate the pain before administration.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Routine antacid request versus time-sensitive medication</strong></td>
        <td>Give the time-sensitive medication first.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Tip:</strong> A correct medication can still be the wrong answer if it is not the most time-sensitive action.
</div>

<div class="key-findings" markdown="1">
<h3>📋 Medication Safety Big Picture</h3>

* Use at least two client-specific identifiers.
* Never use room number or diagnosis as the main identifiers.
* Use leading zeros and avoid trailing zeros.
* Do not crush extended-release, delayed-release, or enteric-coated medications.
* Controlled-substance waste requires a witness.
* Hold enteral medications when aspiration is suspected.
* After a medication error, assess the client first.
* Incident reports do not belong in the medical record.
* Stop a transfusion immediately when a reaction is suspected.
* Give time-sensitive medications before routine requests.

</div>

<div class="mh-col" markdown="1">

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Act Immediately</h3>

* Wrong medication or wrong dose administered
* Respiratory depression after a medication
* Suspected aspiration
* Suspected transfusion reaction
* Anaphylaxis
* Severe hypotension or altered consciousness
* Unsafe or unclear medication order
* Missing controlled substance

</div>

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">
<h3>ATI Tip</h3>

Medication-safety questions often ask for the **first action**. Protect and assess the client before paperwork, reporting forms, or routine follow-up.

</div>

<div class="mh-table-card" markdown="1">

<h3>Never Do This</h3>

| Unsafe Action                                      | Correct Principle                         |
| -------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| **Use room number as identification**              | Use two client-specific identifiers       |
| **Write 5.0 mg**                                   | Write 5 mg                                |
| **Crush an extended-release tablet**               | Verify dosage form and keep it intact     |
| **Leave an opioid at bedside**                     | Keep controlled substances attended       |
| **Waste a controlled substance alone**             | Use a witness                             |
| **Continue feeding during aspiration signs**       | Stop feeding and hold enteral medications |
| **Document the incident report in the chart**      | Document facts only                       |
| **Continue a blood transfusion during a reaction** | Stop it immediately                       |
| **Guess at an unclear prescription**               | Clarify before administering              |

</div>

<div class="mh-columns" markdown="1">

<div class="quick-review" markdown="1">
<h3>Quick Review</h3>

1. Use two identifiers before every medication.
2. Never use trailing zeros.
3. Do not crush modified-release medications.
4. Witness controlled-substance waste.
5. Stop enteral feeding for aspiration signs.
6. Assess the client first after a medication error.
7. Keep incident reports separate from the medical record.
8. Stop a transfusion immediately for a suspected reaction.

</div>

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">
<h3>Do Not Miss</h3>

* Do not guess when an order is unclear.
* Do not leave a controlled substance unattended.
* Do not continue medications through a feeding tube when aspiration is suspected.
* Do not complete paperwork before checking the client.
* Do not document blame or opinions.
* Do not delay a stat medication for routine care.
* Do not continue a transfusion while notifying the provider.

</div>
