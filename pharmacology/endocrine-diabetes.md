# Endocrine & Diabetes Medications

<p class="mh-subtitle">Insulin, oral diabetes medications, thyroid drugs, corticosteroids, and bone medications</p>

### Insulin

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Insulin Type</th>
        <th>Example</th>
        <th>High-Yield ATI Cue</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Rapid acting</strong></td>
        <td>Lispro, aspart</td>
        <td>Give when food is immediately available.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Short acting</strong></td>
        <td>Regular</td>
        <td>Usually given about 30 minutes before meals; may be given IV.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Intermediate acting</strong></td>
        <td>NPH</td>
        <td>Cloudy; gently roll; has a noticeable peak.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Long acting</strong></td>
        <td>Glargine</td>
        <td>Do not mix with other insulins.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Danger</strong>

* Hypoglycemia
* Confusion
* Seizures
* Loss of consciousness

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Hypoglycemia Signs</strong>

* Shaking
* Sweating
* Hunger
* Tachycardia
* Irritability
* Confusion

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Mixing Rules</strong>

* Draw up clear before cloudy.
* Inject air into cloudy, then clear.
* Draw up regular insulin before NPH.
* Do not mix glargine.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Administration Safety</strong>

* Use the correct insulin syringe.
* Administer subcutaneously unless IV regular insulin is ordered.
* Rotate sites within the same anatomical area.
* Never give rapid insulin without food available.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Client Status</th>
        <th>Treatment</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Conscious and able to swallow</strong></td>
        <td>Give 15 g rapid carbohydrate and recheck glucose in 15 minutes.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Unconscious or unable to swallow</strong></td>
        <td>Give glucagon or IV dextrose as prescribed.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> A rapid-acting insulin dose is unsafe when the meal is delayed, unavailable, or the client cannot eat.
</div>

</div>

### Oral Diabetes Medications

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Medication</th>
        <th>What It Does</th>
        <th>Most-Tested Risk</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Metformin</strong></td>
        <td>Decreases liver glucose production</td>
        <td>Lactic acidosis</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Glipizide</strong></td>
        <td>Stimulates insulin release</td>
        <td>Hypoglycemia</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Pioglitazone</strong></td>
        <td>Improves insulin sensitivity</td>
        <td>Fluid retention and heart failure</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Acarbose</strong></td>
        <td>Delays carbohydrate absorption</td>
        <td>GI effects; take with first bite</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Repaglinide</strong></td>
        <td>Stimulates short-term insulin release</td>
        <td>Hypoglycemia; take before meals</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Metformin Danger</strong>

* Lactic acidosis
* Muscle pain
* Hyperventilation
* Severe weakness
* Dizziness

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Metformin Monitoring</strong>

* Kidney function
* Blood glucose
* Signs of lactic acidosis
* Hold around iodinated contrast as ordered

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Sulfonylurea Safety</strong>

* Glipizide can cause hypoglycemia.
* Take as directed around meals.
* Avoid skipping meals.
* Use caution with sulfonamide allergy.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Long-Term Effectiveness</strong>

* HbA1c reflects average glucose over 2–3 months.
* A single fasting glucose shows current control only.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Muscle pain, hyperventilation, and dizziness while taking metformin can indicate lactic acidosis and require immediate follow-up.
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Pioglitazone Warning:</strong> Report edema, rapid weight gain, or shortness of breath because these can indicate heart failure.
</div>

</div>

### Injectable Non-Insulin Diabetes Medications

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Example</strong>

* Exenatide
* GLP-1 receptor agonist
* Helps lower blood glucose
* Slows gastric emptying

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Danger</strong>

* Pancreatitis
* Severe persistent abdominal pain
* Pain may occur with vomiting

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Effects</strong>

* Nausea
* Decreased appetite
* Possible weight loss
* GI discomfort

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Hold / Report</strong>

* Severe persistent abdominal pain
* Repeated vomiting
* Signs of dehydration
* Suspected pancreatitis

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Severe abdominal pain is more urgent than expected appetite loss or mild GI upset.
</div>

</div>

### Antithyroid Medications

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Examples</strong>

* Methimazole
* Propylthiouracil
* Reduce thyroid hormone production

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Danger</strong>

* Agranulocytosis
* Severe infection risk
* Fever
* Sore throat

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor</strong>

* Thyroid hormone levels
* CBC as ordered
* Liver function
* Signs of infection

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Therapeutic Effect</strong>

* Decreased T4
* Heart rate returns toward normal
* Reduced tremor and heat intolerance
* Improved weight stability

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Finding</th>
        <th>Meaning</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Fever or sore throat</strong></td>
        <td>Possible agranulocytosis; hold medication and notify provider.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Decreased T4</strong></td>
        <td>Expected therapeutic response.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Weight loss and tachycardia continue</strong></td>
        <td>Hyperthyroidism may not be controlled.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Jaundice or severe abdominal pain</strong></td>
        <td>Possible liver injury, especially with propylthiouracil.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Fever and sore throat are not routine cold symptoms in a client taking an antithyroid medication. Think agranulocytosis.
</div>

</div>

### Corticosteroids

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Examples</strong>

* Prednisone
* Methylprednisolone
* Dexamethasone
* Hydrocortisone

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Most-Tested Effects</strong>

* Hyperglycemia
* Infection risk
* Fluid and sodium retention
* Hypokalemia
* Osteoporosis
* GI irritation

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor</strong>

* Blood glucose
* Potassium
* Blood pressure
* Weight and edema
* WBC and infection signs
* Bone health with long-term use

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Teaching</strong>

* Take with food.
* Do not stop abruptly.
* Avoid people with active infections.
* Report fever or poor wound healing.
* Monitor glucose if diabetic.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Laboratory / Finding</th>
        <th>Expected Effect</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Blood glucose</strong></td>
        <td>Can increase.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Serum potassium</strong></td>
        <td>Can decrease.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Sodium and fluid</strong></td>
        <td>Can be retained.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>WBC count</strong></td>
        <td>Can increase; infection signs may be masked.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Do Not Miss:</strong> Corticosteroids suppress inflammation and immunity, so a serious infection may produce fewer obvious symptoms.
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Safety:</strong> Long-term corticosteroids must be tapered because abrupt withdrawal can cause adrenal insufficiency.
</div>

</div>

### Bone Medications

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Alendronate</strong>

* Bisphosphonate
* Used to reduce bone loss
* Commonly used for osteoporosis

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>How to Take It</strong>

* Take immediately after arising.
* Take on an empty stomach.
* Use 240 mL or 8 oz plain water.
* Wait at least 30 minutes before food or other drinks.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Safety</strong>

* Sit or stand upright for at least 30 minutes.
* Swallow the tablet whole.
* Do not take with antacids.
* Avoid lying down after taking it.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Danger</strong>

* Esophageal irritation
* Esophageal ulceration
* Difficulty swallowing
* Chest pain

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Alendronate administration questions are usually testing prevention of esophageal injury.
</div>

</div>

<div class="mh-columns wide-left" markdown="1">

<div class="key-findings" markdown="1">
<h3>📋 Endocrine Big Picture</h3>

* Rapid-acting insulin requires food immediately.
* Regular insulin is clear and may be administered IV.
* NPH is cloudy and should be gently rolled.
* Draw up clear insulin before cloudy insulin.
* Metformin can cause lactic acidosis.
* Glipizide can cause hypoglycemia.
* Pioglitazone can worsen heart failure.
* Exenatide can cause pancreatitis.
* Antithyroid medications can cause agranulocytosis.
* Corticosteroids increase glucose and infection risk.
* Alendronate must be taken upright with plain water.

</div>

<div class="mh-col" markdown="1">

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

* Confusion, seizure, or loss of consciousness from hypoglycemia
* Muscle pain with hyperventilation while taking metformin
* Severe persistent abdominal pain
* Fever or sore throat while taking an antithyroid medication
* Jaundice or signs of liver injury
* Rapid weight gain, edema, or dyspnea with pioglitazone
* Severe infection signs while taking corticosteroids
* Chest pain or difficulty swallowing after alendronate

</div>

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">
<h3>ATI Tip</h3>

For diabetes medications, distinguish **expected GI effects** from dangerous findings such as hypoglycemia, lactic acidosis, pancreatitis, or heart failure.

</div>

</div>
</div>

<div class="mh-table-card" markdown="1">

<h3>Quick Recognition Chart</h3>

| Clue                               | Think                       |
| ---------------------------------- | --------------------------- |
| **Sweating, shaking, confusion**   | Hypoglycemia                |
| **Clear before cloudy**            | Regular insulin before NPH  |
| **Muscle pain + hyperventilation** | Metformin lactic acidosis   |
| **Severe abdominal pain**          | Exenatide pancreatitis      |
| **Edema + dyspnea**                | Pioglitazone heart failure  |
| **Fever + sore throat**            | Antithyroid agranulocytosis |
| **High glucose + low potassium**   | Corticosteroid effects      |
| **Upright for 30 minutes**         | Alendronate                 |

</div>

<div class="mh-columns" markdown="1">

<div class="quick-review" markdown="1">
<h3>Quick Review</h3>

1. Match insulin timing to food availability.
2. Treat hypoglycemia immediately.
3. Draw up regular insulin before NPH.
4. Do not mix glargine.
5. Watch metformin for lactic acidosis.
6. Hold exenatide for severe abdominal pain.
7. Report fever or sore throat with antithyroid medications.
8. Never abruptly stop long-term corticosteroids.

</div>

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">
<h3>Do Not Miss</h3>

* Do not give rapid-acting insulin when the client cannot eat.
* Do not shake NPH insulin.
* Do not ignore muscle pain and hyperventilation with metformin.
* Do not dismiss severe abdominal pain as routine medication nausea.
* Do not overlook edema or shortness of breath with pioglitazone.
* Do not treat fever or sore throat as minor during antithyroid therapy.
* Do not allow a client to lie down after taking alendronate.

</div>
