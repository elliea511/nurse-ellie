# Anti-Infective Medications

<p class="mh-subtitle">Antibiotics, antivirals, antifungals, tuberculosis medications, toxicities, monitoring, and patient teaching</p>

ATI usually tests anti-infective medications by asking about **allergic reactions, nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity, superinfection, tendon injury, photosensitivity, infusion reactions, liver injury, medication interactions, and completion of therapy.**

<div class="priority-focus">
  <div>
    <h3>Priority Focus</h3>
    Culture Before Therapy • Verify Allergies • Give on Schedule • Complete Therapy • Monitor Kidney and Liver Function • Watch for Superinfection
  </div>
</div>

<div class="mh-columns" markdown="1">

<div class="key-findings" markdown="1">

<h3>Core Rules for All Anti-Infectives</h3>

1. Obtain ordered cultures **before the first dose** whenever possible.
2. Do not delay emergency treatment when cultures cannot be obtained immediately.
3. Give doses at evenly spaced intervals to maintain therapeutic medication levels.
4. Complete the entire prescribed course unless the provider discontinues it.
5. Never save leftover antibiotics or take medication prescribed for someone else.
6. Monitor for allergic reactions, organ toxicity, and superinfection.
7. Severe or persistent diarrhea can indicate **antibiotic-associated colitis or C. difficile**.
8. Review pregnancy status, allergies, kidney function, liver function, and medication interactions.

</div>

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">

<h3>Birth-Control Clarification</h3>

Most antibiotics do **not** directly make hormonal contraception ineffective.

The most important ATI-tested exceptions are:

* Rifampin
* Rifapentine
* Rifabutin

These medications increase the breakdown of hormonal contraceptives. The client may need an additional or alternative contraceptive method.

Vomiting or severe diarrhea can also reduce absorption of oral contraceptive pills.

### Penicillins and Cephalosporins

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Penicillins</strong>

* Penicillin G
* Penicillin V
* Amoxicillin
* Ampicillin
* Piperacillin-tazobactam

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Cephalosporins</strong>

* Cephalexin
* Cefazolin
* Ceftriaxone
* Cefotetan
* Medication names commonly begin with **cef-** or **ceph-**

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Most-Tested Risks</strong>

* Rash
* Urticaria
* Anaphylaxis
* Nausea or diarrhea
* C. difficile infection
* Possible cross-sensitivity

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Teaching</strong>

* Complete the entire prescription.
* Report rash, wheezing, or swelling.
* Report severe or persistent diarrhea.
* Do not use leftover medication.
* Take at evenly spaced intervals.

</div>

#### Allergy Assessment

Before administration, ask the client:

* What medication caused the reaction?
* What exactly happened?
* How quickly did the reaction occur?
* Did the client experience hives, wheezing, swelling, or anaphylaxis?
* Was the “allergy” actually nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea?

GI upset is an adverse effect, but it is not the same as an immediate hypersensitivity reaction.

#### Administration Notes

* **Penicillin G benzathine is administered IM and must never be given IV.**
* Penicillin V is generally absorbed best on an empty stomach, but follow product-specific instructions.
* Amoxicillin may generally be taken with or without food.
* Food may reduce GI upset for medications that allow administration with meals.
* Probenecid decreases renal excretion of penicillin and can increase penicillin levels.

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> A client who develops throat tightness, wheezing, hypotension, or facial swelling after an antibiotic requires immediate treatment for possible anaphylaxis.

### Aminoglycosides

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Examples</strong>

* Gentamicin
* Amikacin
* Tobramycin
* Streptomycin
* Neomycin

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Dangers</strong>

* Nephrotoxicity
* Ototoxicity
* Vestibular damage
* Neuromuscular blockade
* Medication accumulation

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor</strong>

* Serum creatinine
* BUN
* Urine output
* Hearing
* Tinnitus
* Dizziness or balance changes
* Medication levels when ordered

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Report Immediately</strong>

* Tinnitus
* Hearing loss
* Vertigo
* Balance problems
* Decreased urine output
* Rising creatinine
* Muscle weakness
* Respiratory difficulty

</div>

</div>

#### Route Matters

Aminoglycosides are poorly absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract.

* **IV or IM administration:** Produces systemic effects.
* **Oral neomycin:** Acts mainly inside the intestine and may be prescribed for bowel decontamination or reduction of ammonia-producing intestinal bacteria.
* Oral therapy does not provide reliable systemic treatment for an infection elsewhere in the body.

#### Peak and Trough Levels

* **Peak:** Helps determine whether the medication reaches an effective concentration.
* **Trough:** Helps determine whether too much medication remains before the next dose.
* The trough is generally drawn shortly before the next dose.
* Peak timing depends on the medication, route, infusion time, and facility protocol.
* An elevated trough suggests impaired clearance and increased toxicity risk.
* Do not memorize one universal therapeutic range because target levels differ by medication and clinical indication.

#### Neuromuscular Blockade

Aminoglycosides can worsen neuromuscular weakness.

Use cautiously in clients with:

* Myasthenia gravis
* Other neuromuscular disorders
* Concurrent neuromuscular-blocking medications

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Memory Cue:</strong> Aminoglycosides damage the organs shaped like beans or ears: **kidneys and hearing**.

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Tinnitus or reduced urine output is more urgent than mild nausea because it can indicate permanent ototoxicity or nephrotoxicity.

</div>

</div>

### Vancomycin

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Uses</strong>

* Serious gram-positive infections
* MRSA
* Other resistant organisms
* C. difficile infection when given orally

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Dangers</strong>

* Nephrotoxicity
* Vancomycin infusion reaction
* Hypotension
* Phlebitis
* Possible ototoxicity

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Infusion Reaction</strong>

* Flushing
* Itching
* Erythematous rash
* Face, neck, or upper-torso involvement
* Tachycardia
* Hypotension
* Associated with rapid IV infusion

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor</strong>

* Serum creatinine
* Urine output
* Blood pressure
* IV site
* Infusion rate
* AUC or trough levels when ordered
* Hearing when clinically indicated

</div>

</div>

#### Route Matters

| Route                                      | Primary Use                                     |
| ------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------- |
| **IV vancomycin**                          | Systemic infections such as MRSA bacteremia     |
| **Oral vancomycin**                        | Intestinal C. difficile infection               |
| **Oral vancomycin for systemic infection** | Not appropriate because oral absorption is poor |

#### If an Infusion Reaction Occurs

1. Stop or slow the infusion according to facility protocol and provider instructions.
2. Assess airway, breathing, circulation, and blood pressure.
3. Notify the provider.
4. Administer prescribed antihistamines or supportive medications.
5. Restart at a slower rate only when ordered and clinically appropriate.

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Do Not Confuse:</strong> A vancomycin infusion reaction is related to rapid histamine release and does not automatically mean the client has a true IgE-mediated allergy.

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Hypotension, airway symptoms, or extensive flushing during an infusion requires immediate assessment and intervention.

</div>

</div>

### Macrolides

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Examples</strong>

* Azithromycin
* Clarithromycin
* Erythromycin
* Medication names commonly end in **-thromycin**

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Uses</strong>

* Respiratory infections
* Atypical pneumonia
* Chlamydial infections
* Pertussis
* Alternative for some clients with penicillin allergy
* H. pylori regimens may include clarithromycin

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Risks</strong>

* GI upset
* Hepatotoxicity
* QT prolongation
* Dysrhythmias
* Medication interactions

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Teaching</strong>

* Take exactly as directed.
* Report palpitations or fainting.
* Report jaundice or dark urine.
* Review all medications for interactions.
* Do not crush enteric-coated tablets.

</div>

</div>

#### Erythromycin Administration

* Some erythromycin products are acid-sensitive or enteric coated.
* Do not crush or chew enteric-coated or delayed-release tablets.
* Food instructions vary by formulation.
* Follow the instructions for the specific product rather than applying one rule to every macrolide.

#### QT-Prolongation Risk

Use cautiously in clients with:

* Existing prolonged QT interval
* Hypokalemia or hypomagnesemia
* Bradycardia
* Concurrent QT-prolonging medications
* History of serious ventricular dysrhythmias

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Palpitations, syncope, or a new dysrhythmia is more urgent than expected nausea.

</div>

</div>

### Tetracyclines

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Examples</strong>

* Doxycycline
* Tetracycline
* Minocycline
* Medication names commonly contain **-cycline**

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Uses</strong>

* Acne
* Chlamydia
* Tick-borne infections
* Atypical respiratory infections
* Certain zoonotic infections

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Teaching</strong>

* Take with a full glass of water.
* Remain upright for at least 30 minutes.
* Use sunscreen and protective clothing.
* Complete the prescription.
* Report severe headache or vision changes.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Separate From</strong>

* Antacids
* Calcium supplements
* Iron supplements
* Magnesium
* Zinc
* Dairy near the dose when instructed

</div>

</div>

#### Why Minerals Matter

Calcium, iron, magnesium, aluminum, and zinc can bind with tetracyclines and decrease absorption.

Follow the medication-specific instructions for how many hours to separate the doses.

#### Pregnancy and Children

Tetracyclines can affect developing teeth and bone.

They are generally avoided during:

* Pregnancy
* Breastfeeding when contraindicated for the prescribed agent
* Childhood younger than 8 years unless the expected benefit outweighs the risk

Doxycycline may still be used for certain serious infections, such as some tick-borne diseases, when clinically indicated.

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Taking doxycycline with little water immediately before lying down increases the risk of esophagitis and esophageal ulceration.

</div>

</div>

### Fluoroquinolones

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Examples</strong>

* Ciprofloxacin
* Levofloxacin
* Moxifloxacin
* Medication names commonly end in **-floxacin**

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Dangers</strong>

* Tendinitis
* Tendon rupture
* Peripheral neuropathy
* CNS effects
* QT prolongation
* Dysglycemia
* C. difficile infection

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Higher Tendon Risk</strong>

* Older adults
* Corticosteroid use
* Organ-transplant recipients
* Kidney disease
* History of tendon disorders

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Teaching</strong>

* Report tendon pain immediately.
* Stop strenuous activity if symptoms occur.
* Report numbness, burning, or tingling.
* Use sunscreen.
* Maintain hydration if allowed.
* Complete therapy as prescribed.

</div>

</div>

#### Absorption Interactions

Separate fluoroquinolones from:

* Antacids
* Iron
* Calcium supplements
* Magnesium
* Aluminum
* Zinc
* Multivitamins containing minerals

These products can significantly reduce medication absorption.

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> New heel, shoulder, or tendon pain is not expected exercise soreness. Hold the medication, restrict stress on the tendon, and notify the provider.

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Also Report:</strong> Burning, numbness, tingling, confusion, hallucinations, severe blood-glucose changes, or palpitations.

</div>

</div>

#### Nitrofurantoin

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Use</strong>

* Primarily treats lower urinary-tract infections.
* Does not achieve adequate tissue levels for many systemic infections.
* Is not the preferred medication for pyelonephritis.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Teaching</strong>

* Take with food when directed.
* Complete the prescription.
* Urine may become dark yellow or brown.
* Report persistent cough or shortness of breath.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Serious Risks</strong>

* Pulmonary toxicity
* Hepatotoxicity
* Peripheral neuropathy
* Hemolysis in clients with G6PD deficiency

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor</strong>

* Kidney function
* Respiratory symptoms
* Liver-injury symptoms
* Neurologic changes

</div>

</div>

#### Methenamine

* Used for prevention or suppression of recurrent UTIs in selected clients.
* Works best when the urine remains acidic.
* It is not used to treat severe systemic infection.
* The client should follow prescribed urine-acidification instructions.
* Do not automatically recommend large amounts of cranberry juice without checking for contraindications and medication interactions.

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Distinction:</strong> Phenazopyridine relieves urinary pain. It does not kill the organism and does not replace an antibiotic.

</div>

</div>

### Chloramphenicol

Chloramphenicol is generally reserved for serious infections when safer alternatives are not appropriate.

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Potential Uses</strong>

* Selected severe bacterial infections
* Serious infections when alternatives are ineffective or contraindicated
* Use is limited because of toxicity

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Dangers</strong>

* Reversible bone-marrow suppression
* Irreversible aplastic anemia
* Leukopenia
* Thrombocytopenia
* Anemia

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Gray Baby Syndrome</strong>

* Abdominal distention
* Vomiting
* Hypotension
* Cyanosis or gray discoloration
* Cardiovascular collapse

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor</strong>

* CBC
* Reticulocyte count when ordered
* Medication levels when ordered
* Infection signs
* Bleeding
* Fatigue and pallor

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Fever, sore throat, unusual bleeding, bruising, or profound fatigue can indicate bone-marrow toxicity.

</div>

</div>

### Tuberculosis Medications

Active tuberculosis is treated with **multiple medications** to cure infection and prevent resistance.

A common medication pattern is remembered as **RIPE**:

* **R**ifampin
* **I**soniazid
* **P**yrazinamide
* **E**thambutol

Treatment length varies according to the regimen, drug susceptibility, age, disease severity, response, and other clinical factors.

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Rifampin</strong>

* Orange-red urine, sweat, saliva, and tears are expected.
* Soft contact lenses can become permanently stained.
* Review all medication interactions.
* Report jaundice, anorexia, or abdominal pain.
* Use additional or alternative contraception as instructed.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Isoniazid</strong>

* Monitor liver function when indicated.
* Avoid alcohol.
* Report numbness or tingling.
* Vitamin B6 helps prevent peripheral neuropathy.
* Review interactions, including phenytoin.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Pyrazinamide</strong>

* Can increase uric-acid levels.
* Can trigger joint pain or gout.
* Can cause liver injury.
* Report severe nausea, anorexia, or jaundice.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Ethambutol</strong>

* Obtain baseline and follow-up visual assessment as ordered.
* Report blurred vision.
* Report reduced visual acuity.
* Report difficulty distinguishing red from green.

</div>

#### Additional TB Medications

**Streptomycin**

* Aminoglycoside
* Ototoxicity
* Nephrotoxicity
* Often administered IM when used
* Monitor hearing and kidney function

**Para-aminosalicylic acid**

* GI irritation is common.
* May be given with food when prescribed.
* Is not part of the usual first-line RIPE abbreviation.

#### Treatment Rules

* Take every medication exactly as prescribed.
* Do not stop medications when symptoms improve.
* Keep laboratory and vision-monitoring appointments.
* Review all other medications for interactions.
* Directly observed therapy or video-observed therapy may be used.
* Failure to adhere can result in treatment failure and drug resistance.

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Vision changes with ethambutol, numbness with isoniazid, jaundice with RIPE medications, or severe medication interactions with rifampin require prompt reporting.

</div>

</div>

### Antivirals and HIV Therapy

#### Herpes Antivirals

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Examples</strong>

* Acyclovir
* Valacyclovir
* Famciclovir

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Purpose</strong>

* Reduce severity and duration of outbreaks
* Reduce viral shedding
* Increase time between outbreaks
* Do not eliminate latent herpes virus

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Safety</strong>

* Encourage hydration if not contraindicated.
* Monitor kidney function.
* Adjust doses for renal impairment.
* Begin therapy early when prescribed.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Teaching</strong>

* The medication does not cure herpes.
* Transmission can occur without visible lesions.
* Avoid sexual contact during active lesions.
* Follow safer-sex recommendations.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Decreased urine output or rising creatinine during IV acyclovir can indicate kidney injury or crystalluria.

</div>

#### HIV Antiretroviral Therapy

Antiretroviral therapy, or **ART**, suppresses HIV replication and helps preserve immune function.

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>ART Goals</strong>

* Achieve viral suppression
* Maintain or improve CD4 count
* Prevent opportunistic infections
* Prevent progression to AIDS
* Reduce transmission

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Adherence</strong>

* Take medications exactly as prescribed.
* Missing doses allows HIV to replicate.
* Inconsistent therapy can cause resistance.
* Do not stop therapy because the client feels well.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor</strong>

* Viral load
* CD4 count
* CBC
* Kidney and liver function
* Glucose and lipid levels for selected medications
* Medication-specific adverse effects

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Transmission Teaching</strong>

* Sustained viral suppression prevents sexual HIV transmission.
* ART does not prevent other sexually transmitted infections.
* Follow current recommendations for condoms, STI prevention, and needle safety.
* Viral suppression must be maintained through adherence.

</div>

</div>

#### Zidovudine

Zidovudine is an older nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitor that may still appear in ATI questions.

**Priority adverse effects:**

* Anemia
* Neutropenia
* Bone-marrow suppression
* Fatigue
* Infection risk

**Monitor:**

* CBC
* Hemoglobin and hematocrit
* Neutrophil count
* Signs of infection
* Pallor and fatigue

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Correction:</strong> A client who achieves and maintains an undetectable viral load does not sexually transmit HIV. This is known as **U=U: Undetectable = Untransmittable**.

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">

For the full disease process, transmission, stages, laboratory findings, opportunistic infections, and nursing priorities, review <a href="{{ '/immunologic/hiv-aids.html' | relative_url }}"><strong>HIV & AIDS</strong></a>.

</div>

</div>

### Antifungals

#### Amphotericin B

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Use</strong>

* Serious systemic fungal infections
* Potentially life-threatening fungal disease
* High-risk medication because of significant toxicity

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Infusion Reactions</strong>

* Fever
* Chills
* Headache
* Nausea
* Hypotension
* Rigors

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Major Toxicities</strong>

* Nephrotoxicity
* Hypokalemia
* Hypomagnesemia
* Anemia
* Dysrhythmias
* Liver injury

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor</strong>

* Creatinine and BUN
* Urine output
* Potassium
* Magnesium
* CBC
* Liver function
* Blood pressure

</div>

</div>

#### Administration

* A test dose may be prescribed.
* Premedications may include acetaminophen, diphenhydramine, corticosteroids, or antiemetics.
* Monitor closely during the infusion.
* Maintain hydration as prescribed.
* Report decreased urine output, palpitations, severe weakness, or hypotension.

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Memory Cue:</strong> “Ampho-terrible” affects the **kidneys, potassium, magnesium, blood count, and blood pressure**.

</div>

#### Nystatin

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Uses</strong>

* Oral candidiasis
* Skin candidiasis
* Vaginal candidiasis
* Local fungal infection

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Oral Suspension Teaching</strong>

* Shake the suspension.
* Swish thoroughly around the mouth.
* Swallow if prescribed as “swish and swallow.”
* Avoid eating or drinking immediately afterward.
* Continue for the prescribed duration.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Topical Teaching</strong>

* Clean and dry the affected area.
* Apply as prescribed.
* Avoid occlusive dressings unless ordered.
* Continue treatment after symptoms improve when prescribed.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Expected Outcome</strong>

* Decreased white patches
* Reduced oral discomfort
* Improved swallowing
* Resolution of local fungal symptoms

</div>

</div>

#### Azole Antifungals

Common examples include:

* Fluconazole
* Ketoconazole
* Voriconazole

Priority considerations can include:

* Hepatotoxicity
* QT prolongation
* Medication interactions
* GI upset
* Monitoring liver function with systemic therapy

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Decreased urine output during amphotericin B therapy is more urgent than expected fever or chills because it can indicate nephrotoxicity.

</div>

<div class="mh-columns wide-left" markdown="1">

<div class="key-findings" markdown="1">

<h3>📋 Anti-Infective Big Picture</h3>

* Obtain cultures before the first dose whenever possible.
* Maintain evenly spaced medication levels.
* Verify the specific allergy reaction.
* Complete therapy exactly as prescribed.
* Aminoglycosides affect the kidneys and ears.
* Vancomycin must be infused at the prescribed rate.
* Fluoroquinolones can cause tendon and nerve injury.
* Tetracyclines interact with minerals and cause photosensitivity.
* Sulfonamides can cause severe skin reactions.
* RIPE medications require medication-specific monitoring.
* ART requires consistent adherence to prevent resistance.
* Amphotericin B can cause severe renal and electrolyte toxicity.

</div>

<div class="mh-col" markdown="1">

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">

<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

* Wheezing, facial swelling, or throat tightness
* Severe watery diarrhea
* Blistering rash or mucosal sores
* Hearing loss, tinnitus, or vertigo
* Decreased urine output
* New tendon pain
* Numbness, burning, or tingling
* Hypotension during an infusion
* Jaundice or severe abdominal pain
* Blurred or altered color vision
* Severe weakness or palpitations
* Fever with neutropenia

</div>

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">

<h3>ATI Priority Rule</h3>

When several adverse effects are listed, prioritize:

1. Airway or anaphylaxis
2. Unstable vital signs
3. Kidney injury
4. Hearing or vision loss
5. Severe skin reactions
6. Tendon or neurologic injury
7. Liver injury
8. Severe superinfection

</div>

</div>
</div>

<div class="mh-columns" markdown="1">

<div class="quick-review" markdown="1">

<h3>Quick Review</h3>

1. Culture before therapy when possible.
2. Verify allergies before administration.
3. Complete the entire prescribed course.
4. Monitor kidney function with aminoglycosides, vancomycin, acyclovir, and amphotericin B.
5. Report tinnitus during aminoglycoside therapy.
6. Report tendon pain during fluoroquinolone therapy.
7. Take doxycycline with water and remain upright.
8. Infuse IV vancomycin slowly as ordered.
9. Remember RIPE for tuberculosis.
10. Give vitamin B6 with isoniazid when prescribed.
11. Report vision changes with ethambutol.
12. Monitor ART adherence and medication-specific toxicity.

</div>

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">

<h3>Do Not Miss</h3>

* Never administer benzathine penicillin IV.
* Do not dismiss a new antibiotic rash.
* Do not collect a urine culture from the drainage bag.
* Do not ignore an elevated aminoglycoside trough.
* Do not continue strenuous activity with tendon pain.
* Do not take tetracyclines or fluoroquinolones at the same time as mineral supplements.
* Do not rapidly infuse vancomycin.
* Do not tell every client that all antibiotics reduce birth-control effectiveness.
* Do not confuse oral and IV vancomycin indications.
* Do not stop TB medications when symptoms improve.
* Do not ignore blurred vision with ethambutol.
* Do not describe ART as a cure for HIV.

</div>
