### Beta Blockers

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Examples</strong>

* Metoprolol
* Atenolol
* Propranolol
* Carvedilol

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>What They Do</strong>

* Slow heart rate.
* Decrease blood pressure.
* Reduce myocardial oxygen demand.
* Slow conduction through the AV node.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Most-Tested Adverse Effects</strong>

* Bradycardia
* Hypotension
* Fatigue
* Dizziness
* Depression
* Worsening heart failure

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor</strong>

* Apical pulse
* Blood pressure
* Signs of heart failure
* Blood glucose in clients with diabetes

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>ATI Cue</th>
        <th>Nursing Meaning</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Heart rate below parameter</strong></td>
        <td>Hold the medication and notify the provider.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Diabetes mellitus</strong></td>
        <td>Beta blockers can mask tachycardia associated with hypoglycemia.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Peripheral edema or dyspnea</strong></td>
        <td>Can indicate worsening heart failure.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Sudden discontinuation</strong></td>
        <td>Can cause rebound tachycardia, hypertension, or angina.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Check the pulse before administration. A client with significant bradycardia should not receive the scheduled dose until the provider is notified.
</div>

</div>

### ACE Inhibitors

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Examples</strong>

* Enalapril
* Lisinopril
* Captopril

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>How ATI Identifies Them</strong>

* Medication names often end in **-pril**.
* Used for hypertension, heart failure, and kidney protection in selected clients.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Most-Tested Adverse Effects</strong>

* Persistent dry cough
* Hyperkalemia
* Hypotension
* Dizziness
* Angioedema

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor</strong>

* Blood pressure
* Potassium
* Kidney function
* Facial or airway swelling

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Finding</th>
        <th>Action</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Persistent dry cough</strong></td>
        <td>Notify the provider; another medication class may be prescribed.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Facial, tongue, or throat swelling</strong></td>
        <td>Emergency angioedema. Hold medication and protect the airway.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Elevated potassium</strong></td>
        <td>Hold and notify the provider as ordered.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Pregnancy</strong></td>
        <td>ACE inhibitors are contraindicated.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Do Not Miss:</strong> Avoid potassium supplements or salt substitutes containing potassium unless specifically prescribed.
</div>

</div>

### Diuretics

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Class</th>
        <th>Example</th>
        <th>Major Electrolyte Risk</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Loop</strong></td>
        <td>Furosemide</td>
        <td>Hypokalemia</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Thiazide</strong></td>
        <td>Hydrochlorothiazide</td>
        <td>Hypokalemia, hyponatremia, hypercalcemia</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Potassium-sparing</strong></td>
        <td>Eplerenone, spironolactone</td>
        <td>Hyperkalemia</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Loop Diuretics</strong>

* Strong diuresis
* Hypokalemia
* Dehydration
* Hypotension
* Possible ototoxicity at high doses

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Thiazide Diuretics</strong>

* Hypokalemia
* Hyponatremia
* Hypomagnesemia
* Hypercalcemia
* Hyperglycemia may occur

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Potassium-Sparing Diuretics</strong>

* Hyperkalemia
* Hyponatremia
* Avoid potassium supplements unless ordered
* Monitor kidney function

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Teaching</strong>

* Take earlier in the day.
* Change positions slowly.
* Monitor daily weight if instructed.
* Report weakness, palpitations, or reduced urine output.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Furosemide Teaching:</strong> Increase potassium-rich foods if prescribed, take no later than late afternoon, and rise slowly to prevent falls.
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Allergy Cue:</strong> Furosemide, thiazides, and sulfonylureas are chemically related to sulfonamides. A severe prior sulfonamide reaction requires provider review.
</div>

</div>

### Nitroglycerin

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>What It Does</strong>

* Causes vasodilation.
* Reduces cardiac workload.
* Decreases myocardial oxygen demand.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Adverse Effects</strong>

* Headache
* Dizziness
* Orthostatic hypotension
* Reflex tachycardia
* Blurred vision

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Sublingual Form</strong>

* Used for acute chest pain.
* Sit or lie down before taking.
* Follow prescribed emergency instructions.
* Seek emergency care if pain is not relieved as directed.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patch Teaching</strong>

* Not for acute chest pain.
* Rotate application sites.
* Wear for about 12–14 hours.
* Remove for a 10–12 hour medication-free interval.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Situation</th>
        <th>Correct Teaching</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Acute angina while wearing a patch</strong></td>
        <td>Use prescribed short-acting sublingual nitroglycerin; do not apply a second patch.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Headache after starting therapy</strong></td>
        <td>Common due to vasodilation; a mild analgesic may be prescribed.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Dizziness when standing</strong></td>
        <td>Change positions slowly to prevent falls.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Alcohol use</strong></td>
        <td>Limit or avoid because severe hypotension can occur.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Major Interaction:</strong> Nitroglycerin must not be combined with erectile-dysfunction medications such as sildenafil because life-threatening hypotension can occur.
</div>

</div>

### Digoxin

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>What It Does</strong>

* Strengthens cardiac contraction.
* Slows heart rate.
* Used for selected clients with heart failure or dysrhythmias.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Toxicity Signs</strong>

* Nausea or vomiting
* Anorexia
* Fatigue
* Bradycardia
* Dysrhythmias
* Yellow-green vision
* Halos or blurred vision

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor</strong>

* Apical pulse for 1 full minute
* Heart rhythm
* Potassium
* Kidney function
* Digoxin level when ordered

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Hold / Report</strong>

* Pulse below prescribed parameter
* New dysrhythmia
* Visual changes
* Unexplained nausea or anorexia
* Signs of toxicity

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Do Not Miss:</strong> Hypokalemia increases digoxin toxicity. Clients taking both digoxin and a loop diuretic need close potassium monitoring.
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Antidote:</strong> Digoxin immune Fab.
</div>

</div>

<div class="mh-detail orange" id="panel-statins" markdown="1">

### Statins

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Examples</strong>

* Simvastatin
* Atorvastatin
* Rosuvastatin

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>What They Do</strong>

* Lower LDL cholesterol.
* Reduce cardiovascular risk.
* Decrease cholesterol production in the liver.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Danger</strong>

* Myopathy
* Rhabdomyolysis
* Liver injury

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor / Report</strong>

* Report unexplained muscle pain or tenderness.
* Report dark urine or severe weakness.
* Monitor liver enzymes as ordered.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Muscle pain is not simply expected soreness. It can indicate rhabdomyolysis and requires prompt follow-up.
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Bleeding Precautions</strong>

* Use a soft toothbrush.
* Use an electric razor.
* Avoid unnecessary IM injections.
* Apply pressure longer after venipuncture.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Report Immediately</strong>

* Black or tarry stools
* Hematuria
* Vomiting blood
* Severe headache
* Unusual bruising
* Bleeding that will not stop

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Warfarin Teaching</strong>

* Maintain consistent vitamin K intake.
* Keep INR appointments.
* Many medications and foods interact.
* Do not double missed doses.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Enoxaparin Technique</strong>

* Inject into the abdomen.
* Do not expel the air bubble from a prefilled syringe.
* Do not rub or massage the site.
* Rotate injection locations.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Heparin Safety:</strong> A major platelet decrease can indicate heparin-induced thrombocytopenia. Stop the medication and notify the provider as ordered.
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Aspirin Toxicity:</strong> Tinnitus, sweating, nausea, and rapid breathing can indicate salicylate toxicity.
</div>

</div>

### Blood Cell Medications

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Epoetin Alfa</strong>

* Stimulates red blood cell production.
* Used for anemia related to kidney disease or selected treatments.
* Expected effect: increased hemoglobin.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor</strong>

* Hemoglobin and hematocrit
* Blood pressure
* Iron status
* Signs of thrombosis

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Risks</strong>

* Hypertension
* Blood clots
* Stroke or cardiovascular events if hemoglobin rises too high

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Cue</strong>

* Epoetin does not improve kidney function.
* It does not increase white blood cells or platelets.
* The desired response is improved hemoglobin.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Do Not Miss:</strong> Adequate iron is needed for epoetin to work effectively.
</div>

</div>

<div class="mh-columns wide-left" markdown="1">

<div class="key-findings" markdown="1">
<h3>📋 Cardiovascular Big Picture</h3>

* Beta blockers slow the heart; check pulse and blood pressure.
* ACE inhibitors cause cough, hyperkalemia, hypotension, and possible angioedema.
* Loop and thiazide diuretics commonly lower potassium.
* Potassium-sparing diuretics can cause hyperkalemia.
* Nitroglycerin causes headache and hypotension; patches require a daily medication-free interval.
* Digoxin toxicity causes GI changes, visual changes, bradycardia, and dysrhythmias.
* Statin muscle pain can indicate rhabdomyolysis.
* Anticoagulants require bleeding precautions and medication-specific monitoring.
* Epoetin should raise hemoglobin, not improve kidney function.

</div>

<div class="mh-col" markdown="1">

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

* Severe bradycardia or hypotension
* Facial, tongue, or throat swelling
* New dysrhythmia or syncope
* Yellow-green vision or halos
* Unexplained muscle pain or dark urine
* Black stools, hematuria, or uncontrolled bleeding
* Severe headache while anticoagulated
* Markedly decreased urine output
* Rapid weight gain, edema, or worsening dyspnea

</div>

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">
<h3>ATI Tip</h3>

Before giving a cardiovascular medication, ask: **What vital sign or laboratory value could make this dose unsafe?**

</div>

<div class="mh-columns" markdown="1">

<div class="quick-review" markdown="1">
<h3>Quick Review</h3>

1. Check pulse before beta blockers and digoxin.
2. Beta blockers can mask hypoglycemia.
3. ACE inhibitors can cause cough and angioedema.
4. Loop and thiazide diuretics commonly lower potassium.
5. Nitroglycerin patches are not for acute chest pain.
6. Muscle pain while taking a statin must be reported.
7. Heparin uses aPTT; warfarin uses PT/INR.
8. Bleeding is the major danger with anticoagulants.

</div>

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">
<h3>Do Not Miss</h3>

* Do not abruptly stop beta blockers.
* Do not give potassium supplements with potassium-sparing diuretics unless ordered.
* Do not combine nitroglycerin with erectile-dysfunction medications.
* Do not massage an enoxaparin injection site.
* Do not ignore tinnitus in a client taking aspirin.
* Do not dismiss dark stools or a severe headache in an anticoagulated client.
* Hypokalemia increases digoxin toxicity.

</div>
