# High-Alert Medications & Toxicities

<p class="mh-subtitle">Recognize dangerous findings, hold medications safely, identify antidotes, and choose the priority nursing action</p>

High-alert medications can cause **serious injury, respiratory depression, dysrhythmias, bleeding, organ damage, seizures, or death** when toxicity occurs. ATI commonly tests the first sign of toxicity, the finding that requires the medication to be held, and the nurse’s immediate action.

### Opioids

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Examples</strong>

* Morphine
* Oxycodone
* Hydromorphone
* Fentanyl
* Codeine

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Danger</strong>

* Respiratory depression
* Excessive sedation
* Low oxygen saturation
* Hypotension
* Aspiration risk

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor</strong>

* Respiratory rate
* Oxygen saturation
* Sedation level
* Blood pressure
* Pain level
* Bowel function

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Hold / Report</strong>

* Respiratory rate below ordered parameters
* Oxygen saturation below expected range
* Difficult to arouse
* Severe hypotension
* New confusion or airway compromise

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Finding</th>
        <th>Meaning / Action</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Respiratory depression</strong></td>
        <td>Priority emergency. Hold opioid, support breathing, notify provider, and prepare naloxone.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Sedation</strong></td>
        <td>Implement fall and aspiration precautions; reassess before additional doses.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Constipation</strong></td>
        <td>Expected adverse effect; fluids, fiber, activity, and stool softener may be needed.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Urinary retention</strong></td>
        <td>Risk is increased in clients with benign prostatic hyperplasia.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Breakthrough cancer pain</strong></td>
        <td>May require an additional prescribed dose even when scheduled opioids are given.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> When opioid findings include nausea, constipation, sedation, hypotension, and a low respiratory rate, choose the respiratory finding first.
</div>

</div>

### Insulin

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Danger</strong>

* Hypoglycemia
* Confusion
* Seizures
* Loss of consciousness

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Hypoglycemia Signs</strong>

* Shaking
* Sweating
* Hunger
* Tachycardia
* Irritability
* Confusion

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Timing</strong>

* Rapid acting: food should be immediately available.
* Regular: usually given about 30 minutes before meals.
* NPH: intermediate acting and has a noticeable peak.
* Glargine: long acting with no pronounced peak.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Safety Rules</strong>

* Draw up clear insulin before cloudy insulin.
* Do not mix glargine with other insulins.
* Regular insulin can be administered IV.
* Gently roll NPH; do not shake.
* Rotate sites within the same anatomical area.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Client Status</th>
        <th>Treatment</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Conscious and able to swallow</strong></td>
        <td>Give 15 g of rapid-acting carbohydrate and recheck glucose in 15 minutes.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Unconscious or unable to swallow</strong></td>
        <td>Give glucagon or IV dextrose as prescribed.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Never administer rapid-acting insulin unless the meal is available and the client can eat.
</div>

</div>

### Digoxin

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>What It Does</strong>

* Improves cardiac contraction.
* Slows heart rate.
* Used for selected clients with heart failure or dysrhythmias.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Toxicity Signs</strong>

* Nausea or vomiting
* Anorexia
* Fatigue or weakness
* Bradycardia
* Dysrhythmias
* Yellow-green vision
* Halos or blurred vision

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor</strong>

* Apical pulse for 1 full minute
* Heart rhythm
* Potassium
* Kidney function
* Digoxin level when ordered

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Hold / Report</strong>

* Pulse below prescribed parameter
* New dysrhythmia
* Visual changes
* Unexplained nausea or anorexia
* Signs of toxicity

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Do Not Miss:</strong> Hypokalemia increases the risk for digoxin toxicity. A client taking furosemide and digoxin needs close potassium monitoring.
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Antidote:</strong> Digoxin immune Fab.
</div>

</div>

### Lithium

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Early Toxicity</strong>

* Nausea
* Diarrhea
* Fine tremor
* Weakness
* Drowsiness

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Worsening Toxicity</strong>

* Coarse tremor
* Confusion
* Ataxia
* Slurred speech
* Severe vomiting or diarrhea

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Severe Toxicity</strong>

* Seizures
* Coma
* Dysrhythmias
* Severe neurologic decline

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Teaching</strong>

* Maintain consistent sodium intake.
* Drink adequate fluids.
* Avoid dehydration.
* Take with food or milk if GI upset occurs.
* Report vomiting, diarrhea, or heavy sweating.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Situation</th>
        <th>Why It Raises Toxicity Risk</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Low sodium intake</strong></td>
        <td>The kidneys retain more lithium when sodium is low.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Dehydration</strong></td>
        <td>Lithium becomes more concentrated in the blood.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Vomiting or diarrhea</strong></td>
        <td>Fluid and sodium loss can rapidly increase toxicity.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Some NSAIDs or diuretics</strong></td>
        <td>Can increase lithium levels.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> A coarse tremor, ataxia, confusion, or severe diarrhea is not an expected mild effect. Hold the medication and notify the provider.
</div>

</div>

### Magnesium Sulfate

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Use</strong>

* Prevents seizures in severe preeclampsia.
* Can also be used for selected electrolyte or obstetric indications.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Toxicity Signs</strong>

* Respiratory rate below 12/min
* Hyporeflexia or absent reflexes
* Urine output below 25–30 mL/hr
* Muscle weakness
* Bradycardia
* Decreased level of consciousness

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor</strong>

* Respiratory rate
* Deep tendon reflexes
* Urine output
* Level of consciousness
* Serum magnesium when ordered

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Action</strong>

* Stop or hold the infusion.
* Notify the provider.
* Support respirations.
* Prepare the antidote.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Antidote:</strong> Calcium gluconate.
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Magnesium is excreted by the kidneys. Low urine output greatly increases toxicity risk.
</div>

</div>

### High-Risk Anti-Infectives

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Medication</th>
        <th>Major Danger</th>
        <th>What to Monitor</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Gentamicin / aminoglycosides</strong></td>
        <td>Nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity</td>
        <td>Peak/trough levels, kidney function, urine output, hearing</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Vancomycin</strong></td>
        <td>Nephrotoxicity and infusion reaction</td>
        <td>Kidney function, infusion rate, blood pressure, flushing</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Amphotericin B</strong></td>
        <td>Nephrotoxicity, hypokalemia, anemia, hypotension</td>
        <td>Kidney function, potassium, CBC, blood pressure, urine output</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Gentamicin Toxicity</strong>

* Elevated trough level
* Decreased urine output
* Rising creatinine
* Hearing loss
* Tinnitus
* Balance problems

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Vancomycin Infusion Reaction</strong>

* Flushing
* Urticaria
* Tachycardia
* Hypotension
* Usually related to rapid infusion

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Amphotericin B Toxicity</strong>

* Decreased urine output
* Hypokalemia
* Dysrhythmias
* Anemia
* Hypotension

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Teaching</strong>

* Report hearing changes.
* Report reduced urination.
* Keep laboratory appointments.
* Do not ignore dizziness or severe weakness.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> An elevated aminoglycoside trough level indicates the medication is not clearing and toxicity risk is increasing.
</div>

### Organ-Damaging Medications

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Medication</th>
        <th>Priority Danger</th>
        <th>Report Immediately</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Valproic acid</strong></td>
        <td>Hepatotoxicity and pancreatitis</td>
        <td>Abdominal pain, nausea, anorexia, jaundice</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Methotrexate</strong></td>
        <td>Bone marrow, liver, and kidney toxicity</td>
        <td>Fever, sore throat, unusual bleeding, decreased urine output</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Metformin</strong></td>
        <td>Lactic acidosis</td>
        <td>Muscle pain, hyperventilation, severe weakness, dizziness</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Exenatide</strong></td>
        <td>Pancreatitis</td>
        <td>Severe persistent abdominal pain with or without vomiting</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Valproic Acid</strong>

* Monitor liver function.
* Monitor platelets.
* Report abdominal pain or jaundice.
* Hair loss and weight gain are less urgent than organ injury.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Methotrexate</strong>

* Increase fluids if not contraindicated.
* Monitor CBC, liver, and kidney function.
* Report infection or bleeding.
* Leucovorin may be used as rescue therapy.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Metformin</strong>

* Monitor kidney function.
* Watch for lactic acidosis.
* Severe muscle pain or hyperventilation requires immediate follow-up.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Exenatide</strong>

* Monitor for pancreatitis.
* Hold and notify the provider for severe abdominal pain.
* Mild appetite loss is less urgent than persistent pain.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>ATI Priority:</strong> Severe or persistent abdominal pain can signal pancreatitis or liver injury and should not be treated as routine GI upset.
</div>

</div>

<div class="key-findings" markdown="1">
<h3>📋 High-Alert Big Picture</h3>

* Opioids: respiratory depression is the priority.
* Insulin: hypoglycemia can rapidly become a neurologic emergency.
* Digoxin: GI changes, bradycardia, dysrhythmias, and visual changes suggest toxicity.
* Lithium: dehydration and low sodium increase toxicity.
* Magnesium sulfate: watch respirations, reflexes, and urine output.
* Gentamicin: think kidney and hearing damage.
* Vancomycin: rapid infusion can cause flushing and hypotension.
* Amphotericin B: think kidneys, potassium, anemia, and blood pressure.
* Severe abdominal pain can signal pancreatitis or hepatotoxicity.

</div>

<div class="mh-col" markdown="1">

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

* Respiratory rate below prescribed parameters
* Oxygen saturation below expected range
* Difficult to arouse
* New dysrhythmia or severe bradycardia
* Yellow-green vision or halos
* Coarse tremor, ataxia, confusion, or seizures
* Absent reflexes or urine output below 25–30 mL/hr
* Hearing loss or rising aminoglycoside trough
* Severe persistent abdominal pain
* Markedly decreased urine output

</div>

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">
<h3>ATI Tip</h3>

Expected adverse effects do not automatically require stopping the medication. Look for the finding that signals **toxicity, organ damage, or immediate physiologic instability**.

</div>

<h3>Quick Toxicity Recognition</h3>

| Clue                                       | Think                                |
| ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------ |
| **Low respirations + sedation**            | Opioid toxicity                      |
| **Sweating + shaking + confusion**         | Hypoglycemia                         |
| **Nausea + yellow-green vision**           | Digoxin toxicity                     |
| **Coarse tremor + ataxia**                 | Lithium toxicity                     |
| **Absent reflexes + low respirations**     | Magnesium toxicity                   |
| **Hearing loss + elevated trough**         | Aminoglycoside toxicity              |
| **Flushing + hypotension during infusion** | Vancomycin infusion reaction         |
| **Low urine output + hypokalemia**         | Amphotericin B toxicity              |
| **Muscle pain + hyperventilation**         | Metformin-associated lactic acidosis |
| **Severe persistent abdominal pain**       | Pancreatitis                         |

<div class="mh-columns" markdown="1">

<div class="quick-review" markdown="1">
<h3>Quick Review</h3>

1. Assess respiratory status before giving additional opioids.
2. Make sure food is available before rapid-acting insulin.
3. Check the apical pulse before digoxin.
4. Maintain consistent fluids and sodium with lithium.
5. Check respirations, reflexes, and urine output with magnesium sulfate.
6. Monitor peak and trough levels for aminoglycosides.
7. Slow vancomycin infusions to reduce infusion reactions.
8. Treat severe abdominal pain as a possible organ-toxicity warning.

</div>

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">
<h3>Do Not Miss</h3>

* Naloxone can wear off before the opioid does; continue monitoring.
* Hypokalemia increases digoxin toxicity.
* Low sodium increases lithium toxicity.
* Low urine output increases magnesium and other medication toxicities.
* Never ignore a new hearing change with an aminoglycoside.
* Do not dismiss severe abdominal pain as routine nausea.
* Toxicity often appears first as a change from the client’s normal baseline.

</div>
