# Intrapartum

## Overview

**Intrapartum** refers to the period of labor and birth. During this time, the nurse monitors both the mother and fetus, supports coping and pain management, identifies complications early, and responds quickly to changes in maternal or fetal status.

Labor can feel intimidating for new nurses because it involves pain, unpredictability, intimacy, and situations that may change quickly.

### Main Nursing Priorities

| Priority        | What the Nurse Is Watching For                               |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Maternal status | Vital signs, pain, coping, bleeding, infection, labor progress |
| Fetal status    | FHR baseline, variability, accelerations, decelerations      |
| Labor progress  | Contractions, dilation, effacement, station, descent         |
| Safety          | Prevent infection, identify emergencies, respond to fetal compromise |
| Comfort         | Positioning, breathing, pain relief, emotional support       |
| Birth support   | Promote bonding, prepare for delivery, support family        |

------

# The Four Ps of Labor

The birth process depends on the **Four Ps**:

| Four Ps       | Meaning                                    | Nursing Connection                               |
| ------------- | ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------ |
| **Powers**    | Uterine contractions and maternal pushing  | Moves fetus through birth canal                  |
| **Passage**   | Maternal pelvis and soft tissues           | Must be large/stretchy enough for birth          |
| **Passenger** | Fetus, placenta, membranes, amniotic fluid | Size, position, and presentation affect delivery |
| **Psyche**    | Emotional and mental state                 | Anxiety, fear, culture, and support affect labor |

------

# 1. Powers of Labor

## Uterine Contractions

Uterine contractions begin in the **fundus** and sweep downward over the uterus like a wave.

They occur during the first stage of labor and help the cervix:

- Efface
- Dilate
- Move the fetus downward

Contractions are **involuntary smooth muscle contractions**, meaning the patient cannot consciously start or stop them.

### Factors That Can Influence Contractions

- Walking
- Medications
- Maternal anxiety
- Vaginal exams
- Hydration status
- Bladder distention
- Fetal position


------

## Contraction Assessment

The nurse assesses contractions by evaluating:

- Frequency

- Duration

- Intensity

- Resting tone

- Interval/rest period


  <img width="624" height="197" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/47a4bfa2-b25a-44bd-ba08-e5db72ff7f77" />


| Term             | Meaning                                 | How to Measure                                    | Why It Matters                                               |
| ---------------- | --------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Frequency**    | How often contractions occur            | Beginning of one contraction to beginning of next | Too frequent can reduce fetal oxygen                         |
| **Duration**     | How long one contraction lasts          | Beginning to end of same contraction              | Longer than 90 seconds can reduce fetal oxygen               |
| **Intensity**    | Strength of contraction                 | Palpation or IUPC                                 | Strong contractions help labor progress but can stress fetus |
| **Resting tone** | Uterine relaxation between contractions | Palpation or IUPC                                 | Fetus receives oxygen best during relaxation                 |
| **Interval**     | Rest time between contractions          | End of one contraction to start of next           | Short rest periods decrease fetal recovery time              |

------

## Frequency

**Frequency** is measured from the beginning of one contraction to the beginning of the next contraction.

Example:

- Contraction starts at 10:00
- Next contraction starts at 10:03
- Frequency = every 3 minutes

### Nursing Concern

Contractions occurring more often than every **2 minutes** may reduce fetal oxygen supply.

------

## Duration

**Duration** is measured from the beginning of a contraction to the end of that same contraction.

Example:

- Contraction starts at 10:00:00
- Contraction ends at 10:00:50
- Duration = 50 seconds

### Nursing Concern

Contractions consistently lasting longer than **90 seconds** may reduce fetal oxygen supply.

------

## Intensity

Intensity is the strength of the contraction.

| Intensity       | Palpation Finding                         | Feels Like  |
| --------------- | ----------------------------------------- | ----------- |
| **Mild**        | Fundus easily indented                    | Tip of nose |
| **Moderate**    | Fundus can be indented but more difficult | Chin        |
| **Strong/Firm** | Fundus cannot be easily indented          | Forehead    |

------

## Resting Tone

Resting tone is how relaxed the uterus is between contractions.

This is important because fetal oxygenation improves when the uterus relaxes. If the uterus does not relax well, placental blood flow can decrease, causing fetal compromise.

------

# 2. Cervical Changes

Contractions push the fetus downward and pull the cervix upward. This causes the cervix to thin and open.
 <img width="540" height="388" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/57315efd-a751-4f66-ac95-8fca81388093" />


The two major cervical changes are:

- Effacement
- Dilation

------

## Effacement

**Effacement** means thinning and shortening of the cervix.

It is measured as a percentage.

| Effacement | Meaning                   |
| ---------- | ------------------------- |
| **0%**     | Cervix is thick           |
| **50%**    | Cervix is halfway thinned |
| **100%**   | Cervix is fully thinned   |

Effacement is determined by vaginal exam.

------

## Dilation

**Dilation** means opening of the cervix.

It is measured in centimeters.

| Dilation   | Meaning            |
| ---------- | ------------------ |
| **0 cm**   | Closed             |
| **4–7 cm** | Active labor range |
| **10 cm**  | Fully dilated      |

A cervix is considered fully dilated at **10 cm**.
<img width="390" height="390" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/6914e321-03c2-4e2b-8c66-e53bb8cb0ac0" />


### Nursing Note

The patient should not actively push until the cervix is fully dilated unless instructed by the provider. Pushing too early can cause cervical swelling and slow labor progress.

As the patient effaces and dilates, **bloody show increases**.

------

# 3. Maternal Pushing

Maternal pushing occurs during the second stage of labor.

The patient should push when:

- Cervix is fully dilated
- Patient has an urge to push
- Nurse/provider confirms it is safe

### Common Feelings During Pushing

- Stretching
- Pressure
- Splitting sensation
- Rectal pressure
- Feeling like needing to have a bowel movement

### Normal Findings During Second Stage

- Stool may be involuntarily expelled
- Anus may become patulous and everted
- Patient may grunt or bear down
- Perineum may bulge
- Presenting part may become visible

### Two Forces of Birth

| Force                                  | Description                      |
| -------------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| **Involuntary uterine contractions**   | Uterus pushes fetus downward     |
| **Voluntary intra-abdominal pressure** | Patient pushes with contractions |

------

# 4. Passage

The **passage** is the maternal birth canal.

It includes:

- Bony pelvis
- Cervix
- Vagina
- Perineal tissues

The passage must be adequate for the fetal head and body to pass through.

Gynecoid is the BEST shape for childbirth. 

------

## Cephalopelvic Disproportion

**Cephalopelvic disproportion**, or **CPD**, occurs when the fetal head is too large or the maternal pelvis is too small for vaginal birth.

### Possible Signs of CPD

- Poor fetal descent
- Prolonged labor
- Failure to progress
- Excessive molding
- Need for cesarean birth

------

## Soft Tissues

Soft tissues include the cervix, vagina, and perineum.

Patients who have had previous vaginal births often deliver faster because the soft tissues have already stretched before.

------

# 5. Passenger

The **passenger** includes:

- Fetus
- Placenta
- Amniotic membranes
- Amniotic fluid

The fetus affects labor through:

- Size
- Lie
- Attitude
- Presentation
- Position
- Station

------

## Fetal Skull

The fetal skull is important because it must mold to pass through the pelvis.

The skull bones are separated by sutures. Fontanels are the spaces where sutures meet.

| Fontanel               | Shape             | Location             |
| ---------------------- | ----------------- | -------------------- |
| **Anterior fontanel**  | Diamond-shaped    | Front of fetal skull |
| **Posterior fontanel** | Triangular-shaped | Back of fetal skull  |

Fontanels help determine fetal position during vaginal exams.

------

## Molding

<img width="400" height="320" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/e1e68f93-bc0a-4231-8609-ebd90cbb636c" />

**Molding** is the overlapping of fetal skull bones during labor.

This helps the fetal head fit through the pelvis. Some molding is normal, but excessive molding may suggest difficulty passing through the pelvis.

## Biparietal Diameter

The **biparietal diameter** is the transverse diameter of the fetal head.

This matters because the fetal head is usually the largest part that must pass through the birth canal.

# 6. Fetal Lie, Attitude, Presentation, and Position

## Fetal Lie

**Fetal lie** is the relationship between the long axis of the fetus and the long axis of the mother.

<img width="444" height="376" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/38c5ce8c-a80e-4c6a-af12-1069dfbf0a8c" />


| Lie                       | Description          | Nursing Meaning                                              |
| ------------------------- | -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Longitudinal/vertical** | Fetus is up and down | Most common and favorable                                    |
| **Transverse**            | Fetus is horizontal  | Shoulder may present; often not compatible with vaginal birth |

------

## Fetal Attitude

**Attitude** refers to the degree of fetal flexion.

 <img width="300" height="227" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/678538b1-4cae-4d18-8a18-bc4dcc362b39" />

Normal attitude is **flexion**.

In normal flexion:

- Head is flexed forward
- Chin is toward chest
- Arms and legs are flexed
- Fetus is curled into a compact position

Good flexion allows the smallest diameter of the head to present.

------

## Fetal Presentation

**Presentation** is the fetal body part that enters the pelvis first.

| Presentation | Types                        | Notes                          |
| ------------ | ---------------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| **Cephalic** | Vertex, military, brow, face | Head presents first            |
| **Breech**   | Complete, frank, footling    | Buttocks or feet present first |
| **Shoulder** | Transverse/shoulder          | Usually requires intervention  |

<img width="444" height="388" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/7ac9dbfa-23d5-41a8-871c-9171e3cac457" />

### Most Favorable Presentation

**Vertex** is the most common and most favorable cephalic presentation.

------

## Fetal Position

**Position** describes the relationship of the presenting fetal part to the mother’s pelvis.

The pelvis is divided into four quadrants:

- Right anterior
- Left anterior
- Right posterior
- Left posterior

------

## Fetal Landmarks

| Presentation | Landmark                    |
| ------------ | --------------------------- |
| **Vertex**   | Occiput                     |
| **Face**     | Mentum/chin                 |
| **Breech**   | Sacrum                      |
| **Shoulder** | Scapula or acromion process |

------

| **SA** | Sacrum           |

# 7. Station

**Station** describes how far the presenting part has descended into the pelvis.

The reference point is the maternal **ischial spines**.

| Station              | Meaning                                                      |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Negative station** | Presenting part is above the ischial spines                  |
| **0 station**        | Presenting part is at the ischial spines; engaged            |
| **Positive station** | Presenting part is below the ischial spines and moving toward birth |

<img width="337" height="414" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/74fca706-3f77-46a6-ac66-bfa1ebcbefa2" />

### Examples

| Station | Interpretation            |
| ------- | ------------------------- |
| **-3**  | High                      |
| **0**   | Engaged                   |
| **+3**  | Low and close to delivery |

### Nursing Connection

A high presenting part with ruptured membranes increases the risk for **cord prolapse**.

------

# 8. Psyche

**Psyche** refers to the emotional and mental state of the laboring patient.

Factors include:

- Anxiety
- Fear
- Culture
- Pain tolerance
- Previous birth experiences
- Support person involvement
- Expectations about birth
- Sense of control
- Trust in staff

Anxiety can increase pain and interfere with effective labor.

### Nursing Care

- Give clear explanations
- Use a calm tone
- Provide encouragement
- Maintain privacy
- Respect culture
- Support birth preferences when safe
- Include the partner/support person

------

# 9. Signs of Impending Labor

Signs that labor may be approaching include:

- Braxton Hicks contractions
- Lightening
- Increased vaginal discharge
- Bloody show 
- Rupture of membranes
- Energy spurt, or nesting
- Weight loss

------

## Signs Explained

| Sign                            | Meaning                                                      |
| ------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Braxton Hicks contractions**  | Irregular practice contractions; do not cause progressive cervical change |
| **Lightening**                  | Fetus descends lower into pelvis                             |
| **Increased vaginal discharge** | Cervical changes may increase mucus/discharge                |
| **Bloody show**                 | Pink or blood-tinged mucus from cervical change              |
| **Rupture of membranes**        | Amniotic sac breaks; may be gush or trickle                  |
| **Nesting**                     | Sudden energy spurt before labor                             |
| **Weight loss**                 | May occur shortly before labor                               |

------

# 10. When to Go to the Hospital or Birth Center

The patient should go to the hospital or birth center for:

- Contractions every 5 minutes for 1 hour with first baby
- Contractions every 10 minutes for 1 hour with second or later baby
- Rupture of membranes
- Bleeding other than bloody show
- Decreased fetal movement
- Any concern that worries the patient

### NCLEX Priority Reasons to Go In

| Finding                      | Why It Matters                                     |
| ---------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| **Rupture of membranes**     | Risk for cord prolapse and infection               |
| **Decreased fetal movement** | Possible fetal compromise                          |
| **Heavy bleeding**           | Possible placenta previa, abruption, or hemorrhage |
| **Severe abdominal pain**    | Possible abruption or rupture                      |
| **Fever**                    | Possible infection                                 |
| **Nonreassuring FHR**        | Fetal oxygenation concern                          |

------

# 11. True Labor vs. False Labor

| Finding                      | True Labor                          | False Labor              |
| ---------------------------- | ----------------------------------- | ------------------------ |
| Contractions                 | Regular pattern                     | Irregular                |
| Frequency/duration/intensity | Increase over time                  | Do not increase          |
| Pain location                | Lower back and lower abdomen        | Abdomen and groin        |
| Walking                      | Usually does not stop contractions  | May relieve contractions |
| Bloody show                  | May be present                      | Usually absent           |
| Cervical change              | Progressive effacement and dilation | No cervical change       |

### Key Point

The true sign of labor is **progressive cervical effacement and dilation**.

# 14. Signs of Impending Birth

Signs that birth may be close include:

- Sitting on one buttock
- Grunting
- Bearing down with contractions
- Saying “the baby’s coming”
- Bulging perineum
- Presenting part visible
- Increasing rectal pressure
- Involuntary pushing
- Anus becoming patulous/everted

### Priority Nursing Action

Do **not** leave the patient alone if birth appears imminent.

# 15. Monitoring the Woman in Labor

## Vital Signs

| Vital Sign              | Frequency                  |
| ----------------------- | -------------------------- |
| Temperature             | Every 4 hours              |
| Temperature if ROM      | Every 2 hours              |
| BP, pulse, respirations | Every 1 hour or per policy |

### Report

Temperature greater than **100.4°F**.

Fever may indicate infection, especially after rupture of membranes.

------

## Contraction Monitoring

Contractions can be assessed by:

- Palpation
- External fetal monitor/toco
- Internal monitor/IUPC

When palpating:

- Use fingertips over the fundus
- Keep fingers still
- Assess frequency, duration, intensity, and relaxation

------

## Vaginal Exams

Vaginal exams assess:

- Dilation
- Effacement
- Station
- Presentation
- Position
- Membrane status

### Nursing Note

Limit vaginal exams to reduce infection risk, especially after membranes rupture.

------

## Intake and Output

Monitor bladder status.

A full bladder can:

- Increase pain
- Slow fetal descent
- Interfere with contractions
- Increase postpartum hemorrhage risk

The nurse should check for bladder distention every **1 to 2 hours**.

Oral intake depends on facility policy and may include:

- Ice chips
- Water
- Popsicles
- Hard candy

------

# 16. Helping the Woman Cope With Labor

Nursing support includes:

- Teaching
- Encouragement
- Breathing techniques
- Position changes
- Relaxation
- Partner support
- Explaining cervical changes
- Guiding pushing when appropriate

------

## Positioning During Labor

Helpful positions include:

| Position            | Why It Helps                            |
| ------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| **Side-lying**      | Improves uteroplacental blood flow      |
| **Upright**         | Uses gravity for descent                |
| **Walking**         | May help labor progress                 |
| **Sitting**         | Promotes comfort and descent            |
| **Squatting**       | Opens pelvic outlet                     |
| **Hands-and-knees** | May help with back labor or malposition |
| **Knee-chest**      | Emergency position for cord prolapse    |

Avoid prolonged supine positioning because it can reduce venous return and uteroplacental blood flow.

# 18. First Stage of Labor

The first stage begins with true labor and ends at 10 cm dilation.

It has three phases:

- Latent
- Active
- Transition

------

## First Stage Phases

| Phase          | Dilation | Contractions                                 | Maternal Signs                                  | Nursing Care                                                 |
| -------------- | -------- | -------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Latent**     | 0–3 cm   | Mild to moderate                             | Excited, talkative, manageable pain             | Rest, fluids if allowed, reassurance                         |
| **Active**     | 4–7 cm   | Moderate to strong, 2–5 min apart, 40–60 sec | Increased discomfort, bloody show, possible ROM | Monitor FHR, support coping, position changes                |
| **Transition** | 8–10 cm  | Strong, q 2–3 min                            | N/V, leg tremors, irritability, urge to push    | Stay with patient, discourage early pushing, prepare for birth |

------

## Latent Phase

Dilation: beginning of labor to about **3 cm**.

Characteristics:

- Contractions mild to moderate
- Patient may be excited or talkative
- Pain is usually manageable
- Contractions gradually become more organized

Nursing care:

- Assess maternal/fetal status
- Encourage rest
- Encourage fluids if allowed
- Teach breathing
- Encourage voiding
- Provide reassurance

------

## Active Phase

Dilation: **4 to 7 cm**.

Characteristics:

- Effacement continues
- Contractions become stronger
- Contractions become longer
- Contractions become closer together
- Average frequency: every 2 to 5 minutes
- Duration: 40 to 60 seconds
- Intensity: moderate to strong
- Discomfort increases
- Bloody show increases
- Spontaneous ROM may occur

Nursing care:

- Monitor FHR and contractions
- Promote position changes
- Encourage voiding
- Provide pain management
- Assess coping
- Prepare for possible epidural if desired
- Limit vaginal exams

------

## Transition Phase

Dilation: **8 to 10 cm**.

Characteristics:

- Short but intense phase
- Contractions every 2 to 3 minutes
- Increased bloody show
- Urge to push
- Leg tremors
- Nausea/vomiting
- Irritability
- Fear of losing control
- Patient may say “I can’t do this”

Nursing care:

- Stay with patient
- Provide calm, direct instructions
- Discourage pushing until fully dilated
- Use breathing/blowing techniques if urge to push occurs too early
- Prepare for birth
- Monitor fetal status closely

------

# 19. Second Stage of Labor: Expulsion

The second stage begins with complete dilation and ends with birth of the newborn.

Characteristics:

- Cervix 10 cm
- 100% effaced
- Strong contractions
- Contractions every 1.5 to 3 minutes
- Duration up to 1 minute
- Urge to push
- Crowning occurs

The stage usually lasts about 1 hour, but duration varies.

------

## Nursing Care During Second Stage

- Assess FHR frequently
- Coach pushing
- Support patient’s position
- Encourage rest between contractions
- Monitor perineum for bulging
- Observe for crowning
- Prepare delivery supplies
- Notify provider
- Provide emotional support
- Do not leave patient alone if birth is near

------

# 20. Third Stage of Labor: Placental Stage

The third stage begins with birth of baby and ends with expulsion of placenta.

This is usually the shortest stage, lasting **5 to 30 minutes**.

The placenta may be delivered by:

- Natural bearing down
- Gentle pressure on contracted uterus by provider

Pitocin may be given IV to stimulate uterine contractions and decrease bleeding.

------

## Signs of Placental Separation

| Sign                             | Meaning                              |
| -------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Uterus becomes globular and firm | Placenta separating                  |
| Uterus rises upward in abdomen   | Placenta has detached and moved down |
| Cord lengthens/descends          | Placenta moving into vagina          |
| Gush of blood appears            | Placental separation                 |

------

## Nursing Care During Third Stage

- Monitor bleeding
- Assess uterine tone
- Observe for placental separation
- Promote newborn bonding if stable
- Administer oxytocin as ordered
- Watch for excessive bleeding
- Ensure placenta is examined for intactness

### Nursing Connection

Retained placental fragments can cause postpartum hemorrhage.

------

# 21. Fourth Stage of Labor: Recovery

The fourth stage is the first **4 hours** after delivery of the placenta.

This is a critical time for detecting postpartum hemorrhage and maternal instability.

During this stage, the myometrium contracts and retracts to compress blood vessels.

------

## Nursing Care During Fourth Stage

- Assess fundus
- Assess lochia
- Monitor vital signs
- Assess bladder
- Monitor pain
- Promote bonding
- Encourage breastfeeding if desired
- Assess perineum
- Monitor for hemorrhage
- Provide warmth and comfort
- Support early parent-infant interaction

------

# 22. Nursing Responsibilities During Birth

The nurse may be responsible for:

- Preparing delivery instruments
- Preparing infant equipment
- Perineal scrub
- Administering medications to mother/infant
- Providing initial newborn care
- Assessing Apgar score
- Assessing newborn for obvious abnormalities
- Examining placenta for intactness
- Applying ID bands
- Obtaining footprints/fingerprints per policy
- Promoting parent-infant bonding

------

## Baseline Fetal Heart Rate

Normal fetal heart rate is:

**110 to 160 beats/min**

| FHR Pattern     | Meaning              |
| --------------- | -------------------- |
| **Normal**      | 110–160 bpm          |
| **Bradycardia** | Less than 110 bpm    |
| **Tachycardia** | Greater than 160 bpm |

------

## Bradycardia

Possible causes:

- Fetal hypoxia
- Maternal hypotension
- Cord compression
- Uterine tachysystole
- Medication effects

------

## Tachycardia

Possible causes:

- Maternal fever
- Infection
- Fetal hypoxia
- Maternal dehydration
- Medications
- Fetal anemia

------

# 24. Variability

**Variability** means fluctuations in fetal heart rate.

It reflects fetal oxygenation and nervous system function.

| Variability Type           | Description                                  |
| -------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| **Short-term variability** | Beat-to-beat changes; most accurate with FSE |
| **Long-term variability**  | Periodic fluctuations/accelerations          |

------

## Variability Categories

| Category     | BPM Change                | Meaning                                         |
| ------------ | ------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| **Absent**   | No detectable fluctuation | Concerning                                      |
| **Minimal**  | Less than 5 bpm           | Can be sleep/meds, but concerning if persistent |
| **Moderate** | 6–25 bpm                  | Reassuring                                      |
| **Marked**   | Greater than 25 bpm       | Can indicate stress                             |

------

## Factors That Decrease Variability

- Fetal sleep
- Narcotics
- Sedatives
- Stadol
- Magnesium sulfate
- Alcohol
- Illicit drugs
- Fetal tachycardia
- Gestation less than 28 weeks
- CNS defect such as anencephaly
- Fetal hypoxia
- Maternal hypoxia

------

# 25. Accelerations and Decelerations

## Accelerations

Accelerations are temporary increases in fetal heart rate.

They are usually reassuring.

Standard finding:

- Increase of 15 bpm
- Lasting at least 15 seconds
- Often associated with fetal movement

------

## Deceleration Chart

| Deceleration Type | Cause                        | Appearance                                                   | Nursing Meaning       | Intervention                                                 |
| ----------------- | ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Early**         | Fetal head compression       | Mirrors contraction                                          | Usually benign        | Continue monitoring                                          |
| **Variable**      | Cord compression             | Abrupt drop and return; shape varies                         | Requires intervention | Reposition, assess for cord prolapse                         |
| **Late**          | Uteroplacental insufficiency | Begins after contraction starts, returns after contraction ends | Concerning            | Side-lying, stop Pitocin, IV fluids, oxygen, notify provider |

------

## Early Decelerations

Cause:

- Fetal head compression

Characteristics:

- Occur with contractions
- Mirror the contraction
- Begin and end with contraction
- Return to baseline by end of contraction
- Not associated with fetal compromise

Nursing action:

- Continue monitoring
- No additional intervention usually needed

Memory connection:

**Early = head compression = okay**

------

## Variable Decelerations

Cause:

- Umbilical cord compression

Characteristics:

- Abrupt drop in FHR
- Abrupt return to baseline
- Shape, depth, and duration vary
- Can occur with or without contractions

Nursing interventions:

- Reposition patient
- Assess for cord prolapse if indicated
- Stop Pitocin if running
- Increase IV fluids
- Administer oxygen as ordered/facility policy
- Notify provider if persistent
- Prepare for further intervention if severe

Memory connection:

**Variable = cord compression = change position**

------

## Late Decelerations

Cause:

- Uteroplacental insufficiency

Characteristics:

- Begin after contraction starts
- Often start near contraction peak
- Return to baseline after contraction ends
- Indicate impaired placental exchange
- Concerning because fetal oxygen supply is decreased

Nursing interventions:

- Turn patient to side
- Stop Pitocin
- Increase IV fluids
- Administer oxygen as ordered/facility policy
- Assess maternal BP
- Correct hypotension
- Notify provider
- Prepare for delivery if unresolved

Memory connection:

**Late = placental problem = bad**

------

# 27. Uterine Activity and Hypertonic Activity

Uterine activity includes:

- Duration
- Intensity

------

## Hypertonic Uterine Activity

| Sign                                   | Why It Is Concerning                 |
| -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Contraction longer than 90 seconds     | Decreases fetal oxygen recovery time |
| Contractions less than 2 minutes apart | Too frequent                         |
| Relaxation less than 30 seconds        | Uterus not resting enough            |
| Resting tone about 20 mm Hg            | Increased baseline tone              |
| Peak pressure higher than 80 mm Hg     | Excessively strong contraction       |
| Montevideo units greater than 400      | Excessive uterine activity           |
| Late decelerations                     | Fetal oxygenation problem            |

# 28. Nonreassuring Fetal Patterns

Nonreassuring patterns include:

- Tachycardia
- Bradycardia
- Decreased variability
- Absent variability
- Late decelerations
- Severe variable decelerations
- Prolonged decelerations
- Hypertonic uterine activity

Variable decelerations are especially concerning when they fall below 70 bpm for longer than 60 seconds.

------

## Conditions Associated With Fetal Compromise

- FHR outside 110 to 160
- Little or no variability
- Persistent slowing after contractions
- Meconium-stained amniotic fluid
- Cloudy/yellow/foul-smelling fluid
- Contractions longer than 90 seconds
- Incomplete uterine relaxation
- Intervals shorter than 60 seconds
- Maternal hypotension
- Maternal hypertension
- Maternal fever

------

## Nursing Response to Nonreassuring FHR Patterns

| Action                                    | Purpose                                 |
| ----------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Identify cause                            | Guides correction                       |
| Assess maternal vital signs               | Detect hypotension, fever, hypertension |
| Check for prolapsed cord if appropriate   | Cord prolapse is an emergency           |
| Stop oxytocin/Pitocin                     | Reduces excessive contractions          |
| Reposition away from supine               | Improves uteroplacental blood flow      |
| Increase nonadditive IV fluids            | Improves maternal circulating volume    |
| Administer oxygen by face mask 8–10 L/min | Supports fetal oxygenation              |
| Initiate/continue EFM                     | Ongoing fetal assessment                |
| Notify provider                           | Escalates care                          |
| Prepare for delivery if needed            | Safety if unresolved                    |

### Priority Idea

Fix maternal oxygenation, circulation, and uterine blood flow to improve fetal oxygenation.

------

# 29. Amniotic Fluid Assessment

When membranes rupture, assess:

- Color
- Odor
- Amount
- Time of rupture
- Fetal heart rate response

------

## Amniotic Fluid Findings

| Fluid Finding                | Meaning            |
| ---------------------------- | ------------------ |
| Clear, possibly white flecks | Normal             |
| Green-stained                | Meconium           |
| Cloudy                       | Possible infection |
| Yellow                       | Possible infection |
| Foul/offensive odor          | Infection concern  |

------

## Nursing Action After ROM

Assess fetal heart rate for a full minute after rupture of membranes.

### Why?

Rupture of membranes can allow the umbilical cord to slip below the presenting part, causing **cord prolapse**.

------

## Testing for Rupture of Membranes

**Nitrazine Test**

* Tests vaginal fluid **pH**
* Amniotic fluid is more **alkaline**
* **Blue color = positive for ROM**
* Can have false positives from blood, semen, infection, or urine

**Fern Test**

* Fluid sample is placed on a slide and dried
* Amniotic fluid forms a **fern-like/crystal pattern**
* **Fern pattern = positive for ROM**
* Confirms presence of amniotic fluid under microscope

Systemic medications affect the whole body and may cross the placenta.

# 34. Regional Blocks

- No systemic depressive effects

# 39. Induction and Augmentation of Labor

## Definitions

| Term             | Meaning                                                  |
| ---------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Induction**    | Starting labor before it begins naturally                |
| **Augmentation** | Stimulating contractions after labor has already started |

------

## Before Induction/Augmentation

Assess:

- Fetal lung maturity
- Cervical readiness
- Bishop score
- Maternal/fetal status
- Contraction pattern
- Fetal heart rate

Bishop score greater than **7** suggests cervical readiness in a primipara.

Continuous monitoring of contractions and FHR is needed.

------

## Methods of Induction

| Method             | Type                                  |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------- |
| Amniotomy          | Mechanical                            |
| Prostaglandins     | Pharmacologic cervical ripening       |
| Laminaria          | Mechanical cervical ripening          |
| Cytotec            | Pharmacologic cervical ripening       |
| Pitocin/oxytocin   | Pharmacologic contraction stimulation |
| Walking            | Nonpharmacologic                      |
| Nipple stimulation | Nonpharmacologic                      |

------

## Pitocin/Oxytocin

Pitocin is the most common pharmacologic method.

Given:

- IVPB into primary line
- On IV pump

Requires:

- IV access
- Continuous EFM
- Vital signs
- FHR monitoring
- Contraction monitoring
- Intake and output

------

## Complications of Pitocin

- Fetal compromise
- Uterine rupture
- Water intoxication
- Excessive uterine contractions

Fetal compromise can occur because excessive contractions reduce placental blood flow.

Water intoxication is more likely postpartum if large amounts of fluid/Pitocin are given after birth.

------

## When to Stop Pitocin

Stop Pitocin if:

- FHR is outside normal range
- Fetal compromise occurs
- Contractions are more frequent than every 2 minutes
- Abnormal resting tone occurs
- Excessive uterine activity occurs

------

## Nursing Interventions for Pitocin Complications

1. Stop Pitocin
2. Increase primary IV rate
3. Change maternal position
4. Give oxygen 8–10 L/min by face mask
5. Notify provider if not corrected
6. Continue fetal monitoring
7. Record intake and output

------

# 52. Premature Rupture of Membranes

## PROM

**PROM** means spontaneous rupture of membranes at least 1 hour before labor contractions begin.

## PPROM

**PPROM** means preterm premature rupture of membranes.

It is rupture of membranes before term, with or without uterine contractions.

## Causes of PROM/PPROM

- Cervical infection
- Weak amniotic sac
- Hydramnios
- Fetal malpresentation
- Incompetent cervix
- Recent sexual intercourse
- Stress
- Nutritional deficiencies

------

## Diagnosis

- Nitrazine paper
- Ferning test/slide analysis

------

## Treatment

Treatment depends on risks and benefits for mother and baby.

Consider:

- Chorioamnionitis
- Fetal lung maturity
- Gestational age
- Maternal infection
- Fetal status

Management may include:

- Antibiotics
- Steroids
- Labor induction
- Cesarean birth

------

## Nursing Care

Report:

- Temperature greater than 100.4°F
- Fetal tachycardia
- Tenderness over uterine area
- Foul-smelling fluid
- Cloudy/yellow fluid
- Signs of infection

------

## PPROM Without Immediate Induction

Teach patient to:

- Report temperature greater than 100.4°F
- Avoid sexual intercourse
- Avoid inserting anything into vagina
- Avoid orgasm because it stimulates contractions
- Avoid breast stimulation
- Maintain prescribed activity restriction
- Report uterine contractions
- Report reduced fetal activity
- Report signs of infection
- Record fetal kick counts daily
- Report fewer than 10 kicks in 2 hours

------

# 53. Preterm Labor

Preterm labor occurs after 20 weeks and before the end of 37 weeks gestation.

Main risk:

- Newborn immaturity

Preterm birth is a major cause of perinatal morbidity and mortality.

Early prenatal care can help prevent preterm labor or identify at-risk patients.

------

## Signs and Symptoms of Impending Preterm Labor

- Contractions that may be painful or painless
- Feeling like fetus is “balling up”
- Menstrual-like cramps
- Pelvic pressure
- Feeling fetus is pushing down
- Change in vaginal discharge
- Abdominal cramps with or without diarrhea
- Pain/discomfort in vulva or thighs
- “Just feeling bad”
- Feeling like “coming down with something”

------

## Identifying Preterm Labor

Criteria include:

- Gestation from 20 weeks to before 37 weeks
- Persistent uterine contractions
- 4 contractions in 20 minutes
- 8 contractions in 60 minutes
- Documented cervical change
- Cervical effacement 80% or greater
- Cervical dilation 1 cm or greater

Other tests:

- Transvaginal ultrasound to assess shortened cervix
- Cervical/vaginal fibronectin test

------

# 54. Stopping Preterm Labor

Initial measures include:

- Identify and treat infection
- Restrict activity
- Hydrate patient
- Administer tocolytics as ordered

------

## Tocolytic Therapy

Goal:

Stop contractions and keep fetus in utero until lungs are mature enough for extrauterine life.

Tocolytics include:

- Magnesium sulfate
- Terbutaline/Brethine
- Indomethacin
- Calcium channel blockers/Procardia

Additional therapy:

- Betamethasone
- Antibiotics

------

## Contraindications to Tocolytics

- Preeclampsia
- Placenta previa
- Abruptio placentae
- Chorioamnionitis
- Fetal demise

------

## Tocolytic Medication Chart

| Medication               | Class/Type                        | Major Nursing Concerns                                       |
| ------------------------ | --------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Terbutaline/Brethine** | Beta-adrenergic agonist           | Maternal tachycardia, hypokalemia, hyperglycemia, pulmonary edema |
| **Magnesium sulfate**    | CNS depressant                    | Depressed respirations, decreased DTRs, oliguria             |
| **Indomethacin**         | Prostaglandin synthesis inhibitor | Used to reduce contractions                                  |
| **Procardia/Nifedipine** | Calcium channel blocker           | Monitor BP                                                   |
| **Betamethasone**        | Corticosteroid                    | Promotes fetal lung maturity                                 |

------

# 55. Brethine/Terbutaline

Brethine is a beta-adrenergic agonist.

Maternal side effects:

- Hypokalemia
- Hyperglycemia
- Pulmonary edema

Nursing responsibility:

- Assess maternal pulse

Concern:

- Do not give i pulse greater than 120 bpm

------

# 56. Magnesium Sulfate

Magnesium sulfate is a CNS depressant.

Antidote:

- Calcium gluconate

Maternal side effects:

- Depressed deep tendon reflexes
- Depressed respirations
- Oliguria
- Hypotension
- Diarrhea

------

## Stop Magnesium Sulfate If

| Finding                         | Concern                    |
| ------------------------------- | -------------------------- |
| Respiratory rate less than 12   | Respiratory depression     |
| Urine output less than 30 mL/hr | Drug accumulation/toxicity |
| Absent or diminished DTRs       | Magnesium toxicity         |

------

# 57. Accelerating Fetal Lung Maturity

Antenatal corticosteroids help fetal lungs mature.

| Medication        | Dose From Slides                   |
| ----------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| **Betamethasone** | 12 mg IM x 2 doses, 24 hours apart |
| **Dexamethasone** | 6 mg x 4 doses, 12 hours apart     |

Indicated:

- Between 24 and 34 weeks gestation

Important point:

Delaying birth for even 24 hours can benefit the fetus.

Expected temporary changes:

- Increased WBC
- Increased glucose

------

# 58. Nursing Care During Preterm Labor

- Bed rest in side-lying position
- Monitor vital signs
- Monitor fetal heart tones
- Monitor intake and output
- Provide emotional support
- Administer medications
- Hydrate patient
- Assess for pulmonary edema
- Assess contraction pattern
- Watch for medication side effects

Patients may feel guilt, fear, or anxiety. Emotional support is important.

------

# 59. Prolapsed Umbilical Cord

A prolapsed cord occurs when the umbilical cord comes before the presenting part.

The cord can be compressed between the fetus and pelvis, cutting off fetal oxygen and blood flow.

This is an obstetric emergency.

## Classifications

| Type                  | Description                                             |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Complete prolapse** | Cord is visible at vaginal opening                      |
| **Palpated prolapse** | Cord cannot be seen but pulsating structure can be felt |
| **Occult prolapse**   | Cord is hidden; suspected with FHR abnormalities        |

------

## Risk Factors

- Ruptured membranes
- Presenting part at high station
- Poor fit between fetus and pelvic inlet
- Small fetus
- Abnormal presentation
- Hydramnios/excessive amniotic fluid

------

## Nursing Interventions

1. Call for help/provider.
2. Relieve pressure on the cord.
3. Place patient in knee-chest position or Trendelenburg.
4. Manually elevate presenting part off cord.
5. Administer oxygen to mother at 10 L/min.
6. Monitor FHTs.
7. Prepare for C-section if needed.
8. Stay with patient.

------

## Important Safety Points

Never attempt to push the cord back into the vagina.

This can cause:

- More compression
- Knotting
- Kinking

If cervix is not fully dilated, cesarean birth is usually needed.

Remain calm.

Never leave the patient alone.

Support the support person.

------

# 60. Uterine Rupture

Uterine rupture is tearing of the uterine wall.

It is life-threatening for mother and fetus.

------

## Causes/Risk Factors

- Classical uterine incision
- Improper use of forceps
- Improper use of oxytocin
- Trauma from motor vehicle accident
- Faulty fetal presentation
- Grand multiparity
- Multiple gestation
- Uterine atony
- VBAC/history of uterine surgery

------

## Signs and Symptoms

- Unremitting uterine contractions
- Uterine tenderness
- Pain in chest
- Pain between scapula with inspiration
- Pathologic retraction ring
- Feeling that “something is giving away”
- Signs of shock

Other possible findings:

- Sudden abdominal pain
- Loss of fetal station
- Abnormal FHR
- Vaginal bleeding
- Cessation of contractions

------

## Nursing Care

- Recognize signs quickly
- Notify provider immediately
- Stop oxytocin if infusing
- Monitor maternal vital signs
- Monitor fetal heart rate
- Prepare for emergency surgery
- Prepare for blood replacement
- Support patient and family

------

# 61. Inversion of the Uterus

Uterine inversion occurs when the uterus turns inside out.

It may present with:

- Large sudden gush of blood from vagina
- Severe hemorrhage
- Shock
- Severe pain
- Uterus not felt where expected

------

## Causes

- Traction applied to umbilical cord
- Fundal pressure without contractions
- Fundal implantation of placenta
- Extreme uterine atony

------

## Major Concern

Profound shock due to blood loss.

This is an emergency.

------

## Management

- Provider attempts to replace uterus vaginally
- If unable to replace, hysterectomy may be required
- Tocolytic may be administered to relax uterus
- Pitocin is given IV after reinsertion to control blood loss
- IV antibiotics
- Blood replacement

------

## Nursing Care

- Monitor vital signs
- Monitor intake and output
- Assess bleeding
- Palpate uterus for firmness after replacement
- Administer medications as ordered
- Prepare for blood products
- Provide emotional support
- Watch for shock

## After ROM, First Nursing Action

Assess fetal heart rate.

Why?

Cord prolapse risk.

------

## Pitocin Problem Signs

- Contractions longer than 90 seconds
- Contractions closer than every 2 minutes
- Poor uterine relaxation
- Abnormal FHR
- Late decelerations
- Decreased variability

### Priority Action

Stop Pitocin first.

------

## Magnesium Toxicity Signs

| Toxicity Sign                   | What To Do                     |
| ------------------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Respirations less than 12       | Stop infusion, notify provider |
| Urine output less than 30 mL/hr | Stop infusion, notify provider |
| Absent/diminished DTRs          | Stop infusion, notify provider |

Antidote:

**Calcium gluconate**

------

## Prolapsed Cord Priority

Relieve pressure on the cord.

Do this by:

- Knee-chest
- Trendelenburg
- Manual elevation of presenting part

Do not push the cord back in.

------

## Infection Clues

- Maternal fever
- Fetal tachycardia
- Foul-smelling fluid
- Cloudy/yellow fluid
- Uterine tenderness

------

## Shoulder Dystocia Priority

- Call for help
- McRoberts maneuver
- Suprapubic pressure
- Assess newborn for clavicle fracture

------

## Fourth Stage Priority

Prevent and detect postpartum hemorrhage.

Assess:

- Fundus
- Lochia
- Vital signs
- Bladder
- Perineum
