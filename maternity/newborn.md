## Big Picture: What the Newborn Is Adapting To

At birth, the newborn has to quickly transition from being supported by the placenta to independently maintaining:

- **Breathing**
- **Circulation**
- **Temperature**
- **Blood glucose**
- **Fluid balance**
- **Digestion**
- **Elimination**
- **Immune protection**
- **Feeding**

The nurse’s priority is to assess whether the baby is adapting normally or showing signs of distress.

---

# Immediate Care of the Newborn

## Priority Goals After Birth

| Priority | What the Nurse Does | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Maintain airway and breathing | Dry, stimulate, position, suction if needed | Newborn must initiate respirations independently |
| Maintain thermoregulation | Dry quickly, skin-to-skin, hat, warm blankets, radiant warmer | Newborns lose heat fast and cold stress can cause hypoglycemia and respiratory distress |
| Support circulation | Assess color, HR, tone, perfusion | Cardiopulmonary transition is occurring |
| Prevent infection | Handwashing, gloves, eye prophylaxis, cord care | Newborn immune system is immature |
| Promote bonding | Skin-to-skin, early breastfeeding, parent interaction | Supports attachment and temperature/glucose stability |
| Give ordered medications | Vitamin K and erythromycin | Prevents bleeding and eye infection |

---

## Immediate Interventions

### Thermoregulation

The newborn should be placed in a **neutral thermal environment**.

Interventions:

- Dry the infant quickly and vigorously.
- Remove wet blankets.
- Place skin-to-skin with mother if stable.
- Place hat on head.
- Wrap in warm blankets.
- Use radiant warmer if needed.
- Avoid drafts.
- Prewarm equipment when possible.

### Cardiorespiratory Function

Crying is one of the best signs that the newborn is moving air.

Interventions:

- Gently wipe face, nose, and mouth.
- Suction with bulb syringe only if needed.
- Suction **mouth first, then nose**.
  - Remember: **M before N** because suctioning the nose first can trigger gasping/aspiration.
- Apply cord clamp.
- Assess for signs of respiratory distress.

# Signs of Respiratory Distress

Respiratory distress in a newborn is always important because newborns can decline quickly.

| Sign | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Persistent central cyanosis | Indicates poor oxygenation |
| Grunting | Newborn is trying to keep alveoli open |
| Nasal flaring | Increased work of breathing |
| Retractions | Increased effort to breathe |
| Tachypnea | RR greater than 60/min |
| Sustained HR greater than 160 or less than 110 | Can indicate distress |
| Seesaw respirations | Chest and abdomen move opposite directions; abnormal |
| Asymmetry of chest movement | May indicate lung or structural issue |

## Acrocyanosis vs Central Cyanosis

| Finding | Meaning | Nursing Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Acrocyanosis** | Bluish hands and feet | Can be normal during first day or when cold |
| **Central cyanosis** | Bluish lips, tongue, trunk, mucous membranes | Abnormal; indicates oxygenation problem |

# APGAR Scoring

Apgar is performed at:

- **1 minute**
- **5 minutes**

Apgar is a snapshot of how the newborn is adapting immediately after birth.  
It is **not** a predictor of future intelligence, ability, or disability.

## APGAR Components

| Category | 0 Points | 1 Point | 2 Points |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Appearance / Color** | Blue or pale | Body pink, extremities blue | Completely pink |
| **Pulse / Heart Rate** | Absent | Less than 100/min | Greater than 100/min |
| **Grimace / Reflex Irritability** | No response | Grimace | Cry, cough, sneeze, pull away |
| **Activity / Muscle Tone** | Limp | Some flexion | Active motion |
| **Respiration** | Absent | Slow or irregular | Good cry |

## APGAR Interpretation

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 7-10 | Generally good adaptation |
| 4-6 | Moderate difficulty; may need support |
| 0-3 | Severe distress; needs immediate resuscitation support |

### NCLEX Note

Do not delay necessary care just to complete the Apgar.  
If the baby is not breathing well, intervene.

# Newborn Medications

## Erythromycin Eye Ointment

| Medication | Purpose | Nursing Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Erythromycin ophthalmic ointment | Prevents ophthalmia neonatorum | Protects against eye infections, especially from STIs |

### Administration Notes

- Applied to eyes after birth.
- Place ointment from inner to outer canthus.
- Do not touch tube tip to eye.
- Can temporarily blur vision, but is important for infection prevention.

---

## Vitamin K

| Medication | Purpose | Why Newborns Need It |
|---|---|---|
| Vitamin K / AquaMEPHYTON | Helps with blood clotting | Newborn gut is sterile, so they do not yet make enough vitamin K |

### Key Points

- Given IM, usually in the vastus lateralis.
- Helps prevent hemorrhagic disease of the newborn.
- Vitamin K is needed for clotting factors **II, VII, IX, and X**.
- These clotting factors are produced by the liver.

## Handwashing

| When | Why |
|---|---|
| Initial scrub | Removes transient organisms before newborn care |
| Between infants | Prevents cross-contamination |
| Before and after care | Protects newborn and nurse |
| Teach parents | Parents need to know infection prevention too |

Wear gloves until the newborn has had the first bath because maternal blood and body fluids may still be present.

Use standard precautions with all newborns.

# Newborn Vital Signs

| Vital Sign | Normal / Expected |
|---|---|
| Heart rate | Usually 120-160/min |
| Respiratory rate | 30-60/min |
| Temperature | Maintain normal newborn temperature; avoid cold stress |
| Blood pressure | Around 80/50 may be seen |

## Assessment Tips

- Count **apical heart rate for one full minute**.
- Count respirations for **one full minute**.
- Count respirations by watching the abdomen because newborns are abdominal breathers.
- Respirations can be irregular, but persistent distress signs are abnormal.

# Initiation of Respirations

Newborn breathing is triggered by:

| Factor | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Chemical | Changes in oxygen, carbon dioxide, and pH stimulate the respiratory center |
| Mechanical | Chest compression during vaginal birth helps remove lung fluid |
| Thermal | Exposure to cooler air stimulates breathing |

The respiratory center is located in the **medulla**.

---

# Cardiovascular Adaptation

Before birth, the placenta oxygenates the fetus. After birth, the lungs must oxygenate the newborn.

## Ductus Arteriosus

- With the first few breaths, oxygen levels rise.
- Increased oxygen causes the **ductus arteriosus to constrict**.
- The ductus arteriosus gradually closes.
- Permanent closure usually occurs by **3-4 weeks**.
- Closure is promoted by increased oxygen and loss of placental prostaglandins.

# Thermoregulation of the Newborn

Newborns have poor temperature control because they:

- Have thin skin.
- Have little subcutaneous fat.
- Have a large body surface area compared with weight.
- Cannot shiver effectively.
- Depend on brown fat metabolism for heat.

## Methods of Heat Loss

| Method | Meaning | Example | Nursing Prevention |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Evaporation** | Heat loss when liquid on skin turns to vapor | Wet newborn after birth | Dry immediately and thoroughly |
| **Conduction** | Heat loss to a cooler surface touching the baby | Cold scale, cold stethoscope, cold bed | Prewarm surfaces and equipment |
| **Convection** | Heat loss to moving air | Drafts, fans, open doors | Keep away from drafts |
| **Radiation** | Heat loss to nearby cooler objects not touching baby | Infant near cold window/wall | Keep away from cold walls/windows |

## Environmental Temperature

The newborn needs a warm environment, approximately **89.6-92.3°F**.

## Hyperthermia Risk

Newborns also respond poorly to overheating.

Monitor temperature carefully if the infant is:

- Under a radiant warmer
- Under phototherapy lights
- Overdressed
- Swaddled too heavily

---

# Cold Stress

Cold stress occurs when the newborn has to use energy and oxygen to produce heat.

## Effects of Cold Stress

| Effect | Why It Happens |
|---|---|
| Increased oxygen need | Baby uses more oxygen to make heat |
| Respiratory distress | Increased oxygen demand stresses lungs |
| Decreased surfactant production | Can worsen breathing difficulty |
| Hypoglycemia | Glucose is used quickly for heat production |
| Metabolic acidosis | Poor oxygenation and increased metabolism can cause acid buildup |
| Jaundice | Stress can worsen bilirubin handling |

### NCLEX Connection

Cold baby = stressed baby.

Cold stress can lead to:

**Hypothermia → increased metabolism → hypoglycemia → respiratory distress → acidosis**

# Hematologic Adaptation

## Newborn Blood Values

Newborns normally have:

- More RBCs than adults
- Higher hemoglobin and hematocrit than adults
- Fetal hemoglobin that carries more oxygen than adult hemoglobin

Fetal hemoglobin can carry up to **50% more oxygen** than adult hemoglobin.

## RBC Breakdown and Jaundice

Newborn RBCs have a shorter lifespan.  
When they break down, bilirubin is produced.

Too much bilirubin can lead to:

- Jaundice
- Hyperbilirubinemia
- Kernicterus if severe

---

## Cord Clamping and Blood Volume

If the cord remains unclamped for a few minutes after birth, the baby may receive extra blood volume.

Possible concern:

- Polycythemia
- Increased RBC breakdown
- Increased risk for jaundice

## Heel Stick vs Venous Blood

Blood samples from heel sticks may show higher hemoglobin and hematocrit.

If precise measurement is needed, venous blood is preferred.

---

# Gastrointestinal System

## Newborn GI Adaptation

| Feature | Nursing Meaning |
|---|---|
| Small stomach capacity | Feedings are small and frequent |
| Stomach capacity about 6 mL/kg at birth | Do not overfeed |
| Expands to about 90 mL in first week | Feeding volume increases gradually |
| Long intestines compared with body size | More absorption surface |
| More prone to water loss with diarrhea | Diarrhea can dehydrate newborn quickly |
| Normal flora established in first few days | Feeding helps establish gut bacteria |

### Note

30 mL = 1 oz

# Newborn Stools

| Type of Stool | Appearance | Timing / Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Meconium** | Greenish-black, thick, sticky, tar-like | First stool; usually passed within 12 hours, should pass by 24-48 hours |
| **Transitional stool** | Greenish-brown, looser | Occurs as milk intake increases |
| **Breastfed stool** | Yellow, seedy, mustard-like, sweet/sour smell | More frequent than formula-fed |
| **Formula-fed stool** | Pale yellow to light brown, firmer, stronger fecal odor | Usually fewer stools per day |

## Abnormal Stool Findings

| Finding | Concern |
|---|---|
| No meconium by 48 hours | Possible obstruction |
| Water ring around solid stool | Diarrhea |
| Persistent diarrhea | Risk for dehydration |
| Bloody stool | Needs further assessment |
| Pale/white stool | Possible biliary issue |

# Hepatic System

The newborn liver is immature.

Important liver-related concerns:

- Blood glucose regulation
- Bilirubin conjugation
- Clotting factor production

---

## Blood Glucose

Hypoglycemia is generally considered blood glucose less than **40-45 mg/dL**.

## Why Newborns Become Hypoglycemic

| Cause | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Cold stress | Uses glucose quickly for heat production |
| Poor feeding | Inadequate intake |
| Infant of diabetic mother | Baby may produce excess insulin |
| Small for gestational age | Limited glycogen stores |
| Large for gestational age | Often related to maternal diabetes |
| Prematurity | Poor glycogen and fat stores |

## Signs of Hypoglycemia

| Sign | Nursing Meaning |
|---|---|
| Poor muscle tone | Low glucose affects energy and neuro function |
| Tremors/jitteriness | Common sign |
| Sweating | Autonomic response |
| Hypothermia | Can be both cause and symptom |
| High-pitched cry | Neurologic stress |
| Poor feeding | Lack of energy |
| Lethargy | Worsening hypoglycemia |

### NCLEX Note

Jitteriness can be caused by:

- Hypoglycemia
- Low calcium
- Prenatal drug exposure

Always assess blood glucose when a newborn is jittery.

---

# Hyperbilirubinemia and Jaundice

## Bilirubin Basics

Bilirubin is produced when RBCs break down.

The newborn liver may not be mature enough to conjugate bilirubin efficiently.

Too much bilirubin can cross into the brain and cause **kernicterus**, which can cause severe brain damage.

---

## Physiologic vs Pathologic Jaundice

| Type | Timing | Is It Normal? | Cause |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Physiologic jaundice** | Usually appears day 2-3 | Expected/transient | Immature liver and normal RBC breakdown |
| **Pathologic jaundice** | Present at birth or within first 24 hours | Abnormal | Excessive RBC destruction or disease process |

### NCLEX Priority

Jaundice in the **first 24 hours** is always concerning.

---

## Assessing Jaundice

- Press on the infant’s nose or sternum to blanch the skin.
- Observe yellowing when skin blanches.
- The farther down the body jaundice spreads, the higher the bilirubin level may be.

---

# Phototherapy

Phototherapy is used to lower bilirubin levels.

## Phototherapy Nursing Care

| Intervention | Why |
|---|---|
| Expose as much skin as possible | More skin exposure helps break down bilirubin |
| Cover eyes | Protects retina from light |
| Diaper usually stays on | Protects genital area |
| Monitor temperature | Lights can cause overheating or dehydration |
| Monitor I&O | Loose stools and fluid loss can occur |
| Reposition infant | Ensures even exposure |
| Encourage feeding | Promotes stooling and bilirubin excretion |

### Key Memory

Bilirubin leaves through **poop**.

More feeding → more stooling → more bilirubin excretion.

---

# Urinary System

## Normal Urinary Findings

| Finding | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Small amounts of glucose/protein in urine | Can be normal due to immature kidneys |
| Pink/orange stains in diaper | Usually uric acid crystals, not blood |
| Voids within 12-24 hours | Expected |
| Voids 6-10 times/day after day 4 | Expected hydration sign |

## Urine Output

Normal newborn urine output:

- **1-3 mL/kg/hr**

Be careful with IV fluids because newborn kidneys cannot handle large fluid shifts well.

---

# Fluid Balance

Newborns are at high risk for dehydration.

## Why Fluid Balance Is Fragile

| Reason | Nursing Meaning |
|---|---|
| Newborn body is about 78% water | Fluid shifts matter more |
| Vomiting/diarrhea can rapidly dehydrate | Can become life-threatening |
| High respiratory rate increases insensible water loss | Tachypnea can worsen dehydration |
| Low humidity increases water loss | Important in warmers/incubators |
| Kidneys are immature | Cannot handle fluid overload well |

## Signs of Dehydration

- Depressed fontanels
- Dry mucous membranes
- Poor skin turgor
- Decreased wet diapers
- Lethargy
- Weight loss beyond expected range

---

# Immune System

The newborn immune system is immature.

## Key Points

| Concept | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Immature hypothalamus | Fever response may not be reliable |
| Immature inflammatory response | Infection signs may be subtle |
| Susceptible to pathogens | Newborns can get sick from organisms that do not seriously affect older children |
| Maternal antibodies | Full-term infants receive antibodies during the last trimester |
| Breast milk antibodies | Breast milk, especially colostrum, provides immune protection |

## Teaching

Do not expose newborns to sick people, including people with rashes.

---

# Psychosocial Adaptation

Newborns have periods of activity and rest after birth.

## Periods of Reactivity

| Period | What Happens | Nursing Importance |
|---|---|---|
| First period of reactivity | Begins at birth; newborn is awake and alert | Great time for bonding and breastfeeding |
| Sleep period | Deep sleep may last several hours | Normal after initial alert period |
| Second period of reactivity | Interested in feeding, may pass meconium | Good time to reassess feeding and elimination |

### Note

Newborns often sleep **16-20 hours/day**.

Sleeping between feedings can be a good sign if feeding and output are adequate.

| Measurement | Normal Finding |
|---|---|
| Weight | 5 lb 8 oz to 8 lb 13 oz |
| Average weight | About 7.5 lb |
| Expected weight loss | 5-10% in first few days |
| Regain birth weight | By 10-14 days |

## Barlow and Ortolani

These tests assess for developmental dysplasia of the hip.

| Test | What It Checks |
|---|---|
| Barlow | Attempts to dislocate an unstable hip |
| Ortolani | Attempts to relocate a dislocated hip |

Abnormal findings may include:

- Hip click or clunk
- Unequal gluteal folds
- Limited abduction
- Unequal leg length

# Neurologic Assessment

## Newborn Reflexes

| Reflex | How It Appears | Normal Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Moro/startle | Arms extend then flex when startled | Neurologic response |
| Rooting | Turns head toward cheek stimulation | Helps locate nipple |
| Sucking | Sucks when mouth/palate stimulated | Needed for feeding |
| Palmar grasp | Grips finger placed in palm | Normal newborn reflex |
| Babinski | Toes fan upward when sole stroked | Normal in infants |
| Stepping | Makes stepping motions when held upright | Normal reflex |
| Fencing/tonic neck | Head turned to one side; arm/leg extend on that side | Normal reflex |

## Abnormal Neurologic Signs

| Finding | Possible Meaning |
|---|---|
| Jitteriness/tremors | Hypoglycemia, low calcium, drug exposure |
| Seizures | CNS abnormality |
| High-pitched cry | Neurologic irritation |
| Cat-like mewing cry | Abnormal; needs evaluation |
| Hoarse cry | Abnormal |
| Poor tone | Hypoglycemia, prematurity, neurologic problem |
| Weak suck | Feeding or neurologic issue |

# Sensory Assessment

## Ears

Normal ear placement:

- Draw imaginary line from outer canthus of eye to upper ear attachment.
- Ear should line up with the eye.

Abnormal findings:

- Low-set ears
- Abnormally angled ears

These can be associated with genetic conditions.

Ear cartilage and pinna incurving can also help estimate gestational age.

## Hearing

- Assess by reaction to sudden loud noise.
- Failed hearing screen requires referral.

## Eyes

Normal findings:

- Symmetric eyes
- Equal size
- Pupils equal and reactive
- Antibiotic prophylaxis given at birth

Remember:

**PERRLA = Pupils Equal, Round, Reactive to Light and Accommodation**

# GI Assessment

## Mouth

Assess for:

- Strong suck reflex
- Intact palate
- Cleft lip
- Cleft palate
- Thrush
- Excessive secretions
- Ability to coordinate suck-swallow-breathe

### Thrush

| Finding | Details |
|---|---|
| Appearance | White, milk-curd-like lesions on tongue/cheeks |
| Important clue | Bleeds if scraped off |
| Cause | Candida albicans |
| Treatment | Nystatin suspension |

### Cleft Lip / Palate

Occurs when lip or palate fails to close.

Can occur:

- Alone
- Together

Nursing concerns:

- Feeding difficulty
- Aspiration risk
- Need for special nipples/feeding support

# Initial Feeding Assessment

During initial feeding, observe the baby’s ability to:

- Suck
- Swallow
- Breathe
- Maintain color
- Feed without choking or coughing

## Stop Feeding If Baby Has:

- Choking
- Coughing
- Cyanosis
- Respiratory distress

Intervention:

1. Stop feeding.
2. Position safely.
3. Suction if needed.
4. Notify provider if persistent.

## Poor Feeding May Indicate

| Problem | Why |
|---|---|
| Cardiac abnormality | Feeding increases workload |
| Tracheoesophageal fistula | Milk can enter airway |
| Esophageal atresia | Milk cannot pass normally |
| Respiratory distress | Baby cannot coordinate breathing with feeding |
| Hypoglycemia | Baby lacks energy to feed |

# Genitalia Assessment

## Female Newborn

Normal findings:

- Labia majora large
- Labia majora covers clitoris and labia minora
- Edema may be present
- White mucoid vaginal discharge may occur
- Pseudomenstruation may occur due to maternal hormones

## Male Newborn

Normal findings:

- Scrotum should be pendulous
- Testes should be palpated for descent
- Meatus should be at tip of penis

Abnormal findings:

| Finding | Concern |
|---|---|
| Hydrocele | Enlargement of one or both sides of scrotum |
| Hypospadias | Urethral opening on underside of penis |
| Epispadias | Urethral opening on upper side of penis |
| Undescended testes | Needs follow-up |

### Circumcision Note

Do not circumcise if hypospadias or epispadias is present because foreskin may be needed for surgical repair.

Newborn skin is fragile. Examine every inch.

## Common Skin Findings

| Finding | Appearance | Normal/Abnormal | Nursing Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vernix caseosa | Thick, white, cheese-like substance | Normal | Protects skin; more common in preterm |
| Lanugo | Fine hair on body | Normal | More common in preterm |
| Milia | Small white cysts on face | Normal | Do not pop; resolves without treatment |
| Erythema toxicum | Red blotchy rash with white/yellow papules | Normal | Common; appears 24-48 hrs; resolves without treatment |
| Mongolian spots | Bluish-black areas resembling bruises | Normal | Document so not mistaken for abuse |
| Nevus simplex / stork bite | Flat pink/red mark from dilated capillaries | Normal | Often fades by age 2 |
| Jaundice | Yellow skin/sclera | May be normal or abnormal depending on timing | Assess bilirubin risk |
| Meconium staining | Greenish-brown discoloration of skin/nails/cord | Concerning | Watch for respiratory difficulty |

## Safe Handling

- Support head and neck.
- Support buttocks.
- Maintain a secure grasp.
- Use football hold when helpful.
- Swaddle for comfort/security.
- Use kangaroo care for skin-to-skin contact.

## Positioning

| Position | When Used |
|---|---|
| Supine | Safe sleep position |
| Side-lying | Sometimes used for feeding support, but not for sleep |
| Football hold | Helpful for breastfeeding, especially large breasts or C-section |

# Bathing and Hygiene

Bathing is a good time for assessment.

## Bathing Teaching

- Pay special attention to skin folds and creases.
- Demonstrate bathing to parents.
- Wash head last to prevent heat loss.
- Dry thoroughly.
- Avoid powders.
- Be cautious with lotions due to allergy/irritation risk.

## No Powders

Powders are avoided because zinc stearate can irritate the lungs.

---

# Dressing the Infant

Teach parents:

- Dress seasonally.
- Avoid overdressing.
- Use an extra sleeper if temperature instability is present, especially in preterm infants.
- Use a cap to cover the head when needed.
- Avoid overheating.

### NCLEX Safety Note

Overdressing and overheating are associated with increased SIDS risk.

# Circumcision Care

Circumcision is the surgical removal of the foreskin/prepuce.

## General Points

| Point | Nursing Meaning |
|---|---|
| Elective procedure | Not medically required for all infants |
| Consent required | Treat like a procedure/surgery |
| Infant restrained on U-board | Used during procedure |
| Anesthesia may be used | Penile block may be used |
| Sucrose/sugar nipple may be used | Comfort measure |
| Types | Gomco clamp or Plastibell |

---

## Nurse Responsibilities Before Circumcision

- Verify provider order.
- Witness consent.
- Check infant ID bands.
- Prepare sterile equipment.
- Position infant on circumcision board.
- Maintain safety and comfort.

---

## Post-Circumcision Care

| Care | Why |
|---|---|
| Observe for bleeding | Hemorrhage is priority complication |
| Keep area clean and dry | Prevent infection |
| Use Vaseline gauze with Gomco | Prevents sticking to diaper |
| Monitor first void | Confirms urinary function |
| Feed after procedure | Comforts infant |
| Teach parents care | Needed for discharge safety |

## Gomco vs Plastibell

| Type | Care |
|---|---|
| Gomco clamp | Petroleum/Vaseline gauze usually applied |
| Plastibell | Plastic ring remains and falls off in about 7-10 days |

## Parent Teaching After Circumcision

Teach parents:

- Small amount of swelling is expected.
- Yellowish crust can be normal healing.
- Do not remove crust from penis.
- Change diapers frequently.
- Apply petroleum as instructed.
- Report no voiding, excessive bleeding, foul drainage, or signs of infection.

---

# Caring for the Uncircumcised Male

## Key Teaching

Do **not** forcibly retract the foreskin.

Why:

- Prepuce is still developing.
- It is continuous with the epidermis of the glans.
- Forced retraction can cause adhesions and injury.

Complete retraction may not be possible until between **3 years and puberty**.

## Complication to Know

**Paraphimosis** can occur if the foreskin is retracted and gets stuck behind the glans.

# Newborn Screenings

## PKU: Phenylketonuria

PKU is a disorder of protein metabolism.

| Feature | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Problem | Phenylalanine cannot be converted to tyrosine |
| Enzyme missing | Phenylalanine hydroxylase |
| Risk if untreated | Cognitive impairment/intellectual disability |
| Screening | Newborn blood screening |
| Best timing | After ingestion of formula or breast milk |
| Retesting | May be repeated 2-3 weeks after discharge |
| Treatment | Phenylalanine-restricted diet |

### Teaching Note

Never touch the blood collection filter paper/card directly because contamination can affect results.

---

## Congenital Hypothyroidism

Screened by checking T4/thyroid levels.

Possible signs:

- Short neck
- Large/protruding tongue
- Poor feeding
- Lethargy
- Constipation
- Hypotonia

Treatment:

- Lifelong thyroid hormone replacement, such as Synthroid/levothyroxine.

# Breast Milk and Formula

## Breast Milk Advantages

Breast milk is species-specific for human infants.

Advantages:

- Nutrients are proportioned for the neonate.
- Composition changes to meet newborn needs.
- Provides immune protection.
- Easily digested.
- Helps establish normal intestinal flora.
- Decreases respiratory infections.
- Decreases GI infections.
- Decreases ear infections.
- Decreases SIDS risk.

---

# Composition of Breast Milk

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Colostrum | Early milk; rich in protein, vitamins, minerals, and immunoglobulins |
| Transitional milk | Between colostrum and mature milk |
| Mature milk | Contains nutrients in proportions needed by newborn; easy to digest |

## Colostrum

Colostrum is especially important because it is rich in:

- Protein
- Vitamins
- Minerals
- Immunoglobulins

It helps protect the newborn from infection.

---

# Breast Milk Carbohydrates

The main carbohydrate in breast milk is **lactose**.

Lactose helps:

- Improve absorption of calcium
- Improve absorption of phosphorus
- Improve absorption of magnesium
- Promote growth of normal intestinal flora

# Formula

Most commercial formulas are made from modified cow’s milk.

## Formula Modifications

| Modification | Reason |
|---|---|
| Reduced protein | Decreases renal solute load |
| Added vitamins/nutrients | Simulates breast milk |
| Adjusted composition | Makes formula more appropriate for infants |

Examples:

- Similac

# Breastfeeding Recommendations

AAP recommendations include:

- Continue breastfeeding after solids are introduced.
- Continue until at least 12 months if desired/possible.

---

# Combination Feeding

Combination feeding means breast milk and formula are both used.

## Important Considerations

| Issue | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Decreased breastfeeding frequency | Can reduce milk production |
| Less stimulation | Less prolactin/oxytocin response |
| Shorter breastfeeding duration | More likely than exclusive breastfeeding |
| Formula before lactation established | Can interfere with supply |

Unless medically indicated, formula is best delayed until lactation is established.

However, the nurse should support the mother’s decision and help the infant receive benefits from breast milk whenever possible.

---

# Normal Breastfeeding Physiology

## Milk Production

Milk is produced in the alveoli of the breast.

## Hormones

| Hormone | Source | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Prolactin | Anterior pituitary | Increases milk production |
| Oxytocin | Posterior pituitary | Causes milk let-down reflex |

## Supply and Demand

The more the baby suckles effectively, the more milk is produced.

Poor latch or infrequent feeding can decrease supply.

## Infant Feeding Readiness

Signs the infant is ready to feed:

### Late Sign

Crying is a late sign of hunger.

Try to feed before the baby is crying hard because latching can be more difficult.

# Breastfeeding Techniques

Teach:

- Position of infant
- Position of mother’s hands
- Palmar or C-hold
- Scissor or V-hold
- Latch-on technique
- How to elicit latch
- Mouth position
- How to remove infant from breast
- Feeding frequency
- Length of feeding

## Good Latch Signs

- Mouth opens wide
- Lips flanged outward
- Chin touches breast
- More areola visible above top lip than below bottom lip
- Rhythmic suckling
- Audible swallowing
- No clicking/smacking
- Mother feels tugging but not sharp pain

# Preventing Breastfeeding Problems

A common reason for early weaning is the mother’s perception of insufficient milk supply.

## Teach Signs Baby Is Getting Enough Milk

| Sign | Expected Finding |
|---|---|
| Wet diapers | At least 3 wet diapers by day 3; increases after milk comes in |
| Stools | At least 3 stools by day 3 |
| Weight | Weight gain begins by about day 5 |
| Daily gain | 15-30 g/day during early months |
| Behavior | Sleeps between feeds and seems satisfied |
| Feeding | Nurses effectively for about 15 minutes or more |

---

# Breastfeeding Evaluation

Evaluate:

- Infant nurses for 15 minutes or more per feeding.
- Good latch is present.
- Nutritive suckling occurs.
- Correct maternal positioning.
- Correct latch technique.
- Mother expresses confidence.

## Bottle and Nipple Care

Equipment options include:

- Glass bottles
- Plastic bottles
- Disposable liners
- Different nipple types

Selection depends on individual preference and infant needs.

## Cleaning

If water supply is safe:

- Sterilization is usually not necessary.
- Wash bottles and nipples in hot, soapy water.
- Use a brush to clean well.
- Rinse and air dry.
- Dishwasher can be used, but nipples may deteriorate faster.

If aseptic method is required:

- Boil bottles/nipples for 5 minutes.
- Boil water separately.
- Use sterilized tongs.
- Add water and formula.
- Cap bottles.
- Refrigerate.

# High-Yield NCLEX Connections

## Cold Stress Chain

Cold stress can quickly become dangerous.

**Cold stress → increased oxygen use → increased glucose use → hypoglycemia → respiratory distress → metabolic acidosis**

Priority intervention: **warm the baby** and monitor respiratory status/glucose.
