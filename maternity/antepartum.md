# 1. Big Picture of Pregnancy

Pregnancy is a **temporary, physiological, normal process** where all body systems adapt to support the growing fetus.

The nurse’s job during antepartum care is not just to “know pregnancy facts.” The nurse is constantly asking:

> **Is this normal pregnancy adaptation, or is this a danger sign?**  
> **Is mom okay? Is baby okay? What needs to be taught or reported?**

| Phase                     | Meaning      |
| ------------------------- | ------------ |
| **Antepartum / Prenatal** | Before birth |
| **Intrapartum**           | During birth |
| **Postpartum**            | After birth  |

Pregnancy is divided into **three trimesters**, each about **13 weeks** long.

---

## Main Nursing Goals During Pregnancy

| Goal                | What the Nurse Is Focused On                                 |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Health teaching     | Teaching the mother how to maintain good health              |
| Risk identification | Finding risk factors early                                   |
| Maternal monitoring | Monitoring vital signs, symptoms, weight, urine, labs        |
| Fetal monitoring    | Monitoring fetal growth, fetal heart rate, and fetal movement |
| High-risk support   | Helping mothers with medical conditions improve health as much as possible |
| Safety teaching     | Teaching warning signs that must be reported immediately     |

- **Smoking** increases risk for pregnancy complications and low birth weight.
- **Diabetes** increases risk for large babies, stillbirth, and placental/perfusion issues.

# 2. Confirming Pregnancy

Pregnancy signs are divided into three groups:

1. **Presumptive**
2. **Probable**
3. **Positive**

The key is knowing which signs actually confirm pregnancy.

| Type            | Meaning                                       | Examples                                                     | Confirms Pregnancy? |
| --------------- | --------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------- |
| **Presumptive** | Subjective symptoms reported by patient       | Amenorrhea, nausea, breast tenderness, fatigue, urinary frequency, quickening | No                  |
| **Probable**    | Objective signs, but could have another cause | Positive pregnancy test, Goodell’s, Hegar’s, Chadwick’s, Braxton Hicks, ballottement, striae | No                  |
| **Positive**    | Direct evidence of fetus                      | Fetal heartbeat, fetal movement felt by examiner, ultrasound visualization | Yes                 |

---

## Presumptive Signs of Pregnancy

These are **subjective**. The patient reports them.

| Sign                   | Meaning                        |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Amenorrhea             | Missed period                  |
| Nausea                 | Common early pregnancy symptom |
| Breast tenderness      | Hormonal breast changes        |
| Deepening pigmentation | Darkening skin changes         |
| Urinary frequency      | Increased urination            |
| Quickening             | Mother feels fetal movement    |
| Fatigue                | Common early pregnancy symptom |

> **NCLEX Tip:** Presumptive signs are not enough to confirm pregnancy because stress, illness, weight changes, and hormonal issues can also cause them.

---

## Probable Signs of Pregnancy

These are **objective**, but still do not fully confirm pregnancy.

| Sign                       | Meaning                                                |
| -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| Goodell’s sign             | Softening of cervix                                    |
| Hegar’s sign               | Softening of lower uterine segment                     |
| Braxton Hicks contractions | Irregular contractions                                 |
| Ballottement               | Examiner pushes fetus and fetus rebounds               |
| Striae                     | Stretch marks                                          |
| Positive pregnancy test    | hCG present                                            |
| Chadwick’s sign            | Bluish/purple discoloration from increased vascularity |

> **Important:** A positive pregnancy test is **probable**, not positive. It shows hCG, but it does not directly show the fetus.

---

## Positive Signs of Pregnancy

These are the only signs that confirm pregnancy.

| Positive Sign                   | Why It Confirms Pregnancy     |
| ------------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Audible fetal heartbeat         | Fetal life is heard           |
| Fetal movement felt by examiner | Examiner feels fetal movement |
| Ultrasound visualization        | Fetus is seen                 |

**Normal fetal heart rate:** `110–160 bpm`

# 3. Pregnancy Terms and GTPAL

| Term                 | Meaning                                      | Key Point                                     |
| -------------------- | -------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| **Gravida**          | Total number of pregnancies                  | Includes current pregnancy                    |
| **Para**             | Births at 20 weeks or greater                | Does not depend on whether baby is living now |
| **Age of viability** | Point where fetus may survive outside uterus | Usually around 20–24 weeks, but varies        |

---

## GTPAL System

GTPAL describes pregnancy history.

| Letter | Meaning                            |
| ------ | ---------------------------------- |
| **G**  | Gravida: total pregnancies         |
| **T**  | Term births, greater than 37 weeks |
| **P**  | Preterm births, less than 37 weeks |
| **A**  | Abortions, spontaneous or induced  |
| **L**  | Living children                    |

### Important Multiples Rule

Twins count as **one pregnancy** for gravida.

**Example:** A woman pregnant once with twins is still **G1**, not **G2**.

# 4. Naegele’s Rule

Naegele’s Rule is used to estimate the due date.

| Step | What To Do                                            |
| ---- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| 1    | Start with the first day of the last menstrual period |
| 2    | Count back 3 months                                   |
| 3    | Add 7 days                                            |
| 4    | Correct the year if needed                            |

## Example

| Step                | Date            |
| ------------------- | --------------- |
| LMP                 | April 2, 2026   |
| Count back 3 months | January 2, 2026 |
| Add 7 days          | January 9, 2026 |
| Correct year        | January 9, 2027 |

# 5. Initial Prenatal Visit

The first prenatal visit establishes **baseline data**. This is where the nurse/provider figures out where the patient is starting from before monitoring changes throughout pregnancy.

---

## Initial Prenatal Visit: What Happens

| Category                | What Is Assessed                                             |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Physical assessment     | Complete physical exam                                       |
| Reproductive assessment | Pelvic exam; evaluation of pelvis and reproductive organs    |
| Pregnancy dating        | Estimated date of delivery, ultrasound if needed             |
| Labs                    | Routine prenatal labs                                        |
| Risk review             | Medical, pregnancy, social, nutritional, and safety risk factors |



| Lab / Screening               | Why It Matters                                          |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Hemoglobin / hematocrit       | Screens for anemia                                      |
| WBC count                     | Infection clue, though WBC can be elevated in pregnancy |
| Platelets                     | Bleeding/clotting concerns                              |
| Blood type                    | Needed for transfusion/Rh status                        |
| Rh factor                     | Determines need for RhoGAM                              |
| Rubella status                | Determines immunity                                     |
| Gonorrhea/chlamydia           | Infection risk to mom/baby                              |
| Syphilis                      | Can harm fetus                                          |
| HIV                           | Treatment reduces transmission risk                     |
| Hepatitis B                   | Newborn protection planning                             |
| Urinalysis                    | Protein, glucose, ketones, infection                    |
| Group B strep status if known | Labor antibiotic planning                               |

---

## Rh Factor and RhoGAM

If the mother is **Rh negative**, she may need **RhoGAM** depending on the situation.

| Situation                         | Why                                    |
| --------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| Around 28 weeks                   | Prevent sensitization during pregnancy |
| After miscarriage                 | Possible fetal blood exposure          |
| After trauma                      | Possible fetal-maternal blood mixing   |
| After invasive procedures         | Possible fetal blood exposure          |
| Postpartum if baby is Rh positive | Prevent problems in future pregnancies |

---

# 6. Routine Assessment at Each Prenatal Visit

At every prenatal visit, think:

> **BP, weight, pee, baby.**

That simple phrase keeps the visit organized.

---

## Routine Visit Assessment Chart

| Category         | What to Check                                     | Why It Matters                                               |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **BP**           | Same arm, same position each time                 | BP should not normally rise; rising BP may suggest preeclampsia |
| **Weight**       | Pattern of weight gain                            | Too much or too little can signal problems                   |
| **Urine**        | Protein, glucose, ketones                         | Screens for preeclampsia, gestational diabetes, poor intake/dehydration |
| **Baby**         | Fundal height, FHR, movement, Leopold’s maneuvers | Checks fetal growth, position, and well-being                |
| **Risk factors** | Old and new risks                                 | Pregnancy risk can change over time                          |
| **Nutrition**    | Diet, intake, supplements                         | Supports maternal/fetal health                               |
| **Discomforts**  | New or worsening symptoms                         | Determines normal discomfort vs danger sign                  |

---

<div class="page-break"></div>

## Urine Findings

| Finding | Possible Concern                           |
| ------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Protein | Possible preeclampsia                      |
| Glucose | Possible gestational diabetes              |
| Ketones | Poor intake, dehydration, starvation state |

---

## Fundal Height

Fundal height helps determine:

- Whether fetus is growing as expected
- Possible amniotic fluid issues
- Possible abnormal fetal size

In general, after about **20 weeks**, fundal height in centimeters should roughly equal weeks of gestation.

**Example:** `24 weeks pregnant ≈ 24 cm fundal height`

# 7. Normal Physiological Changes in Pregnancy

> **What is normal, and what would make it unsafe?**

| System             | Normal Pregnancy Changes                                 | Nursing Concern                                         |
| ------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Reproductive       | Uterus grows, cervix softens, mucus plug forms           | Bleeding, infection, preterm cervical change            |
| Vagina             | Leukorrhea, acidic pH, increased vascularity             | Foul odor, itching, burning, abnormal discharge         |
| Breasts            | Tenderness, fullness, colostrum                          | Avoid nipple stimulation unless instructed              |
| Respiratory        | Mild dyspnea, increased O2 needs, congestion             | Severe SOB, chest pain, cyanosis                        |
| Cardiovascular     | Increased blood volume, cardiac output, clotting factors | HTN, clots, supine hypotension, heart failure worsening |
| GI                 | Nausea, heartburn, constipation, slowed motility         | Persistent vomiting, dehydration                        |
| Urinary            | Frequency, ureter dilation, increased UTI risk           | UTI/pyelo can trigger preterm labor                     |
| Fluid/Electrolytes | Sodium retention, edema                                  | Face/hand swelling, water intoxication                  |
| Skin               | Striae, linea nigra, melasma, spider nevi                | Usually cosmetic/normal                                 |

---

## Reproductive System

### Uterus

During pregnancy, the uterus:

- Increases in length
- Increases in depth
- Increases in width
- Increases in weight
- Wall thickens
- Volume increases
- Blood flow increases

The uterus size is similar to an **upside-down pear** prior to pregnancy.

### Cervix

The cervix becomes:

- More vascular
- Edematous
- Softer

| Term            | Meaning                                          |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| Operculum       | Mucous plug that helps seal out bacteria         |
| Effacement      | Thinning of cervix, measured 0–100%              |
| Dilation        | Opening of cervix, measured 0–10 cm              |
| Goodell’s sign  | Softening of cervix                              |
| Chadwick’s sign | Bluish/purple discoloration of cervix and vagina |

### Mucous Plug

The mucous plug protects against bacteria during pregnancy.

### Effacement and Dilation

Effacement usually occurs before major dilation, especially in a first-time pregnancy.

- **Effacement** = cervix thins
- **Dilation** = cervix opens from 0–10 cm

---

## Vagina

Normal changes:

- Leukorrhea
- More acidic pH
- Increased vascularity
- Chadwick’s sign

### Leukorrhea

Leukorrhea is a white vaginal discharge that can occur throughout pregnancy.

Teaching:

- This can be normal.
- Report foul odor, itching, burning, pain, or green/yellow discharge.

### Acidic pH

The vagina becomes more acidic, which can help reduce some infections but may increase yeast infections.

---

## Breasts

Normal changes:

- Fullness
- Tingling
- Tenderness
- Areola darkening/enlargement
- Colostrum may appear by 16 weeks

### Colostrum

| Feature        | Description                                                  |
| -------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Appearance     | Thin, watery                                                 |
| Nutrition      | High in protein                                              |
| Immune benefit | Rich in maternal antibodies                                  |
| Role           | Precursor to breast milk                                     |
| Timing         | Secreted during first 2–3 days after birth                   |
| Teaching       | Encourage mother to breastfeed at least once so baby benefits |

> **Important:** Do not express/pump colostrum during pregnancy unless specifically instructed, because breast stimulation can trigger uterine contractions.

---

<div class="page-break"></div>

## Respiratory System

| Normal Change                       | Why It Happens / Teaching                       |
| ----------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Mild shortness of breath            | Can happen as oxygen demand increases           |
| Oxygen consumption increases 15–20% | Pregnancy increases maternal/fetal oxygen needs |
| Nasal stuffiness/congestion         | Estrogen can cause congestion                   |

> **NCLEX Tip:** Mild dyspnea can be normal, but severe shortness of breath, chest pain, cyanosis, or respiratory distress is not normal.

---

## Cardiovascular System

Pregnancy causes a major increase in circulating volume. The body is working harder to perfuse both mom and baby.

| Change                                       | Meaning / Why It Matters                                     |
| -------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Blood volume increases by 50% or 1.5x normal | Supports placenta and fetus                                  |
| Cardiac output increases                     | Heart works harder                                           |
| Iron needs increase                          | Needed for RBC production                                    |
| Physiological anemia can occur               | Plasma volume increases more than RBCs, diluting blood       |
| Palpitations may occur                       | Heart workload increases                                     |
| Edema and varicosities may occur             | Venous return is slowed                                      |
| Clotting factors increase                    | Body prepares for birth, but clot risk increases             |
| Functional murmur may be heard               | Increased cardiac workload can make murmur noticeable. Might require Echocardiogram. |
| BP normally does not rise                    | Rising BP is concerning                                      |
| Pregnancy worsens heart failure              | Increased volume increases cardiac workload                  |
| Blood lipids falsely elevated                | Do not draw routine lipid levels during pregnancy            |
| Orthostatic hypotension common               | Vessels dilate to accommodate increased volume               |

---

<div class="page-break"></div>

### Iron and Physiological Anemia

Physiological anemia occurs because plasma volume increases more than red blood cells. The blood becomes diluted, so hemoglobin/hematocrit may look lower. Monitor and give body time to catch up. 

Iron is in prenatal vitamins, but the patient may require an additional supplement if she has a history of iron deficiency anemia.

Common complaints with iron:

- Nausea
- Constipation

### Iron Deficiency Anemia

Anemia if hemoglobin is less than **11 g/dL**.

Signs/symptoms may include:

- Fatigue
- Weakness
- Dizziness
- Pallor
- Shortness of breath

---

## Edema, Varicosities, and Clot Risk

| Issue                      | Teaching / Nursing Concern                                   |
| -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Edema                      | Elevate legs above heart                                     |
| Varicose veins             | Increase risk of blood clots                                 |
| TED hose                   | May be used prophylactically especially with hx. varicose veins. |
| Hemorrhoids                | Can occur from varicosities/pressure                         |
| Face/finger swelling       | Concerning for preeclampsia                                  |
| Calf pain, warmth, redness | Report; possible clot                                        |
| Sudden SOB/chest pain      | Emergency; possible pulmonary embolism                       |

> Redheads more at risk for bleeding.

---

<div class="page-break"></div>

## Blood Pressure

Blood pressure normally does **not** rise during pregnancy.

> **NCLEX Tip:** A rising blood pressure in pregnancy is concerning for hypertensive disorders, especially preeclampsia.

---

## Supine vs Orthostatic Hypotension

| Type                        | Cause                                         | Symptoms                                            | Nursing Teaching                                     |
| --------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| **Supine hypotension**      | Pregnant uterus compresses inferior vena cava | Lightheadedness, faintness, palpitations, dizziness | Avoid lying flat; side-lying, preferably left        |
| **Orthostatic hypotension** | Dilated vessels + position change             | Dizziness when standing                             | Change positions slowly; dangle feet before standing |

### Why Supine Hypotension Matters

Compression of the vena cava decreases venous return. This can decrease cardiac output and reduce blood flow to the fetus.

---

## Gastrointestinal System

Pregnancy hormones slow down the GI tract. Food moves more slowly, and the growing uterus pushes on the stomach and intestines. This is why GI discomforts are so common.

| Change                   | Why It Happens / Concern                |
| ------------------------ | --------------------------------------- |
| Nausea and vomiting      | Hormonal changes and slowed GI movement |
| Heartburn                | Slowed emptying + pressure from uterus  |
| Constipation             | Slowed peristalsis                      |
| Flatulence               | Slowed GI motility                      |
| Gingivitis/bleeding gums | Increased estrogen/hyperemia            |
| Gallstones               | Bile changes and decreased emptying     |
| Itching/jaundice         | Possible bile-related changes           |

---

## Urinary System

Normal urinary changes:

- Glycosuria may be more common.
- Proteinuria may be more common.
- Ureters increase in diameter.
- Ureters lose tone.
- Frequency increases in first and last trimesters.
- Risk for bladder infection and pyelonephritis increases.

The bladder holds more, so the patient should empty the bladder frequently to prevent back flow into the kidneys.

### Why UTIs Matter in Pregnancy

UTIs can trigger **preterm labor**.

| Report This                   | Why                     |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------------- |
| Burning with urination        | Possible UTI            |
| Frequency with pain           | Possible UTI            |
| Fever                         | Infection concern       |
| Back/flank pain               | Possible pyelonephritis |
| Cloudy or foul-smelling urine | Infection concern       |

### Urine Collection

- Clean catch urine **every time (avoid contamination/false positives)**
  - Wash hands before collecting the specimen.
  - Open the sterile cup without touching the inside of the cup or lid.
  - Separate the labia with one hand.
  - Clean the urinary area from **front to back** using the provided wipes.
  - Begin urinating into the toilet first.
  - After the urine stream has started, place the cup into the stream.
  - Collect the needed amount of urine.
  - Remove the cup before finishing urination.
  - Finish urinating into the toilet.
  - Secure the lid tightly without touching the inside of the cup.
  - Give the specimen to the nurse promptly.
- If patient cannot obtain clean catch specimen, an in/out catheter may be needed.

---

## Fluid and Electrolyte Balance

Normal changes:

- Sodium retention can cause edema.
- Edema can contribute to carpal tunnel syndrome.

### Pitocin and Water Intoxication

Pitocin has an antidiuretic effect, meaning it can cause the body to retain water.

| Possible Sign          | Concern                   |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------- |
| Agitation              | Water intoxication        |
| Delirium               | Water intoxication        |
| Confusion              | Water intoxication        |
| Headache               | Water intoxication        |
| Decreased urine output | Fluid retention           |
| Seizures               | Severe water intoxication |

I&O is monitored during labor and the immediate postpartum period.

---

## Integumentary System

| Change                       | Meaning                                                      |
| ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Striae gravidarum            | Stretch marks, usually genetic/from rapid weight gain        |
| Diastasis recti              | Separation of rectus abdominal muscles                       |
| Linea nigra                  | Dark line from umbilicus to pubis                            |
| Melasma/chloasma             | Mask of pregnancy; darker skin around eyes; common in certain ethnicities |
| Vascular spiders/spider nevi | Small visible blood vessels, usually on thighs               |
| Palmar erythema              | Redness/itching of palms                                     |

Most pigment changes are caused by increased estrogen and fade after pregnancy.

# 8. Common Discomforts of Pregnancy and Teaching

| Discomfort                | Teaching                                                     |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Nausea**                | Dry toast/crackers before getting out of bed; fluids between meals; small frequent meals; avoid greasy, spicy, strong odor foods |
| **Vaginal discharge**     | Daily bathing; loose cotton underwear; wipe front to back; no douching unless ordered |
| **Fatigue**               | Sleep 8–10 hours/night; nap if possible; promote rest        |
| **Backache**              | Maintain posture; low-heeled shoes; squat instead of bending; use pillows for support |
| **Constipation**          | 8 glasses water/day; fiber; exercise; bowel schedule; limit cheese/sweets; laxatives/enemas only if ordered |
| **Varicose veins**        | Avoid tight clothing/crossing knees; elevate legs; support hose; avoid standing in one position too long |
| **Hemorrhoids**           | Sitz bath; witch hazel; anesthetic ointment if approved; avoid constipation |
| **Heartburn**             | Small frequent meals; avoid fatty foods; reduce caffeine; avoid smoking; sit upright after meals; sleep elevated; antacids if approved |
| **Nasal stuffiness**      | Saline drops; **cool mist humidifier only**                  |
| **Dyspnea**               | Rest with upper torso propped up; avoid overexertion         |
| **Leg cramps**            | Extend affected leg; keep knee straight; dorsiflex foot; elevate legs periodically |
| **Lower extremity edema** | Rest; elevate legs above heart; avoid tight clothing; monitor for sudden swelling of face/hands |

---

## Normal vs Concerning Edema

| Normal                       | Concerning                         |
| ---------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Mild ankle/leg edema         | Swelling of face                   |
| Worse after standing         | Swelling around eyes               |
| Improves with rest/elevation | Sudden swelling of hands           |
| No other symptoms            | Headache, vision changes, RUQ pain |

> Mild ankle/leg edema can be normal. Swelling of the **face, hands, or around the eyes** is more concerning and may indicate preeclampsia.

---

# 9. Danger Signs of Pregnancy

These findings should be reported immediately because they can point to hemorrhage, infection, preeclampsia, fetal distress, dehydration, or preterm labor.

| Danger Sign                               | Possible Concern                         |
| ----------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| Vaginal bleeding with or without pain     | Placenta issue, miscarriage, hemorrhage  |
| Rupture of membranes/leaking fluid        | PROM, infection risk, cord prolapse risk |
| Swelling of fingers, face, or around eyes | Preeclampsia                             |
| Visual disturbances                       | Preeclampsia                             |
| Persistent/severe abdominal pain          | Labor, abruption, emergency              |
| Chills or fever                           | Infection                                |
| Painful urination                         | UTI/pyelonephritis                       |
| Persistent vomiting                       | Dehydration, electrolyte imbalance       |
| Change in fetal movement                  | Possible fetal distress                  |

---

## Decreased Fetal Movement

If baby is not moving:

1. Have mother drink something cold.
2. Have mother eat if she has not eaten.
3. Have mother lie on side.
4. Have mother focus on fetal movement.
5. If movement does not improve, call provider/go in for evaluation.

# 10. Psychological Adaptation to Pregnancy

Psychological adaptation works best chronologically by trimester.

| Trimester            | Main Emotional Task      | Common Findings                                              |
| -------------------- | ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **First trimester**  | Accepting the pregnancy  | Ambivalence                                                  |
| **Second trimester** | Accepting the baby       | Baby feels more real; quickening; follows prenatal instructions |
| **Third trimester**  | Preparing for parenthood | Nesting, prenatal classes, preparing for birth, making plans for baby |

### Ambivalence

Ambivalence means feeling both pleased and not pleased about the pregnancy. This can be normal.

### Prenatal Classes

Prenatal classes are most beneficial during the **third trimester** because the information is more fresh.

# 11. Nutrition During Pregnancy

Pregnancy increases nutritional needs, but the patient does not need to “eat for two.” The increase is more specific.

| Situation     | Extra Calories Needed  |
| ------------- | ---------------------- |
| Pregnancy     | 300 extra calories/day |
| Breastfeeding | 500 extra calories/day |

Breastfeeding mothers need about **200 calories and 5 g protein more** than pregnant women.

---

## Weight Gain

| Time                  | Expected Weight Gain |
| --------------------- | -------------------- |
| First trimester       | 2–3 lb total         |
| After first trimester | About 1 lb per week  |

### Recommended Total Weight Gain

| Prepregnancy Weight | Recommended Total Gain |
| ------------------- | ---------------------- |
| Normal BMI          | 25–35 lb               |
| Underweight         | 28–40 lb               |
| Overweight          | 15–25 lb               |
| Obese               | About 15 lb            |
| Twin pregnancy      | 35–45 lb               |

---

<div class="page-break"></div>

## Iron

Iron supplements are often prescribed.

**Common dose:** `30 mg/day`

| Teaching Point                             | Why It Matters             |
| ------------------------------------------ | -------------------------- |
| Best absorbed on empty stomach             | Improves absorption        |
| Take with orange juice                     | Vitamin C helps absorption |
| Do not take with coffee, tea, or milk      | Decreases absorption       |
| May cause nausea/heartburn/epigastric pain | Common side effects        |
| May cause constipation or diarrhea         | Common side effects        |
| May cause black stools                     | Expected finding           |
| Keep away from children                    | Iron overdose can be fatal |
| Liquid iron: use straw and rinse mouth     | Prevents staining teeth    |

---

## Folic Acid

Folic acid helps prevent neural tube defects.

Low folic acid increases risk for:

- Spina bifida
- Other neural tube defects

Alcohol use can increase risk for folic acid deficiency.

---

<div class="page-break"></div>

## Nutrition Risk Factors

| Risk Factor                  | Why It Matters                                   |
| ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| Culture                      | Affects food choices                             |
| Age                          | Adolescents may have different nutritional needs |
| Nutritional knowledge        | Affects choices and supplement use               |
| Socioeconomic status         | Healthier foods may be more expensive            |
| Adolescence                  | May eat less to conceal pregnancy                |
| Vegetarianism                | May need protein/iron/B12 planning               |
| Lactose intolerance          | More common in African Americans                 |
| Nausea/vomiting              | Can reduce intake                                |
| Anemia                       | May be associated with pica                      |
| Abnormal prepregnancy weight | Affects recommended gain                         |
| Eating disorders             | Can affect maternal/fetal nutrition              |
| Multiparity                  | May increase nutritional demands                 |
| Substance use/abuse          | Can impair nutrition and fetal growth            |

---

## Pica

Pica means craving or eating non-food substances.

Examples:

- Dirt
- Clay
- Ice
- Starch

Pica may be associated with iron deficiency. Report it because it may indicate nutritional deficiency and can expose mother/fetus to harmful substances.

# 12. Prenatal Diagnostic Testing

Prenatal diagnostic tests are used to:

- Detect congenital anomalies
- Monitor fetal well-being in high-risk pregnancy
- Provide accurate gestational age
- Guide interventions

---

## Indications for Prenatal Testing

| Indication                                | Why It May Matter                             |
| ----------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Diabetes                                  | Placental/perfusion and fetal growth concerns |
| Maternal age greater than 35              | Higher chromosomal risk                       |
| Previous infant over 4000 g               | May suggest diabetes/fetal growth concerns    |
| Excessive weight gain                     | May suggest complications                     |
| Previous chromosomal abnormality          | Increased genetic risk                        |
| Family history of chromosomal abnormality | Increased genetic risk                        |
| Previous neural tube defect               | Increased recurrence risk                     |
| Three or more spontaneous abortions       | Possible genetic/maternal issue               |
| Elevated maternal serum AFP               | Possible fetal anomaly                        |
| Maternal Rh sensitization                 | Fetal hemolytic disease concern               |

---

<div class="page-break"></div>

## Ultrasound

Ultrasound visualizes the fetus.

| Used For           | Why                         |
| ------------------ | --------------------------- |
| Gestational age    | Dating pregnancy            |
| Fetal growth       | Checks size                 |
| Fetal position     | Presentation                |
| Placenta location  | Rules out location concerns |
| Amniotic fluid     | Too much/too little fluid   |
| Fetal anomalies    | Anatomy assessment          |
| Multiple gestation | Twins/multiples             |

### Full Bladder Teaching

Before the second trimester, the uterus is still low in the pelvis. A full bladder may be needed to act as a “window” for visualization.

Later in pregnancy, this may not be needed because the uterus rises into the abdomen.

---

## AFP Screening

AFP stands for **alpha-fetoprotein**.

It can be measured in:

- Maternal serum: **MSAFP**
- Amniotic fluid: **AFAFP**

| AFP Result | Possible Meaning                                             |
| ---------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| High AFP   | May indicate open neural tube defect                         |
| Low MSAFP  | May be associated with chromosomal anomalies such as trisomy 21 |

> **Important:** AFP is a **screening test**, not diagnostic. Abnormal AFP requires follow-up testing, such as high-resolution ultrasound or amniocentesis.

## Multiple Marker Screening

Multiple marker screening may include:

| Marker               |
| -------------------- |
| AFP                  |
| hCG                  |
| Unconjugated estriol |
| Inhibin A            |

It helps increase detection of:

- Trisomy 18
- Trisomy 21

> **Important:** This is also **not diagnostic**. It identifies risk and need for further testing.

## Amniocentesis

Amniocentesis is aspiration of amniotic fluid. It is ultrasound-guided and performed by a physician.

| Category  | Notes                                                        |
| --------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Sample    | Amniotic fluid                                               |
| Timing    | First/second trimester for genetic testing; third trimester for lung maturity/hemolytic disease |
| Results   | May take 2–4 weeks                                           |
| Main risk | Spontaneous contractions, infection, bleeding, leaking fluid, pregnancy loss |

### Uses

Amniocentesis can be used to:

- Examine fetal cells
- Identify genetic abnormalities
- Detect high AFP
- Evaluate fetal condition if mother is Rh sensitized
- Diagnose amnionitis/intrauterine infection
- Test amniotic fluid AFP after abnormal MSAFP
- Determine fetal lung maturity in third trimester
- Determine fetal bilirubin if Rh incompatibility is present

### Nursing Considerations

| Before Procedure                     | After Procedure                                              |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Explain procedure                    | Monitor fetal heart rate                                     |
| Verify informed consent              | Monitor for contractions                                     |
| Assess fetal heart rate              | Monitor for vaginal bleeding                                 |
| Assist with positioning              | Monitor for leaking fluid                                    |
| Ensure bladder status is appropriate | Teach to report fever, chills, bleeding, leaking fluid, or contractions |

---

## Chorionic Villus Sampling

**CVS = diagnostic prenatal test** that samples chorionic villi from the placenta.

It may not be covered by insurance.

| Category            | Notes                                                        |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Sample              | Chorionic villi from placenta                                |
| Timing              | 10–13 weeks                                                  |
| Advantage           | Earlier than amniocentesis                                   |
| Results             | Diagnosis may be available in 2–4 days                       |
| Important use       | Helps with earlier decision-making/possible earlier termination |
| Rh-negative patient | May need RhoGAM after CVS                                    |
| Risk                | Can be associated with limb defects if performed too early   |

### Used to Diagnose

- Down syndrome
- Trisomy 18/13
- Sickle cell disease
- Cystic fibrosis
- Tay-Sachs

### Nursing Priorities

| Before                             | After                                        |
| ---------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Verify informed consent            | Monitor for bleeding                         |
| Check maternal VS                  | Monitor for cramping                         |
| Assess fetal status if appropriate | Monitor for leaking fluid                    |
| Check Rh status                    | Monitor for fever/chills and infection signs |

## Fetal Lung Maturity

Amniocentesis can check fetal lung maturity if delivery is being considered before **38 weeks**.

### L/S Ratio

L/S ratio refers to:

- Lecithin
- Sphingomyelin

A ratio of **2:1** generally suggests fetal lung maturity.

### Why It Matters

Surfactant keeps alveoli open by reducing surface tension.

Without enough surfactant:

- Alveoli collapse
- Respiratory distress can occur after birth
  - Pneumothorax, or lung collapse

# 13. Antepartum Fetal Surveillance

These tests evaluate fetal well-being before birth.

| Test                    | What It Evaluates                     | Good/Reassuring Finding       |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Nonstress test          | FHR response to fetal movement        | Reactive accelerations        |
| Contraction stress test | FHR response to contractions          | No concerning decelerations   |
| Biophysical profile     | Five fetal well-being parameters      | 8–10                          |
| Modified BPP            | AFI + NST                             | Normal fluid and reactive NST |
| Kick counts             | Maternal perception of fetal movement | Adequate movement             |

---

## Nonstress Test

NST evaluates fetal heart rate response to fetal movement.

### Purpose

To check if the fetal heart rate accelerates with movement.

This suggests:

- Adequate oxygenation
- Healthy neural pathway

### NST Results

| Result      | Meaning                                                      |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Reactive    | Reassuring                                                   |
| Nonreactive | Expected accelerations are not present; baby may be sleeping or further testing may be needed |

### Reactive NST Criteria

A reactive NST means:

- At least 2 accelerations
- Each acceleration is at least 15 bpm above baseline
- Each lasts at least 15 seconds
- Occurs within a 20-minute period

### Nonreactive NST: Possible Next Steps

- Continue monitoring
- Stimulate fetus
- Give mother food/fluids if appropriate
- Further testing such as CST or BPP

> **Easy Memory:** NST = looking for accelerations. Accelerations are good.

### Placement Note

For fetal monitoring, one monitor is placed to trace **fetal heart rate**, and the other is placed to trace **uterine activity/contractions**. The exact placement depends on fetal position and where the strongest fetal heart tones are heard.

---

## Contraction Stress Test

CST evaluates how the fetus responds to contractions.

### Purpose

During contractions, fetal oxygenation can decrease. The CST checks whether the fetus can tolerate that temporary stress.

### When Used

May be done if NST is nonreactive.

### How Contractions Are Caused

- Nipple stimulation
- Low-dose oxytocin

### CST Results

| Result       | Meaning                                                |
| ------------ | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| Negative CST | Reassuring; no concerning late decelerations           |
| Positive CST | Bad; decelerations occur with 50% or more contractions |

> **Important NCLEX Point:** Positive CST is bad. It means the fetus may not tolerate labor well.

### Oxytocin Safety

- Always piggyback oxytocin into the main IV line.
- Never run as the main bag because too much could cause excessive contractions.

Monitor for:

- Uterine tachysystole
- Fetal distress
- Late decelerations

---

## NST vs CST

| Test | What It Measures               | Good Result            | Bad Result                    |
| ---- | ------------------------------ | ---------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| NST  | FHR response to fetal movement | Reactive accelerations | Nonreactive                   |
| CST  | FHR response to contractions   | No concerning decels   | Decels with ≥50% contractions |

> **Memory Trick:**  
> **NST:** accelerations are good  
> **CST:** positive is bad

---

<div class="page-break"></div>

## Biophysical Profile

BPP evaluates fetal well-being during the antepartum period.

It assesses five parameters:

1. Nonstress test
2. Fetal breathing movements
3. Gross fetal movements
4. Fetal tone
5. Amniotic fluid volume

### BPP Scoring

Each category can receive:

- **2 points** = normal
- **0 points** = abnormal

Maximum score: **10**

| Score     | Meaning    |
| --------- | ---------- |
| 8–10      | Reassuring |
| 6         | Equivocal  |
| 4 or less | Abnormal   |

A normal BPP can allow conservative treatment of high-risk patients and may delay delivery if fetal well-being is reassuring.

### BPP Parameters

| Parameter           | Normal                                  |
| ------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| NST                 | Reactive                                |
| Fetal breathing     | At least 1 episode in 30 min            |
| Gross body movement | At least 3 movements in 30 min          |
| Fetal tone          | At least 1 episode of extension/flexion |
| Amniotic fluid      | At least one adequate pocket of fluid   |

---

<div class="page-break"></div>

## Modified Biophysical Profile

Modified BPP may use only:

- Amniotic fluid index
- Nonstress test

It is a shorter way to assess fetal well-being.

## Maternal Assessment of Fetal Movement

Also called **kick counts**.

This is done by the mother. Fetal movement is associated with fetal condition.

### Kick Count Notes

| Topic                    | Notes                                          |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Who does it?             | Mother                                         |
| How often?               | May be done 30 minutes, 3 times per day        |
| When to evaluate further | Fewer than 4 movements in 30 minutes to 1 hour |

> Some mothers may not feel movement as strongly, and babies also sleep. However, decreased movement should still be evaluated and should not be ignored.

# 15. TORCH Infections

TORCH infections can cross the placenta and harm the fetus. These infections can be especially harmful in the first trimester because fetal organs are developing.

| Letter | Infection            |
| ------ | -------------------- |
| T      | Toxoplasmosis        |
| O      | Other infections     |
| R      | Rubella              |
| C      | Cytomegalovirus      |
| H      | Herpes simplex virus |

---

## Toxoplasmosis

Often associated with exposure to:

- Cat feces/litter box
- Undercooked meat
- Contaminated soil

### Teaching

- Avoid changing litter box if possible.
- Wear gloves when gardening.
- Wash hands well.
- Cook meat thoroughly.

---

## Herpes Simplex Virus

If active genital lesions are present at birth, cesarean birth may be needed.

Reason:

- To prevent neonatal exposure during vaginal birth

---

## Rubella

Rubella may cause mild illness in the mother but serious fetal effects.

### Rubella Overview

| Category         | Findings                                                     |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Maternal effects | Mild rash, mild systemic illness                             |
| Fetal effects    | Deafness, mental challenges, motor challenges, cataracts, cardiac defects, PDA, pulmonary stenosis, IUGR/SGA, thrombocytopenic purpura, cleft lip/palate |
| Immunity         | Titer greater than 1:8 suggests immunity                     |
| Vaccine          | Not given during pregnancy                                   |
| Postpartum       | May be given postpartum if mother is not immune              |
| Newborn          | Infants born to mothers who had rubella may need isolation in nursery |
