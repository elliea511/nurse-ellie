# Postpartum

# 1. Postpartum Period

## Puerperium

The **postpartum period**, also called the **puerperium**, is the **6-week period following childbirth**.

It is also called the:

* **Fourth trimester**
* **Period of involution**
* **Beginning of the parenting role**

## ATI / NCLEX Focus

The postpartum period is about monitoring for:

| Priority Area                | Why It Matters                                        |
| ---------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| **Hemorrhage**               | Most dangerous in the first 24 hours                  |
| **Infection**                | Can occur in uterus, wound, urinary tract, breast     |
| **Thromboembolism**          | Pregnancy/postpartum state increases clotting factors |
| **Bladder distention**       | Can prevent uterine contraction and cause hemorrhage  |
| **Pain control**             | Promotes rest, bonding, breastfeeding, and ambulation |
| **Maternal-newborn bonding** | Supports attachment and parenting role                |
| **Mood disorders**           | Need to distinguish blues from depression/psychosis   |

---

# 2. Uterine Involution

## Involution

**Involution** is the process by which the reproductive organs return to their normal nonpregnant state.

After birth, the uterus must contract firmly. This contraction compresses the blood vessels at the placental site and prevents excessive bleeding.

## Why Uterine Contraction Matters

After the placenta detaches, the uterus has open blood vessels where the placenta was attached. A firm uterus acts like a natural clamp.

| Uterus Finding                       | Meaning                                                |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Firm fundus**                      | Expected; uterus is contracting                        |
| **Boggy fundus**                     | Uterine atony; risk for hemorrhage                     |
| **High fundus**                      | Possible bladder distention, clots, or poor involution |
| **Fundus deviated to the right**     | Usually bladder distention                             |
| **Firm fundus + continued bleeding** | Suspect laceration                                     |

---

## Fundal Descent

The **fundus** is the upper portion of the uterus.

### Expected Fundal Location

| Time After Birth   | Expected Finding                               |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Within a few hours | Fundus rises to level of umbilicus             |
| First 24 hours     | Fundus remains around umbilicus                |
| After 24 hours     | Descends about 1 cm or 1 fingerbreadth per day |
| By 10-14 days      | Usually no longer palpable abdominally         |
| By 6 weeks         | Uterus returns close to nonpregnant size       |

## Factors That Can Alter Involution

These increase the risk for poor uterine contraction and postpartum hemorrhage:

* Multiple fetuses
* Hydramnios
* Exhaustion
* Excessive anesthesia
* Retained placental fragments
* Full bladder

## ATI / NCLEX Connection

A uterus that has been **overstretched** has a harder time contracting.

Common causes of uterine overdistention:

* Twins or multiple gestation
* Large infant
* Polyhydramnios / hydramnios
* Grand multiparity

# 3. Afterpains

## Definition

**Afterpains** are intermittent uterine contractions after birth.

They are more common and more painful in:

* Multiparas
* Patients with uterine overdistention
* Breastfeeding mothers

## Why Breastfeeding Causes Cramping

Breastfeeding stimulates the release of **oxytocin**. Oxytocin causes:

* Milk letdown
* Uterine contraction
* Increased afterpains

## Expected Finding

Afterpains usually decrease after about **48 hours**.

## Nursing Interventions

* Explain that cramping is expected, especially with breastfeeding
* Encourage voiding before breastfeeding
* Administer prescribed analgesics before breastfeeding if needed
* Use relaxation and positioning techniques
* Reassure the patient that contractions help prevent hemorrhage

## NCLEX Tip

If a postpartum mother says,
**“I have cramping every time I breastfeed,”**
the nurse should explain that breastfeeding releases oxytocin, which causes the uterus to contract.

# 4. Lochia

## Definition

**Lochia** is postpartum vaginal discharge.

It consists of:

* Blood
* Decidua
* WBCs
* Mucus
* Bacteria

## Lochia Progression

| Type              |              Time Frame | Color / Description              |
| ----------------- | ----------------------: | -------------------------------- |
| **Lochia rubra**  |                Days 1-3 | Red, bloody, fleshy, earthy odor |
| **Lochia serosa** |               Days 4-10 | Pink or pinkish-brown            |
| **Lochia alba**   | Day 10 to about 3 weeks | White, cream, or light yellow    |

## Expected Lochia Pattern

Lochia should gradually:

1. Decrease in amount
2. Lighten in color
3. Progress from rubra → serosa → alba

## Abnormal Lochia Findings

| Finding                                              | Possible Meaning                    |
| ---------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Foul odor                                            | Infection                           |
| Persistent rubra                                     | Subinvolution or retained fragments |
| Return to bright red bleeding after it had lightened | Late postpartum hemorrhage          |
| Large clots                                          | Excessive bleeding                  |
| Saturating pad rapidly                               | Hemorrhage                          |
| Scant lochia with severe pain                        | Possible retained clots             |

CDC lists heavy postpartum bleeding, passing clots larger than an egg, tissue passage, and foul-smelling discharge as urgent warning signs after pregnancy. ([CDC][1])

---

## Documenting Lochia Amount

| Amount            | Pad Saturation                                                  |
| ----------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Scant**         | < 2-inch stain; about 10 mL                                     |
| **Small**         | < 4-inch stain; about 10-25 mL                                  |
| **Moderate**      | < 6-inch stain; about 25-50 mL                                  |
| **Heavy / Large** | > 6-inch stain; about 50-80 mL; one pad saturated within 1 hour |
| **Excessive**     | Saturation of pad within 15 minutes                             |

## NCLEX Priority

**Saturating more than one pad per hour is not normal.**

The nurse should assess:

1. Fundus
2. Lochia amount
3. Vital signs
4. Bladder status
5. Level of consciousness / signs of shock

---

# 5. Perineum

## Expected Findings

The perineum may be:

* Edematous
* Bruised
* Tender
* Affected by episiotomy
* Affected by lacerations
* Affected by hemorrhoids

An episiotomy may take **4-6 months** to completely heal.

## Perineal Comfort Measures

| Time Period       | Intervention                                          |
| ----------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| First 12-24 hours | Cold packs                                            |
| After 24 hours    | Heat, warm packs, sitz baths                          |
| Ongoing           | Peri bottle, anesthetic spray, witch hazel/Tucks pads |

## Perineal Care Teaching

* Cleanse perineum after each voiding or bowel movement
* Use peri bottle from front to back
* Pat dry from front to back
* Apply and remove pads from front to back
* Wash hands before and after pad changes
* Change pads frequently
* Report increasing pain, swelling, foul drainage, or separation of wound edges

---

# 6. Cardiovascular System

## Expected Blood Loss

| Type of Birth  | Expected Blood Loss |
| -------------- | ------------------: |
| Vaginal birth  |        About 500 mL |
| Cesarean birth |       About 1000 mL |

Some current clinical definitions use **blood loss of 1000 mL or blood loss with signs/symptoms of hypovolemia within 24 hours** to define postpartum hemorrhage. Older teaching materials often distinguish >500 mL after vaginal birth and >1000 mL after cesarean birth. ([Merck Manuals][2])

## Cardiac Output

Cardiac output increases for about **48 hours** postpartum, then decreases and returns to normal around **12 weeks**.

## Fluid Shifts

Postpartum patients commonly experience:

* Diuresis
* Diaphoresis
* Orthostatic hypotension

Diuresis up to **3000 mL/day** may occur.

## ATI / NCLEX Focus

The body is getting rid of extra pregnancy fluid. The patient may sweat a lot and urinate large amounts. This can be normal, but the nurse must watch for:

* Dizziness
* Hypotension
* Tachycardia
* Signs of hypovolemia
* Poor intake

---

## Blood Values

### WBC Count

WBC count may increase up to **30,000**, with an average range of **14,000-16,000**.

This can be normal postpartum due to:

* Inflammation
* Pain
* Stress
* Tissue healing

### Hematocrit

With each **250 mL blood loss**, there may be about a **4-point drop in HCT**.

### Clotting Factors

Clotting factors remain elevated for **3-4 weeks**.

## NCLEX Tip

Postpartum patients are at increased risk for blood clots because clotting factors are elevated. This is why early ambulation is important.

---

# 7. Gastrointestinal System

## Expected Findings

The mother is usually:

* Hungry
* Thirsty
* At risk for constipation

## Constipation Causes

Constipation is common due to:

* Decreased muscle tone
* Dehydration
* Iron supplements
* Opioid pain medication
* Fear of pain with bowel movement
* Perineal trauma
* Hemorrhoids
* Decreased activity

## Expected Bowel Pattern

* First stool usually occurs within **2-3 days**
* Normal pattern usually returns within **8-14 days**

## Nursing Interventions

* Encourage fluids
* Encourage fiber
* Encourage ambulation
* Give stool softeners as prescribed
* Give laxatives if prescribed
* Teach not to strain
* Support perineum with a clean pad during bowel movement if painful

## Patient Teaching

Foods that help prevent constipation:

* Prunes
* Whole-grain cereals
* Whole-grain bread
* Whole-grain pasta
* Fruits
* Vegetables
* Fluids, especially water

---

# 8. Urinary System

## Expected Findings

The bladder has increased capacity due to loss of muscle tone.

The patient may not feel the need to void even when the bladder is distended.

## Postpartum Urinary Output

UOP may increase to **1500-3000 mL/day**.

## Why Bladder Distention Is Dangerous

A full bladder can push the uterus:

* Upward
* To the side, usually right
* Away from midline

This prevents the uterus from contracting effectively and can lead to postpartum hemorrhage.

## Signs of Distended Bladder

| Sign                        | Meaning                         |
| --------------------------- | ------------------------------- |
| Fundus above expected level | Bladder may be full             |
| Fundus displaced to right   | Classic bladder distention sign |
| Excessive lochia            | Uterus cannot contract well     |
| Bladder discomfort          | Retention                       |
| Bulge above symphysis       | Distended bladder               |
| Frequent voids <150 mL      | Overflow retention              |

## Nursing Interventions

* Assist patient to void
* Measure first voids if ordered
* Encourage voiding every 2-3 hours
* Use warm water over perineum
* Provide privacy
* Assist to bathroom when safe
* Use bedpan if needed
* Catheterize if ordered and unable to void

## NCLEX Priority

If the fundus is **boggy and displaced to the right**, the first action is usually to help the patient **empty the bladder**, then reassess and massage the fundus if needed.

---

# 9. Musculoskeletal System

## Expected Findings

The abdominal walls stretch to accommodate the fetus, and muscle tone is diminished.

The woman may find her abdominal muscles:

* Weak
* Soft
* Flabby

## Diastasis Recti

**Diastasis recti** is separation of the longitudinal abdominal muscles during pregnancy.

It can be:

* Minimal
* Severe

## Nursing Teaching

* Avoid intense abdominal exercises immediately postpartum
* Start gentle postpartum exercises as approved
* Use good body mechanics
* Gradually increase activity
* Report severe pain or bulging

---

# 10. Integumentary System

After childbirth, hormone levels decline and the skin gradually returns to the prepregnancy state.

## Expected Changes

| Skin Change       | Postpartum Change                          |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Chloasma          | Disappears                                 |
| Linea nigra       | Disappears                                 |
| Striae gravidarum | Fade to silvery lines but do not disappear |

---

# 11. Endocrine System

## Hormonal Changes

After the placenta is expelled:

* Placental hormones decline
* Adrenal hormones return to prepregnancy levels
* Prolactin initiates milk production within **2-3 days**
* Oxytocin is necessary for milk letdown

## Ovulation

Ovulation may occur before the first menstrual period.

## NCLEX Teaching Point

A postpartum patient can become pregnant before her first period. Contraception should be discussed before discharge.

---

# 12. Immune Considerations

## Rhogam

Rh immune globulin, or Rhogam, is given to prevent Rh sensitization.

## When Rhogam Is Given

| Situation                                            | Indication                                                             |
| ---------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Rh-negative mother during pregnancy                  | Usually around 28 weeks                                                |
| Rh-negative mother gives birth to Rh-positive infant | Within 72 hours postpartum                                             |
| Possible fetal-maternal blood mixing                 | May be needed after trauma, miscarriage, abortion, amniocentesis, etc. |

## Route

Rhogam is administered **IM**, commonly in the deltoid muscle.

## NCLEX Tip

Rhogam is given to the **mother**, not the baby.

---

## Rubella Vaccine

Rubella titers are usually checked in early pregnancy.

If the mother is not immune, she should receive rubella vaccine in the immediate postpartum period. CDC recommends that pregnant women without evidence of rubella immunity be vaccinated immediately after giving birth. ([CDC][3])

## Rubella Vaccine Teaching

* Given postpartum, not during pregnancy
* Given SQ in the upper arm
* Avoid pregnancy after vaccination

### Important Update

Your slide says to avoid pregnancy for **3 months** after rubella immunization. Current ACOG guidance states to avoid pregnancy for **28 days** after receiving MMR. For school testing, follow your instructor’s material if that is what they are testing, but for current clinical guidance, know the 28-day recommendation. ([ACOG][4])

# 13. Initial Postpartum Assessment

## Standard Assessment Includes

* Vital signs
* Skin color
* Fundus
* Lochia
* Perineum
* Pain
* Urinary output
* Abdominal incision if present
* Level of feeling and ability to move after anesthesia

## Postpartum Assessment Priorities

Use the nursing process:

1. **Assess fundus**
2. **Assess bleeding**
3. **Assess bladder**
4. **Assess vital signs**
5. **Assess pain**
6. **Assess perineum/incision**
7. **Assess lower extremities**
8. **Assess emotional status**

---

# 14. Vital Signs

## Blood Pressure

Measure BP with the mother in the same position each time.

Compare BP with predelivery values.

| BP Finding   | Concern                              |
| ------------ | ------------------------------------ |
| Increased BP | Possible postpartum preeclampsia     |
| Decreased BP | Dehydration, hypovolemia, hemorrhage |

ACOG identifies postpartum preeclampsia, postpartum hemorrhage, and postpartum endometritis as important serious postpartum conditions that can worsen quickly without prompt recognition and treatment. ([ACOG][5])

---

## Pulse

Average postpartum pulse rate is **60-90 bpm**.

A pulse of **50-60 bpm** may be normal postpartum.

## Tachycardia May Indicate

* Excitement
* Fatigue
* Pain
* Dehydration
* Hypovolemia
* Anemia
* Infection

## Nursing Action for Tachycardia

Assess:

* Fundus
* Lochia
* H&H
* Vital signs
* Skin color
* Level of consciousness

## NCLEX Tip

Tachycardia may be an early sign of hemorrhage or shock.

---

## Respirations

Normal respirations: **12-20 breaths/minute**

Assess breath sounds carefully if the mother:

* Had a cesarean section
* Is a smoker
* Has frequent or recent URI
* Is receiving magnesium sulfate
* Has a history of asthma

## NCLEX Tip

Magnesium sulfate can cause respiratory depression. Always monitor respirations, reflexes, and level of consciousness.

---

## Temperature

A temperature up to **100.4°F** can be common during the first 24 hours.

Possible causes:

* Dehydration
* Normal postpartum leukocytosis

## Abnormal Temperature

Suspect infection if:

* Temperature persists longer than 24 hours
* Temperature exceeds 100.4°F
* Fever is accompanied by chills, foul lochia, uterine tenderness, wound drainage, dysuria, or breast redness

---

# 15. Pain Assessment

Pain is the **fifth vital sign**.

## Assess

* Type
* Location
* Severity
* Timing
* Aggravating factors
* Relief measures
* Response to medication

## Common Postpartum Pain Sources

| Pain Source        | Description                        |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------- |
| Afterpains         | Cramping, worse with breastfeeding |
| Perineal pain      | Episiotomy, lacerations, swelling  |
| Breast tenderness  | Engorgement or breastfeeding       |
| Incisional pain    | Cesarean birth                     |
| Hemorrhoids        | Rectal pain/pressure               |
| Severe rectal pain | Possible hematoma                  |

## Nonverbal Signs of Pain

* Inability to relax
* Inability to sleep
* Restlessness
* Irritability
* Facial grimacing
* Changes in vital signs

---

# 16. Fundus Assessment

## Expected Finding

The fundus should be:

* Firm
* Midline
* At or near the level of the umbilicus shortly after birth

## Abnormal Findings

| Finding                                | Possible Cause | Nursing Action                 |
| -------------------------------------- | -------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Boggy fundus                           | Uterine atony  | Massage fundus                 |
| Fundus high and to right               | Full bladder   | Assist to void                 |
| Fundus firm but bleeding continues     | Laceration     | Notify provider                |
| Fundus higher than expected days later | Subinvolution  | Assess lochia, notify provider |

## Fundal Massage

If the uterus is boggy:

1. Support lower uterine segment with one hand
2. Massage fundus gently with other hand
3. Continue until firm
4. Assess lochia
5. Reassess frequently
6. Notify provider if it does not stay firm

## NCLEX Priority

**Do not aggressively massage a firm fundus.**
Massage is used for a boggy uterus.

---

# 17. Breast Assessment

## Expected Breast Changes

| Time                | Finding              |
| ------------------- | -------------------- |
| 2-3 days postpartum | Full but soft        |
| Around day 3        | Firm, lumpy          |
| Later               | Possible engorgement |

## Breastfeeding Mothers

Teaching:

* Wash nipples with water only
* Avoid soap on nipples
* Keep nipples dry between feedings
* Wear supportive bra
* Ensure correct latch
* Break suction before removing infant from breast
* Alternate breasts
* Report redness, warmth, fever, or severe localized pain

## Non-Breastfeeding Mothers

Goal: suppress lactation.

Teaching:

* Wear tight/supportive bra
* Avoid breast stimulation
* Avoid hot showers directly on breasts
* Avoid pumping
* Avoid massaging breasts
* Avoid sexual foreplay involving breasts
* Use ice packs for discomfort
* Take analgesics as prescribed

---

# 18. Lower Extremity Assessment

## Assess For Thrombophlebitis

Look for:

* Heat
* Redness
* Edema
* Tenderness
* Pain
* Possible absence of pedal pulses

## Homan’s Sign

Homan’s sign is discomfort in the calf with sharp dorsiflexion of the foot.

If positive, assess for:

* Redness
* Tenderness
* Warmth
* Edema

## NCLEX Safety Note

Do not rely only on Homan’s sign. Current nursing practice focuses more on full DVT assessment findings, because Homan’s sign is not very reliable.

# 20. Nursing Care After Cesarean Birth

## Assessments

| Area                  | Nursing Focus                                      |
| --------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Uterus                | Fundus check same as vaginal birth                 |
| Lochia                | Routine intervals; usually less than vaginal birth |
| Dressing              | Drainage                                           |
| Incision              | REEDA assessment                                   |
| Urinary catheter      | Output, patency                                    |
| Respiratory status    | TCDB, incentive spirometer                         |
| Thrombosis prevention | Ambulation, SCDs                                   |
| Pain control          | PCA, epidural, oral analgesics                     |

## REEDA

| Letter | Meaning       |
| ------ | ------------- |
| R      | Redness       |
| E      | Edema         |
| E      | Ecchymosis    |
| D      | Discharge     |
| A      | Approximation |

## Cesarean Interventions

* Provide pain relief regularly
* Encourage early ambulation
* Assist with coughing and deep breathing
* Use pillow to splint abdomen
* Encourage incentive spirometer
* Use antiembolism stockings or SCDs
* Position with pillow behind back and between knees when side-lying

## NCLEX Tip

Pain control is not just comfort. It helps the patient:

* Ambulate
* Deep breathe
* Prevent pneumonia
* Prevent thrombophlebitis
* Bond with infant
* Breastfeed

# 21. Postpartum Teaching

## Involution Teaching

Teach the mother:

* What fundus should feel like
* How lochia should change
* When bleeding is abnormal
* How a full bladder affects bleeding
* When to call the provider

## Handwashing

Wash hands:

* Before touching breasts
* After diaper changes
* After bladder elimination
* After bowel elimination
* Before handling infant

---

## Breast Care for Lactating Mothers

* Avoid soap on nipples
* Use water only
* Keep nipples dry between feedings
* Wear supportive, well-fitting bra
* Ensure correct latch
* Report signs of mastitis

---

## Lactation Suppression Teaching

For mothers who are not breastfeeding:

* Wear supportive bra or bind breasts 24 hours/day until soft
* Use ice for discomfort
* Use analgesics as prescribed
* Avoid shower water hitting breasts
* Do not pump
* Do not massage breasts
* Avoid nipple stimulation

---

## Cesarean Incision Teaching

* Shower with steri-strips in place if allowed
* Steri-strips will gradually detach
* Incision is closed and unlikely to come apart
* Call provider if incision separates
* Call provider if drainage increases
* Call provider if drainage has foul smell

---

## Perineal Care Teaching

* Cleanse after voiding, bowel movements, and PRN
* Use front-to-back motion
* Pat dry
* Apply and remove pads front to back
* Wash hands before and after pad changes
* Report foul odor, severe pain, drainage, or increasing swelling

---

## Kegel Exercises

Kegels strengthen the **pubococcygeal muscle**, which surrounds the vagina and urinary meatus.

Benefits:

* Prevent loss of muscle tone
* Improve urinary control
* Support pelvic floor recovery

How to perform:

* Contract muscles around vagina
* Hold tightly for 10 seconds
* Relax for 10 seconds
* Repeat 30 cycles

---

## Rest and Sleep Teaching

* Fatigue is normal
* Rest when infant sleeps
* Limit visitors
* Restrict phone calls
* Ask for help
* Group care activities
* Conserve energy

## ATI / NCLEX Teaching Point

Exhaustion can interfere with:

* Healing
* Milk production
* Bonding
* Mood stability
* Ability to learn newborn care

---

## Bowel Elimination Teaching

Prevent constipation with:

* Walking
* Adequate fluids
* 8 glasses of water/day
* Fiber
* Prunes
* Whole grains
* Regular bowel routine
* Stool softeners if prescribed

Teach ways to reduce pain from:

* Perineal trauma
* Hemorrhoids
* Episiotomy
* Lacerations

---

## Body Mechanics Teaching

* Use proper lifting technique
* Avoid heavy lifting
* Tighten abdominal muscles before moving
* Turn to side before getting out of bed
* Gradually increase activity
* Follow provider instructions after cesarean

---

## Follow-Up Appointments

The mother usually returns to the provider between **2-6 weeks** postpartum.

Purpose:

* Identify complications early
* Assess healing
* Discuss contraception
* Assess mood
* Assess breastfeeding
* Review activity and sexual activity
* Monitor BP if needed

---

# 22. Warning Signs to Report

Teach mother and at least one family member to report:

* Fever
* Localized redness, swelling, or pain in either breast not relieved by support or analgesics
* Persistent abdominal tenderness
* Pelvic fullness or pelvic pressure
* Persistent perineal pain
* Frequency, urgency, or burning with urination
* Increased lochia
* Bright red bleeding after it had lightened
* Clots
* Foul odor
* Localized tenderness, redness, or warmth of legs
* Separation of cesarean incision
* Foul drainage from cesarean incision

## ATI / NCLEX Red Flags

| Symptom                           | Possible Complication                     |
| --------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Heavy bleeding                    | Postpartum hemorrhage                     |
| Foul-smelling lochia              | Infection/endometritis                    |
| Severe headache or vision changes | Postpartum preeclampsia                   |
| Chest pain or dyspnea             | Pulmonary embolism                        |
| Leg redness/swelling/pain         | DVT                                       |
| Fever/chills                      | Infection                                 |
| Thoughts of harming self or baby  | Postpartum depression/psychosis emergency |
| Severe abdominal pain             | Infection, hemorrhage, retained fragments |
| Burning with urination            | UTI                                       |

# 23. Maternal-Newborn Bonding

## Bonding

Bonding takes place in the first **30-60 minutes**.

## Attachment

Attachment is the process by which an enduring bond to the child develops through pleasurable, satisfying parent-child interactions.

## Maternal Touch

The mother may progress through a discovery phase:

1. Holds baby in **en face** position
2. Engages in mutual gazing
3. Uses fingertips to explore infant’s face, fingers, and toes
4. Strokes baby’s back, chest, and legs with palm
5. Uses entire hand to enfold infant
6. Identifies specific features of baby

## Nursing Interventions

* Promote skin-to-skin contact
* Encourage eye contact
* Encourage parents to talk to infant
* Support breastfeeding if desired
* Reduce unnecessary interruptions
* Involve support person
* Respect cultural preferences

# 24. Maternal Role Adaptation

## Rubin’s Puerperal Phases

| Phase           | Time Frame / Description                                                  | Nursing Focus                          |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| **Taking-in**   | Lasts 1-2 days; mother focused on own needs; talks about birth experience | Listen, provide rest, meet basic needs |
| **Taking-hold** | Mother becomes more independent and assumes mothering tasks               | Best time for teaching baby care       |
| **Letting-go**  | Mother and father adjust to new roles and relinquish old roles            | Support family adjustment              |

## NCLEX Tip

If a mother repeatedly wants to talk about her labor and delivery experience during the first day postpartum, this is expected in the **taking-in phase**.

# 25. Postpartum Blues

## Description

Postpartum blues are mild depressive symptoms after birth.

They:

* Affect more than 70% of women who have given birth
* Begin in the first week
* Usually last no longer than 2 weeks
* Are self-limiting
* Require empathy and support

## Symptoms May Include

* Tearfulness
* Mood swings
* Irritability
* Anxiety
* Fatigue
* Feeling overwhelmed

## Nursing Care

* Reassure that mild mood changes can be common
* Encourage rest
* Encourage support
* Teach when to seek help
* Screen for worsening symptoms

---

# 26. Postpartum Depression and Psychosis

## Postpartum Depression

A nonpsychotic depressive illness usually manifested within 2 weeks after delivery.

Symptoms may include:

* Persistent sadness
* Loss of interest
* Fatigue
* Anxiety
* Feelings of worthlessness
* Difficulty bonding
* Sleep disturbance unrelated to infant care
* Appetite changes
* Thoughts of self-harm

## Postpartum Psychosis

Postpartum psychosis is much less common but more dangerous.

The woman has an impaired sense of reality.

May involve:

* Bipolar disorder
* Major depression
* Hallucinations
* Delusions
* Paranoia
* Confusion
* Thoughts of harming self or baby

## NCLEX Priority

Postpartum psychosis is a psychiatric emergency.

If the mother expresses thoughts of harming herself or the baby:

1. Stay with the patient
2. Ensure safety of mother and infant
3. Notify provider immediately
4. Initiate emergency mental health care

---

## Mood Disorder Treatment

Treatment may include:

* Psychotherapy
* Increased social support
* Antidepressants

## Nursing Care

* Recognize signs of depression
* Be supportive
* Encourage follow-up
* Promote safety
* Assess support systems
* Teach family warning signs

---

# 27. Grieving Parents

Postpartum is not always a happy area. Grief may occur when:

* Infant condition is poor
* Infant dies
* Infant is stillborn
* Infant has a birth defect

## Grief Responses

* Shock
* Disbelief
* Anger, often directed at healthcare providers
* Guilt about what they could have done
* Sadness
* Depression
* Gradual resolution of sadness

## Nursing Interventions

* Allow parents to see/hold baby at their own rate
* Do not force interaction
* Use the baby’s name if appropriate
* Provide privacy
* Support cultural/religious rituals, such as baptism
* Offer chaplain or spiritual care
* Provide memory items

## Memory Packet May Include

* Lock of hair
* Footprint

# 30. Postpartum Hemorrhage

## Definition

Postpartum hemorrhage is excessive bleeding after birth.

Traditional teaching:

* > 500 mL after vaginal delivery
* > 1000 mL after cesarean birth
* Drop in HCT of 10%
* Need for blood transfusion

The greatest danger period is the **first 24 hours**.

---

## Early Postpartum Hemorrhage

Occurs in the first **24 hours** after childbirth.

## Two Main Causes

1. Uterine atony
2. Trauma to birth canal

## Early Signs

* Uncontracted uterus
* Large gush of blood
* Slow, steady trickle of blood
* Saturation of more than one peripad per hour
* Severe, unrelieved perineal or rectal pain
* Tachycardia

---

# 31. Uterine Atony

## Definition

**Uterine atony** means lack of uterine muscle tone.

It is the most common cause of early postpartum hemorrhage.

## Assessment Findings

* Uterus difficult to feel
* Fundus feels soft or boggy
* Fundal height may be high
* Lochia increased
* Clots may be present

## Nursing Action

When uterus is boggy, the nurse should massage it until firm.

## Uterine Atony Interventions

| Intervention                        | Rationale                      |
| ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Massage fundus PRN                  | Stimulates uterine contraction |
| Monitor uterine consistency q15 min | Detects recurrent atony        |
| Monitor vital signs                 | Detects shock                  |
| Monitor lochia flow                 | Assesses bleeding              |
| Type and cross high-risk patients   | Prepares for transfusion       |
| Increase IV fluids                  | Supports circulating volume    |
| Administer oxytocin IVPB            | Promotes uterine contraction   |
| Put infant to breast                | Stimulates natural oxytocin    |
| Give Methergine if ordered          | Contracts uterus               |
| Avoid Methergine in hypertension    | Can increase BP                |
| Keep bladder empty                  | Allows uterus to contract      |
| Give oxygen if respiratory distress | Supports tissue oxygenation    |
| Hysterectomy                        | Last resort only               |

## NCLEX Priority Sequence for Boggy Fundus

1. Massage fundus
2. Assess lochia
3. Assist patient to void / catheterize if needed
4. Increase IV fluids as ordered
5. Administer uterotonic medications as ordered
6. Notify provider if bleeding continues

---

# 32. Trauma: Cervical, Vaginal, and Perineal Lacerations

## Risk Factors

Lacerations occur most often with:

* Difficult delivery
* Precipitous delivery
* Large babies >9 lb
* Lithotomy position
* Forceps delivery
* Primigravidas

## Key Assessment Clue

**Fundus is firm, but bleeding persists.**

This suggests a laceration.

## Nursing Interventions

* Monitor vital signs
* Monitor lochia flow
* Notify provider
* Prepare for repair
* Provide reassurance
* Assure mother baby is fine during repair if appropriate

## Treatment

* Volume replacement PRN
* Prophylactic antibiotics
* Good nutrition
* Increase fiber/fluids
* Iron supplements
* Stool softeners
* Provider locates and repairs laceration
* Vaginal packing may be placed
* Document packing presence
* Packing usually removed in 24-48 hours
* Foley may be inserted

## Important Safety Teaching

Avoid the following with 3rd or 4th degree episiotomy/laceration:

* Rectal temperatures
* Rectal suppositories
* Enemas

---

# 33. Late Postpartum Hemorrhage

## Definition

Late postpartum hemorrhage occurs **after 24 hours up to 6 weeks postpartum**.

## Common Cause

Subinvolution.

## Causes

* Placenta does not separate cleanly
* Placenta manually removed
* Placenta deeply implanted
* Retained placental fragments

## Treatment

* Oxytocin
* Methergine
* Prostaglandins
* Ultrasound to locate fragments
* D&C
* Antibiotics

## Nursing Care

* Inspect placenta carefully after delivery
* Monitor vital signs
* Monitor lochia flow
* Teach mother to report persistent bright red bleeding
* Teach mother to report return to bright red bleeding after lochia changed to pink or white

---

# 34. Hypovolemic Shock

## Definition

Hypovolemic shock occurs when blood volume is depleted and cannot fill the circulatory system.

## Body’s Response

| Finding                        | Why It Happens                |
| ------------------------------ | ----------------------------- |
| Increased HR                   | First sign; body compensates  |
| Increased RR                   | Body tries to increase oxygen |
| Narrow pulse pressure          | Decreased circulating volume  |
| Pale, cold, clammy skin        | Blood shunted to vital organs |
| Decreased blood flow to organs | Shock progression             |

## Signs of Shock

* Pulse >110 bpm
* Systolic BP <90 mm Hg
* Cold, clammy skin
* Pallor
* Respiratory rate >30/min
* Anxiety
* Confusion
* Unconsciousness

## Medical Management

* Stop blood loss
* Administer IV fluids
* Blood transfusions
* Oxygen
* Foley catheter
* Possible ICU care

## Nursing Care

* Vital signs q15 minutes
* Observe closely for shock
* Assess vaginal bleeding
* Check fundus
* Maintain IV access
* Monitor urine output
* Educate patient/family as appropriate

## NCLEX Priority

The first sign of hypovolemic shock is often **tachycardia**, not hypotension.

Hypotension is usually a later sign.

---

# 35. Subinvolution of the Uterus

## Definitions

**Involution:** return of uterus to nonpregnant condition.
**Subinvolution:** slower than expected return to nonpregnant condition.

## Main Causes

* Infection
* Retained placental fragments

## Signs and Symptoms

* Fundal height greater than expected
* Persistent lochia rubra
* Slowed progression through lochia stages
* Pelvic pain
* Pelvic heaviness
* Fatigue

## Treatment

* Methergine
* Antibiotics
* D&C

## Teaching

Teach mother to report:

* Signs of infection
* Persistent bright red bleeding
* Foul-smelling lochia
* Pelvic pain
* Fundus not descending as expected, if taught to palpate

# 36. Thromboembolic Disorders

## Types

| Disorder                             | Description                                                                                            |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Superficial vein thrombosis, SVT** | Painful, hard, reddened, warm vein easily seen                                                         |
| **Deep vein thrombosis, DVT**        | Pain, calf tenderness, leg edema, color changes, pain when walking                                     |
| **Pulmonary embolism, PE**           | Sudden chest pain, cough, dyspnea, decreased LOC, palpitations, hemoptysis, faintness, low-grade fever |

## Risk Factors

* Inactivity
* Obesity
* Cesarean birth
* Smoking
* History of thrombosis
* Varicose veins
* Diabetes mellitus
* Prolonged time in stirrups during second stage of labor
* Maternal age older than 35
* Parity greater than 3

## Treatment

* Analgesics
* Local heat
* Elevation of legs
* Anticoagulants for DVT
* Antibiotics if indicated

## Nursing Care

* Prevent clots
* Do not allow crossing legs
* Encourage early ambulation
* Teach coughing and deep breathing
* Pad stirrups
* Teach anticoagulant administration at home if prescribed

## NCLEX Priority

For suspected pulmonary embolism:

* Sudden chest pain
* Shortness of breath
* Cough
* Hemoptysis
* Decreased LOC

This is an emergency.

# 38. Endometritis

## Definition

Endometritis is infection of the uterine lining.

It is usually evident **48-72 hours after delivery**, but may begin around **5 days postpartum**.

## Risk Factors

* Prolonged labor
* Prolonged rupture of membranes
* Increased number of vaginal exams

## Assessment Findings

* Temperature >100.4°F for 2 or more days
* Chills
* Tachycardia
* Headache
* Loss of appetite
* General malaise
* Extreme lower abdominal tenderness
* Backache
* Uterine tenderness
* Foul lochia
* Medium-dark brown lochia
* Poor uterine involution
* Severe prolonged afterpains

## Nursing Care

* Monitor temperature
* Assess uterine tenderness
* Assess lochia
* Administer antibiotics as ordered
* Encourage fluids
* Promote rest
* Teach to report worsening symptoms

# 39. Wound Infections

## Suture Line Infection Signs

* Localized symptoms
* Pus or drainage

Pyelonephritis is kidney infection.

# 41. Mastitis

## Definition

Mastitis is infection of the breast that usually enters through a cracked or fissured nipple.

It may occur from the **7th day postpartum to weeks/months after delivery**.

Common organisms:

* E. coli
* Staphylococcus

## Signs and Symptoms

* Redness and heat in breast
* Tenderness
* Edema
* Heaviness in breast
* Purulent drainage

## Prevention

* Prevent nipple fissures
* Treat nipple damage at once
* Wear supportive bra
* Observe breastfeeding technique
* Do not leave infant on breast too long
* Break suction before removing infant
* Handwashing
* Air dry nipples
* Apply breastmilk to nipples if recommended

## Treatment

* Warm compresses
* Hot shower
* Continue breastfeeding or pump every 2 hours
* Antibiotics
* Rest
* Analgesics
* Increase fluids to 3000 cc/day
* May become localized abscess requiring incision and drainage

## NCLEX Tip

With mastitis, the mother is usually encouraged to **continue breastfeeding or pumping** unless specifically contraindicated.

# 42. Case Study: Nita

## Scenario

Nita is a multipara who vaginally delivered twin boys 4 hours ago.

* Baby 1: 6 lb
* Baby 2: 5 lb 6 oz
* Uneventful recovery
* Temp: 97.9°F
* Pulse: 60 bpm
* Respirations: 20
* BP: 110/70
* Fundus slightly soft and located to right of umbilicus
* Lochia moderate
* Midline episiotomy intact with slight edema

---

## Interpretation

The vital signs are stable, but the fundus is **slightly soft and to the right of the umbilicus**.

This suggests:

* Bladder distention
* Increased risk for uterine atony
* Increased risk for postpartum hemorrhage

Risk factors include:

* Multiparity
* Twin pregnancy
* Uterine overdistention

---

## First Intervention

The nurse should assist Nita to **empty her bladder**.

Then reassess:

* Fundus location
* Fundus firmness
* Lochia amount

If the fundus remains boggy, massage the fundus.

---

## Immediate Teaching

Teach Nita that:

* A full bladder can prevent the uterus from contracting
* She should void frequently, about every 2-3 hours
* She should call the nurse if bleeding increases
* She may have more cramping because she delivered twins and is multiparous
* Breastfeeding can increase cramping because it releases oxytocin

---

## 8 Hours After Birth

Vitals:

* BP 112/80
* Temp 99°F
* Pulse 52
* Respirations 18

### Interpretation

These vital signs are within expected postpartum limits.

Pulse of 52 may be normal postpartum bradycardia.

### Nursing Interventions

* Continue routine monitoring
* Assess fundus and lochia
* Encourage fluids
* Encourage voiding
* Monitor for symptoms

---

## Breastfeeding and Cramping

Nita says:

> “I want to breastfeed, but I really have a lot of cramping when I nurse. I don’t remember having that with the other two children.”

### Best Response

“The cramping is caused by your uterus contracting when you breastfeed. Breastfeeding releases oxytocin, which helps your milk let down and also helps your uterus return to normal size. Since you have had other children and delivered twins, the cramping can feel stronger.”

### Why It Is Worse

* Multiparity causes reduced uterine muscle tone
* Twin pregnancy overstretched the uterus
* Breastfeeding releases oxytocin

### Intervention

* Give prescribed analgesic before breastfeeding
* Encourage voiding before feeding
* Use relaxation and comfort measures
* Reassure that it usually improves after 48 hours

---

## Rhogam and Rubella

### Rhogam Given When

* Mother is Rh negative
* Infant is Rh positive
* Given within 72 hours after birth

### Rubella Vaccine Given When

* Mother is not immune to rubella
* Given in immediate postpartum period

### Teaching

* Rhogam prevents antibody formation against Rh-positive blood
* Rubella/MMR is not given during pregnancy
* Avoid pregnancy after rubella/MMR vaccination according to facility policy/current guidance
* Report reaction to vaccine
* Breastfeeding is generally not contraindicated with rubella vaccination
