# 1. Postpartum Period

## Puerperium

The **postpartum period**, also called the **puerperium**, is the **6-week period following childbirth**.

It is also called the:

* **Fourth trimester**
* **Period of involution**
* **Beginning of the parenting role**

## ATI / NCLEX Focus

The postpartum period is about monitoring for:

| Priority Area                | Why It Matters                                        |
| ---------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| **Hemorrhage**               | Most dangerous in the first 24 hours                  |
| **Infection**                | Can occur in uterus, wound, urinary tract, breast     |
| **Thromboembolism**          | Pregnancy/postpartum state increases clotting factors |
| **Bladder distention**       | Can prevent uterine contraction and cause hemorrhage  |
| **Pain control**             | Promotes rest, bonding, breastfeeding, and ambulation |
| **Maternal-newborn bonding** | Supports attachment and parenting role                |
| **Mood disorders**           | Need to distinguish blues from depression/psychosis   |

# 2. Uterine Involution

## Involution

**Involution** is the process by which the reproductive organs return to their normal nonpregnant state.

After birth, the uterus must contract firmly. This contraction compresses the blood vessels at the placental site and prevents excessive bleeding.

## Why Uterine Contraction Matters

After the placenta detaches, the uterus has open blood vessels where the placenta was attached. A firm uterus acts like a natural clamp.

| Uterus Finding                       | Meaning                                                |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Firm fundus**                      | Expected; uterus is contracting                        |
| **Boggy fundus**                     | Uterine atony; risk for hemorrhage                     |
| **High fundus**                      | Possible bladder distention, clots, or poor involution |
| **Fundus deviated to the right**     | Usually bladder distention                             |
| **Firm fundus + continued bleeding** | Suspect laceration                                     |

---

## Fundal Descent

The **fundus** is the upper portion of the uterus.

### Expected Fundal Location

| Time After Birth   | Expected Finding                               |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Within a few hours | Fundus rises to level of umbilicus             |
| First 24 hours     | Fundus remains around umbilicus                |
| After 24 hours     | Descends about 1 cm or 1 fingerbreadth per day |
| By 10-14 days      | Usually no longer palpable abdominally         |
| By 6 weeks         | Uterus returns close to nonpregnant size       |

## Factors That Can Alter Involution

These increase the risk for poor uterine contraction and postpartum hemorrhage:

* Multiple fetuses
* Hydramnios
* Exhaustion
* Excessive anesthesia
* Retained placental fragments
* Full bladder

## ATI / NCLEX Connection

A uterus that has been **overstretched** has a harder time contracting.

Common causes of uterine overdistention:

* Twins or multiple gestation
* Large infant
* Polyhydramnios / hydramnios
* Grand multiparity

# 3. Afterpains

## Definition

**Afterpains** are intermittent uterine contractions after birth.

They are more common and more painful in:

* Multiparas
* Patients with uterine overdistention
* Breastfeeding mothers

## Why Breastfeeding Causes Cramping

Breastfeeding stimulates the release of **oxytocin**. Oxytocin causes:

* Milk letdown
* Uterine contraction
* Increased afterpains

## Expected Finding

Afterpains usually decrease after about **48 hours**.

## Nursing Interventions

* Explain that cramping is expected, especially with breastfeeding
* Encourage voiding before breastfeeding
* Administer prescribed analgesics before breastfeeding if needed
* Use relaxation and positioning techniques
* Reassure the patient that contractions help prevent hemorrhage

## NCLEX Tip

If a postpartum mother says,
**"I have cramping every time I breastfeed,"**
the nurse should explain that breastfeeding releases oxytocin, which causes the uterus to contract.

# 4. Lochia

## Definition

**Lochia** is postpartum vaginal discharge.

It consists of:

* Blood
* Decidua
* WBCs
* Mucus
* Bacteria

## Lochia Progression

| Type              |              Time Frame | Color / Description              |
| ----------------- | ----------------------: | -------------------------------- |
| **Lochia rubra**  |                Days 1-3 | Red, bloody, fleshy, earthy odor |
| **Lochia serosa** |               Days 4-10 | Pink or pinkish-brown            |
| **Lochia alba**   | Day 10 to about 3 weeks | White, cream, or light yellow    |

## Expected Lochia Pattern

Lochia should gradually:

1. Decrease in amount
2. Lighten in color
3. Progress from rubra → serosa → alba

## Abnormal Lochia Findings

| Finding                                              | Possible Meaning                    |
| ---------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Foul odor                                            | Infection                           |
| Persistent rubra                                     | Subinvolution or retained fragments |
| Return to bright red bleeding after it had lightened | Late postpartum hemorrhage          |
| Large clots                                          | Excessive bleeding                  |
| Saturating pad rapidly                               | Hemorrhage                          |
| Scant lochia with severe pain                        | Possible retained clots             |

CDC lists heavy postpartum bleeding, passing clots larger than an egg, tissue passage, and foul-smelling discharge as urgent warning signs after pregnancy. ([CDC][1])

---

## Documenting Lochia Amount

| Amount            | Pad Saturation                                                  |
| ----------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Scant**         | < 2-inch stain; about 10 mL                                     |
| **Small**         | < 4-inch stain; about 10-25 mL                                  |
| **Moderate**      | < 6-inch stain; about 25-50 mL                                  |
| **Heavy / Large** | > 6-inch stain; about 50-80 mL; one pad saturated within 1 hour |
| **Excessive**     | Saturation of pad within 15 minutes                             |

## NCLEX Priority

**Saturating more than one pad per hour is not normal.**

The nurse should assess:

1. Fundus
2. Lochia amount
3. Vital signs
4. Bladder status
5. Level of consciousness / signs of shock

---

# 5. Perineum

## Expected Findings

The perineum may be:

* Edematous
* Bruised
* Tender
* Affected by episiotomy
* Affected by lacerations
* Affected by hemorrhoids

An episiotomy may take **4-6 months** to completely heal.

## Perineal Comfort Measures

| Time Period       | Intervention                                          |
| ----------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| First 12-24 hours | Cold packs                                            |
| After 24 hours    | Heat, warm packs, sitz baths                          |
| Ongoing           | Peri bottle, anesthetic spray, witch hazel/Tucks pads |

## Perineal Care Teaching

* Cleanse perineum after each voiding or bowel movement
* Use peri bottle from front to back
* Pat dry from front to back
* Apply and remove pads from front to back
* Wash hands before and after pad changes
* Change pads frequently
* Report increasing pain, swelling, foul drainage, or separation of wound edges

# 6. Cardiovascular System

## Expected Blood Loss

| Type of Birth  | Expected Blood Loss |
| -------------- | ------------------: |
| Vaginal birth  |        About 500 mL |
| Cesarean birth |       About 1000 mL |

Some current clinical definitions use **blood loss of 1000 mL or blood loss with signs/symptoms of hypovolemia within 24 hours** to define postpartum hemorrhage. Older teaching materials often distinguish between "early" hemorrhage within 24 hours and "late" hemorrhage after 24 hours.

## Cardiac Output

Cardiac output increases for about **48 hours** postpartum, then decreases and returns to normal around **12 weeks**.

## Fluid Shifts

Postpartum patients commonly experience:

* Diuresis
* Diaphoresis
* Orthostatic hypotension

Diuresis up to **3000 mL/day** may occur.

## ATI / NCLEX Focus

The body is getting rid of extra pregnancy fluid. The patient may sweat a lot and urinate large amounts. This can be normal, but the nurse must watch for:

* Dizziness
* Hypotension
* Tachycardia
* Signs of hypovolemia
* Poor intake

---

## Blood Values

### WBC Count

WBC count may increase up to **30,000**, with an average range of **14,000-16,000**.

This can be normal postpartum due to:

* Inflammation
* Pain
* Stress
* Tissue healing

### Hematocrit

With each **250 mL blood loss**, there may be about a **4-point drop in HCT**.

### Clotting Factors

Clotting factors remain elevated for **3-4 weeks**.

## NCLEX Tip

Postpartum patients are at increased risk for blood clots because clotting factors are elevated. This is why early ambulation is important.

---

# 7. Gastrointestinal System

## Expected Findings

The mother is usually:

* Hungry
* Thirsty
* At risk for constipation

## Constipation Causes

Constipation is common due to:

* Decreased muscle tone
* Dehydration
* Iron supplements
* Opioid pain medication
* Fear of pain with bowel movement
* Perineal trauma
* Hemorrhoids
* Decreased activity

## Expected Bowel Pattern

* First stool usually occurs within **2-3 days**
* Normal pattern usually returns within **8-14 days**

## Nursing Interventions

* Encourage fluids
* Encourage fiber
* Encourage ambulation
* Give stool softeners as prescribed
* Give laxatives if prescribed
* Teach not to strain
* Support perineum with a clean pad during bowel movement if painful

## Patient Teaching

Foods that help prevent constipation:

* Prunes
* Whole-grain cereals
* Whole-grain bread
* Whole-grain pasta
* Fruits
* Vegetables
* Fluids, especially water

---

# 8. Urinary System

## Expected Findings

The bladder has increased capacity due to loss of muscle tone.

The patient may not feel the need to void even when the bladder is distended.

## Postpartum Urinary Output

UOP may increase to **1500-3000 mL/day**.

## Why Bladder Distention Is Dangerous

A full bladder can push the uterus:

* Upward
* To the side, usually right
* Away from midline

This prevents the uterus from contracting effectively and can lead to postpartum hemorrhage.

## Signs of Distended Bladder

| Sign                        | Meaning                         |
| --------------------------- | ------------------------------- |
| Fundus above expected level | Bladder may be full             |
| Fundus displaced to right   | Classic bladder distention sign |
| Excessive lochia            | Uterus cannot contract well     |
| Bladder discomfort          | Retention                       |
| Bulge above symphysis       | Distended bladder               |
| Frequent voids <150 mL      | Overflow retention              |

## Nursing Interventions

* Assist patient to void
* Measure first voids if ordered
* Encourage voiding every 2-3 hours
* Use warm water over perineum
* Provide privacy
* Assist to bathroom when safe
* Use bedpan if needed
* Catheterize if ordered and unable to void

## NCLEX Priority

If the fundus is **boggy and displaced to the right**, the first action is usually to help the patient **empty the bladder**, then reassess and massage the fundus if needed.

---

# 9. Musculoskeletal System

## Expected Findings

The abdominal walls stretch to accommodate the fetus, and muscle tone is diminished.

The woman may find her abdominal muscles:

* Weak
* Soft
* Flabby

## Diastasis Recti

**Diastasis recti** is separation of the longitudinal abdominal muscles during pregnancy.

It can be:

* Minimal
* Severe

## Nursing Teaching

* Avoid intense abdominal exercises immediately postpartum
* Start gentle postpartum exercises as approved
* Use good body mechanics
* Gradually increase activity
* Report severe pain or bulging

---

# 10. Integumentary System

After childbirth, hormone levels decline and the skin gradually returns to the prepregnancy state.

## Expected Changes

| Skin Change       | Postpartum Change                          |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Chloasma          | Disappears                                 |
| Linea nigra       | Disappears                                 |
| Striae gravidarum | Fade to silvery lines but do not disappear |

---

# 11. Endocrine System

## Hormonal Changes

After the placenta is expelled:

* Placental hormones decline
* Adrenal hormones return to prepregnancy levels
* Prolactin initiates milk production within **2-3 days**
* Oxytocin is necessary for milk letdown

## Ovulation

Ovulation may occur before the first menstrual period.

## NCLEX Teaching Point

A postpartum patient can become pregnant before her first period. Contraception should be discussed before discharge.

---

# 12. Immune Considerations

## Rhogam

Rh immune globulin, or Rhogam, is given to prevent Rh sensitization.

## When Rhogam Is Given

| Situation                                            | Indication                                                             |
| ---------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Rh-negative mother during pregnancy                  | Usually around 28 weeks                                                |
| Rh-negative mother gives birth to Rh-positive infant | Within 72 hours postpartum                                             |
| Possible fetal-maternal blood mixing                 | May be needed after trauma, miscarriage, abortion, amniocentesis, etc. |

## Route

Rhogam is administered **IM**, commonly in the deltoid muscle.

## NCLEX Tip

Rhogam is given to the **mother**, not the baby.

---

## Rubella Vaccine

Rubella titers are usually checked in early pregnancy.

If the mother is not immune, she should receive rubella vaccine in the immediate postpartum period. CDC recommends that pregnant women without evidence of rubella immunity be vaccinated immediately postpartum.

## Rubella Vaccine Teaching

* Given postpartum, not during pregnancy
* Given SQ in the upper arm
* Avoid pregnancy after vaccination

### Important Update

Your slide says to avoid pregnancy for **3 months** after rubella immunization. Current ACOG guidance states to avoid pregnancy for **28 days** after receiving MMR. For school testing, follow your course materials. For clinical practice, check current ACOG/CDC guidelines.

# 13. Initial Postpartum Assessment

## Standard Assessment Includes

* Vital signs
* Skin color
* Fundus
* Lochia
* Perineum
* Pain
* Urinary output
* Abdominal incision if present
* Level of feeling and ability to move after anesthesia

## Postpartum Assessment Priorities

Use the nursing process:

1. **Assess fundus**
2. **Assess bleeding**
3. **Assess bladder**
4. **Assess vital signs**
5. **Assess pain**
6. **Assess perineum/incision**
7. **Assess lower extremities**
8. **Assess emotional status**

---

# 14. Vital Signs

## Blood Pressure

Measure BP with the mother in the same position each time.

Compare BP with predelivery values.

| BP Finding   | Concern                              |
| ------------ | ------------------------------------ |
| Increased BP | Possible postpartum preeclampsia     |
| Decreased BP | Dehydration, hypovolemia, hemorrhage |

ACOG identifies postpartum preeclampsia, postpartum hemorrhage, and postpartum endometritis as important serious postpartum conditions that can worsen quickly without prompt recognition and treatment.

---

## Pulse

Average postpartum pulse rate is **60-90 bpm**.

A pulse of **50-60 bpm** may be normal postpartum.

## Tachycardia May Indicate

* Excitement
* Fatigue
* Pain
* Dehydration
* Hypovolemia
* Anemia
* Infection

## Nursing Action for Tachycardia

Assess:

* Fundus
* Lochia
* H&H
* Vital signs
* Skin color
* Level of consciousness

## NCLEX Tip

Tachycardia may be an early sign of hemorrhage or shock.

---

## Respirations

Normal respirations: **12-20 breaths/minute**

Assess breath sounds carefully if the mother:

* Had a cesarean section
* Is a smoker
* Has frequent or recent URI
* Is receiving magnesium sulfate
* Has a history of asthma

## NCLEX Tip

Magnesium sulfate can cause respiratory depression. Always monitor respirations, reflexes, and level of consciousness.

---

## Temperature

A temperature up to **100.4°F** can be common during the first 24 hours.

Possible causes:

* Dehydration
* Normal postpartum leukocytosis

## Abnormal Temperature

Suspect infection if:

* Temperature persists longer than 24 hours
* Temperature exceeds 100.4°F
* Fever is accompanied by chills, foul lochia, uterine tenderness, wound drainage, dysuria, or breast redness

---

# 15. Pain Assessment

Pain is the **fifth vital sign**.

## Assess

* Type
* Location
* Severity
* Timing
* Aggravating factors
* Relief measures
* Response to medication

## Common Postpartum Pain Sources

| Pain Source        | Description                        |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------- |
| Afterpains         | Cramping, worse with breastfeeding |
| Perineal pain      | Episiotomy, lacerations, swelling  |
| Breast tenderness  | Engorgement or breastfeeding       |
| Incisional pain    | Cesarean birth                     |
| Hemorrhoids        | Rectal pain/pressure               |
| Severe rectal pain | Possible hematoma                  |

## Nonverbal Signs of Pain

* Inability to relax
* Inability to sleep
* Restlessness
* Irritability
* Facial grimacing
* Changes in vital signs

---

# 16. Fundus Assessment

## Expected Finding

The fundus should be:

* Firm
* Midline
* At or near the level of the umbilicus shortly after birth

## Abnormal Findings

| Finding                                | Possible Cause | Nursing Action                 |
| -------------------------------------- | -------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Boggy fundus                           | Uterine atony  | Massage fundus                 |
| Fundus high and to right               | Full bladder   | Assist to void                 |
| Fundus firm but bleeding continues     | Laceration     | Notify provider                |
| Fundus higher than expected days later | Subinvolution  | Assess lochia, notify provider |

## Fundal Massage

If the uterus is boggy:

1. Support lower uterine segment with one hand
2. Massage fundus gently with other hand
3. Continue until firm
4. Assess lochia
5. Reassess frequently
6. Notify provider if it does not stay firm

## NCLEX Priority

**Do not aggressively massage a firm fundus.**
Massage is used for a boggy uterus.

---

# 17. Breast Assessment

## Expected Breast Changes

| Time                | Finding              |
| ------------------- | -------------------- |
| 2-3 days postpartum | Full but soft        |
| Around day 3        | Firm, lumpy          |
| Later               | Possible engorgement |

## Breastfeeding Mothers

Teaching:

* Wash nipples with water only
* Avoid soap on nipples
* Keep nipples dry between feedings
* Wear supportive bra
* Ensure correct latch
* Break suction before removing infant from breast
* Alternate breasts
* Report redness, warmth, fever, or severe localized pain

## Non-Breastfeeding Mothers

Goal: suppress lactation.

Teaching:

* Wear tight/supportive bra
* Avoid breast stimulation
* Avoid hot showers directly on breasts
* Avoid pumping
* Avoid massaging breasts
* Avoid sexual foreplay involving breasts
* Use ice packs for discomfort
* Take analgesics as prescribed

---

# 18. Lower Extremity Assessment

## Assess For Thrombophlebitis

Look for:

* Heat
* Redness
* Edema
* Tenderness
* Pain
* Possible absence of pedal pulses

## Homan's Sign

Homan's sign is discomfort in the calf with sharp dorsiflexion of the foot.

If positive, assess for:

* Redness
* Tenderness
* Warmth
* Edema

## NCLEX Safety Note

Do not rely only on Homan's sign. Current nursing practice focuses more on full DVT assessment findings, because Homan's sign is not very reliable.

# 19. Immediate Postpartum Care: First 24 Hours

## Perineal Care

| Time              | Intervention                |
| ----------------- | --------------------------- |
| First 12-24 hours | Cold packs                  |
| After 24 hours    | Heat, warm packs, sitz bath |

## Comfort Measures

* Peri bottle
* Anesthetic spray
* Tucks pads
* Sitz bath
* Analgesics

## Common Analgesics

* Tylenol
* Motrin
* Percocet
* Lortab
* Toradol IV

## Toradol Safety

Toradol can increase bleeding risk, so monitor for prolonged bleeding.

# 20. Nursing Care After Cesarean Birth

## Assessments

| Area                  | Nursing Focus                                      |
| --------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Uterus                | Fundus check same as vaginal birth                 |
| Lochia                | Routine intervals; usually less than vaginal birth |
| Dressing              | Drainage                                           |
| Incision              | REEDA assessment                                   |
| Urinary catheter      | Output, patency                                    |
| Respiratory status    | TCDB, incentive spirometer                         |
| Thrombosis prevention | Ambulation, SCDs                                   |
| Pain control          | PCA, epidural, oral analgesics                     |

## REEDA

| Letter | Meaning       |
| ------ | ------------- |
| R      | Redness       |
| E      | Edema         |
| E      | Ecchymosis    |
| D      | Discharge     |
| A      | Approximation |

## Cesarean Interventions

* Provide pain relief regularly
* Encourage early ambulation
* Assist with coughing and deep breathing
* Use pillow to splint abdomen
* Encourage incentive spirometer
* Use antiembolism stockings or SCDs
* Position with pillow behind back and between knees when side-lying

## NCLEX Tip

Pain control is not just comfort. It helps the patient:

* Ambulate
* Deep breathe
* Prevent pneumonia
* Prevent thrombophlebitis
* Bond with infant
* Breastfeed

# 21. Postpartum Teaching

## Involution Teaching

Teach the mother:

* What fundus should feel like
* How lochia should change
* When bleeding is abnormal
* How a full bladder affects bleeding
* When to call the provider

## Handwashing

Wash hands:

* Before touching breasts
* After diaper changes
* After bladder elimination
* After bowel elimination
* Before handling infant

---

## Breast Care for Lactating Mothers

* Avoid soap on nipples
* Use water only
* Keep nipples dry between feedings
* Wear supportive, well-fitting bra
* Ensure correct latch
* Report signs of mastitis

---

## Lactation Suppression Teaching

For mothers who are not breastfeeding:

* Wear supportive bra or bind breasts 24 hours/day until soft
* Use ice for discomfort
* Use analgesics as prescribed
* Avoid shower water hitting breasts
* Do not pump
* Do not massage breasts
* Avoid nipple stimulation

---

## Cesarean Incision Teaching

* Shower with steri-strips in place if allowed
* Steri-strips will gradually detach
* Incision is closed and unlikely to come apart
* Call provider if incision separates
* Call provider if drainage increases
* Call provider if drainage has foul smell

---

## Perineal Care Teaching

* Cleanse after voiding, bowel movements, and PRN
* Use front-to-back motion
* Pat dry
* Apply and remove pads front to back
* Wash hands before and after pad changes
* Report foul odor, severe pain, drainage, or increasing swelling

---

## Kegel Exercises

Kegels strengthen the **pubococcygeal muscle**, which surrounds the vagina and urinary meatus.

Benefits:

* Prevent loss of muscle tone
* Improve urinary control
* Support pelvic floor recovery

How to perform:

* Contract muscles around vagina
* Hold tightly for 10 seconds
* Relax for 10 seconds
* Repeat 30 cycles

---

## Rest and Sleep Teaching

* Fatigue is normal
* Rest when infant sleeps
* Limit visitors
* Restrict phone calls
* Ask for help
* Group care activities
* Conserve energy

## ATI / NCLEX Teaching Point

Exhaustion can interfere with:

* Healing
* Milk production
* Bonding
* Mood stability
* Ability to learn newborn care

---

## Bowel Elimination Teaching

Prevent constipation with:

* Walking
* Adequate fluids
* 8 glasses of water/day
* Fiber
* Prunes
* Whole grains
* Regular bowel routine
* Stool softeners if prescribed

Teach ways to reduce pain from:

* Perineal trauma
* Hemorrhoids
* Episiotomy
* Lacerations

---

## Body Mechanics Teaching

* Use proper lifting technique
* Avoid heavy lifting
* Tighten abdominal muscles before moving
* Turn to side before getting out of bed
* Gradually increase activity
* Follow provider instructions after cesarean

---

## Follow-Up Appointments

Mother should:

* Schedule 6-week postpartum visit
* Report concerning symptoms before visit
* Ask about postpartum depression screening
* Discuss contraception
* Discuss return to work/activity
