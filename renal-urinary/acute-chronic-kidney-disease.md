# Acute & Chronic Kidney Disease

<p class="mh-subtitle">PN-level review of acute kidney injury, chronic kidney disease, causes, phases, fluid and electrolyte changes, anemia, diet, medications, and priority nursing care</p>

The kidneys remove waste and excess fluid, regulate electrolytes and acid-base balance, help control blood pressure, and produce hormones needed for red blood cell production and bone health.

When kidney function decreases, the client can develop **fluid overload, electrolyte imbalance, metabolic acidosis, anemia, confusion, and uremia.**

### Acute Kidney Injury

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

**Acute kidney injury (AKI)** is a sudden decrease in kidney function, so waste, fluid, potassium, and acids can build up quickly. It was previously called **acute renal failure**. AKI develops over hours to days as kidney filtration suddenly decreases; urine output may decrease, but not every client becomes oliguric. It may be reversible if treated promptly, though severe AKI may require temporary dialysis.

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">
<div markdown="1">
##### Priority Risks

* Hyperkalemia
* Cardiac dysrhythmias
* Fluid overload
* Pulmonary edema
* Metabolic acidosis
* Uremia
* Infection
* Medication toxicity

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Common Manifestations

* Oliguria or anuria
* Edema
* Rapid weight gain
* Hypertension
* Crackles
* Shortness of breath
* Fatigue
* Confusion
* Nausea
* Elevated BUN and creatinine

</div>
</div>

Possible outcomes include complete recovery, partial recovery, progression to CKD, the need for temporary dialysis, permanent kidney failure, or death if severe complications occur.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Causes of Acute Kidney Injury

<div class="detail-table" markdown="1">

| Type | What Is Wrong? | Common Causes |
|---|---|---|
| **Prerenal** | Not enough blood reaches the kidneys | Dehydration, hemorrhage, shock, sepsis, severe hypotension, heart failure |
| **Intrarenal** | Direct damage occurs inside the kidneys | Nephrotoxic medications, contrast dye, glomerulonephritis, infection, prolonged ischemia |
| **Postrenal** | Urine cannot leave because of an obstruction | Kidney stones, enlarged prostate, tumor, blood clot, blocked catheter |

</div>

<div class="renal-strip" markdown="1">
<div markdown="1">
##### Prerenal

*The kidneys are not receiving enough blood.* Causes include severe dehydration, vomiting or diarrhea, hemorrhage, burns, shock, sepsis, severe hypotension, heart failure, and excessive diuretic use. Treatment focuses on restoring circulation and correcting the cause without producing fluid overload.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Intrarenal

*The kidney tissue itself is damaged.* Causes include aminoglycosides, vancomycin, NSAIDs, iodinated contrast, glomerulonephritis, acute tubular injury, severe infection, prolonged poor perfusion, and toxins. Treatment includes stopping the damaging cause when possible and supporting kidney function.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Postrenal

*Urine is blocked after it is formed.* Causes include a kidney stone, enlarged prostate, bladder tumor, pelvic tumor, urethral stricture, blood clot, or a kinked or obstructed catheter. Treatment focuses on relieving the obstruction.

</div>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Memory Cue:</strong> Prerenal = before the kidney. Intrarenal = inside the kidney. Postrenal = after the kidney.
</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Phases of Acute Kidney Injury

Not every client follows every phase exactly, but this pattern is commonly tested.

<h5>Oliguric phase</h5>

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">
<div markdown="1">
##### Urine Output

Urine output is reduced. Class notes may define oliguria as less than 400 mL in 24 hours, but the client may still have AKI without oliguria. Report sudden reductions in urine output.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### What Builds Up?

Fluid, potassium, hydrogen ions, nitrogen wastes, phosphorus, creatinine, and medications cleared by the kidneys.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Expected Problems

Edema, hypertension, weight gain, crackles, hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis, elevated BUN and creatinine, and confusion.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Nursing Priority

Strict intake and output, daily weights, monitor potassium and ECG, assess lungs, follow fluid restriction, avoid nephrotoxins, and prepare for dialysis if ordered.

</div>
</div>

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">
<div markdown="1">
##### Diuretic Phase

Kidney filtration begins to improve, but the tubules may not yet concentrate urine well. Expect increasing urine output, large amounts of dilute urine, decreasing edema, and falling body weight, with risk for dehydration, hypotension, hypokalemia, hyponatremia, weakness, and dizziness.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Recovery Phase

Kidney function gradually stabilizes, BUN and creatinine improve, urine concentration improves, and fluid and electrolyte balance becomes easier to maintain. Recovery can take weeks to months, and some clients do not return to their previous kidney function.

</div>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Important:</strong> More urine does not mean the kidneys are fully recovered. During the diuretic phase, the client can lose too much water and electrolytes.
</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Diagnostics

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">
<div markdown="1">
##### Blood Tests

BUN, serum creatinine, potassium, sodium, calcium, phosphorus, bicarbonate, and CBC.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Urine Assessment

Hourly urine output, urinalysis, urine specific gravity, urine sodium when ordered, urine sediment, and color and clarity.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Imaging

Bladder scan, renal ultrasound, CT scan, imaging for obstruction, and assessment of kidney size and structure.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Medication Review

NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast exposure, diuretics, ACE inhibitors or ARBs, herbal products, and recent medication changes.

</div>
</div>

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

* Potassium continues to rise
* Peaked T waves or dysrhythmia
* New shortness of breath
* Crackles or falling oxygen saturation
* Anuria
* Severe hypertension
* Chest pain
* New confusion or seizure
* Rapid weight gain
* Severe metabolic acidosis

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX Priority:</strong> In AKI, hyperkalemia and pulmonary edema are usually more immediately dangerous than an elevated BUN.
</div>

</section>

</div>

### Chronic Kidney Disease

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

**Chronic kidney disease (CKD)** is long-term kidney damage or reduced filtration that lasts at least 3 months. It was previously called **chronic renal insufficiency** or **chronic renal failure**. It is a progressive loss of functioning nephrons that usually develops slowly; remaining nephrons compensate at first, early disease may have no symptoms, advanced damage is irreversible, and kidney failure may require dialysis or transplant.

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">
<div markdown="1">
##### Most Common Causes

* Diabetes mellitus
* Hypertension
* Chronic glomerular disease
* Polycystic kidney disease
* Recurrent kidney injury
* Repeated obstruction
* Autoimmune disease

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Early vs. Advanced Findings

* **Early:** may be asymptomatic, nocturia, polyuria, dilute urine, mild fatigue, hypertension, protein in urine, gradually rising creatinine.
* **Advanced:** oliguria, edema, hypertension, uremia, pruritus, nausea and anorexia, confusion, anemia, hyperkalemia, bone disease.

</div>
</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### How CKD Progresses

Class notes emphasize this urine-output pattern: in **early CKD**, the kidneys may produce larger amounts of dilute urine while trying to remove waste; in **advanced CKD**, as more nephrons are lost, urine output may fall and the client can become oliguric.

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Class Comparison:</strong> AKI often begins with oliguria and may progress to diuresis during recovery. CKD may begin with polyuria and dilute urine, then progress to oliguria as kidney function worsens.
</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### CKD Stages

CKD is divided into five filtration stages using the estimated glomerular filtration rate, or **eGFR**.

<div class="detail-table" markdown="1">

| Stage | General Kidney Function | PN-Level Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Stage 1** | Kidney damage with normal or high eGFR | Often no symptoms; manage the cause |
| **Stage 2** | Mild decrease | Monitor blood pressure, urine protein, and kidney function |
| **Stage 3** | Moderate decrease | Complications such as anemia may begin |
| **Stage 4** | Severe decrease | Prepare for possible dialysis or transplant |
| **Stage 5** | Kidney failure | Dialysis, transplant, or conservative management may be required |

</div>

You do not need to memorize every exact eGFR range unless your instructor specifically assigns them. Know that a **lower eGFR means poorer filtration**.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Expected Assessment Findings

<div class="detail-table" markdown="1">

| Body System | Possible CKD Findings |
|---|---|
| **Urinary** | Early polyuria/nocturia, later oliguria, dilute urine, proteinuria |
| **Cardiovascular** | Hypertension, edema, heart failure, dysrhythmias |
| **Respiratory** | Crackles, dyspnea, pulmonary edema |
| **Neurologic** | Fatigue, confusion, decreased concentration, neuropathy, seizures late |
| **GI** | Anorexia, nausea, vomiting, metallic taste, uremic breath |
| **Skin** | Dry skin, itching, pallor, bruising, possible uremic frost late |
| **Hematologic** | Anemia, fatigue, pallor, bleeding tendency |
| **Musculoskeletal** | Bone pain, weakness, fractures, calcium-phosphorus imbalance |

</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Uremia

**Uremia** occurs when waste products accumulate and begin causing systemic symptoms such as fatigue, confusion, lethargy, nausea, vomiting, loss of appetite, metallic taste, uremic breath, severe itching, neuropathy, pericarditis, and seizures or coma in severe disease.

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Uremia is not simply a high BUN. It means retained wastes are causing clinical symptoms throughout the body.
</div>

<h5>Uremic frost</h5>

Uremic frost is a rare, late finding in severe untreated uremia in which white crystals appear on the skin after urea is deposited in sweat. Gently cleanse the skin, provide prescribed skin care, avoid vigorous rubbing or scratching, and treat the underlying severe kidney failure.

</section>

### Effects of Decreased Kidney Function

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

When the kidneys fail, several normal kidney jobs are lost.

<div class="detail-table" markdown="1">

| Healthy Kidney Function | Effect of Kidney Failure |
|---|---|
| Removes excess hydrogen ions | Metabolic acidosis |
| Removes potassium | Hyperkalemia and dysrhythmias |
| Removes water and sodium | Edema, hypertension, and pulmonary congestion |
| Removes nitrogen wastes | Elevated BUN, creatinine, and uremic symptoms |
| Removes phosphorus | Hyperphosphatemia |
| Activates vitamin D | Low calcium and renal bone disease |
| Produces erythropoietin | Anemia |
| Helps regulate blood pressure | Hypertension and cardiovascular strain |

</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Hyperkalemia

Potassium is one of the most important renal exam priorities because high potassium can cause fatal dysrhythmias.

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">
<div markdown="1">
##### Possible Findings

* Muscle weakness
* Paresthesia
* Nausea
* Bradycardia
* Irregular pulse
* Peaked T waves
* Widened QRS
* Ventricular dysrhythmias

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Nursing Actions

* Report elevated potassium promptly.
* Place the client on cardiac monitoring when indicated.
* Hold potassium supplements.
* Review medications that increase potassium.
* Follow the prescribed low-potassium diet.
* Administer emergency medications as ordered.
* Prepare for dialysis if potassium remains dangerously elevated.

</div>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Cue:</strong> A potassium of 6.5 mEq/L with ECG changes is more urgent than an elevated creatinine.
</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Metabolic Acidosis

Damaged kidneys cannot remove enough acid or conserve bicarbonate properly. Possible findings include low bicarbonate, decreased blood pH, fatigue, confusion, nausea, deep or rapid respirations, weakness, and hyperkalemia. Treatment may include treating the underlying kidney problem, sodium bicarbonate when prescribed, dialysis in severe cases, and monitoring respiratory and neurologic status.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Fluid Volume Excess

Possible findings include rapid weight gain, peripheral edema, periorbital edema, hypertension, bounding pulse, neck-vein distention, crackles, dyspnea, and decreased oxygen saturation.

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Pulmonary Edema Warning Signs</h3>

Sudden shortness of breath, crackles, pink frothy sputum, restlessness, falling oxygen saturation, severe hypertension, and tachypnea. These findings require immediate intervention.

</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Anemia

The kidneys normally produce **erythropoietin**, which stimulates red blood cell production. When kidney function decreases, erythropoietin decreases, red blood cell production decreases, the client develops anemia, and fatigue, pallor, weakness, and activity intolerance occur.

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">
<div markdown="1">
##### Epoetin Alfa — Monitor

Epoetin alfa may be prescribed to stimulate red blood cell production. Monitor hemoglobin and hematocrit, blood pressure, iron levels, response to therapy, and signs of thrombosis.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Epoetin Alfa — Teaching

The medication does not work immediately, and adequate iron is needed to produce red blood cells. Report severe headache, chest pain, or leg swelling, and blood pressure must be monitored.

</div>
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Class Cue:</strong> Dialysis can remove wastes and fluid, but it does not replace the kidney's erythropoietin production.
</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Calcium and Phosphorus Imbalance

Damaged kidneys cannot remove phosphorus effectively or activate enough vitamin D. This can lead to high phosphorus, low calcium, secondary hyperparathyroidism, calcium pulled from bones, bone pain, weak bones, fractures, and vascular calcification.

<h5>Phosphate binders</h5>

Examples may include calcium acetate, sevelamer, and other prescribed binders. Take phosphate binders **with meals** so the medication binds phosphorus from food in the intestine. Follow prescribed calcium and phosphorus monitoring, and avoid taking the binder at random times away from meals.

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Important Update:</strong> Older notes may mention routine aluminum hydroxide. Aluminum-containing binders are generally avoided for long-term use because aluminum can accumulate in kidney failure.
</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Bleeding Risk

CKD can impair platelet function, so possible findings include easy bruising, nosebleeds, bleeding gums, prolonged bleeding, and GI bleeding. Avoid unnecessary invasive procedures and monitor carefully after venipuncture.

</section>

### Management and Nursing Care

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

Treatment depends on whether the client has AKI or CKD, the cause, laboratory results, urine output, and severity of complications.

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">
<div markdown="1">
##### Intake and Output

* Measure all intake and measure urine accurately.
* Report oliguria or anuria.
* Assess urine color and concentration.
* Include drainage from tubes.
* Follow prescribed fluid limits.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Daily Weight

* Same scale, same time, similar clothing.
* After voiding when possible.
* Record trends—rapid gain usually means retained fluid.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Cardiopulmonary Assessment

Blood pressure, heart rate and rhythm, lung sounds, oxygen saturation, edema, neck-vein distention, and dyspnea.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Neurologic Assessment

Orientation, level of consciousness, confusion, tremor, weakness, seizure activity, and safety awareness.

</div>
</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Diet and Fluid Management

The renal diet is individualized according to labs and urine output.

<div class="detail-table" markdown="1">

| Restriction | Why It May Be Ordered |
|---|---|
| **Sodium** | Reduces thirst, edema, hypertension, and fluid retention |
| **Fluid** | Prevents fluid overload when urine output is reduced |
| **Potassium** | Prevents hyperkalemia and dysrhythmias |
| **Phosphorus** | Helps reduce mineral and bone complications |
| **Protein** | May reduce waste accumulation before dialysis; dialysis clients may need more protein |

</div>

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">
<div markdown="1">
##### Protein Teaching

Do not apply one protein rule to every renal client. A client with advanced CKD who is not receiving dialysis may be prescribed moderate protein restriction, while a client receiving dialysis often needs more protein because protein can be lost during treatment. Follow the individualized renal diet and encourage adequate calories so the body does not break down muscle for energy.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Fluid Allowance

A common class formula may be the **previous 24-hour urine output plus approximately 500 mL** to account for insensible losses, but always follow the actual provider order. Fluids include water, coffee, tea, juice, soup, gelatin, ice cream, ice chips, and frozen treats.

</div>
</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Medication Safety

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">
<div markdown="1">
##### Avoid Nephrotoxins

Use caution with NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, IV contrast, certain antibiotics, herbal supplements, magnesium-containing products, and phosphate-containing enemas.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Renal Dosing

Many drugs leave through the kidneys, so doses may need to be reduced and intervals extended. Monitor for accumulation and toxicity, and never adjust the dose independently.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Blood Pressure Medications

Control of hypertension slows CKD progression. Monitor blood pressure, watch potassium and creatinine, and avoid hypotension that decreases kidney perfusion.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Diuretics

May reduce edema, blood pressure, and pulmonary congestion. Monitor urine output, blood pressure, potassium, dehydration, and daily weight.

</div>
</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Skin, Comfort, and Safety

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">
<div markdown="1">
##### Skin and Comfort Care

For dry skin and pruritus, use lukewarm water, avoid hot baths, use mild soap, apply fragrance-free moisturizer, keep fingernails short, use prescribed antipruritic treatment, prevent scratching and skin breakdown, reposition regularly, and keep linens clean and smooth.

</div>
<div markdown="1">
##### Safety

Clients with uremia or electrolyte imbalance may be confused or weak. Use fall precautions, keep needed items within reach, assist with ambulation, monitor mental status, use seizure precautions when indicated, avoid restraints unless absolutely necessary and ordered, and cluster care to reduce fatigue.

</div>
</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### When Dialysis May Be Needed

Remember **AEIOU**:

<div class="detail-table" markdown="1">

| Letter | Indication |
|---|---|
| **A** | Severe metabolic acidosis |
| **E** | Dangerous electrolyte imbalance, especially hyperkalemia |
| **I** | Certain intoxications or medication overdoses |
| **O** | Fluid overload not responding to treatment |
| **U** | Symptomatic uremia |

</div>

Dialysis is covered in detail on the next page.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Patient Education

* Control blood pressure and diabetes.
* Take medications exactly as prescribed and avoid NSAIDs unless specifically approved.
* Check before taking herbal products or OTC medications.
* Follow fluid, sodium, potassium, phosphorus, and protein instructions.
* Keep laboratory appointments and track daily weight if instructed.
* Report reduced urine output, rapid weight gain, shortness of breath, and muscle weakness or palpitations.
* Protect the skin from scratching and injury.
* Prepare for dialysis access early if advanced CKD is progressing.

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Daily weight is the best simple indicator of overall fluid change. Potassium and respiratory status identify the most immediately dangerous complications.
</div>

</section>

<h3>📋 Acute & Chronic Kidney Disease Big Picture</h3>

* AKI develops suddenly and may be reversible.
* Prerenal AKI means reduced kidney perfusion.
* Intrarenal AKI means direct kidney damage.
* Postrenal AKI means urinary obstruction.
* AKI may progress through oliguric, diuretic, and recovery phases.
* CKD develops gradually and is usually irreversible.
* Early CKD may produce polyuria and dilute urine.
* Advanced CKD can produce oliguria, uremia, fluid overload, and electrolyte imbalance.
* Hyperkalemia and pulmonary edema are immediate dangers.
* Decreased erythropoietin causes anemia.
* High phosphorus and low activated vitamin D contribute to low calcium and bone disease.
* Monitor intake and output, daily weight, blood pressure, lungs, potassium, and mental status.

</div>

<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

* Peaked T waves or dysrhythmia
* Severe muscle weakness
* Sudden shortness of breath
* Crackles or falling oxygen saturation
* Pink frothy sputum
* Anuria
* Rapid weight gain
* Severe hypertension
* New confusion or seizure
* Chest pain
* Signs of pericarditis

</div>

<h3>NCLEX Tip</h3>

When several renal findings are present, prioritize:

1. Airway and breathing
2. Hyperkalemia or ECG changes
3. Severe fluid overload
4. Neurologic changes
5. Urine-output changes
6. Elevated kidney labs

<div class="mh-table-card" markdown="1">

<h3>AKI vs. CKD</h3>

| Feature                 | Acute Kidney Injury                     | Chronic Kidney Disease                                          |
| ----------------------- | --------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Onset**               | Sudden                                  | Gradual                                                         |
| **Duration**            | Hours to days                           | At least 3 months                                               |
| **Reversibility**       | May recover                             | Usually irreversible                                            |
| **Common causes**       | Shock, dehydration, toxins, obstruction | Diabetes, hypertension, chronic disease                         |
| **Early urine pattern** | Often oliguria, but may be nonoliguric  | May have polyuria and dilute urine                              |
| **Later urine pattern** | Diuresis may occur during recovery      | Oliguria may occur with advanced disease                        |
| **Anemia**              | Less likely immediately                 | Common from low erythropoietin                                  |
| **Bone disease**        | Not typical in brief AKI                | Common in advanced CKD                                          |
| **Kidney size**         | Often normal                            | May become small and scarred depending on cause                 |
| **Outcome**             | Recovery, CKD, dialysis, or death       | Slowing progression, dialysis, transplant, or conservative care |

</div>

<div class="mh-table-card" markdown="1">

<h3>Quick Terms</h3>

| Term                     | Simple Meaning                                             |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| **AKI**                  | Sudden loss of kidney function                             |
| **CKD**                  | Kidney damage or poor filtration lasting at least 3 months |
| **Prerenal**             | Reduced blood flow to the kidneys                          |
| **Intrarenal**           | Damage inside the kidney                                   |
| **Postrenal**            | Obstruction after urine is formed                          |
| **Oliguria**             | Abnormally low urine output                                |
| **Anuria**               | Little to no urine output                                  |
| **Polyuria**             | Abnormally high urine output                               |
| **Azotemia**             | Elevated nitrogen waste in the blood                       |
| **Uremia**               | Waste buildup causing systemic symptoms                    |
| **Hyperkalemia**         | High potassium                                             |
| **Metabolic acidosis**   | Too much acid or too little bicarbonate                    |
| **eGFR**                 | Estimate of kidney filtration                              |
| **Erythropoietin**       | Kidney hormone that stimulates red blood cell production   |
| **Phosphate binder**     | Medication that binds food phosphorus in the intestine     |
| **Renal osteodystrophy** | Bone disease caused by CKD mineral imbalance               |

</div>

<div class="mh-columns" markdown="1">

<div class="quick-review" markdown="1">
<h3>Quick Review</h3>

1. AKI may result from low perfusion, kidney damage, or obstruction.
2. CKD is progressive and irreversible.
3. Hyperkalemia can cause fatal dysrhythmias.
4. Fluid overload can cause pulmonary edema.
5. The AKI diuretic phase can cause dehydration and low electrolytes.
6. CKD causes anemia because erythropoietin decreases.
7. CKD causes high phosphorus and often low calcium.
8. Phosphate binders are taken with meals.
9. Daily weight is the best simple fluid indicator.
10. Avoid nephrotoxic medications and adjust renally cleared drugs.
11. Dialysis may be required for acidosis, electrolytes, intoxication, overload, or uremia.

</div>

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">
<h3>Do Not Miss</h3>

* Do not assume all AKI clients are oliguric.
* Do not assume polyuria means full recovery.
* Do not ignore an obstructed catheter as a possible postrenal cause.
* Do not give potassium supplements without reviewing the potassium level.
* Do not push fluids into a client with pulmonary edema.
* Do not use NSAIDs casually in kidney disease.
* Do not give phosphate binders away from meals.
* Do not use the same protein rule for dialysis and nondialysis clients.
* Do not ignore new confusion; it can indicate uremia or electrolyte imbalance.
* Do not prioritize BUN over breathing difficulty or ECG changes.

</div>
