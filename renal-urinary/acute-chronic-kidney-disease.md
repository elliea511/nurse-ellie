# Acute & Chronic Kidney Disease

<p class="mh-subtitle">PN-level review of acute kidney injury, chronic kidney disease, causes, phases, fluid and electrolyte changes, anemia, diet, medications, and priority nursing care</p>

The kidneys remove waste and excess fluid, regulate electrolytes and acid-base balance, help control blood pressure, and produce hormones needed for red blood cell production and bone health.

When kidney function decreases, the client can develop **fluid overload, electrolyte imbalance, metabolic acidosis, anemia, confusion, and uremia.**

### Acute Kidney Injury

**Acute kidney injury (AKI)** is a sudden decrease in kidney function. Waste, fluid, potassium, and acids can build up quickly.

AKI was previously called **acute renal failure**.

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">

<div markdown="1">
##### What It Is

* Develops over hours to days.
* Kidney filtration suddenly decreases.
* Urine output may decrease, but not every client becomes oliguric.
* Waste and electrolytes accumulate.
* May be reversible if treated promptly.
* Severe AKI may require temporary dialysis.

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Priority Risks

* Hyperkalemia
* Cardiac dysrhythmias
* Fluid overload
* Pulmonary edema
* Metabolic acidosis
* Uremia
* Infection
* Medication toxicity

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Common Manifestations

* Oliguria or anuria
* Edema
* Rapid weight gain
* Hypertension
* Crackles
* Shortness of breath
* Fatigue
* Confusion
* Nausea
* Elevated BUN and creatinine

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Possible Outcomes

* Complete recovery
* Partial recovery
* Progression to CKD
* Need for temporary dialysis
* Permanent kidney failure
* Death if severe complications occur

</div>

</div>

#### Causes of Acute Kidney Injury

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Type</th>
        <th>What Is Wrong?</th>
        <th>Common Causes</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Prerenal</strong></td>
        <td>Not enough blood reaches the kidneys</td>
        <td>Dehydration, hemorrhage, shock, sepsis, severe hypotension, heart failure</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Intrarenal</strong></td>
        <td>Direct damage occurs inside the kidneys</td>
        <td>Nephrotoxic medications, contrast dye, glomerulonephritis, infection, prolonged ischemia</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Postrenal</strong></td>
        <td>Urine cannot leave because of an obstruction</td>
        <td>Kidney stones, enlarged prostate, tumor, blood clot, blocked catheter</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">

<div markdown="1">
##### Prerenal AKI

Think: **The kidneys are not receiving enough blood.**

Possible causes:

* Severe dehydration
* Vomiting or diarrhea
* Hemorrhage
* Burns
* Shock
* Sepsis
* Severe hypotension
* Heart failure
* Excessive diuretic use

Treatment focuses on restoring circulation and correcting the cause without producing fluid overload.

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Intrarenal AKI

Think: **The kidney tissue itself is damaged.**

Possible causes:

* Aminoglycosides
* Vancomycin
* NSAIDs
* Iodinated contrast
* Glomerulonephritis
* Acute tubular injury
* Severe infection
* Prolonged poor perfusion
* Toxins

Treatment includes stopping the damaging cause when possible and supporting kidney function.

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Postrenal AKI

Think: **Urine is blocked after it is formed.**

Possible causes:

* Kidney stone
* Enlarged prostate
* Bladder tumor
* Pelvic tumor
* Urethral stricture
* Blood clot
* Kinked or obstructed catheter

Treatment focuses on relieving the obstruction.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Memory Cue:</strong> Prerenal = before the kidney. Intrarenal = inside the kidney. Postrenal = after the kidney.
</div>

#### Phases of Acute Kidney Injury

Not every client follows every phase exactly, but this pattern is commonly tested.

#### Oliguric Phase

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">

<div markdown="1">
##### Urine Output

* Urine output is reduced.
* Class notes may define oliguria as less than 400 mL in 24 hours.
* The client may still have AKI without oliguria.
* Report sudden reductions in urine output.

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### What Builds Up?

* Fluid
* Potassium
* Hydrogen ions
* Nitrogen wastes
* Phosphorus
* Creatinine
* Medications cleared by the kidneys

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Expected Problems

* Edema
* Hypertension
* Weight gain
* Crackles
* Hyperkalemia
* Metabolic acidosis
* Elevated BUN and creatinine
* Confusion

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Nursing Priority

* Strict intake and output
* Daily weights
* Monitor potassium and ECG
* Assess lungs
* Follow fluid restriction
* Avoid nephrotoxins
* Prepare for dialysis if ordered

</div>

</div>

#### Diuretic Phase

Kidney filtration begins to improve, but the tubules may not yet concentrate urine well.

Expected findings:

* Increasing urine output
* Large amounts of dilute urine
* Decreasing edema
* Falling body weight
* Risk for dehydration
* Risk for hypotension
* Risk for hypokalemia
* Risk for hyponatremia
* Weakness and dizziness

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Important:</strong> More urine does not mean the kidneys are fully recovered. During the diuretic phase, the client can lose too much water and electrolytes.
</div>

#### Recovery Phase

* Kidney function gradually stabilizes.
* BUN and creatinine improve.
* Urine concentration improves.
* Fluid and electrolyte balance becomes easier to maintain.
* Recovery can take weeks to months.
* Some clients do not return to their previous kidney function.

#### Diagnostics

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">

<div markdown="1">
##### Blood Tests

* BUN
* Serum creatinine
* Potassium
* Sodium
* Calcium
* Phosphorus
* Bicarbonate
* CBC

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Urine Assessment

* Hourly urine output
* Urinalysis
* Urine specific gravity
* Urine sodium when ordered
* Urine sediment
* Color and clarity

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Imaging

May include:

* Bladder scan
* Renal ultrasound
* CT scan
* Imaging for obstruction
* Assessment of kidney size and structure

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Medication Review

Assess for:

* NSAIDs
* Aminoglycosides
* Contrast exposure
* Diuretics
* ACE inhibitors or ARBs
* Herbal products
* Recent medication changes

</div>

</div>

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

* Potassium continues to rise
* Peaked T waves or dysrhythmia
* New shortness of breath
* Crackles or falling oxygen saturation
* Anuria
* Severe hypertension
* Chest pain
* New confusion or seizure
* Rapid weight gain
* Severe metabolic acidosis

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX Priority:</strong> In AKI, hyperkalemia and pulmonary edema are usually more immediately dangerous than an elevated BUN.
</div>

</div>

### Chronic Kidney Disease

**Chronic kidney disease (CKD)** is long-term kidney damage or reduced filtration that lasts at least 3 months.

CKD was previously called **chronic renal insufficiency** or **chronic renal failure**.

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">

<div markdown="1">
##### What It Is

* Progressive loss of functioning nephrons.
* Usually develops slowly.
* Remaining nephrons compensate at first.
* Early disease may have no symptoms.
* Advanced damage is irreversible.
* Kidney failure may require dialysis or transplant.

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Most Common Causes

* Diabetes mellitus
* Hypertension
* Chronic glomerular disease
* Polycystic kidney disease
* Recurrent kidney injury
* Repeated obstruction
* Autoimmune disease

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Early Findings

* May be asymptomatic
* Nocturia
* Polyuria
* Dilute urine
* Mild fatigue
* Hypertension
* Protein in urine
* Gradually rising creatinine

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Advanced Findings

* Oliguria
* Edema
* Hypertension
* Uremia
* Pruritus
* Nausea and anorexia
* Confusion
* Anemia
* Hyperkalemia
* Bone disease

</div>

</div>

#### How CKD Progresses

Your class notes emphasize this urine-output pattern:

**Early CKD:** The kidneys may produce larger amounts of dilute urine while trying to remove waste.

**Advanced CKD:** As more nephrons are lost, urine output may fall and the client can become oliguric.

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Class Comparison:</strong> AKI often begins with oliguria and may progress to diuresis during recovery. CKD may begin with polyuria and dilute urine, then progress to oliguria as kidney function worsens.
</div>

#### Uremia

**Uremia** occurs when waste products accumulate and begin causing systemic symptoms.

Possible manifestations:

* Fatigue
* Confusion
* Lethargy
* Nausea
* Vomiting
* Loss of appetite
* Metallic taste
* Uremic breath
* Severe itching
* Neuropathy
* Pericarditis
* Seizures or coma in severe disease

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Uremia is not simply a high BUN. It means retained wastes are causing clinical symptoms throughout the body.
</div>

#### Uremic Frost

Uremic frost is a rare, late finding in severe untreated uremia.

* White crystals appear on the skin after urea is deposited in sweat.
* Gently cleanse the skin.
* Provide prescribed skin care.
* Avoid vigorous rubbing or scratching.
* Treat the underlying severe kidney failure.

</div>

### Effects of Decreased Kidney Function

When the kidneys fail, several normal kidney jobs are lost.

  <table>

        <th>Effect of Kidney Failure</th>
      </tr>

        <td>Metabolic acidosis</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td>Removes potassium</td>
        <td>Hyperkalemia and dysrhythmias</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td>Removes water and sodium</td>
        <td>Edema, hypertension, and pulmonary congestion</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>

      </tr>
      <tr>

      </tr>

      </tr>

        <td>Anemia</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>

#### Hyperkalemia

Potassium is one of the most important renal exam priorities because high potassium can cause fatal dysrhythmias.

Possible findings:

* Muscle weakness
* Paresthesia
* Nausea
* Bradycardia
* Irregular pulse
* Peaked T waves
* Widened QRS
* Ventricular dysrhythmias

Nursing actions:

* Report elevated potassium promptly.
* Place the client on cardiac monitoring when indicated.
* Hold potassium supplements.
* Review medications that increase potassium.
* Follow the prescribed low-potassium diet.
* Administer emergency medications as ordered.
* Prepare for dialysis if potassium remains dangerously elevated.

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Cue:</strong> A potassium of 6.5 mEq/L with ECG changes is more urgent than an elevated creatinine.
</div>

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">

<div markdown="1">
##### Metabolic Acidosis

Damaged kidneys cannot remove enough acid or conserve bicarbonate properly.

Possible findings:

* Low bicarbonate
* Decreased blood pH
* Fatigue
* Confusion
* Nausea
* Deep or rapid respirations
* Weakness
* Hyperkalemia

Treatment may include:

* Treating the underlying kidney problem
* Sodium bicarbonate when prescribed
* Dialysis in severe cases
* Monitoring respiratory and neurologic status

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Fluid Volume Excess

Possible findings:

* Rapid weight gain
* Peripheral edema
* Periorbital edema
* Hypertension
* Bounding pulse
* Neck-vein distention
* Crackles
* Dyspnea
* Decreased oxygen saturation

</div>

</div>

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Pulmonary Edema Warning Signs</h3>

* Sudden shortness of breath
* Crackles
* Pink frothy sputum
* Restlessness
* Falling oxygen saturation
* Severe hypertension
* Tachypnea

These findings require immediate intervention.

</div>

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">

<div markdown="1">
##### Anemia

The kidneys normally produce **erythropoietin**, which stimulates red blood cell production.

When kidney function decreases:

* Erythropoietin decreases.
* Red blood cell production decreases.
* The client develops anemia.
* Fatigue, pallor, weakness, and activity intolerance occur.

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Epoetin Alfa

Epoetin alfa may be prescribed to stimulate red blood cell production.

Monitor:

* Hemoglobin and hematocrit
* Blood pressure
* Iron levels
* Response to therapy
* Signs of thrombosis

Teaching:

* The medication does not work immediately.
* Adequate iron is needed to produce red blood cells.
* Report severe headache, chest pain, or leg swelling.
* Blood pressure must be monitored.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Class Cue:</strong> Dialysis can remove wastes and fluid, but it does not replace the kidney’s erythropoietin production.
</div>

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">

<div markdown="1">
##### Calcium and Phosphorus Imbalance

Damaged kidneys cannot remove phosphorus effectively or activate enough vitamin D.

This can lead to:

* High phosphorus
* Low calcium
* Secondary hyperparathyroidism
* Calcium pulled from bones
* Bone pain
* Weak bones
* Fractures
* Vascular calcification

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Phosphate Binders

Examples may include:

* Calcium acetate
* Sevelamer
* Other prescribed binders

Teaching:

* Take phosphate binders **with meals**.
* The medication binds phosphorus from food in the intestine.
* Follow prescribed calcium and phosphorus monitoring.
* Avoid taking the binder at random times away from meals.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Important Update:</strong> Older notes may mention routine aluminum hydroxide. Aluminum-containing binders are generally avoided for long-term use because aluminum can accumulate in kidney failure.
</div>

#### Bleeding Risk

CKD can impair platelet function.

Possible findings:

* Easy bruising
* Nosebleeds
* Bleeding gums
* Prolonged bleeding
* GI bleeding

Avoid unnecessary invasive procedures and monitor carefully after venipuncture.

</div>

### Management and Nursing Care

Treatment depends on whether the client has AKI or CKD, the cause, laboratory results, urine output, and severity of complications.

#### Core Nursing Monitoring

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">

<div markdown="1">
##### Intake and Output

* Measure all intake.
* Measure urine accurately.
* Report oliguria or anuria.
* Assess urine color and concentration.
* Include drainage from tubes.
* Follow prescribed fluid limits.

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Daily Weight

* Same scale
* Same time
* Similar clothing
* After voiding when possible
* Record trends
* Rapid gain usually means retained fluid

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Cardiopulmonary Assessment

* Blood pressure
* Heart rate and rhythm
* Lung sounds
* Oxygen saturation
* Edema
* Neck-vein distention
* Dyspnea

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Neurologic Assessment

* Orientation
* Level of consciousness
* Confusion
* Tremor
* Weakness
* Seizure activity
* Safety awareness

</div>

</div>

#### Diet and Fluid Management

The renal diet is individualized according to labs and urine output.

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Restriction</th>
        <th>Why It May Be Ordered</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Sodium</strong></td>
        <td>Reduces thirst, edema, hypertension, and fluid retention</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Fluid</strong></td>
        <td>Prevents fluid overload when urine output is reduced</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Potassium</strong></td>
        <td>Prevents hyperkalemia and dysrhythmias</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Phosphorus</strong></td>
        <td>Helps reduce mineral and bone complications</td>
      </tr>

      </tr>

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">

<div markdown="1">
##### Protein Teaching

Do not apply one protein rule to every renal client.

* A client with advanced CKD who is not receiving dialysis may be prescribed moderate protein restriction.
* A client receiving dialysis often needs more protein because protein can be lost during treatment.
* Follow the individualized renal diet.
* Encourage adequate calories so the body does not break down muscle for energy.

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Fluid Allowance

A common class formula may be:

**Previous 24-hour urine output plus approximately 500 mL**

This may be used to account for insensible losses, but always follow the actual provider order.

Fluids include:

* Water
* Coffee
* Tea
* Juice
* Soup
* Gelatin
* Ice cream
* Ice chips
* Frozen treats

</div>

</div>

#### Medication Safety

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">

<div markdown="1">
##### Avoid Nephrotoxins

Use caution with:

* NSAIDs
* Aminoglycosides
* IV contrast
* Certain antibiotics
* Herbal supplements
* Magnesium-containing products
* Phosphate-containing enemas

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Renal Dosing

* Many drugs leave through the kidneys.
* Doses may need to be reduced.
* Intervals may need to be extended.
* Monitor for accumulation and toxicity.
* Never adjust the dose independently.

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Blood Pressure Medications

* Control of hypertension slows CKD progression.
* Monitor blood pressure.
* Watch potassium.
* Watch creatinine.
* Avoid hypotension that decreases kidney perfusion.

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Diuretics

May reduce:

* Edema
* Blood pressure
* Pulmonary congestion

Monitor:

* Urine output
* Blood pressure
* Potassium
* Dehydration
* Daily weight

</div>

</div>

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">

<div markdown="1">
##### Skin and Comfort Care

For dry skin and pruritus:

* Use lukewarm water.
* Avoid hot baths.
* Use mild soap.
* Apply fragrance-free moisturizer.
* Keep fingernails short.
* Use prescribed antipruritic treatment.
* Prevent scratching and skin breakdown.
* Reposition regularly.
* Keep linens clean and smooth.

</div>

<div markdown="1">
##### Safety

Clients with uremia or electrolyte imbalance may be confused or weak.

* Use fall precautions.
* Keep needed items within reach.
* Assist with ambulation.
* Monitor mental status.
* Use seizure precautions when indicated.
* Avoid restraints unless absolutely necessary and ordered.
* Cluster care to reduce fatigue.

</div>

</div>

#### When Dialysis May Be Needed

Remember **AEIOU**:

| Letter | Indication                                               |
| ------ | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| **A**  | Severe metabolic acidosis                                |
| **E**  | Dangerous electrolyte imbalance, especially hyperkalemia |
| **I**  | Certain intoxications or medication overdoses            |
| **O**  | Fluid overload not responding to treatment               |
| **U**  | Symptomatic uremia                                       |

Dialysis is covered in detail on the next page.

#### Patient Education

* Control blood pressure and diabetes.
* Take medications exactly as prescribed.
* Avoid NSAIDs unless specifically approved.
* Check before taking herbal products or OTC medications.
* Follow fluid, sodium, potassium, phosphorus, and protein instructions.
* Keep laboratory appointments.
* Track daily weight if instructed.
* Report reduced urine output.
* Report rapid weight gain.
* Report shortness of breath.
* Report muscle weakness or palpitations.
* Protect the skin from scratching and injury.
* Prepare for dialysis access early if advanced CKD is progressing.

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Daily weight is the best simple indicator of overall fluid change. Potassium and respiratory status identify the most immediately dangerous complications.
</div>

</div>

<div class="key-findings" markdown="1">
<h3>📋 Acute & Chronic Kidney Disease Big Picture</h3>

* AKI develops suddenly and may be reversible.
* Prerenal AKI means reduced kidney perfusion.
* Intrarenal AKI means direct kidney damage.
* Postrenal AKI means urinary obstruction.
* AKI may progress through oliguric, diuretic, and recovery phases.
* CKD develops gradually and is usually irreversible.
* Early CKD may produce polyuria and dilute urine.
* Advanced CKD can produce oliguria, uremia, fluid overload, and electrolyte imbalance.
* Hyperkalemia and pulmonary edema are immediate dangers.
* Decreased erythropoietin causes anemia.
* High phosphorus and low activated vitamin D contribute to low calcium and bone disease.
* Monitor intake and output, daily weight, blood pressure, lungs, potassium, and mental status.

</div>

<div class="mh-col" markdown="1">

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

* Peaked T waves or dysrhythmia
* Severe muscle weakness
* Sudden shortness of breath
* Crackles or falling oxygen saturation
* Pink frothy sputum
* Anuria
* Rapid weight gain
* Severe hypertension
* New confusion or seizure
* Chest pain
* Signs of pericarditis

</div>

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">
<h3>NCLEX Tip</h3>

When several renal findings are present, prioritize:

1. Airway and breathing
2. Hyperkalemia or ECG changes
3. Severe fluid overload
4. Neurologic changes
5. Urine-output changes
6. Elevated kidney labs

</div>

<div class="mh-table-card" markdown="1">

<h3>AKI vs. CKD</h3>

| Feature                 | Acute Kidney Injury                     | Chronic Kidney Disease                                          |
| ----------------------- | --------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Onset**               | Sudden                                  | Gradual                                                         |
| **Duration**            | Hours to days                           | At least 3 months                                               |
| **Reversibility**       | May recover                             | Usually irreversible                                            |
| **Common causes**       | Shock, dehydration, toxins, obstruction | Diabetes, hypertension, chronic disease                         |
| **Early urine pattern** | Often oliguria, but may be nonoliguric  | May have polyuria and dilute urine                              |
| **Later urine pattern** | Diuresis may occur during recovery      | Oliguria may occur with advanced disease                        |
| **Anemia**              | Less likely immediately                 | Common from low erythropoietin                                  |
| **Bone disease**        | Not typical in brief AKI                | Common in advanced CKD                                          |
| **Kidney size**         | Often normal                            | May become small and scarred depending on cause                 |
| **Outcome**             | Recovery, CKD, dialysis, or death       | Slowing progression, dialysis, transplant, or conservative care |

</div>

<div class="mh-table-card" markdown="1">

<h3>Quick Terms</h3>

| Term                     | Simple Meaning                                             |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| **AKI**                  | Sudden loss of kidney function                             |
| **CKD**                  | Kidney damage or poor filtration lasting at least 3 months |
| **Prerenal**             | Reduced blood flow to the kidneys                          |
| **Intrarenal**           | Damage inside the kidney                                   |
| **Postrenal**            | Obstruction after urine is formed                          |
| **Oliguria**             | Abnormally low urine output                                |
| **Anuria**               | Little to no urine output                                  |
| **Polyuria**             | Abnormally high urine output                               |
| **Azotemia**             | Elevated nitrogen waste in the blood                       |
| **Uremia**               | Waste buildup causing systemic symptoms                    |
| **Hyperkalemia**         | High potassium                                             |
| **Metabolic acidosis**   | Too much acid or too little bicarbonate                    |
| **eGFR**                 | Estimate of kidney filtration                              |
| **Erythropoietin**       | Kidney hormone that stimulates red blood cell production   |
| **Phosphate binder**     | Medication that binds food phosphorus in the intestine     |
| **Renal osteodystrophy** | Bone disease caused by CKD mineral imbalance               |

</div>

<div class="mh-columns" markdown="1">

<div class="quick-review" markdown="1">
<h3>Quick Review</h3>

1. AKI may result from low perfusion, kidney damage, or obstruction.
2. CKD is progressive and irreversible.
3. Hyperkalemia can cause fatal dysrhythmias.
4. Fluid overload can cause pulmonary edema.
5. The AKI diuretic phase can cause dehydration and low electrolytes.
6. CKD causes anemia because erythropoietin decreases.
7. CKD causes high phosphorus and often low calcium.
8. Phosphate binders are taken with meals.
9. Daily weight is the best simple fluid indicator.
10. Avoid nephrotoxic medications and adjust renally cleared drugs.
11. Dialysis may be required for acidosis, electrolytes, intoxication, overload, or uremia.

</div>

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">
<h3>Do Not Miss</h3>

* Do not assume all AKI clients are oliguric.
* Do not assume polyuria means full recovery.
* Do not ignore an obstructed catheter as a possible postrenal cause.
* Do not give potassium supplements without reviewing the potassium level.
* Do not push fluids into a client with pulmonary edema.
* Do not use NSAIDs casually in kidney disease.
* Do not give phosphate binders away from meals.
* Do not use the same protein rule for dialysis and nondialysis clients.
* Do not ignore new confusion; it can indicate uremia or electrolyte imbalance.
* Do not prioritize BUN over breathing difficulty or ECG changes.

</div>
