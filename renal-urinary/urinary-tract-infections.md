# Urinary Tract Infections

<p class="mh-subtitle">PN-level review of cystitis, pyelonephritis, urine collection, medications, complications, prevention, and priority nursing care</p>

A **urinary tract infection (UTI)** occurs when microorganisms enter and multiply within the urinary tract. A lower UTI usually affects the **urethra or bladder**, while an upper UTI affects the **kidneys**. The most common lower UTI is **cystitis**, and a kidney infection is called **pyelonephritis**.

### Cystitis: Lower Urinary Tract Infection

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>What It Is</strong>

* Infection and inflammation of the bladder.
* Usually caused by bacteria entering through the urethra.
* The most common type of UTI.
* Can spread upward into the kidneys if untreated.
* Women are affected more often because the urethra is shorter and closer to the rectum.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Causes / Risk Factors</strong>

* Female anatomy
* Previous UTI
* Sexual activity
* Pregnancy
* Menopause
* Poor perineal hygiene
* Urinary retention
* Incomplete bladder emptying
* Enlarged prostate
* Kidney stones or obstruction
* Diabetes mellitus
* Indwelling urinary catheter
* Dehydration

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Expected Manifestations</strong>

* Urinary frequency
* Urinary urgency
* Dysuria: pain or burning with urination
* Suprapubic pain or tenderness
* Small amounts of urine with each void
* Cloudy urine
* Strong-smelling urine
* Hematuria
* Pyuria

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Concerns</strong>

* Infection spreading to the kidneys
* Fever or systemic symptoms
* Urinary obstruction
* Dehydration
* Recurrent infection
* Antibiotic resistance
* Sepsis in a vulnerable client

</div>

</div>

#### Diagnostics

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Urinalysis</strong>

May show:

* Leukocyte esterase
* Nitrites
* White blood cells
* Bacteria
* Blood
* Cloudy appearance

A urinalysis supports the diagnosis but does not identify the exact antibiotic sensitivity.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Urine Culture & Sensitivity</strong>

* Identifies the infecting organism.
* Determines which antibiotics will treat it.
* Obtain before the first antibiotic dose whenever possible.
* Do not delay emergency treatment in a severely ill client.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Additional Testing</strong>

May be needed for:

* Recurrent UTIs
* Suspected obstruction
* Kidney stones
* Male clients
* Pregnancy
* Unusual organisms
* Failure to improve

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Monitor</strong>

* Temperature
* Pain
* Urinary symptoms
* Intake and output
* Urine appearance
* Hydration
* Response to antibiotics
* Signs of kidney involvement

</div>

</div>

#### Medications

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Antibiotics</strong>

Possible medications include:

* Nitrofurantoin
* Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole
* Fosfomycin
* Cephalosporins
* Amoxicillin or other antibiotics when appropriate

The medication selected depends on the organism, culture results, allergies, kidney function, pregnancy status, and local resistance patterns.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Antibiotic Teaching</strong>

* Take at evenly spaced times.
* Complete the full prescribed course.
* Do not save leftover antibiotics.
* Report rash, wheezing, swelling, or severe diarrhea.
* Notify the provider if symptoms worsen or do not improve.
* A culture may cause the provider to change the antibiotic.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Phenazopyridine</strong>

Trade name: **Pyridium**

* Urinary tract analgesic.
* Relieves burning, pain, urgency, and frequency.
* Does not kill bacteria.
* Does not replace an antibiotic.
* Usually used only for short-term symptom relief.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Phenazopyridine Teaching</strong>

* Urine becomes bright orange or red-orange.
* May stain clothing.
* May stain contact lenses.
* Take with or after food to reduce GI upset.
* Report yellow skin or sclera.
* Avoid in significant renal impairment.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Medication Distinction:</strong> The antibiotic treats the organism. Phenazopyridine only decreases urinary discomfort while the antibiotic begins working.
</div>

#### Nursing Interventions

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Assessment</strong>

* Assess urinary frequency, urgency, and dysuria.
* Assess suprapubic and flank pain.
* Monitor temperature.
* Assess urine color, clarity, and amount.
* Monitor intake and output.
* Review UTI history and antibiotic allergies.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Hydration</strong>

* Encourage fluids unless contraindicated.
* Water is generally preferred.
* Increased fluid intake promotes urine flow.
* Monitor carefully if the client has heart failure or kidney disease.
* Do not push fluids when the client has a fluid restriction.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Comfort Measures</strong>

* Give prescribed urinary analgesic.
* Give prescribed pain medication.
* Encourage regular bladder emptying.
* A heating pad may be ordered for suprapubic discomfort.
* Avoid bladder irritants if they worsen symptoms.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Evaluation</strong>

Expected improvement includes:

* Less burning
* Less urgency and frequency
* Decreased suprapubic discomfort
* Afebrile status
* Clearer urine
* Adequate urine output
* No flank pain

</div>

</div>

#### Patient Education and Prevention

* Drink adequate fluids unless restricted.
* Do not delay urination when the urge occurs.
* Empty the bladder completely.
* Wipe from front to back.
* Avoid irritating feminine hygiene products.
* Urinate after sexual activity.
* Wear breathable cotton underwear.
* Avoid prolonged exposure to wet clothing.
* Take showers rather than prolonged baths if baths worsen recurrent infections.
* Complete all prescribed antibiotics.
* Contact the provider if symptoms return.
* Seek care promptly for fever, chills, flank pain, nausea, or vomiting.

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Frequency, urgency, dysuria, and suprapubic discomfort suggest a lower UTI. Fever, chills, nausea, vomiting, or flank pain suggest kidney involvement.
</div>

</div>

### Pyelonephritis: Kidney Infection

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>What It Is</strong>

* Infection and inflammation of the renal pelvis and kidney tissue.
* Often begins as a lower UTI or bladder infection.
* Bacteria travel upward through the ureters.
* More serious than cystitis.
* Can cause kidney damage or sepsis if not treated.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Risk Factors / Causes</strong>

* Untreated or recurrent bladder infection
* Urinary obstruction
* Kidney stones
* Enlarged prostate
* Vesicoureteral reflux
* Pregnancy
* Diabetes mellitus
* Indwelling catheter
* Structural urinary abnormality
* Immunosuppression

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Expected Manifestations</strong>

* Fever
* Chills
* Flank or back pain
* Costovertebral-angle tenderness
* Nausea
* Vomiting
* Malaise
* Dysuria
* Frequency and urgency
* Cloudy or bloody urine

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Concerns</strong>

* Sepsis
* Dehydration
* Acute kidney injury
* Urinary obstruction
* Renal abscess
* Recurrent infection
* Permanent kidney damage

</div>

</div>

#### Costovertebral-Angle Tenderness

The costovertebral angle is located over the kidney area at the back, below the ribs.

* Tenderness with gentle percussion supports possible kidney involvement.
* Do not repeatedly or forcefully strike a painful area.
* Assess both sides and compare.
* Document the location and severity of pain.

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Class Cue:</strong> A history of bladder infection plus fever, flank pain, and costovertebral-angle tenderness points toward pyelonephritis.
</div>

#### Diagnostics and Treatment

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Diagnostics</strong>

* Urinalysis
* Urine culture and sensitivity
* CBC
* Kidney-function labs
* Blood cultures if sepsis is suspected
* Imaging if obstruction, stone, or abscess is suspected

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Antibiotic Therapy</strong>

* Antibiotics are the primary treatment.
* Oral antibiotics may be used for stable clients.
* IV antibiotics may be required for severe illness.
* Therapy may be changed after culture results.
* Complete the entire prescribed course.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Supportive Care</strong>

* IV or oral fluids as appropriate
* Antipyretics
* Pain control
* Antiemetics
* Rest
* Monitoring intake and output
* Treatment of obstruction when present

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Hospitalization May Be Needed</strong>

* Severe nausea or vomiting
* Inability to take oral medication
* Dehydration
* Pregnancy
* Sepsis
* Obstruction
* Unstable vital signs
* Significant comorbid illness

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Nursing Interventions</strong>

* Monitor temperature, pulse, respirations, blood pressure, and oxygen saturation.
* Assess flank pain and costovertebral-angle tenderness.
* Monitor mental status.
* Obtain urine and blood cultures as ordered before antibiotics when possible.
* Administer antibiotics on schedule.
* Monitor intake and output.
* Encourage fluids if not contraindicated.
* Monitor kidney-function tests.
* Administer antipyretics, analgesics, and antiemetics as ordered.
* Assess for urinary obstruction.
* Watch closely for sepsis.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Sepsis Warning Signs</strong>

Report or escalate immediately for:

* Hypotension
* Tachycardia
* Tachypnea
* New confusion
* Decreased urine output
* High or abnormally low temperature
* Chills or rigors
* Weakness or mottled skin
* Worsening pain despite treatment

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX Priority:</strong> A client with pyelonephritis who develops hypotension, confusion, tachycardia, or decreased urine output may be developing sepsis.
</div>

</div>

### Urine Collection and Culture

A contaminated specimen can produce inaccurate results. Use proper collection technique and send the specimen to the laboratory promptly.

#### Clean-Catch Midstream Specimen

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Female Client</strong>

1. Perform hand hygiene.
2. Open the sterile specimen container without touching the inside.
3. Separate the labia.
4. Clean from front to back.
5. Use a clean wipe for each stroke.
6. Begin urinating into the toilet.
7. Collect urine midstream.
8. Finish voiding into the toilet.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Male Client</strong>

1. Perform hand hygiene.
2. Retract the foreskin if present.
3. Clean around the urinary meatus from the center outward.
4. Begin urinating into the toilet.
5. Collect urine midstream.
6. Finish voiding into the toilet.
7. Return the foreskin to its normal position.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Do Not</strong>

* Touch the inside of the cup.
* Allow the cup to touch the genital area.
* Collect the first urine stream.
* Place toilet paper in the specimen.
* Leave the specimen sitting at room temperature for an extended period.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>After Collection</strong>

* Secure the lid.
* Label the specimen at the bedside.
* Place it in the proper transport bag.
* Send promptly to the laboratory.
* Refrigerate according to policy if transport is delayed.

</div>

</div>

#### Specimen From an Indwelling Catheter

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Correct Method</strong>

* Use the catheter sampling port.
* Clamp the tubing below the port briefly if required by policy.
* Clean the sampling port.
* Allow fresh urine to collect.
* Aspirate the specimen using sterile technique.
* Transfer it to the specimen container.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Never Collect From</strong>

* The drainage bag
* Urine that has been standing in the tubing
* An unclean connection
* A disconnected system unless specifically required and performed according to policy

Urine in the drainage bag is old and may be contaminated.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Catheter Care</strong>

* Maintain a closed drainage system.
* Keep the bag below bladder level.
* Prevent dependent loops.
* Secure the catheter.
* Perform routine perineal care.
* Remove the catheter as soon as it is no longer needed.

</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Why It Matters</strong>

Indwelling catheters increase the risk of infection because microorganisms can enter the urinary tract along the catheter.

The best prevention is avoiding unnecessary catheterization and removing the catheter promptly.

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Never obtain a urine culture from the drainage bag. Clean the sampling port and collect fresh urine using sterile technique.
</div>

</div>

<div class="key-findings" markdown="1">
<h3>📋 UTI Big Picture</h3>

* Cystitis is a lower urinary tract infection involving the bladder.
* Pyelonephritis is an upper UTI involving the kidneys.
* A bladder infection can travel upward through the ureters.
* Frequency, urgency, dysuria, and suprapubic pain suggest cystitis.
* Fever, chills, nausea, vomiting, flank pain, and CVA tenderness suggest pyelonephritis.
* Obtain ordered urine cultures before antibiotics when possible.
* Antibiotics treat the infection.
* Phenazopyridine treats discomfort but does not kill bacteria.
* Hydration and frequent bladder emptying help maintain urinary flow.
* Pyelonephritis can progress to sepsis.

</div>

<div class="mh-col" markdown="1">

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

* Fever with flank or back pain
* Chills or rigors
* Persistent nausea or vomiting
* Hypotension
* New confusion
* Tachycardia or tachypnea
* Decreased urine output
* Inability to urinate
* Severe worsening pain
* Rash, wheezing, or facial swelling after an antibiotic
* Severe watery diarrhea

</div>

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">
<h3>NCLEX Tip</h3>

Do not treat all urinary symptoms as a simple bladder infection. Once the client develops fever, chills, flank pain, nausea, vomiting, or CVA tenderness, think **pyelonephritis and possible systemic infection**.

</div>

<div class="quick-review" markdown="1">
<h3>Quick Review</h3>

1. Cystitis causes frequency, urgency, dysuria, and suprapubic discomfort.
2. Pyelonephritis causes fever, chills, flank pain, nausea, vomiting, and CVA tenderness.
3. Obtain a clean-catch urine specimen before antibiotics when possible.
4. Collect catheter cultures from the sampling port, not the drainage bag.
5. Complete the entire antibiotic prescription.
6. Phenazopyridine relieves pain but does not treat the infection.
7. Orange-red urine is expected with phenazopyridine.
8. Encourage fluids unless the client is restricted.
9. Monitor pyelonephritis closely for sepsis.

</div>

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">
<h3>Do Not Miss</h3>

* Do not collect a culture from the catheter drainage bag.
* Do not stop antibiotics when symptoms improve.
* Do not mistake phenazopyridine for an antibiotic.
* Do not ignore fever or flank pain in a client with urinary symptoms.
* Do not force fluids in heart failure or kidney failure without checking restrictions.
* Do not delay escalation when the client develops hypotension, confusion, or oliguria.
* Keep the urinary drainage bag below bladder level.
* Remove unnecessary catheters promptly.

</div>
