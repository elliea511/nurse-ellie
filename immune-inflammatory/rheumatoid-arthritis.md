# Rheumatoid Arthritis

<p class="mh-subtitle">PN-level review of rheumatoid arthritis pathophysiology, assessment findings, diagnostics, medications, joint protection, complications, and priority nursing care</p>

**Rheumatoid arthritis (RA)** is a chronic systemic autoimmune disorder that causes inflammation of the synovial lining of the joints. It commonly affects joints on **both sides of the body**, can lead to permanent deformity, and may also affect organs outside the musculoskeletal system.

### Pathophysiology and Risk Factors

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### What Rheumatoid Arthritis Is

RA is an autoimmune disease in which the immune system mistakenly attacks the synovial tissue lining the joints.

The inflammatory process causes:

1. Synovial inflammation and swelling
2. Thickening of the synovial membrane
3. Formation of abnormal inflammatory tissue called a pannus
4. Damage to cartilage, tendons, ligaments, and bone
5. Joint instability, deformity, and loss of function

Unlike osteoarthritis, RA is a **systemic inflammatory disease**, not simply wear and tear of cartilage.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Joint Changes

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">

<div markdown="1">

##### Early Changes

* Synovial inflammation
* Warmth
* Swelling
* Tenderness
* Joint stiffness
* Pain with movement
* Reduced range of motion

</div>

<div markdown="1">

##### Progressive Changes

* Cartilage destruction
* Bone erosion
* Ligament weakening
* Tendon damage
* Joint instability
* Contractures
* Permanent deformity

</div>

</div>

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">
<h3>Memory Cue</h3>

RA is **red, warm, swollen, stiff, and symmetric** because it is an inflammatory autoimmune disease.

</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Risk Factors

RA can occur at any age, but common risk factors include:

* Female sex
* Middle adulthood
* Family history
* Genetic susceptibility
* Smoking
* Obesity
* Certain environmental exposures
* Previous immune-system activation or infection
* Hormonal influences

The exact cause is not fully known. It likely develops from a combination of genetic and environmental factors.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### RA vs. Osteoarthritis

<div class="detail-table" markdown="1">

| Rheumatoid Arthritis | Osteoarthritis |
|---|---|
| Autoimmune inflammatory disease | Degenerative joint disease |
| Usually affects joints symmetrically | May affect one side more than the other |
| Morning stiffness often lasts longer than 30 to 60 minutes | Morning stiffness is usually brief |
| Joint warmth and swelling are common | Bony enlargement and crepitus are common |
| Systemic fatigue and fever may occur | Systemic symptoms are uncommon |
| Commonly affects small joints of hands and feet | Commonly affects weight-bearing joints |
| Can affect organs outside the joints | Primarily affects joints |
| DMARDs are needed to slow disease | Treatment focuses on symptoms and function |

</div>

</section>

</div>

### Assessment Findings

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Classic Joint Findings

* Symmetric joint involvement
* Pain
* Warmth
* Swelling
* Tenderness
* Prolonged morning stiffness
* Decreased range of motion
* Weak grip
* Difficulty with fine-motor activities
* Joint symptoms that improve somewhat with gentle activity

Commonly affected joints include:

* Fingers
* Knuckles
* Wrists
* Feet
* Ankles
* Knees
* Elbows
* Shoulders

The distal finger joints closest to the nails are usually less affected than in osteoarthritis.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### General and Systemic Findings

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">

<div markdown="1">

##### General Findings

* Fatigue
* Weakness
* Low-grade fever
* Loss of appetite
* Weight loss
* Malaise
* Sleep disturbance
* Activity intolerance

</div>

<div markdown="1">

##### Systemic Involvement

RA may also affect:

* Eyes
* Lungs
* Heart
* Blood vessels
* Skin
* Nervous system
* Blood cells

</div>

</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Rheumatoid Nodules

Rheumatoid nodules are firm lumps that may form beneath the skin.

Common locations include:

* Elbows
* Forearms
* Fingers
* Pressure areas
* Achilles tendon region

They are usually painless but may become irritated or interfere with movement.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Common Hand Deformities

<div class="detail-table" markdown="1">

| Deformity | Description |
|---|---|
| **Ulnar deviation** | Fingers shift toward the little-finger side |
| **Swan-neck deformity** | Middle finger joint bends backward while the end joint bends forward |
| **Boutonnière deformity** | Middle finger joint bends forward while the end joint bends backward |
| **Z-thumb deformity** | Thumb joints become misaligned |
| **Subcutaneous nodules** | Firm nodules develop near joints or pressure points |

</div>

These deformities indicate long-term joint damage. Early treatment aims to prevent or delay them.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Flare vs. Remission

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">

<div markdown="1">

##### During a Flare

* Increased pain
* Increased swelling
* Warm joints
* More stiffness
* Fatigue
* Reduced function
* Possible fever
* Increased inflammatory markers

</div>

<div markdown="1">

##### During Remission

* Less pain and swelling
* Improved movement
* Improved energy
* Fewer systemic symptoms
* Better ability to perform daily activities

</div>

</div>

A remission does not mean the disease is cured. Maintenance medications should continue as prescribed.

</section>

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

* New numbness or weakness
* Severe neck pain
* Sudden difficulty walking
* Shortness of breath
* Chest pain
* Fever while taking an immunosuppressive medication
* Hot, intensely painful single joint with fever
* New eye pain or vision changes

</div>

</div>

### Diagnostics and Monitoring

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

No single laboratory test diagnoses RA by itself. Diagnosis is based on the client’s history, joint examination, laboratory findings, and imaging.

#### Common Laboratory Tests

<div class="detail-table" markdown="1">

| Test | Possible Finding | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Rheumatoid factor** | Positive | Supports RA but can be positive in other conditions |
| **Anti-CCP antibody** | Positive | More specific for RA and may predict more erosive disease |
| **ESR** | Elevated | Indicates inflammation |
| **C-reactive protein** | Elevated | Indicates active inflammation |
| **CBC** | Possible anemia | Chronic inflammation may reduce red blood cell production |
| **Platelet count** | May be elevated | Can occur with active inflammation |
| **Liver-function tests** | Baseline and ongoing monitoring | Needed for medications such as methotrexate |
| **Kidney-function tests** | Baseline and ongoing monitoring | Needed for medication safety |
| **Tuberculosis and hepatitis screening** | Completed before selected medications | Reduces risk when starting biologic therapy |

</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Imaging

Possible studies include:

* X-ray
* Ultrasound
* MRI

Imaging may show:

* Soft-tissue swelling
* Joint-space narrowing
* Bone erosion
* Joint deformity
* Synovial inflammation
* Disease progression

Early imaging may appear normal even when inflammation is present.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Ongoing Monitoring

Monitor:

* Pain level
* Duration of morning stiffness
* Joint swelling
* Range of motion
* Ability to complete activities
* Fatigue
* Weight
* CBC
* Liver function
* Kidney function
* Signs of infection
* Medication adverse effects
* Disease activity

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">
<h3>NCLEX Tip</h3>

Rheumatoid factor supports the diagnosis, but **anti-CCP is more specific** for rheumatoid arthritis.

</div>

</section>

</div>

### Rheumatoid Arthritis Medications

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

RA medications have different purposes.

<div class="detail-table" markdown="1">

| Medication Group | Main Purpose |
|---|---|
| **NSAIDs** | Reduce pain and inflammation |
| **Corticosteroids** | Rapidly suppress inflammation |
| **DMARDs** | Slow disease progression and prevent joint damage |
| **Biologic agents** | Target specific parts of the immune response |
| **Analgesics** | Reduce pain but do not slow disease |

</div>

The most important disease-modifying treatment is a **DMARD**, not an NSAID alone.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### NSAIDs

Examples:

* Ibuprofen
* Naproxen
* Celecoxib

Purpose:

* Reduce pain
* Reduce inflammation
* Improve movement

Major risks:

* GI irritation
* Ulcer
* GI bleeding
* Kidney injury
* Fluid retention
* Increased blood pressure
* Cardiovascular risk

Teaching:

* Take with food when appropriate.
* Avoid taking multiple NSAIDs.
* Report black stools or vomiting blood.
* Report decreased urine output.
* Avoid long-term self-treatment without provider guidance.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Corticosteroids

Examples:

* Prednisone
* Methylprednisolone

Corticosteroids may be used:

* During an acute flare
* While waiting for a DMARD to become effective
* At the lowest effective dose
* For severe inflammation

Major risks:

* Infection
* Hyperglycemia
* Hypertension
* Fluid retention
* Mood changes
* Osteoporosis
* Poor wound healing
* Adrenal suppression
* Cataracts
* GI irritation

Teaching:

* Do not stop long-term corticosteroids suddenly.
* Take with food.
* Monitor blood glucose when indicated.
* Report fever or infection.
* Follow bone-protection recommendations.
* Avoid exposure to contagious illness.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Methotrexate

**Methotrexate** is a commonly used first-line DMARD for rheumatoid arthritis.

Purpose:

* Slows disease activity
* Prevents joint destruction
* Improves long-term function

Important teaching:

* Usually taken **once weekly**, not every day.
* Take folic acid as prescribed.
* Avoid pregnancy.
* Avoid alcohol.
* Report mouth sores.
* Report unusual bruising or bleeding.
* Report fever or sore throat.
* Report shortness of breath or persistent cough.
* Keep CBC and liver-function appointments.

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Methotrexate Safety</h3>

Daily methotrexate instead of weekly dosing can cause severe toxicity. Verify the prescribed schedule carefully.

</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Other Conventional DMARDs

<div class="detail-table" markdown="1">

| Medication | Important Safety Points |
|---|---|
| **Hydroxychloroquine** | Requires regular eye examinations; report vision changes |
| **Sulfasalazine** | Monitor CBC and liver function; report rash or infection |
| **Leflunomide** | Can cause liver injury and birth defects; avoid pregnancy |
| **Azathioprine** | Can suppress bone marrow and increase infection risk |

</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Biologic Agents

Examples:

* Etanercept
* Adalimumab
* Infliximab
* Other targeted immune therapies

Biologics suppress specific inflammatory pathways.

Before treatment:

* Screen for tuberculosis.
* Screen for hepatitis.
* Assess for active infection.
* Review vaccination status.
* Obtain baseline laboratory tests.

Teaching:

* Report fever, cough, wounds, or infection.
* Avoid live vaccines unless specifically approved.
* Do not take during a serious active infection unless directed.
* Follow injection or infusion instructions.
* Rotate subcutaneous injection sites.
* Store medications as instructed.

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">
<h3>NCLEX Tip</h3>

A client taking a biologic medication who develops fever or signs of infection should contact the provider promptly.

</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Medication Comparison

<div class="detail-table" markdown="1">

| Medication Type | Works Quickly? | Prevents Joint Damage? |
|---|---:|---:|
| **NSAID** | Yes | No |
| **Corticosteroid** | Yes | Does not provide safe long-term disease control alone |
| **Methotrexate or another DMARD** | Takes weeks | Yes |
| **Biologic** | May take time | Yes |
| **Analgesic** | Yes | No |

</div>

</section>

</div>

### Nursing Care and Joint Protection

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Pain and Inflammation Management

During an acute flare:

* Balance rest with gentle movement.
* Apply cold when joints are acutely hot and swollen.
* Administer prescribed anti-inflammatory medications.
* Support inflamed joints.
* Avoid forceful exercise.
* Cluster nursing care.
* Allow additional rest.

For chronic stiffness:

* Apply warm packs.
* Use warm showers or baths.
* Perform gentle range-of-motion exercises.
* Schedule activity after warmth and medication have reduced stiffness.

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">
<h3>Heat vs. Cold</h3>

Use **cold for an acutely hot, swollen joint**. Use **warmth for chronic stiffness and muscle relaxation**.

</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Exercise and Rest

<div class="renal-two-column" markdown="1">

<div markdown="1">

##### Exercise

* Perform gentle range-of-motion exercises.
* Maintain muscle strength.
* Use low-impact exercise.
* Walk, swim, or use water exercise as tolerated.
* Avoid high-impact joint stress.
* Stop if pain or swelling becomes significantly worse.

</div>

<div markdown="1">

##### Rest

* Schedule rest periods.
* Obtain adequate nighttime sleep.
* Rest during severe flares.
* Avoid prolonged total bed rest.
* Use supportive positioning.
* Prevent contractures.

</div>

</div>

Too much rest causes weakness and stiffness. Too much activity increases inflammation and fatigue.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Joint Protection

* Use larger, stronger joints when possible.
* Avoid tight gripping.
* Avoid repetitive hand motion.
* Use both hands to carry objects.
* Slide heavy objects rather than lifting.
* Avoid holding one position for a long time.
* Maintain proper body alignment.
* Use splints as prescribed.
* Wear supportive shoes.
* Avoid placing pillows under flexed knees for long periods.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Energy Conservation

* Plan activities ahead.
* Alternate activity and rest.
* Perform demanding tasks when energy is highest.
* Sit while completing tasks when possible.
* Keep commonly used items within easy reach.
* Use lightweight equipment.
* Delegate tasks when needed.
* Allow extra time for morning care.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Assistive Devices

Helpful equipment may include:

* Jar openers
* Built-up utensil handles
* Button hooks
* Reachers
* Raised toilet seats
* Shower chairs
* Grab bars
* Canes or walkers
* Splints
* Adaptive clothing

Occupational therapy helps the client adapt daily activities. Physical therapy supports mobility, strength, and joint function.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Positioning and Skin Care

* Maintain joints in functional alignment.
* Reposition regularly.
* Use firm support when prescribed.
* Protect pressure areas.
* Assess skin under splints.
* Avoid pressure over rheumatoid nodules.
* Perform regular hand and foot care.
* Monitor for wounds when sensation or mobility is reduced.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Nutrition and General Health

* Maintain a healthy weight.
* Eat a balanced diet.
* Consume adequate protein.
* Ensure sufficient calcium and vitamin D.
* Limit excess sodium if taking corticosteroids.
* Monitor blood glucose with corticosteroid therapy.
* Stop smoking.
* Maintain vaccination and preventive-care appointments.
* Avoid unproven supplements without discussing interactions.

</section>

### Complications and Safety

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Joint and Mobility Complications

* Permanent deformity
* Contractures
* Reduced range of motion
* Muscle wasting
* Loss of independence
* Falls
* Reduced ability to complete daily activities
* Need for joint-replacement surgery

Early DMARD treatment helps reduce permanent damage.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Cervical Spine Instability

RA can damage the upper cervical spine.

Possible warning signs:

* Severe neck pain
* Occipital headache
* Numbness
* Tingling
* Weakness
* Difficulty walking
* Changes in bowel or bladder control

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Cervical Spine Safety</h3>

Report neurologic symptoms immediately. Cervical instability can compress the spinal cord. Alert anesthesia and procedural teams before neck manipulation or intubation.

</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Infection Risk

RA itself and its medications can weaken immunity.

Monitor for:

* Fever
* Chills
* Sore throat
* Cough
* Burning with urination
* Wounds
* Dental infection
* Redness at injection sites
* Unusual fatigue

A client taking immunosuppressive medication may not develop a high fever. Subtle symptoms still require evaluation.

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Other Systemic Complications

<div class="detail-table" markdown="1">

| System | Possible Complication |
|---|---|
| **Eyes** | Dry eyes, inflammation, vision problems |
| **Lungs** | Pleural inflammation, interstitial lung disease |
| **Heart** | Pericarditis and increased cardiovascular risk |
| **Blood** | Anemia |
| **Nerves** | Entrapment neuropathy, numbness, weakness |
| **Skin** | Rheumatoid nodules, ulcers |
| **Bones** | Osteoporosis from inflammation, inactivity, or corticosteroids |

</div>

</section>

<section class="renal-note-section" markdown="1">

#### Surgery

Surgery may be considered when severe joint damage causes uncontrolled pain or loss of function.

Possible procedures include:

* Synovectomy
* Tendon repair
* Joint fusion
* Total joint replacement

Postoperative nursing care includes:

* Neurovascular assessment
* Pain control
* Infection prevention
* DVT prevention
* Early prescribed mobility
* Physical therapy
* Joint precautions

</section>

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

* Severe neck pain with weakness or numbness
* Fever while taking immunosuppressive medication
* New shortness of breath
* Chest pain
* Sudden eye pain or vision change
* Black stools or vomiting blood
* Severe medication rash
* Unusual bruising or bleeding
* Hot swollen joint with fever
* New difficulty walking

</div>

</div>

<div class="mh-table-card" markdown="1">

<h3>Rheumatoid Arthritis Big Picture</h3>

| Concept | Main Point | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| **Cause** | Autoimmune attack on synovial tissue | Control inflammation early |
| **Joint pattern** | Symmetric small-joint involvement | Compare both sides |
| **Morning stiffness** | Usually prolonged | Warmth and gentle activity |
| **DMARDs** | Slow disease and prevent damage | Adherence and laboratory monitoring |
| **NSAIDs** | Control pain and inflammation | Monitor GI and kidney effects |
| **Corticosteroids** | Rapidly control a flare | Infection, glucose, and bone safety |
| **Biologics** | Target immune pathways | Screen for TB and infection |
| **Exercise** | Preserves movement and strength | Balance with rest |
| **Joint protection** | Reduces stress and deformity | Use adaptive techniques |
| **Cervical instability** | Can compress the spinal cord | Report neurologic changes immediately |

</div>

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

- Severe neck pain, weakness, or numbness
- Fever or signs of infection
- Shortness of breath
- Chest pain
- Sudden vision changes
- Black or bloody stool
- Severe rash
- Unusual bleeding
- Hot swollen joint with systemic illness

<div class="mh-columns" markdown="1">

<div class="quick-review" markdown="1">
<h3>Quick Review</h3>

1. RA is a systemic autoimmune inflammatory disease.
2. It usually affects joints symmetrically.
3. Morning stiffness commonly lasts longer than 30 to 60 minutes.
4. Fatigue, low-grade fever, and weight loss can occur.
5. Rheumatoid factor supports the diagnosis, but anti-CCP is more specific.
6. NSAIDs decrease symptoms but do not prevent joint destruction.
7. DMARDs slow disease progression.
8. Methotrexate is commonly taken once weekly.
9. Biologic therapy increases infection risk.
10. Use cold for an acutely inflamed joint.
11. Use warmth for chronic stiffness.
12. Balance exercise with rest.
13. Protect joints and conserve energy.
14. Report neurologic changes that may indicate cervical instability.
</div>

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">
<h3>Do Not Miss</h3>

- Do not confuse rheumatoid arthritis with normal joint wear.
- Do not treat RA with pain medication alone.
- Do not stop DMARDs during remission unless instructed.
- Do not take methotrexate daily when prescribed weekly.
- Do not ignore fever while taking immunosuppressants.
- Do not give live vaccines without checking medication precautions.
- Do not forcefully exercise a hot, swollen joint.
- Do not use prolonged bed rest.
- Do not ignore severe neck pain or neurologic changes.
- Do not apply heat to an acutely inflamed joint if it worsens swelling.
</div>

</div>
