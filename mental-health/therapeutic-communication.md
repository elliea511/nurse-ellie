# Therapeutic Communication

<div class="note">
Therapeutic communication is one of the biggest mental health NCLEX skills.
The goal is to help the patient feel heard, safe, respected, and supported without judging, arguing, giving advice, or promising things the nurse cannot promise.
</div>

---

## Big Picture

| Therapeutic Communication Means | What the Nurse Does |
|---|---|
| **Builds trust** | Uses honesty, consistency, and respect |
| **Encourages expression** | Lets the patient talk about feelings and concerns |
| **Supports safety** | Asks directly about suicide, violence, abuse, or hallucinations |
| **Avoids judgment** | Does not blame, shame, or criticize |
| **Keeps boundaries** | Stays professional and consistent |
| **Promotes reality** | Does not agree with hallucinations or delusions |

<div class="warning">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> The best mental health answer is usually calm, direct, nonjudgmental, and focused on the patient's feelings or safety.
</div>

---

# Basic Therapeutic Techniques

| Technique | What It Means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Silence** | Allowing quiet time for the patient to think | Sitting quietly after the patient cries |
| **Active listening** | Showing attention through calm presence | Nodding, eye contact, not interrupting |
| **Open-ended questions** | Encourages more than yes/no answers | "Can you tell me more about that?" |
| **Clarification** | Makes unclear statements clearer | "What do you mean by unsafe?" |
| **Restating** | Repeats the main idea | "You feel overwhelmed." |
| **Reflection** | Sends the question/feeling back to the patient | "What do you think would help?" |
| **Focusing** | Brings attention to one important topic | "Let's talk more about the voices." |
| **Offering self** | Offers presence and support | "I can stay with you for a while." |
| **Presenting reality** | States what is real without arguing | "I do not see anyone else in the room." |
| **Exploring** | Encourages deeper discussion | "What happened before you felt this way?" |

---

# Nontherapeutic Communication

| Avoid This | Why It Is Not Therapeutic |
|---|---|
| **Asking "why"** | Sounds blaming |
| **False reassurance** | Dismisses the patient's fear |
| **Giving advice** | Takes control away from the patient |
| **Changing the subject** | Avoids the patient's concern |
| **Arguing** | Increases defensiveness |
| **Agreeing with delusions** | Reinforces false belief |
| **Disapproval** | Makes patient feel judged |
| **Approval** | Can make patient seek nurse approval instead of healthy choices |
| **Defending staff/family** | Shifts focus away from patient |
| **Promising secrecy** | Unsafe if harm or abuse is involved |

---

## Common NCLEX Traps

| Trap Answer | Why It Is Wrong |
|---|---|
| "Everything will be okay." | False reassurance |
| "You should call your mother." | Giving advice |
| "Why did you do that?" | Sounds blaming |
| "That is not true." | Arguing/confronting |
| "I know exactly how you feel." | The nurse does not know exactly |
| "At least it was not worse." | Minimizes feelings |
| "You have so much to live for." | Dismisses suicide risk |
| "I promise I will not tell anyone." | Unsafe and not legal if safety is at risk |

<div class="warning">
<strong>Never promise secrecy</strong> when the patient reports suicidal thoughts, homicidal thoughts, abuse, or danger to self or others.
</div>

---

# Feelings vs Facts

Mental health responses often focus on the **feeling** instead of arguing about the facts.

| Patient Says | Therapeutic Focus |
|---|---|
| "The staff is trying to hurt me." | Fear/safety |
| "The voices are yelling at me." | Distress/safety |
| "I am worthless." | Hopelessness/suicide risk |
| "Everyone hates me." | Feeling rejected |
| "I cannot breathe. I'm dying." | Panic/safety |
| "My partner controls everything." | Abuse/safety |

---

# Suicide Communication

Suicide questions should be **direct**. Do not hint around it. Do not avoid the word "suicide."

## Best Suicide Questions

| Assessment Need | What to Ask |
|---|---|
| Suicidal thoughts | "Are you thinking about killing yourself?" |
| Plan | "Do you have a plan?" |
| Lethality | "What is your plan?" |
| Means/access | "Do you have access to that method?" |
| Timing | "When were you planning to do this?" |
| Past attempts | "Have you tried to hurt yourself before?" |
| Safety | "Do you feel safe being alone right now?" |
| Support | "Who can we call to support you?" |

---

## Suicide Response Examples

| Patient Says | Best Response |
|---|---|
| "I do not want to live anymore." | "Are you thinking about killing yourself?" |
| "My family would be better off without me." | "Are you having thoughts of suicide?" |
| "I already know how I would do it." | "What is your plan?" |
| "Promise you won't tell anyone." | "I cannot promise secrecy if your safety is at risk." |
| "I feel calm now. I'm fine." | "I still need to make sure you are safe. Are you having thoughts of suicide?" |

<div class="warning">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Asking directly about suicide is therapeutic. It does not put the idea in the patient's head.
</div>

---

# Abuse Communication

With abuse, the nurse should be calm, private, supportive, and nonjudgmental.

## Best Abuse Responses

| Patient Situation | Best Response |
|---|---|
| Patient reports abuse | "I believe you. I'm glad you told me." |
| Patient feels responsible | "This was not your fault." |
| Patient is afraid | "You are safe here right now." |
| Patient is not ready to talk | "That is okay. We can go at your pace." |
| Patient asks for secrecy | "I cannot promise secrecy if someone's safety is at risk." |
| Patient fears going home | "Do you feel safe going home today?" |

---

## Avoid With Abuse

| Avoid Saying | Why |
|---|---|
| "Why didn't you leave?" | Blames the victim |
| "Why did you go back?" | Judgmental |
| "You need to leave now." | Leaving can be dangerous |
| "Are you sure?" | Sounds like disbelief |
| "What did you do to make them mad?" | Blames the victim |
| "Everything happens for a reason." | Minimizes trauma |

<div class="note">
For suspected abuse, try to interview the patient alone. Do not confront the suspected abuser.
</div>

---

# Sexual Assault Communication

Sexual assault care should restore control to the patient.

| Helpful Response | Why It Helps |
|---|---|
| "I believe you." | Supports disclosure |
| "This was not your fault." | Reduces shame |
| "You are safe here." | Promotes safety |
| "You are in control of what happens next." | Restores control |
| "I will explain each step before we do anything." | Reduces fear |

Avoid:

- "Why were you there?"
- "Why didn't you fight back?"
- "Are you sure?"
- "Why did you wait to report?"
- "You should have called sooner."

---

# Hallucination Communication

A hallucination is a sensory experience that is not real. The nurse should:

1. Assess safety
2. Ask what the hallucination is saying or doing
3. Acknowledge the patient's feelings
4. Present reality
5. Redirect if needed

## Best Hallucination Responses

| Patient Says | Best Response |
|---|---|
| "I hear voices." | "What are the voices saying?" |
| "The voices are telling me to hurt myself." | "I am going to stay with you and help keep you safe." |
| "Do you hear them too?" | "I do not hear the voices, but I understand they are real to you." |
| "The voices are scary." | "That sounds frightening. I can stay with you." |
| "I see bugs on the wall." | "I do not see bugs, but I can tell this is upsetting." |

<div class="warning">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Always assess command hallucinations. Ask what the voices are saying.
</div>

---

# Delusion Communication

A delusion is a fixed false belief. The nurse should not argue with the delusion and should not agree with it.

## Best Delusion Responses

| Patient Says | Best Response |
|---|---|
| "The FBI is watching me." | "That sounds frightening. I do not see anyone watching you." |
| "My food is poisoned." | "You seem worried about the food. Let's talk about what would help you feel safe eating." |
| "I am the president." | "I understand that you believe that. Right now, we are in the hospital." |
| "The nurse is trying to kill me." | "You feel unsafe. I am here to help keep you safe." |
| "The TV is sending me messages." | "I do not receive messages from the TV, but I understand this feels real to you." |

---

## Delusion Rules

| Do | Avoid |
|---|---|
| Focus on feelings | Arguing |
| Present reality calmly | Agreeing with false belief |
| Keep patient safe | Laughing or joking |
| Use short statements | Long explanations |
| Redirect to reality-based activity | Trying to prove them wrong |

<div class="note">
Do not say, "That is not true."<br>
Better: "I do not see that, but I understand this feels frightening."
</div>

---

# Anxiety and Panic Communication

An anxious patient needs simple, calm communication. The higher the anxiety, the shorter and simpler the communication should be.

| Anxiety Level | Communication |
|---|---|
| **Mild** | Teach, problem-solve, discuss coping |
| **Moderate** | Use simple explanations and refocus |
| **Severe** | Short directions, reduce stimuli |
| **Panic** | Stay with patient, short commands, safety first |

---

## Panic Attack Responses

| Patient Says | Best Response |
|---|---|
| "I can't breathe. I'm dying." | "You are safe. I will stay with you. Take a slow breath with me." |
| "I need to get out of here." | "I will stay with you. Let's move to a quieter area." |
| "Something terrible is happening." | "This feels scary. You are safe right now." |
| "I can't think." | "That's okay. Just focus on breathing with me." |

<div class="note">
Do not teach during panic. First stay with the patient, reduce stimulation, and use short directions.
</div>

---

# Mania Communication

A manic patient may have pressured speech, poor impulse control, distractibility, and decreased sleep. Communication should be calm, brief, structured, and limit-setting.

| Manic Behavior | Nurse Response |
|---|---|
| Patient is talking nonstop | Use short, clear statements |
| Patient is intrusive | Set limits calmly |
| Patient is overstimulated | Decrease environmental stimulation |
| Patient refuses meals | Offer high-calorie finger foods |
| Patient is sexually inappropriate | Set clear boundaries |
| Patient is aggressive | Maintain safety and call for help early |

## Examples

| Patient Says/Does | Best Response |
|---|---|
| "I am not sleeping. I have too much to do." | "It is time to rest. You may sit quietly in your room." |
| Patient touches another patient | "Do not touch other patients. You may sit here or walk with staff." |
| Patient talks loudly over group | "It is another patient's turn to speak. You may listen or step out with staff." |
| Patient becomes sexually inappropriate | "That comment is not appropriate. We will continue when you can speak respectfully." |

---

# Personality Disorder Communication

Personality disorder questions often test **boundaries, limits, consistency, and staff teamwork.**

| Pattern | Nursing Focus |
|---|---|
| Manipulation | Consistent limits |
| Splitting | Team communication |
| Dependency | Encourage independence |
| Suspicion | Build trust slowly |
| Attention-seeking | Set limits without shaming |
| Anger/aggression | Safety and clear expectations |

## Examples

| Patient Says | Best Response |
|---|---|
| "You are the only nurse who cares." | "All staff are here to help you." |
| "That nurse is terrible. You are better." | "The treatment plan is the same with all staff." |
| "If you cared, you would break the rule." | "I understand you are upset. The rule is still the same." |
| "I cannot do anything without you." | "I will support you while you try the first step." |

<div class="note">
With splitting or manipulation, staff must respond consistently as one team.
</div>

---

# Depression Communication

Depressed patients may have hopelessness, low energy, guilt, poor concentration, and suicidal thoughts.

| Patient Says | Best Response |
|---|---|
| "I am worthless." | "You sound very hopeless. Are you thinking about harming yourself?" |
| "Nothing matters." | "It sounds hard to see a way forward right now." |
| "I cannot do anything." | "Let's focus on one small step right now." |
| "I am a burden." | "You feel like a burden. Are you having thoughts of suicide?" |

Avoid:

- "Cheer up."
- "Everyone gets sad."
- "You just need to try harder."
- "Other people have it worse."

---

# Grief Communication

Grief care uses presence, silence, and support.

| Patient Says | Best Response |
|---|---|
| "I can't believe this happened." | "This feels unreal right now." |
| "Why did this happen?" | "This is very painful and hard to understand." |
| "It is my fault." | "You are feeling responsible. Tell me more about that." |
| "I don't want to go on." | "Are you thinking about killing yourself?" |

Avoid:

- "They are in a better place."
- "At least they lived a long life."
- "Everything happens for a reason."
- "You need to move on."

---

# Substance Use Communication

Substance use communication should be nonjudgmental and focused on safety.

| Patient Says | Best Response |
|---|---|
| "I can stop anytime." | "Tell me about the last time you tried to stop." |
| "I only drink to relax." | "How often do you drink to relax?" |
| "I don't have a problem." | "What concerns have others shared with you?" |
| "I'm not going to AA." | "What has helped you stay sober before?" |
| "I used again." | "What was happening before you used?" |

Avoid:

- "You need to stop."
- "You're ruining your life."
- "Why can't you just quit?"
- "You should know better."

---

# Anger and Violence Communication

When a patient is escalating, communication should protect safety.

| Do | Avoid |
|---|---|
| Stay calm | Arguing |
| Keep safe distance | Standing too close |
| Keep exit accessible | Blocking the doorway |
| Use short statements | Long explanations |
| Offer choices | Power struggles |
| Set limits | Threatening |
| Call for help early | Managing alone |
| Reduce stimulation | Crowding the patient |

## Examples

| Patient Says/Does | Best Response |
|---|---|
| "I'm about to hit someone." | "I can see you are angry. I cannot allow anyone to be hurt." |
| Patient is pacing and yelling | "Let's move to a quieter area." |
| Patient clenches fists | "I am going to give you space. I want to help you stay safe." |
| Patient threatens staff | "Threatening others is not allowed. Staff are here to maintain safety." |

---

# Limit Setting

Limit setting should be calm, clear, and consistent.

## Good Limit-Setting Formula

**1. Acknowledge feeling. 2. State the limit. 3. Offer acceptable choices.**

Example: "I understand you are angry. I cannot allow yelling at other patients. You may sit in the quiet room or walk with staff."

---

## Limit-Setting Examples

| Situation | Therapeutic Limit |
|---|---|
| Patient yelling | "I want to help, but I cannot talk while you are yelling." |
| Patient threatening | "I cannot allow threats. We can talk when everyone is safe." |
| Patient demanding special treatment | "The rules are the same for everyone." |
| Patient refusing bedtime routine | "You may read quietly in bed or use a relaxation technique." |
| Patient interrupting group | "It is another person's turn. You may listen or step out." |

# NCLEX Cues

<div class="warning">

<strong>Nontherapeutic traps — always wrong:</strong>
<ul>
<li>Do not ask "why" — it sounds blaming.</li>
<li>Do not give false reassurance — avoid "Everything will be okay."</li>
<li>Do not give advice — help the patient explore their own choices.</li>
<li>Do not agree with delusions or hallucinations — present reality calmly.</li>
</ul>

</div>

<div class="warning">

<strong>Situation-specific responses:</strong>
<ul>
<li>Suicide: ask directly — it does not increase risk.</li>
<li>Hallucinations: ask what the voices are saying (content = safety priority).</li>
<li>Panic: stay with the patient, reduce stimulation, use short directions.</li>
<li>Abuse: believe the patient, avoid blame, interview alone.</li>
<li>Manipulation or splitting: keep staff responses consistent.</li>
<li>Anger or violence: maintain safety, set clear limits calmly.</li>
</ul>

</div>

---

# Final Takeaway

<div class="tip">
For therapeutic communication questions, ask:
<br><br>
<strong>1. Is the response safe?</strong><br>
<strong>2. Is it calm and nonjudgmental?</strong><br>
<strong>3. Does it focus on the patient's feelings or safety?</strong><br>
<strong>4. Does it avoid advice, false reassurance, and "why" questions?</strong><br>
<strong>5. Does it keep professional boundaries?</strong>
</div>
