# Depression & Bipolar Disorder

<p class="mh-subtitle">ATI-style review for depression, mania, bipolar disorder, lithium safety, and priority nursing care</p>

Mood disorders affect a patient’s emotional state, energy, sleep, thinking, behavior, relationships, and ability to function. For nursing questions, focus on **suicide risk, safety, sleep, nutrition, hydration, medication teaching, lithium toxicity, and low-stimulation care during mania.**

      <tr>

        <th>What It Is</th>
        <th>Assessment Findings</th>
        <th>Treatment / Nursing Focus</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Major Depressive Disorder</strong></td>
        <td>Persistent sadness, hopelessness, and/or loss of interest in everyday activities. Diagnosis requires at least 5 symptoms nearly every day for 2 weeks, and depression or anhedonia must be present.</td>
        <td>Depression or anhedonia, sleep disturbance, appetite/weight change, suicidal thoughts, indecisiveness, poor concentration, decreased interest, fatigue or low energy.</td>
        <td>Assess suicide risk, promote ADLs, encourage routine, small activities, therapy, medications, nutrition, and sleep support.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Persistent Depressive Disorder</strong></td>
        <td>Long-term milder depression, also called dysthymia. Must last at least 2 years in adults and 1 year in children/adolescents.</td>
        <td>Overeating or poor appetite, insomnia or oversleeping, low energy, low self-esteem, hopelessness. Often begins in teen years and may feel “normal” to the patient.</td>
        <td>Cognitive therapy and antidepressants. Support coping, routine, and long-term treatment adherence.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Seasonal Affective Disorder</strong></td>
        <td>Depression that occurs in a seasonal pattern, often during winter months with less sunlight.</td>
        <td>Carb cravings, lethargy, weight gain, sleeping too much, depressed mood.</td>
        <td>Light therapy/ultraviolet light as ordered, routine, sleep schedule, activity, antidepressants if ordered.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder</strong></td>
        <td>Depression associated with menstruation; severe PMS symptoms during the luteal phase that interfere with work or relationships.</td>
        <td>Depressive symptoms, mood swings, breast tenderness, anxiety, bloating, weight gain.</td>
        <td>Exercise, relaxation, diet as instructed, some birth control pills, antidepressants, and diuretics if ordered.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Risk Factors / Causes</strong>

- Female gender
- Unmarried status
- Low socioeconomic status
- Early childhood trauma
- Family history of depression
- Medical illness
- Recent loss or major life stressor
- History of abuse or trauma
- Substance use
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Assessment Findings</strong>

- Sadness, hopelessness, emptiness
- Anhedonia: loss of interest or pleasure
- Sleep disturbance
- Appetite or weight change
- Fatigue or anergia
- Poor concentration or indecisiveness
- Social isolation
- Poor ADLs
- Suicidal thoughts or statements
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Nursing Interventions</strong>

- Assess suicide risk directly.
- Maintain safety precautions as indicated.
- Promote routine and structure.
- Encourage ADLs without doing everything for the patient.
- Encourage exercise and group attendance as tolerated.
- Offer small, high-calorie meals if appetite is poor.
- Use simple choices and short instructions if concentration is poor.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Education</strong>

- Depression is treatable, but improvement takes time.
- Take medications exactly as prescribed.
- Do not stop antidepressants suddenly.
- Report suicidal thoughts or worsening mood.
- Keep therapy and follow-up appointments.
- Use sleep routine, activity, support system, and coping skills.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Complications / Safety Risks</strong>

- Suicide
- Poor nutrition
- Dehydration
- Poor hygiene/ADLs
- Social isolation
- Substance use
- Relapse if treatment is stopped early
</div>

</div>

### Suicide + Safety

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Assess First</strong>

- Suicidal thoughts
- Plan
- Intent
- Means/access to method
- Previous attempts
- Family history
- Substance use
- Impulsivity
- Sudden mood change from sad to happy
- Social supports
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>High-Risk Cues</strong>

- Specific plan
- Access to lethal means
- Previous attempt
- Giving things away
- Statements like “I won’t be a bother much longer”
- Hopelessness
- Substance use
- Psychosis or command hallucinations
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Nursing Interventions</strong>

- Ensure preventive safety measures.
- Maintain observation level as ordered.
- Remove harmful objects.
- Search belongings per policy with patient present.
- Keep environment safe.
- Communicate risk to the team.
- Do not promise secrecy.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Education</strong>

- Tell staff immediately if suicidal thoughts increase.
- Use crisis plan and support contacts.
- Avoid alcohol/drugs because they increase impulsivity.
- Keep follow-up appointments.
- Take medications as prescribed.
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority:</strong> If the patient is unsafe, safety comes before teaching, insight, coping skills, or discharge planning.
</div>

</div>

### Bipolar + Mania

<summary>📊 Infographic: Bipolar Disorder Mood Patterns</summary>

<span class="infographic-hint">Tap the image to enlarge</span>

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Type</th>
        <th>What It Is</th>
        <th>Assessment Findings</th>
        <th>Nursing Focus</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Bipolar I</strong></td>
        <td>Most severe type. Characterized by at least one manic episode lasting about 1 week. Manic episodes may alternate with depression.</td>
        <td>Mania, depression, possible delusions or hallucinations, impaired judgment, high suicide risk.</td>
        <td>Safety, low stimulation, nutrition, hydration, rest, medication adherence, lithium monitoring.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Bipolar II</strong></td>
        <td>Hypomania alternates with profound depression.</td>
        <td>Hypomanic symptoms plus major depressive episodes. Depression may be severe.</td>
        <td>Assess suicide risk, monitor mood changes, support medication adherence and therapy.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Cyclothymia</strong></td>
        <td>Hypomania alternates with milder depression; highs and lows are not as severe.</td>
        <td>Chronic mood fluctuation; may be chronically depressed.</td>
        <td>Therapy, monitoring, medication as ordered, coping support.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Rapid Cycling</strong></td>
        <td>At least 4 mood changes in a year; may cycle more rapidly with no normal mood periods.</td>
        <td>Frequent mood changes, impaired functioning, high relapse risk.</td>
        <td>Medication adherence, safety assessment, mood tracking, support system education.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Substance/Medication-Induced Bipolar</strong></td>
        <td>Disturbed mood from intoxication or withdrawal of a substance.</td>
        <td>Manic, depressed, irritable, or mixed symptoms linked to substance use or withdrawal.</td>
        <td>Assess substance use, withdrawal safety, and coexisting psychiatric disorders.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Bipolar Due to Medical Condition</strong></td>
        <td>Mania, hypomania, or depression caused by a physical illness.</td>
        <td>Mood symptoms related to medical condition.</td>
        <td>Assess medical cause and coordinate care with provider/team.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-table mania-levels-table">

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Risk Factors / Causes</strong>

- Genetic inheritance
- Brain anomalies
- Inflammation
- Family stress
- Adverse life events in childhood
- Physical, emotional, or sexual abuse
- Neglect
- Dysfunctional family history
- Substance use
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Comorbidities</strong>

**Bipolar I:** ADHD, impulse control disorders, conduct disorders, high substance abuse incidence.

**Bipolar II:** anxiety, eating disorders, substance abuse.

**Cyclothymia:** substance disorders, sleep disorders, ADHD.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Nursing Interventions for Mania</strong>

- Provide calm, non-stimulating environment.
- Provide quiet area where patient can move safely.
- Maintain safety for patient, staff, and other patients.
- Reduce conflict with other patients.
- Encourage fluids and nutrition.
- Offer high-calorie finger foods.
- Give medications as prescribed.
- Promote rest and sleep.
- Use seclusion/restraints only as last resort.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Education</strong>

- Take mood stabilizers as prescribed.
- Keep regular sleep schedule.
- Avoid alcohol and recreational drugs.
- Track mood changes and warning signs.
- Maintain hydration and nutrition.
- Keep lab appointments for lithium/med monitoring.
- Tell provider about pregnancy or plans to become pregnant.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Complications / Safety Risks</strong>

- Suicide risk
- Exhaustion
- Dehydration
- Poor nutrition
- Aggression or injury
- Risky spending/sexual behavior
- Substance abuse
- Psychosis
- Medication toxicity or nonadherence
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> During mania, safety is the number one concern. Decrease stimulation, promote rest, and support nutrition/hydration.
</div>

</div>

      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>SNRI</strong><br>Venlafaxine, duloxetine</td>
        <td>Depression symptoms.</td>
        <td>Monitor BP and serotonin syndrome.</td>
        <td>Take as prescribed and report worsening mood.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>NDRI</strong><br>Bupropion / Wellbutrin</td>
        <td>Depression; also used for smoking cessation.</td>
        <td>Use caution with seizure risk and eating disorder history.</td>
        <td>Report seizures or worsening mood.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>TCA</strong><br>Imipramine</td>
        <td>Depression symptoms.</td>
        <td>Anticholinergic effects and overdose danger.</td>
        <td>Take exactly as prescribed; keep away from overdose risk.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>MAOI</strong><br>Phenelzine, tranylcypromine</td>
        <td>Depression symptoms.</td>
        <td>Tyramine diet restrictions and hypertensive crisis risk.</td>
        <td>Avoid tyramine foods and report severe headache or hypertension symptoms.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>St. John’s Wort</strong></td>
        <td>Herbal sometimes used for depression.</td>
        <td>Major drug interaction risk; serotonin syndrome risk with antidepressants.</td>
        <td>Tell provider before taking herbals.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Lithium Carbonate</strong></td>
        <td>Drug of choice for bipolar illness.</td>
        <td>Therapeutic level: 0.6–1.2 mEq/L. Hold next dose and notify provider for toxicity.</td>
        <td>Maintain fluids and salt intake. Avoid diuretics unless provider approves. Keep lab appointments.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Anticonvulsants</strong></td>
        <td>Mood stabilization if lithium ineffective or not used.</td>
        <td>Monitor for liver problems, coagulation problems, pancreatitis, bone marrow suppression, rash, dizziness, blurred vision, speech difficulty.</td>
        <td>Report rash, bleeding, abdominal pain, severe dizziness, or pregnancy.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Newer Antipsychotics</strong></td>
        <td>Bipolar disorder, especially with psychotic features.</td>
        <td>Monitor cardiac effects, weight gain, insulin resistance, diabetes risk, EPS/NMS.</td>
        <td>Report fever, rigidity, abnormal movements, severe restlessness, or major weight changes.</td>
      </tr>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Lithium Toxicity Cues</strong>

- GI upset
- Tremor
- Confusion
- Ataxia
- Seizures
- Worsening toxicity if sodium/fluid balance changes
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Lithium Teaching</strong>

- Lithium is a salt.
- Maintain consistent fluids.
- Maintain consistent salt intake.
- Conditions that make the body hold sodium can cause toxicity.
- Diuretics are contraindicated unless specifically ordered.
- Avoid use in pregnancy/breastfeeding unless provider determines benefit outweighs risk.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Lithium Monitoring</strong>

- Lithium level: 0.6–1.2 mEq/L
- Thyroid panel before and during treatment
- BUN/creatinine
- Specific gravity
- Monitor for diabetes insipidus and renal failure
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Major Lithium Concerns</strong>

- Hypothyroidism
- Diabetes insipidus
- Renal failure
- Clear urine and extreme thirst
- Not recommended in patients with heart, kidney, or thyroid disease
</div>

</div>

<strong>NCLEX:</strong> If lithium toxicity is suspected, hold the next scheduled dose and notify the provider.

<div class="key-findings" markdown="1">
<h3>📋 Mood Disorders Big Picture</h3>

- Depression requires suicide assessment.
- MDD requires at least 5 symptoms for 2 weeks, and depression or anhedonia must be present.
- Mania requires safety, low stimulation, sleep, food, and fluids.
- Bipolar I involves mania; Bipolar II involves hypomania with profound depression.
- Lithium is the drug of choice for bipolar illness and requires lab monitoring.
- Antidepressants can increase energy before mood improves, increasing suicide risk early in therapy.
</div>

<div class="mh-col" markdown="1">

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

- Suicidal ideation with plan or intent
- Homicidal ideation or threats
- Severe mania with unsafe behavior
- Psychosis, hallucinations, or delusions with safety risk
- Lithium toxicity symptoms
- Severe dehydration, exhaustion, or inability to sleep for days
- New or worsening medication adverse effects
</div>

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">
<h3>NCLEX Tip</h3>

Depression questions usually prioritize suicide risk. Mania questions usually prioritize safety, low stimulation, sleep, nutrition, and hydration.
</div>

<div class="quick-review" markdown="1">
<h3>Quick Review</h3>

1. For depression, assess suicide risk directly.
2. For MDD, remember 5 symptoms for 2 weeks and depression or anhedonia must be present.
3. For mania, decrease stimulation and protect safety.
4. For manic patients, prioritize food, fluids, sleep, and limits.
5. Bipolar I includes mania; Bipolar II includes hypomania and major depression.
6. Lithium requires levels, fluids, salt consistency, renal monitoring, and thyroid monitoring.
7. Suspected lithium toxicity means hold the next dose and notify the provider.
</div>

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">
<h3>Do Not Miss</h3>

- Depression = suicide risk until proven otherwise.
- Antidepressants may increase suicide risk early because energy can improve before mood.
- Mania can lead to exhaustion, dehydration, poor nutrition, aggression, and unsafe impulsive behavior.
- Seclusion/restraints are last resort.
- Lithium toxicity is a priority medication emergency.
- Diuretics can increase lithium toxicity risk.
- Deep brain stimulation carries serious risks such as bleeding, seizures, and stroke.
</div>
