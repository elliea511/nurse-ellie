# Depression & Bipolar Disorder

<div class="note">
Mood disorders affect a person's emotional state, energy, sleep, thinking, behavior, and ability to function.
For NCLEX, mood disorder questions usually focus on: <strong>suicide risk, safety, medication teaching, lithium toxicity, mania interventions, nutrition, hydration, sleep, and therapeutic communication.</strong>
</div>

---

## Big Picture

| Disorder | Main Problem | Nursing Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Depression** | Persistent sadness, hopelessness, low energy, loss of interest | Suicide risk, ADLs, nutrition, sleep, meds, therapy |
| **Bipolar Disorder** | Mood swings between mania/hypomania and depression | Safety, rest, low stimulation, nutrition, lithium teaching |
| **Mania** | Extremely elevated or irritable mood with high energy | Safety, limits, decrease stimulation |
| **Hypomania** | Milder mania without severe impairment or psychosis | Monitor behavior, sleep, impulsivity |
| **Cyclothymia** | Chronic mild mood swings | Coping, meds, therapy, monitoring |

<div class="warning">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Depression = always assess suicide risk.
Mania = safety, sleep, food, fluids, and low stimulation.
</div>

---

# Depression

Depression is a mood disorder with persistent sadness, hopelessness, and loss of interest in everyday activities.

It can affect sleep, appetite, energy, concentration, motivation, self-worth, ADLs, social functioning, and safety.

---

## Types of Depression

| Type | Key Point |
|---|---|
| **Major Depressive Disorder** | More severe depression; symptoms last at least 2 weeks |
| **Persistent Depressive Disorder** | Chronic milder depression; lasts at least 2 years in adults |
| **Seasonal Affective Disorder** | Depression occurs in a seasonal pattern, often winter |
| **Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder** | Severe mood symptoms related to menstrual cycle |

---

# Major Depressive Disorder

Major depressive disorder involves **depression or anhedonia** plus other symptoms that interfere with daily life.

<div class="note">
<strong>Anhedonia</strong> = loss of interest or pleasure in usual activities.
</div>

---

## MDD Diagnostic Pattern

The patient has at least **5 symptoms nearly every day for 2 weeks**, and one must be depressed mood or anhedonia.

| Symptom | What It May Look Like |
|---|---|
| Sleep disturbance | Insomnia or sleeping too much |
| Appetite/weight change | Eating more or less than usual |
| Fatigue | Low energy, tired all the time |
| Poor concentration | Difficulty focusing or making decisions |
| Loss of interest | No longer enjoys usual activities |
| Psychomotor changes | Moving slower or appearing restless |
| Feelings of worthlessness/guilt | Negative self-talk |
| Suicidal thoughts | Thoughts of death or self-harm |

---

## Depression Risk Factors

- Female gender
- Unmarried status
- Low socioeconomic status
- Early childhood trauma
- Family history of depression
- Medical illness
- Substance use
- Recent loss or major life stressor
- History of abuse or trauma

---

## Depression Assessment

| Area | What the Nurse Assesses |
|---|---|
| **Mood** | Sad, empty, hopeless, numb |
| **Affect** | Flat, tearful, blunted, withdrawn |
| **Sleep** | Insomnia or hypersomnia |
| **Appetite** | Weight loss or gain |
| **Energy** | Fatigue, low motivation |
| **ADLs** | Hygiene, grooming, self-care |
| **Thoughts** | Worthlessness, guilt, hopelessness |
| **Safety** | Suicidal ideation, plan, means, intent |
| **Support** | Family, friends, coping resources |

<div class="warning">
<strong>Priority assessment:</strong> Ask directly about suicidal thoughts, plan, access to means, and past attempts.
</div>

---

# Depression Nursing Interventions

| Problem | Nursing Intervention |
|---|---|
| Low energy | Encourage small, manageable activities |
| Poor ADLs | Assist with hygiene, then encourage independence |
| Poor appetite | Offer small, high-calorie meals |
| Social isolation | Encourage group attendance without overwhelming |
| Sleep disturbance | Promote routine and sleep hygiene |
| Hopelessness | Stay supportive and assess suicide risk |
| Poor concentration | Use simple instructions |
| Suicide risk | Remove harmful objects and monitor closely |

---

## Depression Care Tips

| Do | Avoid |
|---|---|
| Encourage routine | Saying "cheer up" |
| Give simple choices | Overwhelming with decisions |
| Praise small progress | Giving false reassurance |
| Assess suicide directly | Avoiding the word suicide |
| Encourage therapy/groups | Forcing too much too soon |
| Support ADLs | Doing everything for the patient |

---

## Depression Treatment Phases

| Phase | Goal | Usual Time Frame |
|---|---|---|
| **Acute phase** | Reduce depressive symptoms | 6–12 weeks |
| **Continuation phase** | Prevent relapse | 4–9 months |
| **Maintenance phase** | Prevent future episodes | 1 year or longer |

---

# Persistent Depressive Disorder

Persistent depressive disorder (also called **dysthymia**) is a long-term, milder form of depression.

| Feature | Key Point |
|---|---|
| Duration in adults | At least 2 years |
| Duration in children/adolescents | At least 1 year |
| Onset | Often teen years |
| Main issue | Patient may feel like "this is just normal for me" |
| Treatment | Cognitive therapy and antidepressants |

Symptoms include low energy, low self-esteem, hopelessness, poor appetite or overeating, and insomnia or sleeping too much.

---

# Seasonal Affective Disorder

Seasonal affective disorder is depression that occurs in a seasonal pattern, commonly in winter when there is less sunlight.

Symptoms include lethargy, sleeping too much, craving carbohydrates, weight gain, and depressed mood.

## Treatment

| Treatment | Nursing Teaching |
|---|---|
| Light therapy | Use prescribed ultraviolet/light box therapy as ordered |
| Routine | Keep consistent sleep/wake schedule |
| Activity | Encourage exercise and outdoor light exposure when possible |
| Medications | Antidepressants may be used if ordered |

<div class="note">
Light therapy helps because exposure to light affects melatonin regulation and can improve seasonal symptoms.
</div>

---

# Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder

PMDD is severe depressive and mood symptoms related to the menstrual cycle. It occurs during the **luteal phase** and interferes with functioning or relationships.

Symptoms include depressive symptoms, mood swings, anxiety, irritability, breast tenderness, bloating, and weight gain.

Treatment includes exercise, relaxation, antidepressants, some birth control pills, diuretics if ordered, and diet changes as instructed.

---

# Depression Medications

## Common Medication Classes

| Class | Examples | Nursing Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **SSRIs** | citalopram, escitalopram, fluoxetine, sertraline | Suicide risk early, serotonin syndrome |
| **SNRIs** | venlafaxine, duloxetine | BP, serotonin syndrome |
| **NDRI** | bupropion/Wellbutrin | Avoid with seizure/eating disorder history |
| **TCAs** | imipramine, amitriptyline | Anticholinergic effects, overdose danger |
| **MAOIs** | phenelzine, tranylcypromine | Tyramine diet, hypertensive crisis |
| **Herbal** | St. John's Wort | Drug interactions, serotonin syndrome risk |

<div class="warning">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Antidepressants may increase energy before mood improves, which can increase suicide risk early in treatment.
</div>

---

## Depression Medication Teaching

| Teaching Point | Why |
|---|---|
| Take medication as prescribed | Prevents relapse |
| Effects may take weeks | Patient may stop early if not taught |
| Do not stop suddenly | Withdrawal/relapse risk |
| Report suicidal thoughts | Safety priority |
| Avoid mixing with alcohol | CNS effects and safety |
| Tell provider about herbals | St. John's Wort has major interactions |
| Watch for serotonin syndrome | Can be life-threatening |

---

# ECT and Other Depression Treatments

## Electroconvulsive Therapy

ECT uses induced seizure activity to treat severe depression. Used for severe MDD, treatment-resistant depression, severe suicidal risk, depression with psychosis, and catatonia.

## ECT Nursing Considerations

| Before ECT | After ECT |
|---|---|
| NPO as ordered | Reorient patient |
| Consent required | Monitor vital signs |
| Remove dentures/jewelry | Assess airway |
| Baseline vitals | Monitor confusion |
| Anesthesia used | Temporary short-term memory loss may occur |

<div class="note">
ECT can cause temporary short-term memory loss, especially after multiple treatments.
</div>

---

## Other Treatment Options

| Treatment | Key Point |
|---|---|
| **TMS** | Noninvasive magnetic stimulation; no memory problems |
| **Vagus nerve stimulation** | Implanted device stimulates vagus nerve |
| **Deep brain stimulation** | Used in treatment-resistant depression; risks include bleeding, seizures, stroke |
| **Psychotherapy** | Helps coping, thought patterns, behavior change |
| **Group therapy** | Support and social connection |

---

# Bipolar Disorder

Bipolar disorder involves mood swings between **mania or hypomania** and depression. The patient may also have periods of normal mood.

Mood episodes can affect sleep, energy, judgment, speech, impulse control, relationships, safety, and substance use risk.

---

## Bipolar I vs Bipolar II

| Type | Key Difference |
|---|---|
| **Bipolar I** | At least one manic episode; most severe |
| **Bipolar II** | Hypomania alternates with major depression |
| **Cyclothymia** | Hypomania alternates with milder depression |
| **Rapid cycling** | At least 4 mood episodes in 1 year |

<div class="note">
<strong>Bipolar I = Mania. Bipolar II = Hypomania + major depression.</strong>
</div>

---

## Bipolar Risk Factors

- Genetic inheritance
- Brain anomalies
- Inflammation
- Family stress
- Adverse childhood events
- Physical, emotional, or sexual abuse
- Neglect
- Dysfunctional family history
- Substance use

<div class="warning">
Bipolar disorder has a high suicide risk, especially during depressive episodes or mixed mood states.
</div>

---

# Mania vs Hypomania

| Feature | Hypomania | Mania |
|---|---|---|
| Mood | Elevated, cheerful, or irritable | Euphoric, irritable, or rapidly changing |
| Function | Usually does not severely impair ADLs | Interferes with ADLs and safety |
| Sleep | Decreased need for sleep | May go days without sleep |
| Speech | Rapid speech | Pressured, nonstop speech |
| Thoughts | Distractible, quick thoughts | Racing thoughts, flight of ideas |
| Behavior | Increased activity, shopping, cleaning | Impulsive, risky, intrusive, unsafe |
| Psychosis | No delusions/hallucinations | May have delusions or hallucinations |
| Hospitalization | Usually not required | May be required for safety |

---

## Types of Mania

| Type | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Euphoric mania** | Elevated mood, exaggerated confidence, bright/flamboyant appearance |
| **Dysphoric mania** | Agitation, irritability, anger, depressed features mixed with mania |

---

# Mania Assessment

| Finding | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Decreased need for sleep | Exhaustion risk |
| Poor intake | Patient is too busy/distracted to eat |
| Dehydration | Constant activity increases fluid needs |
| Poor ADLs | Patient may not stop for hygiene/toileting |
| Pressured speech | Difficult to interrupt |
| Flight of ideas | Rapid topic changes |
| Loose associations | Thoughts may become disconnected |
| Grandiose delusions | "I am famous/powerful" |
| Poor impulse control | Risky spending, sex, driving, aggression |
| Irritability/agitation | Safety concern |
| Sexually inappropriate behavior | Boundary and safety issue |
| Hallucinations/delusions | Severe mania or psychosis |

---

## Mania Priority Problems

| Problem | Nursing Priority |
|---|---|
| Safety risk | Decrease stimulation, remove hazards, monitor behavior |
| Exhaustion | Promote rest and sleep |
| Poor nutrition | Offer high-calorie finger foods |
| Dehydration | Encourage fluids |
| Impulsivity | Set clear limits |
| Aggression | Maintain safety, call for help early |
| Disrupting others | Quiet area, structured environment |
| Psychosis | Assess safety and reality orientation |

<div class="warning">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> During mania, safety is the number one concern. The patient may require seclusion or restraints only as a last resort.
</div>

---

# Nursing Interventions for Mania

## Environment

| Intervention | Why |
|---|---|
| Provide quiet, low-stimulation area | Decreases agitation and distractibility |
| Reduce noise, crowds, and bright stimulation | Prevents escalation |
| Keep routine structured | Helps organize behavior |
| Provide space to move safely | Patient may be constantly active |
| Protect other patients | Manic behavior can irritate or threaten others |

---

## Nutrition and Hydration

| Intervention | Why |
|---|---|
| Offer high-calorie finger foods | Patient may not sit for meals |
| Offer fluids frequently | Prevent dehydration |
| Monitor weight | Tracks intake and health |
| Monitor I&O if needed | Detects hydration problems |
| Avoid caffeine | Can worsen stimulation |

Examples of finger foods: sandwiches, protein bars, fruit, cheese sticks, crackers, smoothies, nutritional drinks.

---

## Communication and Limit Setting

| Do | Avoid |
|---|---|
| Use short, simple statements | Long explanations |
| Set clear limits | Arguing |
| Be calm and consistent | Power struggles |
| Redirect inappropriate behavior | Shaming |
| Maintain boundaries | Laughing at behavior |
| Use team approach | Allowing splitting/manipulation |

Example: "I understand you have a lot of energy. It is time to lower your voice. You may walk with staff or sit in the quiet room."

---

# Cyclothymia and Rapid Cycling

## Cyclothymia

Cyclothymia involves hypomanic symptoms alternating with milder depressive symptoms.

- Mood highs/lows are not as severe as bipolar I or II
- Patient may be chronically depressed
- Can be precursor to bipolar disorder
- Substance use and sleep disorders may occur

---

## Rapid Cycling

Rapid cycling means at least **4 mood changes in 1 year**.

- Mood episodes occur frequently
- Patient may have little or no normal mood between episodes
- Requires close medication and safety monitoring

---

# Bipolar Comorbidities

| Disorder | Common Comorbidities |
|---|---|
| **Bipolar I** | ADHD, impulse control disorders, conduct disorders, substance abuse |
| **Bipolar II** | Anxiety, eating disorders, substance abuse |
| **Cyclothymia** | Substance disorders, sleep disorders, ADHD |

---

# Lithium Carbonate

Lithium is a mood stabilizer and is considered a classic drug of choice for bipolar disorder.

<div class="note">
Lithium is a <strong>salt</strong>, so fluid and sodium balance matter.
</div>

---

## Lithium Therapeutic Level

| Level | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **0.6–1.2 mEq/L** | Therapeutic range |
| Above range | Toxicity risk |

<div class="warning">
If lithium toxicity is suspected: <strong>hold the next dose and notify the provider.</strong>
</div>

---

## Lithium Toxicity

| Early Signs | Severe Signs |
|---|---|
| Nausea/vomiting | Confusion |
| Diarrhea | Ataxia |
| Fine tremor worsening | Severe tremor |
| Drowsiness | Seizures |
| Muscle weakness | Coma |
| Slurred speech | Dysrhythmias |

---

## Lithium Teaching

| Teaching | Why |
|---|---|
| Maintain consistent salt intake | Low sodium increases lithium retention |
| Drink adequate fluids | Dehydration increases toxicity risk |
| Avoid dehydration | Vomiting, diarrhea, sweating increase risk |
| Avoid diuretics unless approved | Diuretics can increase lithium toxicity |
| Report toxicity symptoms | Toxicity can become dangerous |
| Keep lab appointments | Lithium levels must be monitored |
| Tell provider about kidney/heart/thyroid disease | Lithium may be unsafe |

<div class="note">
Dehydration and low sodium increase toxicity risk because the body retains more lithium to compensate for the salt deficit.
</div>

---

## Lithium Monitoring

| Lab/Assessment | Why |
|---|---|
| Lithium level | Keeps drug in therapeutic range |
| BUN/Creatinine | Monitors kidney function |
| Thyroid panel | Lithium can cause hypothyroidism |
| Specific gravity | Screens kidney concentrating ability |
| Fluid intake/output | Detects dehydration or diabetes insipidus |

---

## Lithium Adverse Effects

| Adverse Effect | Clue |
|---|---|
| Hypothyroidism | Fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance |
| Diabetes insipidus | Clear urine, excessive thirst, large urine output |
| Renal impairment/failure | Abnormal BUN/creatinine |
| Tremor | May worsen with toxicity |
| GI upset | Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea |

---

# Other Bipolar Medications

| Medication Type | Why Used | Nursing Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Anticonvulsants** | Mood stabilization | Liver problems, coagulation problems, pancreatitis, bone marrow suppression |
| **Antipsychotics** | Mania with psychosis, hallucinations, delusions | EPS, NMS, weight gain, diabetes risk |
| **Antianxiety meds** | Short-term anxiety/agitation | Sedation, dependence, fall risk |
| **Calcium channel blockers** | Sometimes used as mood stabilizing support | BP, dizziness, edema |

<div class="warning">
Some mood stabilizers can cause birth defects. Pregnancy status and contraception teaching may be important.
</div>

---

# Patient Education for Bipolar Disorder

| Teaching | Why |
|---|---|
| Take meds consistently | Prevents relapse |
| Do not stop meds when feeling better | Relapse risk |
| Keep sleep schedule | Sleep loss can trigger mania |
| Avoid alcohol/drugs | Worsens mood instability |
| Recognize early mania signs | Prevents escalation |
| Monitor for depression/suicide risk | Safety |
| Keep therapy appointments | Supports long-term stability |
| Use support groups | Helps coping and adherence |
| Maintain fluids/salt with lithium | Prevents toxicity |
| Report hallucinations/delusions | May indicate severe episode |

---

# Priority Charts

## Depression Priority Chart

| Situation | First Nursing Action |
|---|---|
| Patient says "I am worthless" | Assess suicide risk |
| Patient suddenly seems happy after severe depression | Assess suicide risk |
| Patient not eating | Offer small, high-calorie meals |
| Patient not bathing | Assist with ADLs and encourage independence |
| Patient has poor concentration | Use simple directions |
| Patient starting antidepressant | Teach that effects take weeks and report suicidal thoughts |

---

## Mania Priority Chart

| Situation | First Nursing Action |
|---|---|
| Patient pacing and not sleeping | Decrease stimulation and promote rest |
| Patient not eating meals | Offer high-calorie finger foods |
| Patient intrusive with others | Set clear limits |
| Patient sexually inappropriate | Set firm boundaries |
| Patient escalating/agitated | Maintain safety and reduce stimuli |
| Patient has hallucinations/delusions | Assess safety and notify provider |
| Patient threatens others | Call for help and maintain safety |

---

## Lithium Priority Chart

| Situation | First Nursing Action |
|---|---|
| Vomiting/diarrhea while on lithium | Hold lithium and notify provider |
| Confusion/ataxia/tremor | Suspect toxicity; hold dose and notify provider |
| Patient starts diuretic | Monitor for lithium toxicity |
| Patient sweating/dehydrated | Increase toxicity risk; notify provider/assess |
| Patient has excessive thirst/clear urine | Assess for diabetes insipidus |
| Patient reports fatigue/weight gain/cold intolerance | Assess thyroid function |

---

# NCLEX Cues

<div class="warning">

<strong>Depression:</strong>
<ul>
<li>Always assess suicide risk — hopelessness is a major clue.</li>
<li>Sudden improvement in severe depression can be a suicide warning sign (decision made, energy returned).</li>
<li>Antidepressants can increase suicide risk early — energy may improve before mood does.</li>
</ul>

</div>

<div class="warning">

<strong>Mania:</strong>
<ul>
<li>Bipolar I = mania. Bipolar II = hypomania + major depression.</li>
<li>Mania priority: safety, low stimulation, rest, fluids, nutrition.</li>
<li>Provide high-calorie finger foods — the patient may not sit long enough for a meal.</li>
</ul>

</div>

<div class="warning">

<strong>Lithium:</strong>
<ul>
<li>Therapeutic range: 0.6–1.2 mEq/L.</li>
<li>Toxicity signs: GI upset, worsening tremor, confusion, ataxia — hold and notify provider.</li>
<li>Low sodium or dehydration increases lithium toxicity risk.</li>
<li>ECT: may cause temporary short-term memory loss.</li>
</ul>

</div>

# Final Takeaway

<div class="tip">
For mood disorder questions, ask:
<br><br>
<strong>1. Is the patient suicidal?</strong><br>
<strong>2. Is the patient manic and unsafe?</strong><br>
<strong>3. Are they eating, drinking, sleeping, and doing ADLs?</strong><br>
<strong>4. Are meds causing toxicity or dangerous side effects?</strong><br>
<strong>5. Does the intervention match the mood state: depression support vs mania structure?</strong>
</div>
