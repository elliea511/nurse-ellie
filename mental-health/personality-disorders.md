# Personality Disorders

<p class="mh-subtitle">ATI-style review for Cluster A, Cluster B, Cluster C, limit setting, safety, and therapeutic boundaries</p>

A **personality trait** is a pattern of behavior. A **personality disorder** occurs when personality traits become **rigid, maladaptive, and cause distress or impaired functioning.** Patients with personality disorders often have long-standing problems with behavior, mood, perception, relationships, empathy, and impulse control.

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>What It Is</strong>

- Personality traits become rigid and maladaptive.
- The patient has long-standing problems with behavior, mood, perception, and relationships.
- They may be self-centered, rigid, inflexible, and lack empathy.
- They often have poor ability to control behavior.
- They may become upset by the behaviors of others.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>External Locus of Control</strong>

- The patient believes other people or events force them to behave certain ways.
- They usually do not accept responsibility for their problems.
- They often do not seek treatment because they do not believe anything is wrong with them.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Risk Factors / Causes</strong>

- Negative childhood events
- Genetic factors
- Environmental factors
- Family dysfunction
- Abuse or neglect
- Inconsistent parenting
- Poor early attachment patterns
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Common Life Problems</strong>

- Difficulty interacting with others
- Difficulty maintaining relationships
- Difficulty keeping a job
- Conflict with coworkers or family
- Manipulation or controlling behaviors
- Usually remain in mainstream society unless another psychiatric disorder is present
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Treatment Principles</strong>

- Short-term treatment focuses on the problem that brought the client to therapy.
- Long-term treatment may be difficult because the client may believe nothing is wrong.
- Group therapy can help if the client commits to attending.
- Role playing and social skill development may be used.
- Antianxiety and antipsychotic medications may be used depending on symptoms.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Nursing Interventions</strong>

- Set clear limits.
- Maintain firm boundaries.
- Be consistent.
- Work as one treatment team.
- Do not allow staff splitting.
- Consult team members if you are having difficulty being therapeutic.
- Document objectively.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Education</strong>

- Use “I” statements instead of blaming statements.
- Practice identifying personal responsibility.
- Learn healthier coping methods.
- Practice social skills.
- Follow treatment plan and group expectations.
- Use relaxation and stress management skills.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Complications / Safety Risks</strong>

- Manipulation of staff
- Aggression or hostility
- Self-harm risk with some disorders
- Substance use
- Legal problems
- Relationship disruption
- Poor treatment adherence
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Personality disorder questions often test boundaries. Be calm, consistent, factual, and firm. The whole team must respond the same way.
</div>

### Cluster A: Odd / Eccentric Disorders

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Disorder</th>
        <th>What It Is</th>
        <th>Assessment Findings</th>
        <th>Nursing Interventions / Treatment</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Paranoid Personality Disorder</strong></td>
        <td>Pattern of distrust and suspicion. The patient interprets others’ motives and behavior as threatening.</td>
        <td>Distrust, suspicion, projection, hypervigilance, scanning environment for danger, jealousy, strained relationships, belief that others are trying to trick or embarrass them.</td>
        <td>Allow participation in care planning, build trust slowly, decrease anxiety, suggest alternative explanations, teach “I” statements, group therapy if tolerated, pimozide/Orap may decrease paranoid thoughts.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Schizoid Personality Disorder</strong></td>
        <td>Withdrawn pattern with limited social relationships and restricted emotional response.</td>
        <td>Prefers to be alone, lacks social skills/goals, detached from others, may have imaginary friends, chooses solitary work, more common in males, may begin childhood/adolescence.</td>
        <td>Psychotherapy, antidepressants, antipsychotics if ordered, support gradual social skills without forcing closeness.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Schizotypal Personality Disorder</strong></td>
        <td>Social deficits with odd beliefs, odd behavior, suspiciousness, and discomfort around others.</td>
        <td>Socially withdrawn, aloof, strange mannerisms/speech/behavior, abstract or rambling speech, ideas of reference, little/no interest in sex, indifferent to approval or criticism.</td>
        <td>Gradually involve in group situations, give positive feedback for appropriate behavior, provide support during interactions, antipsychotics, antidepressants, antianxiety meds if ordered.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Paranoid Key Cues</strong>

- Distrust and suspicion
- Uses projection
- Angry/hostile feelings are placed onto others
- Needs control and power
- Places important meaning on small events
- Beliefs are inflexible
- Rejects information they disagree with
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Schizoid Key Cues</strong>

- Withdrawn
- Avoids interaction
- Restricted emotional response
- Detached from others
- Poor school performance may occur
- May be bullied or behave oddly
- Can precede schizophrenic breakdown
- Runs in families with schizophrenia
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Schizotypal Key Cues</strong>

- More severe/debilitating than schizoid
- Acute discomfort around others
- Suspicious in social settings
- Odd beliefs
- Ideas of reference
- Strange mannerisms
- May be involved in unusual religious groups because of odd beliefs
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Education</strong>

- Practice identifying more than one explanation for others’ behavior.
- Use “I” statements instead of “you” statements.
- Practice social skills gradually.
- Take medications as prescribed.
- Use relaxation skills to lower anxiety.
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> With paranoid personality disorder, do not argue about suspicious beliefs. Build trust, be honest, and involve the patient in care planning when possible.
</div>

</div>

### Cluster B: Dramatic / Erratic Disorders

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Disorder</th>
        <th>What It Is</th>
        <th>Assessment Findings</th>
        <th>Nursing Interventions / Treatment</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Antisocial Personality Disorder</strong></td>
        <td>Pattern of disregard for and violation of the rights of others. Must be 18 or older for diagnosis and often has history of conduct disorder as a teen.</td>
        <td>Manipulative, controlling, charming, selfish, irresponsible, impulsive, lacks empathy, lies/cheats, criminal behavior, no guilt, immediate gratification, may become abusive when denied what they want.</td>
        <td>Safety for all is highest concern, set firm limits, work as one team, prevent manipulation, maintain unit control, provide physical outlets for anger, behavior therapy, group/family/individual therapy, mood stabilizers or antianxiety meds if ordered.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Borderline Personality Disorder</strong></td>
        <td>Impulsive, unstable, unpredictable moods and relationships. Uses splitting and may have self-injurious behavior.</td>
        <td>Rapid mood swings, fear of abandonment, inappropriate anger, intense unstable relationships, boredom when alone, substance use, indiscriminate sex, cutting, projection, black-and-white thinking.</td>
        <td>Be supportive and available while maintaining boundaries, do not support dependency/manipulation, teach problem-solving, find appropriate outlets for anger, behavioral therapy, group therapy, antidepressants, anticonvulsants, lithium, antipsychotics, naltrexone may decrease self-injurious behavior.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Histrionic Personality Disorder</strong></td>
        <td>Dramatic, attention-seeking pattern with exaggerated emotions.</td>
        <td>Excessive emotional expression, temper tantrums when ignored, egocentric, overtly sexual/flamboyant, manipulates for affection, may use suicidal gestures to manipulate, may fake illness for attention.</td>
        <td>Assess suicidal thoughts, psychotherapy to identify feelings and express them appropriately, group therapy if not disruptive, antidepressants, antianxiety meds, antipsychotics if ordered.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Narcissistic Personality Disorder</strong></td>
        <td>Grandiosity, need for admiration, and lack of empathy, often used to compensate for low self-esteem.</td>
        <td>Believes they deserve special treatment, exaggerates importance, manipulates, rationalizes failures, arrogant, exploits others, hypersensitive to criticism/rejection, fear of abandonment.</td>
        <td>Be a role model for empathy, avoid power struggles, do not take negative remarks personally, CBT, group therapy to develop empathy, lithium or antidepressants if ordered.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Antisocial Risk Factors / Causes</strong>

- Genetic inheritance with possible neurological deficits
- Child learns inappropriate behavior from parents
- Parents reward inappropriate behavior by giving in
- Chaotic environment
- Inconsistent/impulsive parenting
- Deprivation in first 5 years of life
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Borderline Risk Factors / Causes</strong>

- Genetic correlation
- Inadequate regulation of serotonin/dopamine
- Impulsive behavior and unstable moods
- Difficulty achieving autonomy in toddlerhood
- Fear/anxiety with separation
- Sexual abuse, often by males
- Inconsistent parenting, often by mother
- Emotional or physical abuse
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Cluster B Safety Risks</strong>

- Manipulation of staff
- Aggression or abuse
- Self-injurious behavior
- Suicidal gestures or suicidal ideas
- Substance use
- Risky sexual behavior
- Legal problems
- Staff splitting
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Education</strong>

- Use coping skills before anger escalates.
- Practice problem-solving instead of impulsive action.
- Use appropriate outlets for anger.
- Practice identifying feelings instead of acting them out.
- Respect unit rules and boundaries.
- Attend therapy/groups consistently.
</div>

</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Cluster B questions often test safety, limit setting, staff splitting, manipulation, and maintaining therapeutic boundaries.
</div>

### Cluster C: Anxious / Fearful Disorders

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Disorder</th>
        <th>What It Is</th>
        <th>Assessment Findings</th>
        <th>Nursing Interventions / Treatment</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Avoidant Personality Disorder</strong></td>
        <td>Oversensitivity to criticism or rejection with social inhibition and feelings of inadequacy.</td>
        <td>Feels inferior, awkward, self-deprecating, wants acceptance but avoids social situations, fears rejection/failure/humiliation, may go to public places but keep distance.</td>
        <td>Be accepting and friendly, help develop social skills, teach assertiveness, psychotherapy, group therapy, antidepressants especially SSRIs, antianxiety meds if ordered.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Dependent Personality Disorder</strong></td>
        <td>Submissive and clingy pattern with excessive need to be taken care of.</td>
        <td>No self-confidence, difficulty making decisions, seeks someone to guide/control them, passive, does not express opinions, separation anxiety, may care for others but not self.</td>
        <td>Encourage decision-making, teach assertiveness, CBT/psychotherapy, antidepressants and antianxiety meds if ordered.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder</strong></td>
        <td>Preoccupation with orderliness, perfectionism, and control. Different from OCD because this is a personality pattern, not obsessions/compulsions.</td>
        <td>Rigid, inflexible, fears failure, critical toward self and others, needs control, perfectionistic.</td>
        <td>Guard against power struggles, assess rationalization/defense mechanisms, maintain awareness of critical nature toward self/others, support flexibility and coping.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Cluster C Big Picture</strong>

- Anxiety and fear drive behavior.
- Patients may want relationships but fear rejection or failure.
- They may depend on others or rigidly control situations to lower anxiety.
- Stress management and relaxation help keep anxiety low.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Dependent Risk Factors / Causes</strong>

- Prolonged illness as a child
- Punished for independent behavior
- Learned helplessness
- Fear of separation
- Relationship anxiety
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Treatment for Anxious/Fearful Disorders</strong>

- Establish caring and consistent relationship.
- Use clear behavioral expectations.
- Teach assertiveness.
- Expect patient to make decisions.
- Identify positive qualities with patient input.
- Give positive feedback for socially appropriate behavior.
- Teach stress management and relaxation.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Education</strong>

- Practice making small decisions.
- Use assertive communication.
- Identify positive personal qualities.
- Practice social skills in small steps.
- Use relaxation skills when anxiety rises.
- Attend therapy/groups consistently.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Complications / Safety Risks</strong>

- Social isolation
- Dependency on others
- Poor self-care
- Relationship strain
- Anxiety and depression
- Difficulty functioning independently
- Power struggles with obsessive-compulsive personality disorder
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> For dependent personality disorder, do not make every decision for the patient. Support the patient while expecting small independent choices.
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Remember:</strong> Obsessive-compulsive personality disorder is about perfectionism/control as a personality style. OCD is about obsessions and compulsions that reduce anxiety.
</div>

</div>

<div class="mh-columns wide-left" markdown="1">

<div class="key-findings" markdown="1">
<h3>📋 Personality Disorders Big Picture</h3>

- Personality disorders are long-standing, rigid, maladaptive behavior patterns.
- Patients often have external locus of control and may blame others.
- Most are not hospitalized unless another psychiatric disorder is present.
- Treatment focuses on boundaries, limits, consistency, and the problem that brought them to treatment.
- Staff must work together as one team.
- Document objectively and avoid power struggles.
</div>

<div class="mh-col" markdown="1">

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

- Threats of harm to self or others
- Aggression or escalating violence
- Self-injurious behavior
- Suicidal statements or gestures
- Severe manipulation affecting safety
- Psychosis, delusions, or hallucinations
- Unsafe behavior toward staff or other patients
</div>

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">
<h3>NCLEX Tip</h3>

Personality disorder questions usually test boundaries. Stay calm, factual, and consistent. Do not get pulled into arguing, rescuing, or power struggles.
</div>

<div class="quick-review" markdown="1">
<h3>Quick Review</h3>

1. Personality disorders are rigid, maladaptive patterns.
2. Assess objectively and document factual findings.
3. Always assess safety, self-harm risk, legal history, coping, and support.
4. Use clear limits and consistent boundaries.
5. Keep the treatment team united.
6. Cluster B has the highest safety/manipulation focus.
7. Cluster C requires support plus encouragement of independence/assertiveness.
</div>

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">
<h3>Do Not Miss</h3>

- Do not argue with suspicious or rigid beliefs.
- Do not allow staff splitting.
- Do not reinforce dependency or manipulation.
- For antisocial personality disorder, safety for all is highest concern.
- For borderline personality disorder, be supportive but maintain clear limits.
- For narcissistic personality disorder, avoid power struggles and do not take insults personally.
- For dependent personality disorder, encourage decision-making instead of making all decisions for them.
</div>
