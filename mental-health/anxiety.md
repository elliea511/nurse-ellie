# Anxiety, OCD & Trauma Disorders

<div class="note">
Anxiety disorders are not just "being nervous." They become a disorder when anxiety is excessive, hard to control, causes avoidance, or interferes with ADLs.
For NCLEX, focus on: <strong>anxiety level, safety, calming the patient first, short/simple communication, coping skills, medications, and gradual exposure.</strong>
</div>

---

## Big Picture

| Disorder | Main Problem | Nursing Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Generalized Anxiety Disorder** | Excessive worry about many things | Reduce anxiety, coping skills, relaxation |
| **Panic Disorder** | Repeated panic attacks | Stay with patient, short directions, breathing |
| **Phobias** | Irrational fear of object/situation | Gradual exposure, avoid reinforcing avoidance |
| **Social Anxiety Disorder** | Fear of embarrassment/judgment | Support, social skills, gradual exposure |
| **Agoraphobia** | Fear of being trapped/unable to escape | Stay with patient, support, gradual exposure |
| **OCD** | Obsessions + compulsions | Reduce rituals gradually, teach coping |
| **PTSD** | Trauma response with flashbacks/avoidance | Safety, trust, trauma-informed care |
| **Acute Stress Disorder** | Trauma symptoms soon after event | Support, safety, problem-solving |
| **Adjustment Disorder** | Emotional response to life change/stressor | Coping, therapy, support system |

<div class="warning">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> During severe anxiety or panic, do not teach first. Stay with the patient, reduce stimulation, and use short directions.
</div>

# Anxiety Basics

Anxiety is a feeling of dread, apprehension, or uneasiness. Anxiety becomes a disorder when it causes problems with thinking, mood, behavior, ADLs, sleep, relationships, physical functioning, or avoidance of normal activities.

---

## Levels of Anxiety

| Level | What It Looks Like | Nursing Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Alert, focused, slightly restless | Teaching and problem-solving can happen |
| **Moderate** | Narrowed focus, tense, some details missed | Use simple explanations and refocus |
| **Severe** | Poor concentration, physical symptoms, pacing | Reduce stimuli, stay calm, short directions |
| **Panic** | Unable to process, feels out of control or dying | Stay with patient, safety, very short directions |

<div class="note">
Mild anxiety can improve focus. Panic-level anxiety prevents learning and problem-solving.
</div>

---

## Common Anxiety Symptoms

| Emotional/Cognitive | Physical/Behavioral |
|---|---|
| Feelings of doom | Tachycardia |
| Fear | Sweating |
| Guilt or shame | Trembling |
| Helplessness | Shortness of breath |
| Worthlessness | GI upset |
| Poor concentration | Insomnia |
| Racing thoughts | Restlessness |
| Irritability | Avoidance behaviors |

---

# Nursing Care for Anxiety

| Nursing Action | Why |
|---|---|
| Assess anxiety level | Determines communication and interventions |
| Stay calm | Patient may mirror nurse's anxiety |
| Use simple directions | Anxiety decreases ability to process |
| Reduce stimulation | Noise/crowds worsen anxiety |
| Encourage breathing/relaxation | Helps decrease physiologic arousal |
| Identify triggers | Helps prevent future episodes |
| Teach coping when calm | Teaching works best after anxiety decreases |
| Encourage routine | Structure decreases uncertainty |

---

## What Not to Do

| Avoid | Why |
|---|---|
| "Calm down." | Can feel dismissive |
| Long explanations during panic | Patient cannot process |
| Crowding the patient | Can worsen anxiety |
| Forcing exposure too quickly | Can increase fear |
| Giving false reassurance | Not therapeutic |
| Encouraging avoidance forever | Reinforces anxiety |

---

# Generalized Anxiety Disorder

Generalized anxiety disorder is excessive anxiety and worry about many things. The person may feel like they cannot "shut off" their brain.

## Assessment

| Finding | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Constant worry | Main feature |
| Trouble concentrating | Anxiety interferes with thinking |
| Insomnia | Worry prevents sleep |
| Fatigue | Anxiety is physically exhausting |
| Muscle tension | Common physical symptom |
| Overreacting to small details | Anxiety attaches to whatever they are thinking about |
| Difficulty making decisions | Worry interferes with choices |

## Nursing Interventions

| Intervention | Why |
|---|---|
| Keep anxiety level low | Prevents escalation |
| Teach relaxation | Helps manage worry |
| Encourage journaling | Gets thoughts out of head |
| Promote sleep routine | Improves functioning |
| Encourage exercise | Reduces anxiety symptoms |
| Give simple choices | Prevents overwhelm |
| Medications as ordered | SSRIs, buspirone, short-term benzos if prescribed |

---

# Panic Disorder

Panic disorder involves repeated panic attacks that occur unpredictably. A panic attack is sudden, intense fear that may feel like dying, losing control, or "going crazy."

## Panic Attack Findings

| Finding |
|---|
| Feeling of impending doom |
| Chest pain |
| Shortness of breath |
| Palpitations |
| Trembling |
| Sweating |
| Dizziness |
| Poor concentration |
| Irrational thoughts |
| Desire to escape |

<div class="note">
Panic attacks usually peak in about <strong>10–15 minutes</strong> and often do not last longer than <strong>30 minutes</strong>.
</div>

---

## Nursing Care During Panic Attack

| Nursing Action | Why |
|---|---|
| Stay with the patient | Reduces fear and promotes safety |
| Maintain calm voice | Helps decrease escalation |
| Move to quiet area | Decreases stimulation |
| Use short directions | Patient cannot process complex teaching |
| Encourage slow breathing | Helps with hyperventilation |
| Avoid touching unless needed | Touch can increase panic |
| Teach coping later | Teaching comes after panic decreases |

Example: "You are safe. I will stay with you. Breathe in slowly with me."

<div class="warning">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> During panic, stay with the patient and use short, clear directions.
</div>

---

# Phobias

A phobia is an intense, irrational fear of a specific object or situation. The patient usually knows the fear is irrational, but still feels unable to control it.

Common examples: spiders, heights, flying, needles, elevators, dogs, storms.

## Assessment

| Finding | Why |
|---|---|
| Anxiety increases near feared object/situation | Triggered fear response |
| Avoidance | Avoidance can restrict life |
| Fear interferes with daily life | Disorder-level concern |
| May use alcohol/drugs to cope | Risk for substance use |

---

## Treatment

| Treatment | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Systematic desensitization** | Gradual exposure while using relaxation |
| **Flooding** | Direct exposure to feared object/situation until anxiety decreases |
| **CBT** | Challenges irrational fear thoughts |
| **Medication** | Antianxiety meds, antidepressants, or beta blockers if ordered |

<div class="note">
Systematic desensitization is gradual. Flooding is intense/direct exposure.
</div>

---

# Social Anxiety Disorder

Social anxiety disorder is fear of embarrassment, judgment, or public ridicule in social situations.

## Assessment

| Finding |
|---|
| Fear of speaking in public |
| Avoids answering questions in class/group |
| Likes to stay unnoticed |
| Fear of being laughed at |
| May feign illness to avoid social situations |
| Social avoidance interferes with life |
| Low self-confidence |

## Nursing Care

| Intervention | Why |
|---|---|
| Encourage gradual exposure | Builds tolerance |
| Teach relaxation | Reduces physical anxiety |
| Support social skills practice | Increases confidence |
| Avoid forcing public speaking suddenly | Can worsen fear |
| Give positive feedback | Reinforces progress |
| Meds as ordered | SSRIs, antianxiety meds, beta blockers |

---

# Agoraphobia

Agoraphobia is fear of being in places where escape may be difficult or help may not be available. It is often described as fear of open spaces, crowds, public transportation, or being trapped.

## Assessment

| Finding |
|---|
| Fear of being trapped |
| Fear of not being able to escape |
| Avoids public places |
| May become homebound |
| Panic symptoms in public |
| Needs support person to leave home |

## Nursing Care

| Intervention | Why |
|---|---|
| Stay with client during high anxiety | Their fear is real |
| Provide support | Promotes safety |
| Teach relaxation | Decreases symptoms |
| Encourage gradual exposure | Reduces avoidance over time |
| Medications as ordered | Benzodiazepines short-term, SSRIs, beta blockers, antihistamines |

---

# Separation Anxiety

Separation anxiety is normal in toddlers, but abnormal when it persists or appears in older children, teens, or adults and interferes with functioning.

## Assessment

| Finding |
|---|
| Clinginess |
| Nightmares |
| Insomnia |
| Fear of separation |
| Lack of self-direction |
| Anxiety when away from attachment figure |

Nursing focus: assess trauma or loss history, encourage independence gradually, teach coping skills, support healthy relationships, and treat coexisting depression/anxiety if ordered.

---

# Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder

OCD involves **obsessions** and **compulsions**.

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Obsession** | Persistent, unwanted thought that causes anxiety |
| **Compulsion** | Repetitive behavior done to reduce anxiety |

Example: Obsessive thought: "My hands are contaminated." Compulsion: Washing hands repeatedly.

---

## OCD Cycle

| Step | What Happens |
|---|---|
| **1. Obsession** | Unwanted thought occurs |
| **2. Anxiety increases** | Tension builds |
| **3. Compulsion** | Patient performs ritual |
| **4. Temporary relief** | Anxiety decreases briefly |
| **5. Obsession returns** | Cycle repeats |

<div class="note">
Obsessions are thoughts. Compulsions are actions.
</div>

---

## OCD Assessment

| Finding |
|---|
| Rituals become time-consuming |
| Patient feels driven to perform behavior |
| Anxiety increases if ritual is blocked |
| Ritual must be performed "correctly" |
| Interferes with ADLs/work/school |
| Patient may feel shame |
| Depression/eating disorders/bipolar may coexist |

Common compulsions: handwashing, checking locks/stove, counting, ordering/arranging, repeating words, cleaning.

---

## OCD Nursing Interventions

| Intervention | Why |
|---|---|
| Allow time for ritual at first if needed | Abruptly stopping can increase anxiety |
| Gradually limit ritual time/frequency | Reduces compulsive behavior |
| Teach relaxation | Helps manage anxiety |
| Use behavior therapy plan | Consistency matters |
| Give positive feedback for progress | Reinforces coping |
| Encourage CBT | Helps challenge obsessive thoughts |
| Medications as ordered | SSRIs, fluvoxamine, TCAs |

<div class="warning">
Do not suddenly block all rituals. Gradually reduce ritual time while teaching coping skills.
</div>

---

## Thought Stopping

Thought stopping is a technique to interrupt an obsessive or negative thought:

1. Say "Stop"
2. Use a cue, such as snapping a rubber band
3. Replace the unwanted thought with a more positive or realistic thought

---

# Body Dysmorphic Disorder

Body dysmorphic disorder involves obsessive thoughts about a perceived body defect that appears normal or minor to others.

## Assessment

| Finding |
|---|
| Preoccupation with perceived body flaw |
| Repeated mirror checking or hiding body part |
| Seeking cosmetic procedures |
| Not satisfied even after surgery |
| Anxiety, depression, or substance use may coexist |

## Nursing Focus

| Intervention | Why |
|---|---|
| Do not reinforce distorted belief | Avoid agreeing with defect |
| Focus on feelings and functioning | Supports coping |
| Encourage CBT | Helps challenge distorted thoughts |
| Monitor for depression/suicide risk | Distress can be severe |
| Limit reassurance-seeking | Reassurance does not fix obsession |

---

# Hoarding Disorder

Hoarding disorder involves difficulty discarding items, even if they seem worthless. The person feels extreme distress when asked to get rid of items.

## Assessment

| Finding |
|---|
| Excessive saving of items |
| Difficulty discarding |
| Living areas cluttered/unusable |
| Family frustration/isolation |
| Anxiety when discarding items |
| May hoard animals |
| Usually worsens with age |

Nursing focus: assess safety of environment, assess fire/fall/infection risks, encourage therapy, avoid forcing sudden cleanout unless safety emergency, work gradually on decision-making and coping.

---

# Trichotillomania and Excoriation

| Disorder | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Trichotillomania** | Repeatedly pulling out hair |
| **Excoriation** | Repeatedly picking skin |

These behaviors may temporarily relieve anxiety.

## Nursing Focus

| Intervention | Why |
|---|---|
| Assess infection/skin damage | Especially with excoriation |
| Assess shame/anxiety | Patient may hide symptoms |
| Teach competing response | Replace behavior with safer action |
| Encourage CBT/habit reversal | Main behavioral treatment |
| Monitor for OCD | Common comorbidity |

---

# PTSD

PTSD can occur after experiencing or witnessing a traumatic event such as assault, war, accident, natural disaster, or prolonged severe stress.

## Core PTSD Symptoms

| Symptom Cluster | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Intrusion** | Flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive thoughts |
| **Avoidance** | Avoids reminders of trauma |
| **Negative mood/thoughts** | Depression, guilt, emotional numbness |
| **Hyperarousal** | Hypervigilance, irritability, poor sleep, exaggerated startle |

<div class="note">
Flashback = the patient feels like the trauma is happening again in the present.
</div>

---

## PTSD Assessment

| Finding |
|---|
| Intrusive memories |
| Nightmares |
| Flashbacks |
| Avoids reminders |
| Hypervigilance |
| Irritability |
| Depression |
| Sleep problems |
| Difficulty trusting others |
| Substance use risk |
| Relationship difficulties |

---

## PTSD Nursing Care

| Nursing Action | Why |
|---|---|
| Establish trust | Trauma patients may have difficulty trusting |
| Explain before touching | Prevents feeling powerless |
| Ask permission when possible | Gives control back |
| Provide calm environment | Reduces hyperarousal |
| Avoid forcing details | Patient may not be ready |
| Encourage grounding | Helps with flashbacks |
| Support therapy/group therapy | Main treatment |
| Assess suicide/substance use | Common risks |

<div class="warning">
With PTSD, do not force the patient to relive or explain the trauma before they are ready. Safety and trust come first.
</div>

---

## Grounding During Flashback

Grounding helps bring the patient back to the present:

- "You are in the hospital. Today is ____. You are safe right now."
- Name 5 things you see
- Feel feet on the floor
- Hold a cold object
- Slow breathing
- Describe the room

---

# Acute Stress Disorder

Acute stress disorder occurs soon after a traumatic event. It is diagnosed when symptoms occur within about **3 days to 1 month** after the event.

## Assessment

| Finding |
|---|
| Derealization |
| Intrusive thoughts |
| Avoiding reminders |
| Hypervigilance |
| Agitation |
| Restlessness |
| Difficulty explaining what happened |
| Sleep disturbance |

## Nursing Care

| Intervention | Why |
|---|---|
| Establish trust | Patient may feel unsafe |
| Support problem-solving | Crisis is recent |
| Involve support people | Promotes recovery |
| Refer to appropriate agencies | Helps with needs after trauma |
| Teach grounding/relaxation | Reduces symptoms |
| Assess suicide risk | Trauma can increase risk |

---

# Adjustment Disorder

Adjustment disorder is an emotional or behavioral response to a stressful life event. It usually occurs within **3 months** of the stressor.

Examples: retirement, moving to a nursing home, death of a loved one, serious diagnosis, divorce, job loss, major life change.

Symptoms include depression, anxiety, anger, social withdrawal, impaired functioning, emptiness, difficulty planning for future, and relationship difficulty.

Treatment includes psychotherapy, family therapy, group therapy, crisis intervention, antidepressants or antianxiety meds if ordered, and support system involvement.

---

# Anti-Anxiety Medications

## Medication Overview

| Medication/Class | Key Point | Nursing Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **SSRIs** | Long-term anxiety treatment | Takes weeks, monitor suicide risk early |
| **Benzodiazepines** | Quick relief | Short-term use, sedation, falls, dependence |
| **Buspirone** | Long-term anxiety treatment | Takes weeks, no tolerance/addiction |
| **Antihistamines** | Can reduce anxiety | Sedation |
| **Beta blockers** | Reduce physical symptoms | Check HR/BP |
| **Fluvoxamine/TCAs** | Used for OCD | Side effects, adherence |
| **Antipsychotics** | Severe symptoms only | EPS, NMS, metabolic effects |

---

## Benzodiazepines

Examples: lorazepam, diazepam, alprazolam, clonazepam.

| Teaching |
|---|
| Use short-term if possible |
| Can cause sedation |
| Avoid alcohol and other CNS depressants |
| Increased fall risk |
| Can cause tolerance/dependence |
| Do not stop suddenly if long-term use |

---

## Buspirone

Buspirone is useful for long-term anxiety.

| Key Teaching |
|---|
| Takes weeks to work |
| Does not cause tolerance/addiction like benzodiazepines |
| Not useful for immediate panic relief |
| Avoid if severe liver/kidney disease unless provider approves |
| Take consistently |

<div class="note">
Buspirone is not a rescue medication. It takes time to become therapeutic.
</div>

# Priority Charts

## Anxiety Priority Chart

| Situation | First Nursing Action |
|---|---|
| Mild anxiety | Teach coping/problem-solving |
| Moderate anxiety | Use simple directions |
| Severe anxiety | Reduce stimuli and stay calm |
| Panic attack | Stay with patient and use short directions |
| Patient hyperventilating | Coach slow breathing |
| Patient cannot focus | Decrease anxiety before teaching |

---

## OCD Priority Chart

| Situation | First Nursing Action |
|---|---|
| Rituals interfere with ADLs | Gradually reduce ritual time |
| Patient becomes anxious when ritual blocked | Support coping/relaxation |
| Obsession causes distress | Teach thought stopping/CBT strategies |
| Skin breakdown from washing/picking | Assess and treat skin |
| Patient feels shame | Use nonjudgmental communication |

---

## Trauma Priority Chart

| Situation | First Nursing Action |
|---|---|
| Flashback | Ground patient to present |
| PTSD patient avoids details | Do not force discussion |
| Acute trauma | Establish safety and trust |
| Hypervigilance | Reduce stimulation |
| Trauma patient has suicidal thoughts | Assess plan/means/intent |
| Sexual/physical assault history | Use trauma-informed care |

# Final Takeaway

<div class="tip">
For anxiety, OCD, and trauma questions, ask:
<br><br>
<strong>1. What level of anxiety is this?</strong><br>
<strong>2. Can the patient learn right now, or do I need to calm them first?</strong><br>
<strong>3. Is the behavior avoidance, ritual, panic, or trauma response?</strong><br>
<strong>4. Do I need to reduce stimuli, stay with them, or teach coping?</strong><br>
<strong>5. Is the medication for quick relief or long-term control?</strong>
</div>
