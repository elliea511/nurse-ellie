# Crisis & Safety Priorities

<div class="note">

This page is the quick mental health priority page.

For NCLEX, crisis and safety questions usually ask: <strong>Who is unsafe? What should the nurse do first? Does the patient need direct assessment, supervision, a safer environment, or emergency intervention?</strong>

</div>

---

## Big Picture

| Crisis Situation | First Priority |
|---|---|
| **Suicide risk** | Ask directly, assess plan/means/intent, remove harmful objects |
| **Self-harm** | Assess intent and maintain safety |
| **Homicide risk** | Ask directly, identify target/plan/means |
| **Violence/aggression** | Protect patient/staff, reduce stimulation, call for help early |
| **Command hallucinations** | Ask what the voices are saying |
| **Delusions/paranoia** | Do not argue; assess safety |
| **Panic attack** | Stay with patient, reduce stimuli, short directions |
| **Mania** | Safety, low stimulation, nutrition, hydration, rest |
| **Abuse** | Interview alone, assess safety, report when required |
| **Withdrawal/overdose** | ABCs, vital signs, seizure precautions, emergency meds |

<div class="warning">

<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Safety comes before teaching, coping skills, long-term therapy, and discharge planning.

</div>

---

# Mental Health Priority Rule

## Ask Yourself First

| Question | Why |
|---|---|
| Is the patient at risk for suicide? | Immediate self-harm risk |
| Is the patient at risk for harming others? | Violence/homicide risk |
| Is there a medical emergency? | Overdose, withdrawal, toxicity, unstable VS |
| Is the patient experiencing psychosis? | Poor reality testing may increase danger |
| Is the patient being abused or controlled? | Needs privacy/safety/resources |
| Can the patient meet basic needs? | Food, fluids, sleep, hygiene, shelter |

---

# Suicide Priorities

## Highest Risk Findings

| Finding | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Specific suicide plan | Patient has thought through method |
| Access to means | Patient can act |
| Chosen time | Imminent risk |
| Previous attempt | Major risk factor |
| Giving away belongings | Preparation |
| Sudden calm after depression | May indicate decision to attempt |
| Substance use | Lowers impulse control |
| Command hallucinations | Voices may tell patient to self-harm |
| Hopelessness | Major suicide clue |

---

## Suicide Priority Actions

| Situation | First Nursing Action |
|---|---|
| Patient says, "I want to die." | Ask directly: "Are you thinking about killing yourself?" |
| Patient says they have a plan | Ask what the plan is |
| Patient has plan + means | Stay with patient and remove harmful objects |
| Newly admitted after suicide attempt | Search patient/belongings for harmful objects |
| Patient hears voices | Ask what the voices are saying |
| Patient suddenly appears happy after depression | Assess suicide risk |
| Patient asks nurse to keep secret | Explain you cannot keep safety concerns secret |

<div class="warning">

Do not leave an actively suicidal patient alone.

</div>

---

# Environmental Safety for Suicide Risk

Remove or secure: belts, shoelaces, cords, plastic bags, razors, scissors, tweezers, nail files, glass, lighters/matches, medications, weapons, and harmful gifts.

| Safety Measure | Why |
|---|---|
| Search belongings with patient present | Maintains safety and dignity |
| Use/count plastic utensils | Prevents hidden sharp objects |
| Keep door open | Allows observation |
| Avoid private room if unsafe | Reduces isolation |
| Monitor bathroom safety | Bathroom can be high risk |
| 1:1 observation if ordered | Constant safety monitoring |

---

# Homicide and Violence Priorities

## Homicide Assessment

Ask directly:

| Question | What It Assesses |
|---|---|
| "Are you thinking about hurting someone?" | Homicidal thoughts |
| "Who are you thinking about hurting?" | Target |
| "Do you have a plan?" | Specific plan |
| "Do you have access to weapons?" | Means |
| "Where is that person now?" | Immediacy |

<div class="warning">

Specific person + specific plan + access to weapon = high risk.

</div>

---

## Violence Warning Signs

- Pacing
- Clenched fists
- Loud voice
- Threats
- Staring
- Agitation
- Paranoia
- Intoxication
- Invading personal space
- History of violence
- Weapon access

---

## De-escalation Priorities

| Do | Avoid |
|---|---|
| Stay calm | Arguing |
| Keep safe distance | Standing too close |
| Keep exit accessible | Blocking doorway |
| Use short statements | Long lectures |
| Offer choices | Power struggles |
| Reduce stimulation | Crowding the patient |
| Call for help early | Managing alone |
| Set clear limits | Threatening the patient |

Example: "I can see you are angry. I cannot allow anyone to be hurt. You may sit in the quiet room or walk with staff."

---

# Psychosis Safety Priorities

## Hallucinations

| Situation | First Nursing Action |
|---|---|
| Patient reports voices | Ask what the voices are saying |
| Voices tell patient to self-harm | Stay with patient and maintain safety |
| Voices tell patient to harm others | Maintain safety and notify team |
| Patient is frightened by voices | Acknowledge fear and present reality |
| Patient asks if nurse hears voices | "I do not hear them, but I understand they are real to you." |

<div class="warning">

Command hallucinations are priority because they may tell the patient to harm self or others.

</div>

---

## Delusions

| Situation | Best Nursing Response |
|---|---|
| "The FBI is watching me." | "That sounds frightening. I do not see anyone watching you." |
| "My food is poisoned." | "You seem worried about the food. Let's talk about what would help you feel safe eating." |
| "I am famous and powerful." | Do not argue; redirect to reality-based activity |
| Paranoid patient becomes aggressive | Maintain safety and reduce stimulation |

<div class="note">

Do not argue with delusions. Do not agree with delusions. Focus on feelings, safety, and reality.

</div>

---

# Panic and Severe Anxiety Priorities

| Anxiety Level | Nursing Priority |
|---|---|
| **Mild** | Teach and problem-solve |
| **Moderate** | Use simple directions |
| **Severe** | Reduce stimuli and stay calm |
| **Panic** | Stay with patient, short commands, safety |

## Panic Attack

| Situation | First Nursing Action |
|---|---|
| Patient says "I'm dying" | Stay with patient and reassure safety |
| Patient cannot focus | Use short, simple directions |
| Patient hyperventilates | Coach slow breathing |
| Patient wants to escape | Move to quiet area if safe |
| Patient is panicking | Do not teach until calm |

Example: "You are safe. I will stay with you. Take one slow breath with me."

---

# Mania Safety Priorities

Mania is a safety issue because the patient may have poor judgment, little sleep, poor intake, impulsivity, and agitation.

| Problem | Nursing Priority |
|---|---|
| No sleep | Promote rest and low stimulation |
| Not eating | Offer high-calorie finger foods |
| Dehydration | Offer fluids often |
| Intrusive behavior | Set clear limits |
| Sexually inappropriate behavior | Set firm boundaries |
| Agitation | Reduce stimulation and maintain safety |
| Psychosis | Assess safety and notify team |

<div class="note">

Mania priority: <strong>safety, low stimulation, rest, fluids, and nutrition.</strong>

</div>

---

# Abuse Safety Priorities

## Abuse Assessment

If abuse is suspected, interview the patient alone.

| Situation | First Nursing Action |
|---|---|
| Partner/caregiver answers for patient | Separate patient safely and interview alone |
| Child has suspicious injuries | Assess and report suspected abuse |
| Elder has bruises/weight loss | Collect physical data and report findings |
| Patient reports sexual assault | Ensure safety and provide nonjudgmental support |
| Patient afraid to go home | Assess immediate danger and safety plan |
| Patient asks for secrecy | Explain safety concerns cannot be secret |

---

## Mandatory Reporting

Report suspected abuse of:

- Children
- Older adults
- Vulnerable/dependent adults
- Disabled adults unable to protect self

<div class="warning">

For NCLEX: suspected child abuse and elder abuse must be reported. Suspicion is enough.

</div>

---

# Withdrawal and Overdose Priorities

## Overdose

| Situation | First Priority |
|---|---|
| Opioid overdose | Airway/breathing, naloxone as ordered |
| Sedative overdose | Airway/breathing |
| Alcohol intoxication with vomiting | Airway/aspiration precautions |
| Stimulant intoxication with chest pain | Cardiac assessment/notify provider |
| Hallucinogen panic/paranoia | Quiet environment and safety |

---

## Withdrawal

| Situation | First Priority |
|---|---|
| Alcohol withdrawal | Monitor VS, seizure precautions, benzos as ordered |
| Delirium tremens | Medical emergency |
| Benzodiazepine/barbiturate withdrawal | Gradual taper, seizure precautions |
| Opioid withdrawal | Comfort, hydration, withdrawal meds |
| Stimulant withdrawal | Assess depression/suicide risk |

<div class="warning">

Alcohol and benzodiazepine/barbiturate withdrawal can cause seizures and may be life-threatening.

</div>

---

# Medication Emergency Priorities

| Emergency | Clues | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| **Lithium toxicity** | GI upset, worsening tremor, confusion, ataxia, seizures | Hold lithium, notify provider |
| **Serotonin syndrome** | Fever, agitation, diarrhea, tremor, hyperreflexia | Hold med, notify provider |
| **NMS** | High fever, muscle rigidity, altered mental status, unstable VS | Emergency, notify provider |
| **Tardive dyskinesia** | Lip smacking, tongue movements | Report/assess |
| **Acute dystonia** | Neck spasm, eyes rolling upward | Report/treat as ordered |
| **Opioid overdose** | Respiratory depression, pinpoint pupils | Airway/breathing, naloxone |
| **Disulfiram reaction** | N/V, flushing, hypotension, chest pain after alcohol | Medical assessment |

---

# Restraints and Seclusion

Restraints and seclusion are **last resort.** Use only when the patient is an immediate danger to self or others and less restrictive measures have failed.

## Try First

- Calm communication
- Reduce stimulation
- Offer quiet room
- Give choices
- Set limits
- Remove audience
- Call for help
- Offer medication if ordered

## During Restraints

| Nursing Care | Why |
|---|---|
| Airway/breathing | Prevent compromise |
| Circulation | Prevent injury |
| Skin | Prevent breakdown |
| Vitals | Monitor status |
| Toileting | Meet basic needs |
| Food/fluids | Meet basic needs |
| Reassess behavior | Remove ASAP |
| Document | Legal/safety requirement |

<div class="warning">

Restraints are never for punishment, convenience, lack of staffing, or staff frustration.

</div>

# Priority "What Do I Do First?" Chart

| Scenario | First Action |
|---|---|
| Suicidal patient admitted | Search belongings/patient for harmful objects |
| Patient says "I want to die" | Ask directly about suicide |
| Patient has plan and means | Stay with patient and remove harmful objects |
| Patient hears voices | Ask what the voices are saying |
| Patient is violent/agitated | Maintain safety and call for help |
| Patient having panic attack | Stay with patient, reduce stimuli |
| Manic patient not eating | Offer high-calorie finger foods |
| Manic patient intrusive | Set clear limits |
| Suspected abuse | Interview patient alone |
| Child/elder abuse suspected | Report |
| Opioid overdose | Support breathing, naloxone |
| Alcohol withdrawal with high VS | Withdrawal protocol/benzos/seizure precautions |
| Fever + rigidity on antipsychotic | Suspect NMS |
| Vomiting/ataxia on lithium | Suspect toxicity; hold lithium |

# NCLEX Cues

<div class="warning">

<strong>Always priority:</strong> suicide, homicide, violence, overdose, withdrawal, abuse, and command hallucinations — before teaching, coping, or discharge.
<ul>
<li>Ask directly about suicide or homicide — do not hint around it.</li>
<li>Command hallucinations: ask what the voices are saying (content = safety priority).</li>
<li>Do not leave an actively suicidal patient alone.</li>
<li>Abuse: interview alone; report child/elder/vulnerable adult abuse — suspicion is enough.</li>
<li>Alcohol withdrawal/DTs: seizure risk; medical emergency.</li>
</ul>

</div>

<div class="note">

<strong>Situational responses:</strong>
<ul>
<li>Delusions: do not argue or agree — present reality calmly, focus on feelings.</li>
<li>Panic: stay with the patient, reduce stimulation, use short directions.</li>
<li>Mania: decrease stimulation, set limits, provide finger foods and fluids.</li>
<li>Restraints/seclusion: last resort only, never for punishment or convenience.</li>
</ul>

</div>

---

# Final Takeaway

<div class="tip">

For crisis and safety questions, ask:

**1. Is anyone in immediate danger?** **2. Is this suicide, homicide, violence, abuse, overdose, or withdrawal?** **3. Do I need to ask directly about plan, means, voices, or weapons?** **4. Do I need to stay with the patient or remove harmful objects?** **5. What is the least restrictive way to keep everyone safe?**

</div>
