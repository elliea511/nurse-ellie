# Abuse, Trauma, and Suicide

<div class="note" markdown="1">

This section is very NCLEX-heavy because it is all about **safety, direct assessment, mandatory reporting, therapeutic communication, and protecting the patient from harm.**

Big idea: **believe the patient, assess safety directly, get the patient alone if abuse is suspected, and never leave an actively suicidal patient alone.**

</div>

---

## Big Picture

Abuse, trauma, and suicide are crisis situations.

The nurse’s priorities are:

| Priority                          | What It Means                                                  |
| --------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Safety first**                  | Protect the patient from self-harm, violence, or further abuse |
| **Believe the patient**           | Respond supportively and nonjudgmentally                       |
| **Assess directly**               | Ask clear questions about suicide, abuse, threats, and safety  |
| **Separate safely**               | Get the patient alone if abuse or trafficking is suspected     |
| **Report when required**          | Follow mandatory reporting laws and facility policy            |
| **Document carefully**            | Use the patient’s exact words when possible                    |
| **Use therapeutic communication** | Calm, simple, supportive responses                             |

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Safety is always the first priority when abuse, suicide, homicide, or violence is possible.

</div>

---

# Crisis Intervention Basics

A crisis happens when a person cannot cope with a stressful or dangerous event using their usual coping skills.

Crisis situations may include:

* Suicidal thoughts
* Self-harm
* Homicidal thoughts
* Abuse
* Domestic violence
* Sexual assault
* Human trafficking
* Traumatic event
* Severe anxiety or panic
* Violent/aggressive behavior
* Grief after sudden loss

---

## Crisis Nursing Priorities

| Priority                        | Nursing Action                                                          |
| ------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Assess risk**                 | Ask about suicide, homicide, abuse, violence, and safety                |
| **Remove threats**              | Remove weapons, harmful objects, unsafe visitors, or triggering stimuli |
| **Stay calm**                   | Calm nurse = less escalation                                            |
| **Stay with patient if unsafe** | Do not leave actively suicidal or severely distressed patient alone     |
| **Call for help early**         | Use team support before situation worsens                               |
| **Use short statements**        | Crisis patients may not process long explanations                       |
| **Follow policy**               | Use facility safety protocols, reporting rules, and chain of command    |

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

Crisis questions usually test: <strong>Is the patient safe right now?</strong>
If not, safety comes before teaching, coping, or long-term planning.

</div>

---

# Therapeutic Communication in Crisis

Crisis communication should be calm, simple, and supportive.

## Helpful Responses

| Therapeutic Response                       | Why It Helps                |
| ------------------------------------------ | --------------------------- |
| “I am here with you.”                      | Offers support              |
| “You are safe right now.”                  | Helps reduce fear           |
| “Tell me what happened.”                   | Encourages expression       |
| “What feels most overwhelming?”            | Focuses on current crisis   |
| “Are you thinking about hurting yourself?” | Direct suicide assessment   |
| “Who can we call for support?”             | Identifies support system   |
| “Let’s focus on the next step.”            | Helps patient feel grounded |

---

## Avoid These Responses

| Avoid Saying                       | Why                                         |
| ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| “Calm down.”                       | Can feel dismissive                         |
| “Everything happens for a reason.” | Not therapeutic during trauma               |
| “I know exactly how you feel.”     | You do not know exactly                     |
| “At least it was not worse.”       | Minimizes the trauma                        |
| “Why didn’t you leave?”            | Sounds blaming                              |
| “You should be over this.”         | Judgmental                                  |
| “I promise I won’t tell anyone.”   | Unsafe and not legal if reporting is needed |

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

<strong>Never promise secrecy</strong> if the patient reports abuse, suicide plans, homicide threats, or danger to self/others.

</div>

---

# Trauma-Informed Care

Trauma-informed care means the nurse understands that trauma can affect how a patient thinks, feels, communicates, and reacts.

The patient may appear:

* Fearful
* Angry
* Withdrawn
* Distrustful
* Tearful
* Numb
* Hypervigilant
* Easily startled
* Unable to remember details clearly
* Hesitant to talk

This does **not** mean the patient is lying.

Trauma affects memory, emotions, and behavior.

---

## Trauma-Informed Nursing Care

| Nursing Action               | Why It Matters             |
| ---------------------------- | -------------------------- |
| Explain before touching      | Prevents feeling powerless |
| Ask permission when possible | Gives patient control      |
| Offer choices                | Helps restore control      |
| Speak calmly                 | Reduces fear               |
| Avoid judgment               | Builds trust               |
| Reduce stimulation           | Helps patient feel safer   |
| Validate feelings            | Shows support              |
| Respect privacy              | Protects dignity           |
| Do not force details         | Patient may not be ready   |

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

With trauma, the nurse should support safety and control.
Do not pressure the patient to give every detail immediately.

</div>

---

# Abuse Overview

Abuse is mistreatment that causes physical, emotional, sexual, financial, or psychological harm.

Abuse can happen to:

* Children
* Adults
* Older adults
* Intimate partners
* Disabled individuals
* Dependent adults
* Patients in care facilities

---

## Types of Abuse

| Type                         | Meaning                              | Examples                                                |
| ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Physical abuse**           | Intentional bodily harm              | Hitting, burning, pushing, choking                      |
| **Emotional abuse**          | Damaging self-worth or mental health | Threats, humiliation, isolation, intimidation           |
| **Sexual abuse**             | Any unwanted sexual contact          | Assault, coercion, exploitation                         |
| **Financial abuse**          | Misusing money/resources             | Stealing money, forcing signature, controlling finances |
| **Neglect**                  | Failure to meet basic needs          | No food, hygiene, meds, supervision, safe shelter       |
| **Verbal abuse**             | Harmful spoken words                 | Insults, threats, yelling                               |
| **Spiritual/cultural abuse** | Controlling beliefs or practices     | Preventing worship/cultural expression                  |

---

# General Abuse Assessment

## Possible Abuse Clues

| Finding                                   | Why It Matters                     |
| ----------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Injuries in different stages of healing   | May show repeated trauma           |
| Delay in seeking care                     | Patient may be afraid              |
| Story does not match injury               | Possible abuse                     |
| Patient seems fearful of visitor          | Visitor may be controlling         |
| Visitor answers for patient               | Patient may not be free to speak   |
| Patient avoids eye contact                | Fear/shame/trauma response         |
| Frequent ED visits                        | Ongoing unsafe situation           |
| Poor hygiene or malnutrition              | Possible neglect                   |
| Depression, anxiety, or suicidal thoughts | Abuse increases mental health risk |

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

If abuse is suspected, try to speak with the patient alone in a safe and private way.

</div>

---

## What the Nurse Should Do

| Nursing Action                                | Why                                                                |
| --------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Separate patient from suspected abuser safely | Patient may not speak honestly in front of abuser                  |
| Ask direct, calm questions                    | Helps identify danger                                              |
| Believe the patient                           | Reduces shame and fear                                             |
| Provide privacy                               | Protects safety and dignity                                        |
| Document objective findings                   | Legal and clinical importance                                      |
| Use exact patient quotes                      | Avoids changing meaning                                            |
| Notify appropriate team members               | Social work, provider, case manager, SANE, authorities if required |
| Follow mandatory reporting laws               | Required for child/elder/vulnerable adult abuse                    |

---

## What the Nurse Should NOT Do

| Avoid                                         | Why                               |
| --------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| Confronting the suspected abuser              | Can increase danger               |
| Asking “Why didn’t you leave?”                | Blames the victim                 |
| Promising secrecy                             | Unsafe and may be illegal         |
| Pressuring the patient to report before ready | Can remove control                |
| Giving personal opinions                      | Not therapeutic                   |
| Telling the patient what they “must” do       | Patient needs support and options |
| Using family as translator                    | Unsafe if abuse is involved       |

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

The nurse’s job is not to investigate like police.
The nurse’s job is to assess, protect, document, report when required, and connect the patient with help.

</div>

---

# Mandatory Reporting

Mandatory reporting means the nurse is legally required to report suspected abuse in certain situations.

Usually report suspected abuse involving:

| Patient Group                                     | Reporting Required?                                                                     |
| ------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Children**                                      | Yes                                                                                     |
| **Older adults**                                  | Yes                                                                                     |
| **Vulnerable/dependent adults**                   | Yes                                                                                     |
| **Disabled adults unable to protect self**        | Yes                                                                                     |
| **Adults experiencing intimate partner violence** | Depends on state/facility policy unless weapon/serious injury/vulnerable status applies |

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

For NCLEX: suspected child abuse, elder abuse, and abuse of vulnerable adults must be reported.

</div>

---

## Documentation for Abuse

Document:

* Patient’s exact words
* Objective injury descriptions
* Location, size, shape, and color of injuries
* Patient behavior
* Who was present
* Patient safety concerns
* Notifications made
* Interventions performed
* Referrals/resources provided

Avoid:

* “Patient was beaten”
* “The husband is abusive”
* “The parent is lying”
* “Patient is dramatic”

Better:

* “Patient states, ‘My husband hit me with a closed fist.’”
* “Purple bruise noted to left upper arm, approximately 4 cm x 3 cm.”
* “Patient became tearful when visitor entered room.”

---

# Domestic Violence / Intimate Partner Violence

Domestic violence is abuse by a current or former partner.

It may involve:

* Physical violence
* Threats
* Emotional control
* Sexual coercion
* Financial control
* Isolation from friends/family
* Stalking
* Threatening children or pets

---

## Cycle of Violence

| Phase                         | What Happens                                                          |
| ----------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Tension-building**          | Stress, arguing, victim may feel like they are “walking on eggshells” |
| **Acute battering/explosion** | Abuse occurs                                                          |
| **Honeymoon/reconciliation**  | Abuser apologizes, promises change, may give gifts                    |
| **Calm**                      | Temporary peace before tension builds again                           |

<div class="memory" markdown="1">

Cycle of violence: <strong>Tension → Explosion → Honeymoon → Calm → repeats</strong>

</div>

---

## Domestic Violence Assessment

Ask when the patient is alone:

* “Do you feel safe at home?”
* “Has anyone hit, kicked, choked, or hurt you?”
* “Has anyone threatened you?”
* “Are you afraid of your partner?”
* “Does anyone control where you go or who you talk to?”
* “Do you have a safe place to go?”

---

## Nursing Care for Domestic Violence

| Nursing Action                       | Why                                  |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------ |
| Separate patient from partner safely | Allows honest disclosure             |
| Assess immediate danger              | Determines urgency                   |
| Validate the patient                 | Reduces shame                        |
| Provide resources                    | Shelters, hotlines, social work      |
| Help develop safety plan             | Prepares for danger                  |
| Document carefully                   | Legal/clinical importance            |
| Respect patient decisions            | Leaving can be dangerous and complex |

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

Do not tell the patient, “You need to leave now.”
Instead, assess safety, provide resources, and help with a safety plan.

</div>

---

# Child Abuse

Child abuse may be physical, emotional, sexual, or neglect.

Children may not disclose abuse clearly, so the nurse must watch for clues.

---

## Child Abuse Clues

| Finding                              | Possible Concern                       |
| ------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------- |
| Bruises in unusual places            | Back, buttocks, ears, neck, upper arms |
| Injuries in different healing stages | Repeated abuse                         |
| Burns with clear patterns            | Cigarette burns, immersion burns       |
| Fractures inconsistent with history  | Possible physical abuse                |
| Fear of parent/caregiver             | Safety concern                         |
| Child seems overly watchful          | Hypervigilance                         |
| Poor hygiene/malnutrition            | Neglect                                |
| Sexualized behavior beyond age level | Possible sexual abuse                  |
| Frequent absences from school        | Possible neglect/abuse                 |

---

## Child Abuse Nursing Priorities

1. **Ensure immediate safety**
2. **Assess injuries**
3. **Report suspected abuse**
4. **Document objectively**
5. **Do not confront caregiver**
6. **Provide emotional support**
7. **Follow facility policy**

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

You do not need proof to report suspected child abuse.
Suspicion is enough.

</div>

---

## Therapeutic Communication With Children

| Do                                         | Avoid                  |
| ------------------------------------------ | ---------------------- |
| Use simple language                        | Leading questions      |
| Allow the child to speak freely            | Pressuring for details |
| Stay calm                                  | Acting shocked         |
| Reassure the child they are not in trouble | Blaming language       |
| Use exact words when documenting           | Changing their wording |

Helpful response:

“I’m glad you told me. You are not in trouble.”

---

# Elder Abuse

Elder abuse can happen at home, in facilities, or by caregivers/family.

Types include:

* Physical abuse
* Emotional abuse
* Sexual abuse
* Financial exploitation
* Neglect
* Abandonment

---

## Elder Abuse Clues

| Finding                       | Possible Concern                   |
| ----------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Poor hygiene                  | Neglect                            |
| Pressure injuries             | Neglect or immobility without care |
| Dehydration/malnutrition      | Neglect                            |
| Missing medications           | Neglect or financial abuse         |
| Unexplained bruises/fractures | Physical abuse                     |
| Fearful around caregiver      | Abuse/control                      |
| Caregiver refuses privacy     | Controlling behavior               |
| Sudden financial problems     | Financial exploitation             |
| Repeated injuries             | Ongoing abuse                      |

---

## Nursing Care for Elder Abuse

| Nursing Action                           | Why                            |
| ---------------------------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Speak with older adult alone if possible | Allows disclosure              |
| Assess physical and emotional safety     | Determines danger              |
| Document injuries objectively            | Supports care/reporting        |
| Report suspected abuse                   | Required for vulnerable adults |
| Involve social work/case management      | Safe placement/resources       |
| Avoid confronting caregiver              | Can worsen danger              |

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

Elder abuse and vulnerable adult abuse are mandatory reporting situations.

</div>

---

# Sexual Assault

Sexual assault care must be trauma-informed, private, and patient-centered.

The patient may feel:

* Fear
* Shame
* Guilt
* Anger
* Numbness
* Embarrassment
* Disbelief
* Loss of control

The nurse should restore as much control as possible.

---

## Sexual Assault Nursing Priorities

| Priority              | Nursing Action                                            |
| --------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Safety**            | Make sure patient is safe from attacker                   |
| **Medical stability** | Treat injuries and urgent needs                           |
| **Emotional support** | Use calm, nonjudgmental communication                     |
| **Preserve evidence** | Avoid actions that destroy evidence if patient wants exam |
| **SANE referral**     | Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner if available                |
| **Consent**           | Patient chooses exam/treatment options                    |
| **Prophylaxis**       | STI/pregnancy prevention per orders                       |
| **Resources**         | Advocacy, counseling, follow-up care                      |

<div class="memory" markdown="1">

SANE = Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner.

</div>

---

## Evidence Preservation Teaching

If the patient may want evidence collected, teach them to avoid:

* Showering
* Bathing
* Changing clothes
* Brushing teeth
* Eating/drinking if oral assault occurred
* Urinating if possible
* Cleaning wounds before evidence collection

If clothes are removed, place each item in a separate paper bag per facility policy.

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

Use paper bags, not plastic bags, for evidence clothing because plastic can trap moisture and damage evidence.

</div>

---

## Therapeutic Communication After Sexual Assault

Helpful responses:

* “I believe you.”
* “You are safe here.”
* “This was not your fault.”
* “You did the right thing coming in.”
* “You are in control of what happens next.”
* “I can explain each step before we do anything.”

Avoid:

* “Why were you there?”
* “Why didn’t you fight back?”
* “Are you sure?”
* “You should have called sooner.”
* “Everything will be fine.”

---

# Human Trafficking

Human trafficking may involve forced labor, forced sex, or both.

Victims may not identify themselves as victims.

They may be afraid because of threats, violence, drug dependence, immigration concerns, shame, or fear for family.

---

## Human Trafficking Clues

| Finding                                 | Possible Concern                 |
| --------------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Controlling companion answers questions | Patient may not be free to speak |
| Patient avoids eye contact              | Fear/control                     |
| Older boyfriend/travel partner          | Possible exploitation            |
| No personal ID/documents                | Controlled by trafficker         |
| Tattoos/branding                        | Ownership/control marker         |
| Multiple injuries                       | Abuse                            |
| History of running away/homelessness    | Vulnerability                    |
| Addiction                               | Control mechanism or coping      |
| Panic, fear, or cowering                | Trauma response                  |
| Inconsistent story                      | Fear or coached answers          |
| Suicidal thoughts                       | Severe distress                  |

---

## Nursing Action for Suspected Trafficking

| Nursing Action               | Why                                                     |
| ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Get patient alone safely     | Patient may not disclose in front of trafficker         |
| Use professional interpreter | Companion may be involved                               |
| Ask direct safety questions  | Identifies control/danger                               |
| Do not confront trafficker   | May increase risk                                       |
| Notify appropriate team      | Provider, social work, security, authorities per policy |
| Provide resources            | Hotline, shelter, advocacy                              |
| Document objectively         | Supports care and safety                                |

---

## Questions to Ask

* “Are you able to leave where you live?”
* “Has anyone forced you to do work or sexual acts?”
* “Do you have control of your ID or documents?”
* “Do you feel safe with the person who came with you?”
* “Does anyone threaten you or your family?”
* “Can you come and go as you please?”

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

NCLEX trafficking clue: <strong>controlling companion + fearful patient + cannot speak alone + injuries or poor health.</strong>

</div>

# Suicide Risk

Suicide risk is always a priority.

Ask directly.

Talking about suicide does **not** put the idea in the patient’s head.

---

## Suicide Risk Factors

| Risk Factor                  | Why It Increases Risk                |
| ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Previous suicide attempt     | Strong predictor of future attempt   |
| Specific plan                | Higher chance of action              |
| Access to means              | Patient can carry out plan           |
| Hopelessness                 | Major warning sign                   |
| Substance use                | Decreases impulse control            |
| Depression                   | Commonly linked with suicide         |
| Recent loss                  | Can trigger crisis                   |
| Chronic pain/illness         | Increases hopelessness               |
| Social isolation             | Less support                         |
| Command hallucinations       | Voices may tell patient to harm self |
| Giving away belongings       | May show preparation                 |
| Sudden calm after depression | May indicate decision to attempt     |

---

## Direct Suicide Assessment Questions

Ask clearly:

| Question                                   | What It Assesses   |
| ------------------------------------------ | ------------------ |
| “Are you thinking about killing yourself?” | Suicidal ideation  |
| “Do you have a plan?”                      | Specificity        |
| “What is your plan?”                       | Lethality          |
| “Do you have access to that method?”       | Means              |
| “When were you planning to do this?”       | Immediacy          |
| “Have you tried to hurt yourself before?”  | Previous attempt   |
| “Do you feel safe being alone?”            | Current safety     |
| “What has stopped you so far?”             | Protective factors |

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

The more specific the plan, the higher the risk.

</div>

---

## Highest Suicide Risk

Highest concern:

| Finding                | Why                                |
| ---------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Specific plan          | Patient has thought through method |
| Access to means        | Patient can act now                |
| Time chosen            | Imminent risk                      |
| Previous attempt       | Major risk factor                  |
| Command hallucinations | Voices may instruct harm           |
| Sudden calmness        | May signal final decision          |
| Giving away belongings | Preparation                        |
| Substance use          | Less impulse control               |

---

# Suicide Precautions

If the patient is actively suicidal:

1. **Stay with the patient**
2. **Remove harmful objects**
3. **Notify provider/crisis team**
4. **Initiate suicide precautions**
5. **Use 1:1 observation if ordered**
6. **Place in safe room if available**
7. **Document exact statements and interventions**

---

## Environmental Safety

Remove or secure:

* Belts
* Shoelaces
* Cords
* Plastic bags
* Razors
* Sharp objects
* Glass
* Medications
* Weapons
* Long tubing if unsafe
* Unnecessary equipment

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

Do not leave an actively suicidal patient alone.

</div>

---

## Suicide Communication Examples

| Patient Says                                | Best Nurse Response                                                           |
| ------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| “I just want to disappear.”                 | “Are you thinking about killing yourself?”                                    |
| “My family would be better off without me.” | “Are you having thoughts of harming yourself?”                                |
| “I already know how I would do it.”         | “What is your plan?”                                                          |
| “Promise you won’t tell anyone.”            | “I cannot promise secrecy if your safety is at risk.”                         |
| “I feel calm now. I’m fine.”                | “I still need to make sure you are safe. Are you having thoughts of suicide?” |

---

# Self-Harm vs Suicide

Self-harm and suicide are not always the same, but both require safety assessment.

| Term                  | Meaning                                                          |
| --------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Self-harm**         | Intentional injury to self, may or may not include intent to die |
| **Suicide attempt**   | Self-injury with intent to die                                   |
| **Suicidal ideation** | Thoughts of killing oneself                                      |
| **Suicide plan**      | Specific method or plan                                          |
| **Suicide intent**    | Desire or decision to act                                        |

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

Always assess whether self-harm included intent to die.

</div>

---

# Homicide Risk

Homicidal thoughts are also a safety priority.

Ask directly if there are threats toward others.

---

## Homicide Assessment

Ask:

* “Are you thinking about hurting someone else?”
* “Who are you thinking about hurting?”
* “Do you have a plan?”
* “Do you have access to weapons?”
* “Have you harmed this person before?”
* “Where is this person now?”

---

## Nursing Priority

If the patient has a specific threat toward another person:

1. Maintain safety
2. Notify provider/team
3. Follow facility policy
4. Protect potential victim according to policy/law
5. Document exact statements

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

Specific person + specific plan + access to means = high risk.

</div>

---

# Violence and Aggression

Violence risk can happen with intoxication, psychosis, mania, severe anxiety, trauma, withdrawal, or frustration.

---

## Warning Signs

| Warning Sign            |
| ----------------------- |
| Pacing                  |
| Clenched fists          |
| Loud voice              |
| Threats                 |
| Staring                 |
| Agitation               |
| Restlessness            |
| Intoxication            |
| Paranoia                |
| History of violence     |
| Weapon access           |
| Invading personal space |

---

## De-escalation

| Do                              | Avoid                            |
| ------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Stay calm                       | Arguing                          |
| Keep safe distance              | Standing too close               |
| Keep exit accessible            | Blocking doorway                 |
| Use short statements            | Long lectures                    |
| Offer choices                   | Power struggles                  |
| Set clear limits                | Threatening                      |
| Call for help early             | Managing alone                   |
| Remove audience                 | Letting crowd escalate situation |
| Use low-stimulation environment | Loud/crowded area                |

---

## Example Limit-Setting

Patient says:
“I’m about to punch someone if I don’t get out of here.”

Therapeutic response:
“I can see you are angry. I cannot allow anyone to be hurt. You may sit in the quiet room or walk with staff in the hallway.”

---

# Restraints and Seclusion in Crisis

Restraints and seclusion are last resort interventions.

Use only when:

* Patient is an immediate danger to self
* Patient is an immediate danger to others
* Less restrictive measures failed

Do **not** use for:

* Punishment
* Convenience
* Staff frustration
* Lack of staffing

---

## Before Restraints

Try:

* Verbal de-escalation
* Reduce stimulation
* Offer quiet room
* Offer choices
* Set limits
* Remove audience
* Call for help
* Offer ordered medication

---

## During Restraints

Nursing care includes:

| Assessment       | Why                            |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Airway/breathing | Prevent respiratory compromise |
| Circulation      | Prevent injury                 |
| Skin             | Prevent breakdown              |
| Vital signs      | Monitor physical status        |
| Mental status    | Reassess safety                |
| Food/fluids      | Meet basic needs               |
| Toileting        | Maintain dignity               |
| Range of motion  | Prevent injury                 |
| Behavior         | Remove ASAP when safe          |

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

Restraints must be discontinued as soon as the patient is safe.

</div>

# Grief After Trauma or Suicide

Grief is a normal response to loss, but traumatic loss may be more intense.

The patient may experience:

* Shock
* Numbness
* Anger
* Guilt
* Sadness
* Fear
* Sleep problems
* Difficulty concentrating
* Questioning “why”
* Feeling disconnected

---

## Nursing Care for Grief

| Nursing Action             | Why                                        |
| -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Stay present               | Shows support                              |
| Use silence                | Allows processing                          |
| Encourage expression       | Helps patient verbalize grief              |
| Avoid false reassurance    | Does not minimize pain                     |
| Assess suicide risk        | Grief can increase risk                    |
| Offer support resources    | Counseling, clergy, family, support groups |
| Respect cultural practices | Grief is expressed differently             |

---

## Therapeutic Grief Responses

| Patient Says                     | Best Response                                           |
| -------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| “I can’t believe this happened.” | “This feels unreal right now.”                          |
| “It’s my fault.”                 | “You are feeling responsible. Tell me more about that.” |
| “Why did this happen?”           | “This is very painful and hard to understand.”          |
| “I don’t want to go on.”         | “Are you thinking about killing yourself?”              |

---

# Patient Education: Safety Plan

A safety plan helps the patient know what to do when they feel unsafe.

Include:

| Safety Plan Part  | Examples                                                     |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Warning signs     | “I stop sleeping,” “I isolate,” “I start giving things away” |
| Coping skills     | Deep breathing, grounding, music, walking                    |
| People to contact | Friend, family member, sponsor                               |
| Professional help | Provider, therapist, crisis line                             |
| Safe places       | ED, trusted friend’s home, shelter                           |
| Remove means      | Secure meds, weapons, sharps                                 |
| Emergency plan    | Call emergency services or go to ED                          |

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

A safety plan should be specific.
“Call someone if sad” is too vague.

</div>

## Suicide Priority Chart

| Situation                                           | First Nursing Action                         |
| --------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Patient says “I want to die”                        | Ask directly about suicidal thoughts         |
| Patient has plan and means                          | Stay with patient and remove harmful objects |

| Patient gives belongings away                       | Assess suicide risk                          |
