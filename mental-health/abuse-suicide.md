# Abuse & Suicide

<p class="mh-subtitle">ATI-style review for suicidal patients, non-suicidal self-injury, abuse types, mandated reporting, safety planning, and nursing priorities</p>

Suicide and abuse questions are **safety-first** questions. Nursing priorities include **direct assessment, environmental safety, observation, mandatory reporting, private interviews, objective documentation, preserving evidence, support systems, and nonjudgmental therapeutic communication.**

### Suicide Safety

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Key Terms</strong>

- **Suicidal ideation:** thinking about killing oneself.
- **Suicide attempt:** self-injurious behavior with intention of death.
- **Suicide:** intentional act of killing oneself by any means.
- **Non-suicidal self-injury:** intentional body tissue damage without conscious suicidal intent.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Risk Factors</strong>

- Previous attempt
- Family history
- Use of drugs/alcohol
- Impulsivity
- Diagnosed mental illness
- Depression or hopelessness
- Limited social supports
- Access to lethal means
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Warning Signs</strong>

- Giving things away
- Statements like “I won’t be a bother much longer”
- Sudden switch from sad to happy mood
- Verbal or nonverbal clues
- Overt statements
- Covert statements
- Withdrawal from support system
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority Assessment</strong>

- Ask directly about suicidal thoughts.
- Assess for a plan.
- Assess ability to follow through.
- Assess lethality of the plan.
- Ask about history of attempts.
- Ask about family history.
- Assess substance use.
- Assess social supports.
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Treatment Options</strong>

- Pharmacotherapy for comorbid disorders
- Brain stimulation therapy to decrease ideation
- Psychological therapies
- Advanced practice roles
- Safety planning
- Support system involvement
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Nursing Interventions</strong>

- Ensure preventive measures.
- Maintain ordered observation level.
- Remove harmful objects.
- Communicate risk to team.
- Use therapeutic communication.
- Do not leave high-risk patient alone.
- Document assessment and safety actions.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Education</strong>

- Tell staff immediately if suicidal thoughts increase.
- Use coping plan and support contacts.
- Avoid drugs/alcohol because they increase impulsivity.
- Keep therapy and medication appointments.
- Remove or secure lethal means at discharge planning.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Complications / Safety Risks</strong>

- Suicide attempt
- Completed suicide
- Impulsive harm
- Substance-related disinhibition
- Poor support system
- Access to lethal methods
- Sudden improvement after depression that may indicate decision to act
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> On admission after a suicide attempt, search the client and belongings for harmful objects with the client present.
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Lethality:</strong> A self-inflicted gunshot wound is considered a high-lethality method compared with pills, wrist cutting, or gas inhalation examples from class.
</div>

### Non-Suicidal Self-Injury

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>What It Is</strong>

- Intentional damage to one’s own body tissue.
- No conscious suicidal intent.
- Not socially or culturally sanctioned.
- Often used to cope with emotional distress.
- Still requires safety assessment.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Assessment</strong>

- Assess injuries.
- Assess suicidal ideation separately.
- Look for triggers.
- Assess coping skills.
- Assess support system.
- Assess communication patterns.
- Be aware of your own transference/self-assessment.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Nursing Diagnoses</strong>

- Risk for self-mutilation
- Self-mutilation
- Ineffective coping
- Impaired social support
- Risk for suicide if suicidal intent is present
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Outcomes</strong>

- Improved coping
- Improved social support
- Treatment adherence
- Improved communication
- Decreased self-injury episodes
- Increased use of safe coping strategies
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Implementation</strong>

- Care for injuries.
- Establish therapeutic alliance.
- Use empathy.
- Teach coping skills.
- Promote reflective dialogue.
- Help identify triggers.
- Encourage safer communication of distress.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Evaluation</strong>

- Continue evaluating communication.
- Continue evaluating perception.
- Assess if coping skills are being used.
- Monitor injury pattern/frequency.
- Reassess suicidal ideation if risk changes.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Education</strong>

- Identify triggers early.
- Use coping skills before urges escalate.
- Tell staff/support person when urges increase.
- Care for wounds as instructed.
- Continue therapy.
- Self-injury is not “attention seeking” to dismiss; it is a sign of distress needing care.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Safety Risks</strong>

- Infection
- Worsening injury
- Escalation to suicidal behavior
- Shame/secrecy
- Poor coping cycle
- Coexisting depression, trauma, or personality disorder symptoms
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Always separate non-suicidal self-injury from suicidal intent by assessing directly. Do not assume there is no suicide risk.
</div>

</div>

### Abuse Types

<div class="detail-table">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Type</th>
        <th>What It Means</th>
        <th>Assessment Cues</th>
        <th>Nursing Focus</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Physical Abuse</strong></td>
        <td>Physical harm or harsh punishment. Victim may not have done anything wrong.</td>
        <td>Bruising, injuries inconsistent with explanation, fearfulness, repeated injuries.</td>
        <td>Assess safety, document objectively, report when required, support without blame.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Sexual Abuse</strong></td>
        <td>Using another person unwillingly for sexual gratification.</td>
        <td>Trauma symptoms, genital injuries, fear, shame, withdrawal, behavioral changes.</td>
        <td>Private interview, preserve evidence, nonjudgmental support, follow reporting law.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Emotional Abuse</strong></td>
        <td>Words/behaviors intended to harm self-worth or well-being.</td>
        <td>Low self-esteem, anxiety, depression, fearfulness, shame.</td>
        <td>Validate feelings, assess safety, support resources, document statements.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Neglect</strong></td>
        <td>Failure to provide basic necessities: food, clothing, supervision, shelter.</td>
        <td>Poor hygiene, malnutrition, untreated needs, unsafe environment.</td>
        <td>Assess physical data, report concerns, connect resources.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Medical Neglect</strong></td>
        <td>Lack of needed medications, doctor visits, immunizations, or medical care.</td>
        <td>Missed appointments, untreated conditions, lack of meds, poor disease control.</td>
        <td>Assess access/barriers, document, report vulnerable populations as required.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Economic / Financial Abuse</strong></td>
        <td>No access to money or financial resources; keeps victim tied to abuser.</td>
        <td>Unable to access money, pension checks taken, forced to sign over property.</td>
        <td>Assess safety/resources, social work referral, legal/community resources.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Exploitation</strong></td>
        <td>Using another person for personal profit.</td>
        <td>Financial control, stolen resources, forced work or dependence.</td>
        <td>Report if vulnerable person, document, connect support services.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Rape Trauma Syndrome</strong></td>
        <td>Form of PTSD that may last for weeks following rape.</td>
        <td>Anxiety, fear, trauma symptoms, sleep problems, avoidance, distress.</td>
        <td>Be empathetic and nonjudgmental. Preserve evidence and support medical/legal options.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Important Abuse Facts</strong>

- Abuse occurs in every segment of society.
- Vulnerable people, especially children and elders, are common victims.
- The younger the victim, the fewer resources they may have.
- Substance abuse in the family increases abuse incidence.
- Abuse may be sexual, verbal, physical, emotional, or financial.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Child Abuse</strong>

- Children are frequent victims of abuse.
- People may be reluctant to interfere in “family business.”
- Emotional abuse is just as damaging as physical abuse.
- Comorbidities: anxiety, depression, PTSD, suicide risk.
- May cause developmental delays.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Teen Abuse</strong>

- Teen-on-teen abuse is serious.
- Gangs/street crimes can increase abuse exposure.
- Teens may fight back at home, leading to more severe injuries.
- Sibling abuse can be unrecognized.
- Sexual abuse is common in teen years.
- Teens may run away, turn to crime, or prostitution for survival.
- Comorbidities: poor impulse control and dissociative disorders.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Elder Abuse</strong>

- Increasing problem.
- Families caring for elders at home may burn out.
- Elder may be threatened with abandonment.
- Financial abuse may include taking pension checks or forcing property transfer.
- Health may be neglected due to cost.
- Be suspicious if elder starts missing appointments.
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> With suspected abuse, interview the patient alone whenever possible.
</div>

</div>

### Reporting + Interventions

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Mandatory Reporting</strong>

- Suspected abuse of children must be reported by healthcare professionals.
- Suspected abuse of the elderly must be reported by healthcare professionals.
- Licensure can be revoked or fines imposed if legal authorities believe you should have suspected abuse and failed to report.
- Follow facility policy and state law.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Interview Priorities</strong>

- Interview suspected victim alone.
- Use a private room.
- Use open-ended questions.
- Use attentive listening.
- Do not pressure the victim to share more than they are ready to discuss.
- Do not blame or judge the victim or either party.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Assessment Priorities</strong>

- Assess client safety.
- Assess anxiety level.
- Assess suicide risk.
- Ask about weapons in the home.
- Assess for substance abuse.
- Be alert when injury explanation does not make sense.
- Assess for support outside the family.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Documentation</strong>

- Document all findings thoroughly.
- Document objective data.
- Document patient statements in quotes when possible.
- Follow guidelines for preserving evidence if charges may be pressed.
- Report findings to charge nurse/provider per policy.
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Safety Plan Items</strong>

- Important papers
- Money
- Duplicate car keys
- Duplicate house keys
- Trusted person nearby
- Safe place to go
- Emergency phone numbers
- Support group or safe house information
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>What Not to Do</strong>

- Do not blame the victim.
- Do not be judgmental.
- Do not force information the victim is not ready to discuss.
- Do not encourage victim to leave immediately unless child/elder protection requires action or there is immediate danger.
- Do not ignore inconsistent injury explanations.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Priority With Older Adult Example</strong>

- First collect physical data.
- Document findings and statements.
- Maintain dignity while assessing.
- Communicate findings to charge nurse.
- Social work/resources come after initial data collection and reporting chain.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Therapeutic Communication</strong>

- Silence
- Active listening
- Clarifying techniques
- Open-ended questions
- Empathy
- Nonjudgmental statements
- Private setting
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> If an older client has unexplained weight loss and extensive bruising, collect physical data and communicate findings first.
</div>

<div class="mh-columns wide-left" markdown="1">

<div class="key-findings" markdown="1">
<h3>📋 Abuse & Suicide Big Picture</h3>

- Suicide care starts with direct assessment of thoughts, plan, means, intent, and past attempts.
- Search the patient and belongings for harmful objects after a suicide attempt.
- Non-suicidal self-injury still requires direct safety assessment.
- Abuse victims should be interviewed alone whenever possible.
- Suspected child and elder abuse must be reported.
- Documentation should be objective, thorough, and include patient statements.
- Safety planning includes money, important papers, duplicate keys, and trusted support.
</div>

<div class="mh-col" markdown="1">

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

- Suicidal thoughts with plan, intent, or means
- Recent suicide attempt
- Command hallucinations to self-harm or harm others
- Child abuse suspicion
- Elder abuse suspicion
- Unsafe home situation with immediate danger
- Serious unexplained injuries
- Sexual assault requiring evidence preservation
</div>

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">
<h3>NCLEX Tip</h3>

Abuse and suicide questions usually ask what keeps the patient safe first. Collect data, remove danger, report when required, and document objectively.
</div>

<div class="mh-columns" markdown="1">

<div class="quick-review" markdown="1">
<h3>Quick Review</h3>

1. Ask directly about suicidal thoughts, plan, means, and intent.
2. Search belongings and patient with the patient present after suicide attempt/admission.
3. Maintain environmental safety and ordered observation.
4. Non-suicidal self-injury still needs a suicide assessment.
5. Interview suspected abuse victims alone.
6. Suspected child and elder abuse must be reported.
7. Document objectively and preserve evidence when needed.
</div>

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">
<h3>Do Not Miss</h3>

- Sudden mood improvement after severe depression can be a warning sign.
- Giving things away is a warning sign.
- Do not promise secrecy if safety is involved.
- Do not pressure abuse victims to disclose more than they are ready to share.
- Do not encourage leaving immediately unless immediate danger or child/elder protection requires action.
- Ask about weapons in the home.
- For suspected abuse, collect data and report through the proper chain.
</div>
