# Addiction & Substance Use

<p class="mh-subtitle">ATI-style review for substance use, overdose, withdrawal, alcohol use disorder, opioids, sedatives, stimulants, tobacco, gambling, and nursing safety</p>

Addictive disorders involve repeated substance use or behaviors that continue despite harm. Nursing priorities include **safety, withdrawal monitoring, respiratory support, seizure precautions, nonjudgmental assessment, relapse prevention, support systems, and treatment of coexisting mental health disorders.**

### Addiction Basics

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>What It Is</strong>

- Addiction is a chronic medical condition.
- Substance use disorder can lead to withdrawal.
- The patient may continue use despite harm.
- Process addictions can involve gambling, shopping, sex, or other behaviors.
- Coexisting mental health disorders are common.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Key Definitions</strong>

- **Addiction:** chronic medical condition.
- **Intoxication:** using a substance to excess.
- **Tolerance:** needing a higher dose to achieve the original effect.
- **Withdrawal:** symptoms that occur when a person stops using a substance.
- **Codependence:** being overly responsible for others while ignoring one’s own needs.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Substances / Behaviors</strong>

- Alcohol
- Caffeine
- Cannabis
- Hallucinogens
- Inhalants
- Opioids
- Sedative-hypnotics
- Stimulants
- Tobacco
- Process addictions: gambling, shopping, sex, etc.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Dual Diagnosis</strong>

- Assess for coexisting psychiatric illness.
- Patient may self-medicate mental health symptoms.
- Depression, anxiety, schizophrenia, and personality disorders may coexist with substance use.
- Treating only the substance use without addressing mental health can increase relapse risk.
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>General Nursing Interventions</strong>

- Provide for client safety.
- Monitor for symptoms of withdrawal.
- Maintain support system.
- Explore interest in a 12-step program.
- Screen for other substances.
- Assess for coexisting psychiatric illness.
- Monitor vital signs and mental status.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Education</strong>

- Withdrawal can be dangerous and may need medical supervision.
- Avoid mixing substances.
- Support programs can help maintain recovery.
- Relapse prevention requires coping skills and support.
- Mental health symptoms should be treated too.
- Take prescribed medications only as directed.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Complications / Safety Risks</strong>

- Overdose
- Respiratory depression
- Withdrawal seizures
- Delirium tremens
- Injury while intoxicated
- Poor nutrition
- Liver disease
- Coexisting depression or psychosis
- Relapse
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Ask about substance use in a nonjudgmental way. Patients often underreport amount used or last time used.

      <tr>

        <td>Assess use, impairment, safety, and coexisting substance use.</td>
        <td>Safety and education.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Hallucinogens</strong></td>
        <td>Profound disturbance in reality.</td>
        <td>Reduce stimuli, protect safety, monitor for psychosis or injury risk.</td>
        <td>Reality disturbance and safety.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td><strong>Inhalants</strong></td>
        <td>Solvents, propellants, thinners, fuels.</td>
        <td>Assess respiratory/cardiac status and exposure risk.</td>
        <td>Airway, breathing, cardiac safety.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Opioid Overdose Priority</strong>

- Promote breathing first.
- Aspirate secretions if needed.
- Insert airway if needed.
- Mechanical ventilation may be needed.
- Naloxone is the opioid antagonist.
- Naloxone may be given intranasal, IM, SQ, or IV as ordered.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Opioid Withdrawal / Maintenance</strong>

- Methadone
- Clonidine
- Buprenorphine/naloxone
- Lofexidine
- Pharmacological maintenance
- Psychotherapies
- Monitor cravings and relapse risk
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Sedative Withdrawal</strong>

- Do not stop abruptly.
- Gradual dose reduction helps prevent seizures.
- Monitor vital signs.
- Implement seizure precautions as ordered.
- Monitor level of consciousness and respiratory status.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Stimulant Withdrawal</strong>

- Group therapy
- Individual therapy
- Possible diazepam for agitation
- Cocaine withdrawal may last 1–2 weeks.
- Depression treatment may begin once withdrawal is complete.
- Bupropion may be used after withdrawal if ordered.
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Opioid overdose = breathing first, then naloxone. Naloxone treats overdose, not routine withdrawal discomfort.
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Sedative-hypnotic withdrawal can cause seizures, so gradual reduction is important.
</div>

</div>

### Alcohol Use Disorder

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>What It Is</strong>

- Alcohol is initially a stimulant but then has depressive effects.
- Alcohol use may be socially accepted, which can hide the problem.
- Main defense mechanism is denial.
- Patient may rationalize problems and blame others.
- Withdrawal can be life-threatening and should be medically supervised.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Class Numbers</strong>

- Legal limit in Alabama: 0.08 g/dL.
- Intoxicated: 0.2 g/dL.
- Death may occur at 0.4 g/dL.
- Withdrawal symptoms may occur within a few hours of stopping.
- Withdrawal symptoms may last up to 10 days.
- Delirium tremens usually occurs within 72 hours of withdrawal.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Comorbidities</strong>

- Depressive disorders
- Schizophrenia
- Personality disorders
- Other substance use
- Nutritional deficiencies
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Alcoholism Cues</strong>

- Denial
- Poor hygiene
- Unexplained injuries
- High tolerance for CNS depressants
- Vitamin/mineral deficiencies
- Secretive drinking
- Drinks any product containing alcohol
- Blackouts
- Violence when intoxicated
- Needs alcohol daily to function
- Unable to cut back or stop alone
</div>

</div>

      </tr>
      <tr>

<div class="detail-grid" markdown="1">

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Alcohol Withdrawal Interventions</strong>

- Screen for other drugs.
- Take vital signs at least every 4 hours.
- Decrease environmental stimuli.
- Push fluids or provide IV fluids as ordered.
- Check corresponding labs.
- Administer B vitamins IM then PO as ordered.
- Librium/chlordiazepoxide may be used and tapered.
- Valium/diazepam may be used for seizures.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Delirium Tremens Treatment</strong>

- Push fluids or IV fluids.
- Librium, Valium, or Ativan as ordered.
- Dose/schedule based on symptom level.
- Seclude only if too agitated and safety requires it.
- Often transferred to med-surg or ICU.
- IV glucose may be needed for hypoglycemia.
- Lasix for ascites if ordered.
- Magnesium sulfate if seizures appear imminent.
- Antiemetics, antidiarrheals, antipsychotics if ordered.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Alcohol Treatment</strong>

- Abstain from alcohol.
- Attend AA.
- AA supports abstinence and does not allow rationalization.
- Disulfiram/Antabuse may be used for aversion therapy.
- Patient must be motivated to take Antabuse regularly.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Antabuse Teaching</strong>

- Prevents alcohol from being metabolized.
- Toxins build up if alcohol is taken.
- Causes acute illness with alcohol.
- Symptoms: nausea/vomiting, flushing, vertigo, increased pulse, hypotension, headache, SOB, diaphoresis, chest pain, confusion.
- Reaction may be fatal.
- If patient stops Antabuse, they can drink without ill effect in about 2 weeks.
</div>

</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Alcohol withdrawal can be life-threatening. Watch vital signs, seizures, hallucinations, and delirium tremens.
</div>

<div class="detail-callout" markdown="1">
<strong>Do Not Miss:</strong> Wernicke’s encephalopathy and Korsakoff’s psychosis are linked to thiamine deficiency and treated with IV thiamine.
</div>

- Withdrawal is distressing.
- Behavioral therapy.
- Hypnosis.
- Nicotine replacement therapies.
- Bupropion.
- Varenicline.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Gambling Disorder Treatment</strong>

- Gamblers Anonymous.
- SSRIs may be used.
- Bupropion/Wellbutrin may be used.
- Mood stabilizers may be used.
- Anticonvulsants may be used.
- Assess financial and relationship consequences.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Relapse Prevention</strong>

- Identify triggers.
- Build support system.
- Attend therapy/groups.
- Avoid high-risk people/places.
- Treat coexisting psychiatric illness.
- Learn coping skills.
- Follow medication plan.
</div>

<div class="detail-mini-card" markdown="1">
<strong>Patient Education</strong>

- Recovery is ongoing.
- Support groups reduce isolation.
- Withdrawal should be medically supervised when dangerous.
- Do not stop sedatives or alcohol suddenly without medical guidance.
- Avoid mixing substances.
- Seek help early if cravings or relapse warning signs appear.
</div>

<div class="key-findings" markdown="1">
<h3>📋 Addiction Big Picture</h3>

- Addiction is a chronic medical condition.
- Tolerance means needing more for the same effect.
- Withdrawal symptoms occur when stopping the substance.
- Always assess substance use in a nonjudgmental way.
- Opioid overdose deaths are usually due to respiratory arrest.
- Alcohol withdrawal can be life-threatening.
- Sedative withdrawal can cause seizures.
- Dual diagnosis is common.
</div>

<div class="mh-col" markdown="1">

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

- Respiratory depression or severe respiratory distress
- Suspected opioid overdose
- Withdrawal seizures
- Delirium tremens symptoms
- Severe agitation or unsafe behavior
- Chest pain or severe vital sign changes
- Severe confusion or decreasing level of consciousness
- Signs of GI bleeding or severe liver disease
</div>

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">
<h3>NCLEX Tip</h3>

Overdose questions usually start with ABCs. Withdrawal questions usually ask what can kill the patient: seizures, delirium tremens, respiratory depression, or severe vital sign changes.

<h3>Quick Terms</h3>

| Concept | Simple Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Addiction** | Chronic medical condition involving continued use/behavior despite harm |
| **Intoxication** | Using a substance to excess |
| **Tolerance** | Higher dose needed for same effect |
| **Withdrawal** | Symptoms after stopping substance |
| **Codependence** | Overly responsible for others while ignoring own needs |
| **Dual diagnosis** | Substance use plus coexisting psychiatric illness |
| **Opioid overdose** | Respiratory depression/arrest risk; treat with breathing support and naloxone |
| **Naloxone** | Opioid antagonist for overdose |
| **Methadone** | Opioid withdrawal/maintenance medication |
| **Buprenorphine/naloxone** | Opioid withdrawal/maintenance medication |
| **Sedative withdrawal** | Gradual reduction needed to prevent seizures |
| **Alcohol withdrawal** | Can include tremors, GI upset, insomnia, vital sign elevation, hallucinations, seizures |
| **Delirium tremens** | Most severe alcohol withdrawal; usually within 72 hours; may be fatal |
| **Formication** | Tactile hallucination of bugs crawling |
| **Wernicke’s encephalopathy** | Thiamine deficiency: blurred vision, staggered gait, memory problems |
| **Korsakoff’s psychosis** | Severe memory/confabulation problem from thiamine deficiency |
| **Antabuse / Disulfiram** | Alcohol aversion medication; alcohol reaction may be fatal |
| **AA** | Alcoholics Anonymous support program |
| **GA** | Gamblers Anonymous support program |

</div>

<div class="mh-columns" markdown="1">

<div class="quick-review" markdown="1">
<h3>Quick Review</h3>

1. Assess substance use nonjudgmentally.
2. Patients often underestimate amount used or last time used.
3. Opioid overdose priority is airway and breathing, then naloxone.
4. Alcohol withdrawal can be life-threatening and needs monitoring.
5. DTs usually occur within 72 hours and may be fatal.
6. Sedative withdrawal requires gradual reduction to prevent seizures.
7. Treat coexisting mental health disorders to reduce relapse risk.
</div>

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">
<h3>Do Not Miss</h3>

- Alcohol withdrawal: watch BP, pulse, tremors, hallucinations, seizures, and DTs.
- Opioid overdose: respiratory arrest is the killer.
- Sedative withdrawal: seizure risk.
- Alcohol use can cause thiamine deficiency, Wernicke’s, and Korsakoff’s.
- Antabuse reaction with alcohol can be fatal.
- Withdrawal should be medically supervised when dangerous.
- Always screen for other drugs and coexisting psychiatric illness.
</div>
