# Mental Health Basics

<div class="note">
Mental health nursing focuses on <strong>safety, assessment, trust, boundaries, coping, and helping the patient function as safely as possible.</strong>
<br><br>
This page is the foundation. It covers the basic psych terms and concepts you need before studying specific disorders.
</div>

---

## Big Picture

| Concept | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Safety first** | Always assess risk for self-harm, suicide, violence, abuse, withdrawal, or inability to care for self |
| **Therapeutic environment** | The unit structure, routines, and staff interactions are part of treatment |
| **Mental status assessment** | The nurse assesses appearance, behavior, mood, thought process, perception, cognition, insight, and judgment |
| **Boundaries** | The nurse keeps the relationship professional, consistent, and safe |
| **Least restrictive care** | Use the safest intervention that limits the patient's freedom the least |
| **Coping support** | Help the patient recognize triggers and use healthier coping skills |

---

# Nursing Priorities in Mental Health

| Priority | What the Nurse Looks For |
|---|---|
| **1. Safety** | Suicide risk, homicide risk, self-harm, violence, abuse, severe withdrawal |
| **2. Physiological needs** | Sleep, food, fluids, elimination, hygiene, medication side effects |
| **3. Mental status changes** | Hallucinations, delusions, confusion, agitation, poor judgment |
| **4. Coping and support** | Anxiety level, stressors, support system, coping skills |
| **5. Education/discharge planning** | Medications, therapy, follow-up care, safety plan |

<div class="warning">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> If the patient or someone else is unsafe, safety comes before teaching, coping, or long-term therapy.
</div>

---

# Mental Status Assessment

The mental status assessment helps the nurse understand how the patient is thinking, feeling, behaving, and perceiving reality.

| Area | What the Nurse Assesses |
|---|---|
| **Appearance** | Hygiene, clothing, posture, grooming, eye contact |
| **Behavior** | Calm, restless, withdrawn, cooperative, aggressive, guarded |
| **Speech** | Fast, slow, pressured, soft, loud, delayed, slurred |
| **Mood** | Patient's stated emotional state |
| **Affect** | Emotional expression the nurse observes |
| **Thought process** | Organized, racing, tangential, circumstantial, loose associations |
| **Thought content** | Delusions, paranoia, suicidal thoughts, homicidal thoughts |
| **Perception** | Hallucinations, illusions, depersonalization, derealization |
| **Cognition** | Orientation, attention, memory, concentration |
| **Insight** | Does the patient understand their condition? |
| **Judgment** | Can the patient make safe decisions? |

---

## Mood vs Affect

| Term | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Mood** | How the patient says they feel | "I feel sad." |
| **Affect** | What the nurse observes | Patient is crying, flat, angry, or smiling |

### Affect Terms

| Affect | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Flat affect** | Little or no emotional expression |
| **Blunted affect** | Decreased emotional expression |
| **Constricted affect** | Limited range of emotional expression |
| **Labile affect** | Rapidly changing emotions |
| **Inappropriate affect** | Emotion does not match the situation |

---

# Thought Process Terms

Thought process is **how the patient's thoughts are connected and organized.**

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Flight of ideas** | Rapidly jumping from one idea to another |
| **Loose associations** | Thoughts are not clearly connected |
| **Tangential speech** | Goes off topic and does not return to the point |
| **Circumstantial speech** | Gives too many details but eventually answers |
| **Thought blocking** | Suddenly stops speaking because thought is interrupted |
| **Perseveration** | Repeats the same word, thought, or idea |
| **Word salad** | Words are mixed together and do not make sense |
| **Clang association** | Words are linked by sound or rhyme, not meaning |
| **Neologism** | Made-up word that only has meaning to the patient |
| **Concrete thinking** | Takes words literally; difficulty with abstract meaning |

### Easy Difference

| Term | Simple Way to Remember |
|---|---|
| **Circumstantial** | Eventually gets to the answer, but takes forever |
| **Tangential** | Goes off topic and never gets back |
| **Loose associations** | Ideas are disconnected |
| **Flight of ideas** | Fast jumping thoughts |
| **Thought blocking** | Thought suddenly stops |
| **Word salad** | Words do not make sense together |

---

# Thought Content Terms

Thought content is **what the patient is thinking about.**

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Delusion** | Fixed false belief |
| **Paranoia** | Belief that others are trying to harm, trick, or watch them |
| **Grandiose delusion** | Belief they are extremely powerful, famous, or important |
| **Persecutory delusion** | Belief they are being harmed or targeted |
| **Delusion of reference** | Belief that things in the environment are personally about them |
| **Somatic delusion** | False belief about the body |
| **Religious delusion** | False belief with religious theme |
| **Nihilistic delusion** | Belief that self, others, or the world does not exist or is ending |
| **Suicidal ideation** | Thoughts of killing oneself |
| **Homicidal ideation** | Thoughts of killing or harming someone else |

<div class="note">
With delusions, do not argue or agree. The nurse focuses on <strong>feelings, safety, and reality-based statements.</strong>
</div>

---

# Perception Terms

Perception is **how the patient experiences the world through the senses.**

| Term | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Hallucination** | Sensory experience without a real stimulus | Hearing voices when no one is speaking |
| **Illusion** | Misinterpretation of something real | Mistaking a coat for a person |
| **Depersonalization** | Feeling detached from one's body | "I feel like I'm outside my body." |
| **Derealization** | Feeling like the world is unreal or changed | "Everything around me feels fake." |

### Hallucination Types

| Type | Example |
|---|---|
| **Auditory** | Hearing voices |
| **Visual** | Seeing people or objects not there |
| **Tactile** | Feeling bugs crawling on skin |
| **Olfactory** | Smelling odors not present |
| **Gustatory** | Tasting something not present |

<div class="warning">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> If a patient reports voices, ask what the voices are saying. Command hallucinations may tell the patient to harm self or others.
</div>

---

# Insight and Judgment

| Term | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Insight** | Understanding of own condition or behavior | "I know I need help managing my depression." |
| **Poor insight** | Does not recognize illness or problem | "I do not need medication. Nothing is wrong." |
| **Judgment** | Ability to make safe decisions | Calling for help when unsafe |
| **Poor judgment** | Unsafe or impulsive decision-making | Leaving hospital while actively suicidal |

---

# Defense Mechanisms

Defense mechanisms are unconscious ways people protect themselves from anxiety, stress, or uncomfortable feelings.

| Defense Mechanism | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Denial** | Refusing to accept reality | "I do not have a drinking problem." |
| **Projection** | Blaming your own feelings on someone else | Angry patient says, "The nurse hates me." |
| **Displacement** | Taking feelings out on a safer target | Yelling at family after being upset with provider |
| **Regression** | Acting younger during stress | Adult becomes dependent or childlike |
| **Rationalization** | Making excuses | "I failed because the teacher hates me." |
| **Repression** | Unconsciously blocking a memory | Cannot remember traumatic event |
| **Suppression** | Consciously choosing not to think about something | "I'll deal with this after my exam." |
| **Sublimation** | Turning feelings into a healthy activity | Exercising to manage anger |
| **Reaction formation** | Acting opposite of true feelings | Being overly kind to someone disliked |
| **Splitting** | Seeing people as all good or all bad | "This nurse is perfect; that nurse is evil." |

<div class="tip">
<strong>Healthiest defense mechanism:</strong> Sublimation, because the person channels stress into a safe activity.
</div>

---

# Coping

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Adaptive coping** | Healthy coping that helps the patient function | Talking, journaling, exercise, breathing techniques |
| **Maladaptive coping** | Unhealthy coping that causes more problems | Substance use, self-harm, isolation, aggression |

The nurse should assess: current stressors, support system, past coping methods, substance use, sleep and appetite, risk for self-harm, ability to perform ADLs, and what has helped before.

---

# Boundaries

Boundaries keep the nurse-patient relationship safe, professional, and therapeutic.

| Do | Avoid |
|---|---|
| Be consistent | Making special exceptions |
| Set clear limits | Arguing or threatening |
| Follow the care plan | Letting the patient split staff |
| Keep promises realistic | Promising secrecy |
| Stay professional | Oversharing personal information |
| Communicate with the team | Keeping patient behavior secret from staff |
| Encourage independence | Doing everything for the patient |

<div class="note">
Patients with manipulation, splitting, dependency, or aggression need <strong>clear limits and consistent staff responses.</strong>
</div>

---

# External vs Internal Locus of Control

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Internal locus of control** | Person believes they have some control over choices/actions | "I can practice coping skills when I feel angry." |
| **External locus of control** | Person believes others/events control their behavior | "They made me act that way." |

The nurse helps the patient recognize choices, accept responsibility when appropriate, identify triggers, use healthier coping skills, and avoid blaming everything on others.

---

# Milieu Therapy

Milieu therapy means the **environment is part of treatment.** The goal is to create a safe, structured, supportive setting where patients can practice healthier behaviors.

- Clear routines
- Safe environment
- Group therapy
- Unit rules
- Therapeutic activities
- Consistent expectations
- Patient responsibilities
- Staff modeling healthy communication

The nurse maintains safety, encourages participation, sets limits, models healthy behavior, reinforces rules consistently, observes patient interactions, reduces triggers and overstimulation, and promotes independence.

---

# Admission Types

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Voluntary admission** | Patient agrees to treatment |
| **Involuntary admission** | Patient is admitted because they are a danger to self, danger to others, or unable to meet basic needs |

Mental health patients still have rights, even when hospitalized. They may refuse some treatments unless there is an emergency, court order, or safety issue.

---

# Patient Rights

| Right | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Privacy** | Personal information is protected |
| **Dignity** | Patient is treated with respect |
| **Informed consent** | Patient understands treatment before agreeing |
| **Refuse treatment** | Unless emergency/legal order applies |
| **Least restrictive care** | Use the safest option with the least restriction |
| **Communication** | Phone/mail/visitors unless restricted for safety |
| **Safe care** | Environment protects patient and others |

<div class="warning">
<strong>Never promise secrecy</strong> if the patient reports suicide plans, homicide threats, abuse, or danger to self/others.
</div>

# Restraints and Seclusion

Restraints and seclusion are **last resort** interventions used only when the patient is an immediate danger to self or others and less restrictive interventions have failed.

**Not used for:** punishment, convenience, staff frustration, lack of staffing, threatening the patient, or making care easier.

### Try First

- Calm approach
- Reduce stimulation
- Offer quiet space
- Remove audience
- Set clear limits
- Offer choices
- Offer medication if ordered
- Call for help early

### Nursing Care During Restraints

| Nursing Action | Why |
|---|---|
| Check airway/breathing | Prevent respiratory compromise |
| Check circulation | Prevent injury |
| Check skin | Prevent breakdown |
| Check vital signs | Monitor physical status |
| Offer food/fluids | Meet basic needs |
| Offer toileting | Maintain dignity |
| Assess mental status | Monitor safety |
| Reassess behavior | Remove restraints ASAP |
| Document clearly | Legal and safety requirement |

<div class="warning">
<strong>NCLEX:</strong> Restraints are the last resort and must be discontinued as soon as the patient is safe.
</div>

# Anxiety Levels

| Level | Signs | Nursing Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Alert, focused, slightly restless | Teaching and problem-solving can happen |
| **Moderate** | Narrowed focus, tense, increased HR | Use simple directions |
| **Severe** | Poor concentration, pacing, physical symptoms | Reduce stimuli, stay calm |
| **Panic** | Unable to process, feels out of control | Stay with patient, short directions, safety |

<div class="note">
Do not try to teach during severe anxiety or panic. First decrease anxiety and maintain safety.
</div>

---

# Medication Safety Overview

| Medication Group | Big Nursing Focus |
|---|---|
| **Antidepressants** | Suicide risk may increase early in treatment |
| **Benzodiazepines** | Sedation, fall risk, dependence |
| **Mood stabilizers** | Toxicity and lab monitoring |
| **Antipsychotics** | EPS, tardive dyskinesia, NMS, metabolic effects |
| **Stimulants** | Appetite loss, insomnia, increased HR/BP |
| **Substance-use meds** | Withdrawal safety, overdose reversal, relapse prevention |

### Medication Emergencies

| Emergency | Clues | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| **Serotonin syndrome** | Fever, agitation, sweating, diarrhea, tremor, hyperreflexia | Hold medication, notify provider |
| **Neuroleptic malignant syndrome** | High fever, muscle rigidity, altered mental status, unstable vitals | Medical emergency |
| **Lithium toxicity** | GI upset, tremor, confusion, ataxia, seizures | Hold lithium, notify provider |
| **Benzodiazepine overdose** | Severe sedation, respiratory depression | Airway and breathing priority |
| **Alcohol withdrawal/DTs** | Tremors, agitation, hallucinations, seizures, high VS | Safety, seizure precautions, medication |

# NCLEX Cues

<div class="warning">

<strong>Safety first:</strong> Suicide, homicide, violence, withdrawal, and abuse are always the priority — before teaching, coping, or discharge planning.

</div>

<div class="note">

<strong>Assessment terminology:</strong>
<ul>
<li>Mood is what the patient reports feeling. Affect is what you observe.</li>
<li>Delusions are fixed false beliefs — do not argue or agree with them.</li>
<li>Hallucinations require safety assessment, especially command hallucinations.</li>
</ul>

</div>

<div class="note">

<strong>Interventions:</strong>
<ul>
<li>Restraints and seclusion are last resort — use least restrictive intervention first.</li>
<li>Do not teach during panic-level anxiety — reduce stimulation first.</li>
<li>Maintain clear boundaries and consistent staff responses across the team.</li>
</ul>

</div>

## Final Takeaway

<div class="tip">
For basic mental health questions, ask:
<br><br>
<strong>1. Is anyone unsafe?</strong><br>
<strong>2. What is the patient's mental status?</strong><br>
<strong>3. Is this mood, affect, thought process, thought content, or perception?</strong><br>
<strong>4. Does the patient need limits, safety, structure, or support?</strong><br>
<strong>5. What is the least restrictive safe intervention?</strong>
</div>
