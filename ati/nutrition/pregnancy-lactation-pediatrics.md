<h2 class="section-divider" id="pregnancy-lactation-infant-and-pediatric-nutrition">Pregnancy, Lactation &amp; Pediatric Nutrition</h2>

<div class="memory">
Every stage on this page shares one driver: <strong>the body is building something</strong> — a fetus, breast milk, or a rapidly growing infant/child. Needs go up, and the consequences of falling short show up in someone other than just the person eating.
</div>

---

<h3 id="big-picture">Big Picture: Supporting Growth</h3>

| Stage | Why Nutrition Matters |
|---|---|
| **Pregnancy** | Supports fetal growth, placenta, maternal blood volume, and tissue changes |
| **Lactation** | Supports milk production and maternal recovery |
| **Infancy** | Rapid growth requires the right calories, fluids, and gradual food introduction |
| **Childhood/adolescence** | Supports growth, bone development, and long-term eating habits |

---

<h3 id="weight-gain">Pregnancy Weight Gain</h3>

Expected weight gain depends on **prepregnancy BMI** — not a single universal number.

| Prepregnancy BMI | Category | Recommended Total Gain |
|---|---|---|
| Less than 18.5 | Underweight | 28–40 lb |
| 18.5–24.9 | Normal weight | 25–35 lb |
| 25.0–29.9 | Overweight | 15–25 lb |
| 30.0 or greater | Obese | 11–20 lb |

<div class="ati-tip">
If the client was <strong>underweight</strong> before pregnancy, don't pick the lowest weight-gain range — that pattern is backwards. Underweight clients need the <strong>most</strong> gain; obese clients need the <strong>least</strong>.
</div>

---

<h3 id="pregnancy-nutrients">Pregnancy Macronutrient &amp; Micronutrient Needs</h3>

| Nutrient | Why It Increases | Key Number |
|---|---|---|
| **Protein** | Builds fetal tissue, placenta, maternal blood volume, uterine/breast tissue | Increases throughout pregnancy |
| **Iron** | Supports expanding maternal blood volume and fetal growth | **30 mg/day** to reduce iron-deficiency anemia risk |
| **Folate** | Fetal neural tube development, RBC formation | Most critical **early** in pregnancy (often before the client even knows they're pregnant) |
| **Fat** | Still needed, but excess/fried fat should be limited if intake is already high | Avoid extreme restriction |

<div class="teaching">
Iron teaching: take with vitamin C to improve absorption; use a straw for liquid iron; expect constipation and dark stools — these are expected effects, not reasons to stop the supplement.
</div>

<h3 id="gdm">Gestational Diabetes</h3>

Gestational diabetes (GDM) develops during pregnancy due to placental hormones increasing insulin resistance. Untreated, it raises the risk for fetal macrosomia and **neonatal hypoglycemia** after birth.

<div class="patho">
The placenta produces hormones that increase insulin resistance as pregnancy progresses. Maternal hyperglycemia crosses the placenta, causing the fetus to produce extra insulin (fetal hyperinsulinemia) — this drives excess fetal growth (macrosomia) and primes the newborn for a glucose crash right after birth, once the maternal glucose supply is suddenly cut off.
</div>

<div class="teaching">
Management mirrors general diabetes nutrition principles: <strong>consistent carbohydrate intake</strong>, small frequent meals, complex carbohydrates over simple sugars, regular moderate exercise (e.g., walking), and glucose monitoring. Diet and exercise control GDM in many clients; others require insulin.
</div>

<div class="ati-tip">
After delivery, insulin needs typically <strong>drop quickly</strong> once the placenta (the source of the insulin-resistant hormones) is delivered — don't expect the same insulin requirements to continue postpartum.
</div>

<h3 id="hyperemesis">Hyperemesis Gravidarum</h3>

Severe nausea/vomiting in pregnancy that goes beyond typical morning sickness, risking dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, weight loss, and ketosis.

<div class="danger">
Think the same priority pattern as any severe vomiting: <strong>fluid volume deficit + electrolyte imbalance</strong>, with the added concern of fetal nutrition if prolonged or severe.
</div>

<div class="teaching">
Helpful strategies: small frequent meals; dry, bland foods (e.g., crackers before getting out of bed in the morning, before the head movement of standing triggers nausea); sip fluids **between** meals rather than with them; avoid trigger smells; take prescribed antiemetics as directed; report inability to keep fluids down.
</div>

---

<h3 id="pica">Pica in Pregnancy</h3>

Pica is craving and eating non-food substances (clay, dirt, ice/pagophagia, laundry starch).

<div class="ati-tip">
Pica during pregnancy is associated with <strong>iron deficiency</strong> — it can be both a sign of existing deficiency and a cause of worsening it (since the substances consumed displace nutritious food and may interfere with iron absorption). Assess for pica when iron-deficiency anemia is suspected, and ask directly — clients may not volunteer this behavior.
</div>

<h3 id="lactation">Lactation &amp; Breastfeeding</h3>

<div class="memory">
Breastfeed <strong>on demand</strong>, typically <strong>8–12 times per day</strong> for a newborn. On the first day, expect roughly <strong>5 minutes per breast</strong> — short, frequent feeds build supply.
</div>

<div class="danger">
Avoid unnecessary <strong>formula, water, and artificial nipples</strong> while establishing breastfeeding — these can interfere with latch and reduce the stimulation needed to build milk supply.
</div>

<div class="note">
Maternal calcium needs do not increase numerically during lactation (still about 1,000 mg/day) — but calcium absorption from the gut increases during lactation to help meet the higher demand without raising the RDA itself.
</div>

---

<h3 id="milk-storage">Breast Milk Storage &amp; Thawing</h3>

| Situation | Teaching |
|---|---|
| **Thawed breast milk** | Use within **24 hr**; never refreeze |
| **Thawing method** | Refrigerator (slow) or warm running water (faster) |
| **Never use** | Microwave — uneven heating risks burns and destroys some protective properties |
| **Frozen storage** | Commonly cited as up to 6 months, with up to 12 months considered acceptable in some guidance |
| **Milk left in a bottle after feeding** | Discard — infant saliva has contaminated it |

<h3 id="infant-growth">Infant Growth &amp; Feeding Milestones</h3>

| Milestone | Expected Timing |
|---|---|
| Regain birth weight | By about **2 weeks** |
| Triple birth weight | By **1 year** |
| Length increase | About **50%** by 1 year |
| Introduce new solid foods | One at a time, every **3–5 days** (to monitor for allergic reaction) |
| Whole milk | Not before **12 months**; used until about age 2, then transition to lower-fat milk |

---

<h3 id="honey-botulism">Infant Safety: Honey &amp; Botulism</h3>

<div class="danger">
Never give honey to an infant <strong>under 12 months</strong> — it can contain <em>Clostridium botulinum</em> spores. An adult's mature gut flora controls these spores, but an infant's immature gut cannot, risking <strong>infant botulism</strong> (a serious, potentially life-threatening illness). This applies to honey in any form — including baked into foods — not just honey eaten directly.
</div>

---

<h3 id="infant-supplements">Infant &amp; Breastfed-Infant Supplementation</h3>

Breast milk is an excellent overall food, but it's reliably low in two nutrients that matter enough to need supplementation.

| Nutrient | Why Supplementation Is Needed | Typical Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| **Vitamin D** | Breast milk is naturally low in vitamin D | **400 IU/day**, starting in the first few days of life, for exclusively or mostly breastfed infants |
| **Iron** | Maternal iron stores in breast milk decline as the infant grows | Supplementation or iron-fortified complementary foods typically introduced around **4–6 months** |

<div class="note">
Formula-fed infants generally don't need extra vitamin D or iron, since commercial formula is already fortified with both — this distinction (breastfed vs. formula-fed) is the detail ATI is testing when it asks "which infant needs supplementation?"
</div>

---

<h3 id="cleft-lip">Cleft Lip Feeding</h3>

A cleft lip makes it hard for the infant to form an effective seal around a nipple, risking poor intake and aspiration from swallowed air.

<div class="teaching">
Helpful strategies: a bottle with a <strong>one-way valve</strong> and a <strong>high-flow nipple</strong>; gently support/squeeze the cheeks to help close the gap during feeding; burp frequently (after each ounce or 2–3 times per feeding) since these infants swallow more air; allow extra time for feeds.
</div>

---

<h3 id="choking">Toddler Choking Hazards</h3>

<div class="danger">
Common toddler choking hazards: <strong>hot dogs, whole grapes, bagels/hard chunks of bread, marshmallows, popcorn, chunks of cheese, raisins, raw carrots, nuts, and seeds.</strong> A soft, ripe banana is <strong>not</strong> a choking hazard and is an appropriate fruit choice for this age group.
</div>

---

<h3 id="division-responsibility">Feeding Toddlers: Division of Responsibility</h3>

<div class="note">
A useful general framework for toddler feeding conflicts: the <strong>caregiver decides what, when, and where</strong> food is offered; the <strong>child decides whether and how much</strong> to eat from what's offered. This reduces power struggles and supports the child's own hunger/fullness regulation — pressuring or forcing a toddler to finish food tends to backfire.
</div>

---

<h3 id="child-adolescent">Child &amp; Adolescent Nutrition</h3>

ATI tests this mostly through **food choice comparisons** — picking the lower-fat, lower-sugar option between two similar foods.

<div class="ati-tip">
General target for fruits/vegetables: about <strong>2.5 cups vegetables</strong> and <strong>2 cups fruit</strong> per day (see Nutrition Basics for the full MyPlate breakdown).
</div>

<div class="danger">
Adolescent nutritional risk factors worth recognizing: skipping more than 2–3 meals per week, frequently skipping breakfast, eating without family/adult supervision, and reliance on fast food — these patterns (not occasional fast food) are the actual red flags.
</div>

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="food-sources">Food Sources Quick Reference</h2>

#### Pregnancy

| Nutrient | Food Sources |
|---|---|
| Iron | Red meat, fortified grains, beans, lentils, leafy greens |
| Folate | Chicken liver, leafy greens, fortified grains, beans, lentils |
| Protein | Lean meats, poultry, fish, eggs, dairy, beans, lentils |

#### Gestational Diabetes (Consistent Carbohydrate Choices)

| Better Choices |
|---|
| Whole grains, legumes, non-starchy vegetables, fruit in consistent portions, lean protein paired with carbohydrate |

#### Lower-Fat Choices for Children/Adolescents

| Instead of This | Choose This |
|---|---|
| High-fat cheese | Cheese with 3 g fat or less per serving |
| Fatty ground beef | Ground beef at least 90% lean |
| Refried beans | Canned pinto beans |
| Pasta with white sauce | Pasta with red sauce |
| Sweetened fruit yogurt | Plain fat-free yogurt with fresh fruit |
| Sausage | Canadian bacon or another lower-fat meat |

#### Toddler-Safe vs. Choking-Hazard Foods

| Safer Choices | Choking Hazards to Avoid |
|---|---|
| Soft ripe banana, well-cooked soft vegetables, ground/shredded meat | Hot dogs, whole grapes, bagels, marshmallows, popcorn, chunks of cheese, raisins, raw carrots, nuts, seeds |

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="section-summary">Section Summary &amp; Test-Taking Tips</h2>

| If the Question Says... | Think... |
|---|---|
| Pregnancy weight gain | Based on prepregnancy BMI — underweight gains most, obese gains least |
| Pregnant client + iron | 30 mg/day; take with vitamin C; expect dark stools/constipation |
| Pregnant client + folate | Critical in early pregnancy for neural tube development |
| Hyperglycemia developing during pregnancy | Gestational diabetes — consistent carb intake, monitor for macrosomia risk |
| GDM client right after delivery | Insulin needs drop quickly — placenta (source of resistance hormones) is gone |
| Severe vomiting in pregnancy | Hyperemesis gravidarum — fluid volume deficit + electrolyte imbalance risk |
| Pregnant client craving non-food substances (ice, clay, dirt) | Pica — assess for iron deficiency |
| Newborn breastfeeding frequency | On demand, 8–12 times/day |
| First-day breastfeeding | About 5 min per breast |
| Establishing breastfeeding | Avoid unnecessary formula, water, artificial nipples |
| Thawed breast milk | Use within 24 hr; never refreeze; never microwave |
| Milk left in the bottle after a feeding | Discard |
| Infant birth weight regained | ~2 weeks |
| Infant 1-year growth | Triples birth weight; length +50% |
| Introducing new solid foods | One at a time, every 3–5 days |
| Whole milk timing | Not before 12 months; used until ~age 2 |
| Honey + infant under 12 months | Never — botulism risk |
| Exclusively/mostly breastfed infant | Needs vitamin D (400 IU/day) and iron (~4–6 months) supplementation |
| Formula-fed infant | Usually doesn't need extra vitamin D/iron — formula is already fortified |
| Infant with cleft lip | One-way valve bottle, high-flow nipple, cheek support, frequent burping |
| Toddler + hot dogs/grapes/popcorn/nuts | Choking hazards — avoid |
| Toddler mealtime power struggle | Caregiver controls what/when/where; child controls whether/how much |
| Adolescent skipping breakfast/meals, eating unsupervised | Nutritional risk factors to flag |
