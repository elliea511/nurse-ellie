<h2 class="section-divider" id="pregnancy-lactation-infant-and-pediatric-nutrition">Pregnancy, Lactation & Pediatric Nutrition</h2>

Every stage on this page shares one driver: **the body is building something** — a fetus, breast milk, or a rapidly growing infant/child. Needs go up, and the consequences of falling short show up in someone other than just the person eating.

---

<h3 id="big-picture">Big Picture: Supporting Growth</h3>

| Stage | Why Nutrition Matters |
|---|---|
| **Pregnancy** | Supports fetal growth, placenta, maternal blood volume, and tissue changes |
| **Lactation** | Supports milk production and maternal recovery |
| **Infancy** | Rapid growth requires the right calories, fluids, and gradual food introduction |
| **Childhood/adolescence** | Supports growth, bone development, and long-term eating habits |

### Growth Stages and Test Focus

| Stage | ATI Usually Wants You to Notice |
|---|---|
| **Pregnancy** | Weight gain based on prepregnancy BMI, iron/folate needs, GDM, dehydration risk with vomiting |
| **Lactation** | Breastfeeding frequency, avoiding artificial nipples early, milk storage safety |
| **Infancy** | Growth milestones, vitamin D/iron supplementation, honey restriction, safe food introduction |
| **Toddler/child** | Choking hazards, picky eating, caregiver/child feeding roles |
| **Adolescent** | Skipping meals, fast food patterns, low calcium/iron intake risk |

<h3 id="weight-gain">Pregnancy Weight Gain</h3>

Expected weight gain depends on **prepregnancy BMI** — not a single universal number.

| Prepregnancy BMI | Category | Recommended Total Gain |
|---|---|---|
| Less than 18.5 | Underweight | 28–40 lb |
| 18.5–24.9 | Normal weight | 25–35 lb |
| 25.0–29.9 | Overweight | 15–25 lb |
| 30.0 or greater | Obese | 11–20 lb |

If the client was **underweight** before pregnancy, don't pick the lowest weight-gain range — that pattern is backwards. Underweight clients need the **most** gain; obese clients need the **least**.

### Pregnancy Weight Gain Pattern

| Pregnancy Point | What to Remember |
|---|---|
| **First trimester** | Weight gain is usually small compared with later pregnancy |
| **Second/third trimester** | Weight gain becomes more consistent as fetal growth increases |
| **Too little gain** | Can increase concern for fetal growth restriction or poor maternal intake |
| **Too much gain** | Can increase concern for macrosomia, hypertension, or excess fluid/weight gain depending on assessment findings |

<h3 id="pregnancy-nutrients">Pregnancy Macronutrient & Micronutrient Needs</h3>

| Nutrient | Why It Increases | Key Number |
|---|---|---|
| **Protein** | Builds fetal tissue, placenta, maternal blood volume, uterine/breast tissue | Increases throughout pregnancy |
| **Iron** | Supports expanding maternal blood volume and fetal growth | **30 mg/day** to reduce iron-deficiency anemia risk |
| **Folate** | Fetal neural tube development, RBC formation | Most critical **early** in pregnancy (often before the client even knows they're pregnant) |
| **Fat** | Still needed, but excess/fried fat should be limited if intake is already high | Avoid extreme restriction |

Iron teaching: take with vitamin C to improve absorption; use a straw for liquid iron; expect constipation and dark stools — these are expected effects, not reasons to stop the supplement.

### Additional Pregnancy Nutrient Quick Chart

| Nutrient | ATI Clue |
|---|---|
| **Calcium/Vitamin D** | Fetal bones/teeth and maternal bone support |
| **Fluids** | Supports increased blood volume and helps constipation |
| **Fiber** | Helps constipation, especially with iron supplementation |

### Foods to Avoid or Limit in Pregnancy

| Food/Drink | Why |
|---|---|
| **Unpasteurized milk/cheese/juice** | Infection risk |
| **Raw or undercooked meat/eggs/seafood** | Foodborne illness risk |
| **High-mercury fish** | Fetal neurologic risk |
| **Alcohol** | No safe amount established in pregnancy |
| **Excess caffeine** | Limit intake based on provider guidance/facility teaching |
| **Deli meats/hot dogs unless heated** | Listeria risk |

<h3 id="gdm">Gestational Diabetes</h3>

Gestational diabetes (GDM) develops during pregnancy due to placental hormones increasing insulin resistance. Untreated, it raises the risk for fetal macrosomia and **neonatal hypoglycemia** after birth.

The placenta produces hormones that increase insulin resistance as pregnancy progresses. Maternal hyperglycemia crosses the placenta, causing the fetus to produce extra insulin (fetal hyperinsulinemia) — this drives excess fetal growth (macrosomia) and primes the newborn for a glucose crash right after birth, once the maternal glucose supply is suddenly cut off.

Management mirrors general diabetes nutrition principles: **consistent carbohydrate intake**, small frequent meals, complex carbohydrates over simple sugars, regular moderate exercise (e.g., walking), and glucose monitoring. Diet and exercise control GDM in many clients; others require insulin.

After delivery, insulin needs typically **drop quickly** once the placenta (the source of the insulin-resistant hormones) is delivered — don't expect the same insulin requirements to continue postpartum.

### GDM Meal Teaching

| Teaching | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Eat meals/snacks consistently | Prevents large glucose swings |
| Pair carbohydrates with protein | Slows glucose rise and improves satiety |
| Avoid skipping meals | Skipping can cause hypoglycemia or rebound overeating |
| Choose complex carbs over simple sugars | Better routine glucose control |
| Monitor glucose as prescribed | Shows whether diet/exercise are enough or insulin is needed |

### GDM Newborn Risk

| Maternal Issue | Newborn Risk |
|---|---|
| Maternal hyperglycemia | Fetal hyperinsulinemia |
| Fetal hyperinsulinemia | Macrosomia before birth |
| Placenta delivered | Maternal glucose supply stops |
| Newborn still has high insulin | Neonatal hypoglycemia |

<h3 id="hyperemesis">Hyperemesis Gravidarum</h3>

Severe nausea/vomiting in pregnancy that goes beyond typical morning sickness, risking dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, weight loss, and ketosis.

Think the same priority pattern as any severe vomiting: **fluid volume deficit + electrolyte imbalance**, with the added concern of fetal nutrition if prolonged or severe.

Helpful strategies: small frequent meals; dry, bland foods (e.g., crackers before getting out of bed in the morning, before the head movement of standing triggers nausea); sip fluids **between** meals rather than with them; avoid trigger smells; take prescribed antiemetics as directed; report inability to keep fluids down.

### Hyperemesis Assessment Cues

| Cue | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Weight loss** | Suggests intake is not meeting needs |
| **Ketonuria** | Suggests starvation/fat breakdown |
| **Dry mucous membranes / poor skin turgor** | Dehydration |
| **Tachycardia / hypotension** | Fluid volume deficit |
| **Low potassium or other electrolyte changes** | Vomiting-related electrolyte imbalance |

<h3 id="pica">Pica in Pregnancy</h3>

Pica is craving and eating non-food substances (clay, dirt, ice/pagophagia, laundry starch).

Pica during pregnancy is associated with **iron deficiency** — it can be both a sign of existing deficiency and a cause of worsening it (since the substances consumed displace nutritious food and may interfere with iron absorption). Assess for pica when iron-deficiency anemia is suspected, and ask directly — clients may not volunteer this behavior.

### Pica Teaching

| Nursing Action | Why |
|---|---|
| Ask directly and nonjudgmentally | Clients may feel embarrassed |
| Assess iron status | Pica is associated with iron deficiency |
| Teach safety risks | Non-food substances can cause toxicity, infection, constipation, or poor intake |
| Encourage nutrient-dense substitutes | Helps meet pregnancy needs without unsafe substances |

<h3 id="lactation">Lactation & Breastfeeding</h3>

Breastfeed **on demand**, typically **8–12 times per day** for a newborn. On the first day, expect roughly **5 minutes per breast** — short, frequent feeds build supply.

Avoid unnecessary **formula, water, and artificial nipples** while establishing breastfeeding — these can interfere with latch and reduce the stimulation needed to build milk supply.

Maternal calcium needs do not increase numerically during lactation (still about 1,000 mg/day) — but calcium absorption from the gut increases during lactation to help meet the higher demand without raising the RDA itself.

### Signs Breastfeeding Is Going Well

| Sign | What It Suggests |
|---|---|
| Audible swallowing after milk comes in | Milk transfer is occurring |
| Infant appears satisfied after feeding | Feeding may be effective |
| Adequate wet/dirty diapers | Intake is likely adequate |
| Weight gain after expected early loss | Growth is on track |
| No severe maternal nipple trauma | Latch may be effective |

### Lactation Nutrition for the Parent

| Teaching | Why |
|---|---|
| Increase fluids to thirst | Supports hydration during milk production |
| Eat balanced meals/snacks | Supports recovery and milk production |
| Avoid crash dieting | Can reduce intake and energy |
| Limit alcohol and time breastfeeding appropriately if consumed | Alcohol can pass into breast milk |
| Continue prenatal vitamins if prescribed | Supports nutrient needs postpartum |

<h3 id="milk-storage">Breast Milk Storage & Thawing</h3>

| Situation | Teaching |
|---|---|
| **Thawed breast milk** | Use within **24 hr**; never refreeze |
| **Thawing method** | Refrigerator (slow) or warm running water (faster) |
| **Never use** | Microwave — uneven heating risks burns and destroys some protective properties |
| **Frozen storage** | Commonly cited as up to 6 months, with up to 12 months considered acceptable in some guidance |
| **Milk left in a bottle after feeding** | Discard — infant saliva has contaminated it |

### Breast Milk Safety Details

| Situation | Teaching |
|---|---|
| Milk separates in storage | Gently swirl; do not shake aggressively |
| Warmed milk | Use promptly and do not keep reheating |
| Previously frozen milk | Never refreeze after thawing |
| Bottle touched infant's mouth | Discard leftover milk after feeding |

<h3 id="infant-growth">Infant Growth & Feeding Milestones</h3>

| Milestone | Expected Timing |
|---|---|
| Regain birth weight | By about **2 weeks** |
| Triple birth weight | By **1 year** |
| Length increase | About **50%** by 1 year |
| Introduce new solid foods | One at a time, every **3–5 days** (to monitor for allergic reaction) |
| Whole milk | Not before **12 months**; used until about age 2, then transition to lower-fat milk |

### Infant Feeding Progression

| Age/Stage | Feeding Focus |
|---|---|
| **Birth to 6 months** | Breast milk or iron-fortified formula is the main nutrition source |
| **Around 6 months** | Begin developmentally appropriate complementary foods when ready |
| **New foods** | Introduce one at a time, every 3–5 days |
| **Before 12 months** | Avoid whole milk and honey |
| **After 12 months** | Whole milk may be used until about age 2, then transition to lower-fat milk |

### Readiness for Solid Foods

| Readiness Cue | Why |
|---|---|
| Good head control | Helps safe swallowing |
| Sits with support | Supports safe feeding posture |
| Shows interest in food | Developmental readiness |
| Diminished tongue-thrust reflex | Helps move food back to swallow |

<h3 id="honey-botulism">Infant Safety: Honey & Botulism</h3>

Never give honey to an infant **under 12 months** — it can contain *Clostridium botulinum* spores. An adult's mature gut flora controls these spores, but an infant's immature gut cannot, risking **infant botulism** (a serious, potentially life-threatening illness). This applies to honey in any form — including baked into foods — not just honey eaten directly.

### Botulism Clues

| Infant Finding | Concern |
|---|---|
| Constipation | Early possible sign |
| Weak cry | Neuromuscular weakness |
| Poor feeding | Weak suck/swallow |
| Floppy tone | “Floppy baby” presentation |
| Respiratory difficulty | Serious progression |

<h3 id="infant-supplements">Infant & Breastfed-Infant Supplementation</h3>

Breast milk is an excellent overall food, but it's reliably low in two nutrients that matter enough to need supplementation.

| Nutrient | Why Supplementation Is Needed | Typical Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| **Vitamin D** | Breast milk is naturally low in vitamin D | **400 IU/day**, starting in the first few days of life, for exclusively or mostly breastfed infants |
| **Iron** | Maternal iron stores in breast milk decline as the infant grows | Supplementation or iron-fortified complementary foods typically introduced around **4–6 months** |

Formula-fed infants generally don't need extra vitamin D or iron, since commercial formula is already fortified with both — this distinction (breastfed vs. formula-fed) is the detail ATI is testing when it asks "which infant needs supplementation?"

### Infant Supplement Decision Chart

| Infant Type | Vitamin D | Iron |
|---|---|---|
| **Exclusively breastfed** | Usually needs 400 IU/day | Around 4–6 months or iron-fortified foods |
| **Mostly breastfed** | Usually needs 400 IU/day | Around 4–6 months or iron-fortified foods |
| **Formula-fed** | Usually not extra if taking enough fortified formula | Usually not extra if taking enough fortified formula |
| **Preterm/low birth weight** | May need individualized supplementation | Higher risk for iron deficiency; follow provider plan |

<h3 id="cleft-lip">Cleft Lip Feeding</h3>

A cleft lip makes it hard for the infant to form an effective seal around a nipple, risking poor intake and aspiration from swallowed air.

Helpful strategies: a bottle with a **one-way valve** and a **high-flow nipple**; gently support/squeeze the cheeks to help close the gap during feeding; burp frequently (after each ounce or 2–3 times per feeding) since these infants swallow more air; allow extra time for feeds.

### Cleft Lip/Palate Feeding Safety

| Teaching | Why |
|---|---|
| Feed in an upright position | Reduces aspiration risk |
| Use specialty bottle/nipple | Helps compensate for poor seal/suction |
| Burp frequently | More air is swallowed |
| Allow extra feeding time | Infant may tire easily |
| Monitor weight gain | Confirms intake is adequate |

<h3 id="choking">Toddler Choking Hazards</h3>

Common toddler choking hazards: **hot dogs, whole grapes, bagels/hard chunks of bread, marshmallows, popcorn, chunks of cheese, raisins, raw carrots, nuts, and seeds.** A soft, ripe banana is **not** a choking hazard and is an appropriate fruit choice for this age group.

### Choking Prevention Teaching

| Teaching | Why |
|---|---|
| Cut round foods lengthwise and into small pieces | Round foods can block the airway |
| Avoid hard, sticky, round, or small foods | These are classic choking hazards |
| Supervise meals/snacks | Toddlers are still learning safe chewing/swallowing |
| Have child sit while eating | Running/playing while eating increases choking risk |

<h3 id="division-responsibility">Feeding Toddlers: Division of Responsibility</h3>

A useful general framework for toddler feeding conflicts: the **caregiver decides what, when, and where** food is offered; the **child decides whether and how much** to eat from what's offered. This reduces power struggles and supports the child's own hunger/fullness regulation — pressuring or forcing a toddler to finish food tends to backfire.

### Toddler Feeding Notes

| Pattern | Teaching |
|---|---|
| Picky eating | Often normal; keep offering healthy foods without forcing |
| Food jags | Toddlers may want the same food repeatedly |
| Small appetite | Growth slows after infancy, so appetite may decrease |
| Refusing a meal | Avoid power struggles; offer food again at the next scheduled meal/snack |
| Excess juice | Can decrease appetite and contribute to diarrhea or dental caries |

<h3 id="child-adolescent">Child & Adolescent Nutrition</h3>

ATI tests this mostly through **food choice comparisons** — picking the lower-fat, lower-sugar option between two similar foods.

General target for fruits/vegetables: about **2.5 cups vegetables** and **2 cups fruit** per day (see Nutrition Basics for the full MyPlate breakdown).

Adolescent nutritional risk factors worth recognizing: skipping more than 2–3 meals per week, frequently skipping breakfast, eating without family/adult supervision, and reliance on fast food — these patterns (not occasional fast food) are the actual red flags.

### Adolescent Nutrition Risks

| Risk | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Skipping meals/breakfast | Lower nutrient intake and poor energy pattern |
| Frequent fast food | Higher sodium, saturated fat, and calories |
| Low calcium/vitamin D intake | Bone development concern |
| Menstruating adolescent with low iron intake | Iron-deficiency anemia risk |
| Sports or dieting pressure | Risk for inadequate intake or disordered eating patterns |

### Healthy Pediatric Food Swaps

| Instead of This | Choose This |
|---|---|
| Sweetened fruit yogurt | Plain fat-free yogurt with fresh fruit |
| Fried foods | Baked, grilled, or broiled foods |
| Whole milk after age 2 | Lower-fat milk unless otherwise prescribed |
| Sugary drinks | Water or milk |
| High-fat meats | Lean meats, poultry, fish, beans, or lentils |

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="food-sources">Food Sources Quick Reference</h2>

#### Pregnancy

| Nutrient | Food Sources |
|---|---|
| Iron | Red meat, fortified grains, beans, lentils, leafy greens |
| Folate | Chicken liver, leafy greens, fortified grains, beans, lentils |
| Protein | Lean meats, poultry, fish, eggs, dairy, beans, lentils |

#### Gestational Diabetes (Consistent Carbohydrate Choices)

| Better Choices |
|---|
| Whole grains, legumes, non-starchy vegetables, fruit in consistent portions, lean protein paired with carbohydrate |

#### Lower-Fat Choices for Children/Adolescents

| Instead of This | Choose This |
|---|---|
| High-fat cheese | Cheese with 3 g fat or less per serving |
| Fatty ground beef | Ground beef at least 90% lean |
| Refried beans | Canned pinto beans |
| Pasta with white sauce | Pasta with red sauce |
| Sweetened fruit yogurt | Plain fat-free yogurt with fresh fruit |
| Sausage | Canadian bacon or another lower-fat meat |

#### Toddler-Safe vs. Choking-Hazard Foods

| Safer Choices | Choking Hazards to Avoid |
|---|---|
| Soft ripe banana, well-cooked soft vegetables, ground/shredded meat | Hot dogs, whole grapes, bagels, marshmallows, popcorn, chunks of cheese, raisins, raw carrots, nuts, seeds |

#### Pregnancy Food Safety Quick Reference

| Avoid/Limit | Safer Choice |
|---|---|
| Unpasteurized dairy/juice | Pasteurized products |
| Raw/undercooked meat, eggs, seafood | Fully cooked foods |
| High-mercury fish | Lower-mercury fish options |
| Deli meats/hot dogs not heated | Heat until steaming if eaten |
| Alcohol | Avoid during pregnancy |

#### Infant Food Safety Quick Reference

| Avoid Before 12 Months | Why |
|---|---|
| Honey in any form | Botulism risk |
| Whole milk as main drink | Too much renal solute load and not enough iron |
| Choking hazards | Airway obstruction risk |
| Excess juice | Poor nutrient density, diarrhea, dental caries risk |
