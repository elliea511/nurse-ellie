<h2 class="section-divider" id="respiratory-and-neurologic-nutrition">Respiratory & Neurologic Nutrition</h2>

For this section, ask two different questions depending on the condition: **(1) is the work of breathing or moving burning more calories than usual?** and **(2) can this client safely swallow?** Most respiratory questions are about #1; most neurologic questions are about #2.

---

<h3 id="big-picture">Big Picture: Two Different Problems</h3>

| Condition | Core Nutrition Problem | Nursing Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **COPD** | Increased work of breathing → increased calorie needs | High-calorie, high-protein nutrition; reduce fatigue with meals |
| **Parkinson's disease** | Muscle rigidity/tremor → increased calorie needs + chewing/swallowing difficulty | High-calorie intake **and** safe swallowing |
| **Dysphagia (any cause)** | Impaired swallowing → aspiration risk | Positioning, food/liquid texture, slow feeding |
| **Stroke** | Variable — weakness, impaired coordination, possible dysphagia | Confirm swallow safety **before** oral intake |

### Respiratory vs. Neurologic Nutrition Pattern

| If the Problem Is... | Think... |
|---|---|
| Increased work of breathing | Calories and protein need to increase |
| Fatigue while eating | Small frequent meals and rest before meals |
| CO₂ retention concern | Lower-to-moderate carbohydrate pulmonary formula may be used |
| Weakness, poor coordination, or altered mental status | Swallow safety comes before food choice |
| Wet voice, coughing, choking, or pocketing | Aspiration risk until proven otherwise |

<h3 id="copd">COPD Nutrition</h3>

Breathing itself uses energy. In COPD, the increased work of breathing can raise calorie needs well above what the client is eating — at the same time, a full stomach pushes on the diaphragm and makes breathing feel harder, creating a cycle of poor intake and weight loss.

A client with COPD who is losing weight or becoming weak may need **more** calories and protein, not fewer — poor nutrition further weakens the respiratory muscles that are already working overtime.

Core teaching: small, frequent meals (a full stomach worsens dyspnea); rest before meals to conserve energy; choose nutrient-dense, high-calorie, high-protein foods; use a bronchodilator before meals if prescribed; drink fluids between meals rather than with them if early fullness is a problem.

### COPD Meal Planning

| Problem | Nutrition Strategy |
|---|---|
| Dyspnea during meals | Small, frequent meals; avoid large meals |
| Early fullness | Drink fluids between meals rather than with meals |
| Fatigue while eating | Rest before meals; use easy-to-prepare foods |
| Weight loss | High-calorie, high-protein foods and supplements if prescribed |
| Weak respiratory muscles | Adequate protein and calories to prevent muscle wasting |
| Bloating/gas worsening breathing | Limit gas-forming foods if they worsen dyspnea |

### COPD Food Choices

| Better Choices | Why |
|---|---|
| Eggs, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese | High-protein, nutrient-dense |
| Fortified shakes/oral nutrition supplements | Helpful when intake is poor |
| Peanut butter, cheese, avocado | Calorie-dense options if safe to swallow |
| Lean meats, beans/lentils if tolerated | Protein for muscle maintenance |
| Small meals/snacks | Less pressure on the diaphragm than large meals |

A full stomach can make breathing harder because it pushes upward on the diaphragm. This is why “eat more” is not enough teaching by itself — the client needs **more nutrition in smaller amounts**.

<h3 id="pulmonary-formula">Pulmonary Nutrition Formulas</h3>

Some enteral formulas are designed specifically for chronic respiratory disease.

Carbohydrate metabolism produces more **CO₂** per calorie than fat metabolism does. For a client who already struggles to clear CO₂, a formula that's **high-calorie, high-protein, lower-to-moderate carbohydrate, and higher fat** can help reduce the CO₂ load the lungs have to clear.

### Pulmonary Formula Pattern

| Formula Feature | Why It Helps |
|---|---|
| **High-calorie** | Meets needs in a smaller volume |
| **High-protein** | Supports respiratory muscle strength and prevents wasting |
| **Lower-to-moderate carbohydrate** | May reduce CO₂ production |
| **Higher fat** | Provides concentrated calories with less CO₂ production than carbohydrate |

Do not apply this idea to every client automatically. The pulmonary formula pattern is most relevant when the client has chronic respiratory disease and difficulty clearing CO₂.

<h3 id="parkinsons">Parkinson's Disease Nutrition</h3>

Constant muscle rigidity and tremor burn calories continuously, even at rest — on top of that, chewing, swallowing, and self-feeding all become physically harder as the disease progresses.

Muscular rigidity in Parkinson's disease increases metabolic rate, so the nurse should anticipate the need for **increased calorie intake** — this is a frequently tested mechanism.

Encourage high-calorie foods if weight loss is occurring; allow extra time for meals; offer foods that are easy to chew and swallow; consider adaptive utensils; monitor closely for dysphagia as the disease progresses; address constipation (common from slowed GI motility, reduced activity, and medications) with gradual fiber increases plus more fluids.

### Parkinson's Nutrition Problems

| Problem | Why It Happens | Nursing/Nutrition Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Weight loss | Tremor/rigidity increase energy use | High-calorie, nutrient-dense foods |
| Fatigue with meals | Movement and swallowing take effort | Extra time, small meals, rest periods |
| Dysphagia | Disease progression affects swallowing muscles | Safe textures, upright positioning, swallow evaluation if needed |
| Self-feeding difficulty | Tremor/rigidity affects hand control | Adaptive utensils, finger foods if appropriate |
| Constipation | Slowed GI motility, less activity, medications | Gradual fiber increase plus fluids |

### Levodopa/Carbidopa and Protein Timing

Protein can interfere with levodopa absorption for some clients. If the client reports that medication seems less effective after high-protein meals, the provider may recommend spreading protein throughout the day or taking most protein later in the day. Do not tell every client to restrict protein completely — the goal is symptom control while still preventing weight loss and malnutrition.

<h3 id="dysphagia">Dysphagia & Aspiration Precautions</h3>

Dysphagia means difficulty swallowing safely. The danger isn't just discomfort — food, fluid, or saliva can enter the airway (aspiration), risking pneumonia or airway obstruction.

| Aspiration Risk Sign | What It Suggests |
|---|---|
| Coughing or choking during meals | Material may be entering the airway |
| Wet, gurgly voice after swallowing | Liquid sitting at/near the vocal cords |
| Drooling or pocketing food in the cheek | Poor oral control, often on the weaker side |
| Recurrent pneumonia | Possible chronic silent aspiration |

A wet voice, coughing with meals, or pocketing food are **stop-and-reassess** signs — don't continue feeding until swallowing safety is addressed.

Position the client **upright at 90 degrees** for meals (not reclined at 30 degrees — that increases aspiration risk). A slight forward head tilt (**chin tuck**) helps protect the airway during the swallow.

If one side of the mouth is weaker, place food on the **stronger/unaffected side**. Check for pocketed food before offering more. Have the client fully swallow and clear the mouth of food **before** giving liquids — don't use a sip of liquid to "wash down" remaining food, since uncoordinated swallowing of mixed textures raises aspiration risk.

A less obvious dry-mouth/choking-prevention strategy: if dry mouth during meals is contributing to difficulty managing food in the mouth, having the client think of (or taste) a sour, salivation-triggering food — like a lemon slice or a dill pickle — can stimulate saliva production and make swallowing safer.

### Aspiration Precautions During Meals

| Nursing Action | Reason |
|---|---|
| Sit upright at 90 degrees | Reduces aspiration risk |
| Use chin tuck if recommended | Helps protect airway during swallowing |
| Feed slowly | Allows time for safe swallowing |
| Use small bites/sips | Reduces choking risk |
| Avoid distractions | Helps client focus on swallowing |
| Place food on stronger side | Uses the side with better oral control |
| Check for pocketing | Prevents retained food from being aspirated later |
| Keep client upright after meals as prescribed | Reduces reflux/aspiration risk |
| Keep suction available if risk is high | Allows rapid response if aspiration occurs |

### Foods and Liquids That Increase Aspiration Risk

| Higher Risk | Why |
|---|---|
| Thin liquids if thickened liquids are ordered | Move too fast and are easier to aspirate |
| Dry, crumbly foods | Break apart and are harder to control |
| Stringy foods | Harder to chew and form into a safe bolus |
| Mixed textures, such as soup with chunks in thin broth | Require coordinating solids and liquids at the same time |
| Large bites | Harder to chew and swallow safely |

### Aspiration Pneumonia Clues

| Finding | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Fever after meals or recurrent pneumonia | Possible aspiration |
| Wet/gurgly voice | Possible material near vocal cords |
| Coughing during or after meals | Possible airway entry |
| Increased lung sounds/crackles after eating | Possible aspiration-related respiratory change |
| Oxygen desaturation during meals | Possible unsafe swallow or aspiration |

<h3 id="iddsi">The IDDSI Framework</h3>

Older dysphagia diets used inconsistent descriptive terms ("nectar-thick," "honey-thick," "pudding-thick") that varied between facilities and even between staff. Current practice uses the **International Dysphagia Diet Standardisation Initiative (IDDSI)** — a standardized numbered scale from 0–7 covering both liquids and foods.

| IDDSI Level | General Category |
|---|---|
| **0** | Thin liquids (regular water-like consistency) |
| **1–3** | Progressively thicker liquids (slightly → mildly → moderately thick) |
| **4** | Pureed / extremely thick — no chewing required |
| **5** | Minced & moist |
| **6** | Soft & bite-sized |
| **7** | Regular, easy-to-chew texture |

You may still see older terms like "nectar-thick" or "honey-thick" in some ATI material or older facility documentation — these map roughly onto IDDSI's middle liquid levels but are being phased out in favor of the standardized numbered system. Know both, but expect current practice (and increasingly, current exam content) to use IDDSI terminology.

Never offer a thinner liquid consistency than what's prescribed — if thickened liquids are ordered, thin liquids (including plain water) are **not** safe to substitute, even "just this once."

### IDDSI Notes for Test Questions

| If the Question Mentions... | Think... |
|---|---|
| Thin liquid | Regular water-like consistency |
| Thickened liquids | Follow the exact ordered consistency |
| Pureed/extremely thick | No chewing required |
| Minced & moist | Small, moist pieces |
| Soft & bite-sized | Soft foods cut into manageable bites |
| Regular/easy-to-chew | Least restricted texture level |

<h3 id="stroke">Stroke Nutrition</h3>

Stroke can cause weakness, impaired coordination, altered level of consciousness, and dysphagia — any combination of which threatens safe oral intake.

Before any oral intake after a stroke, swallowing ability must be confirmed — often via speech-language pathology evaluation. The client may remain **NPO** until swallow safety is established, even if they appear awake and alert.

During feeding: sit upright, feed slowly with small bites, place food on the stronger side, check for pocketing, avoid distractions, and keep suction available if aspiration risk is significant. After feeding: check the mouth for retained food, keep the client upright as prescribed, and monitor for delayed coughing or respiratory changes.

### Stroke Feeding Notes

| Stroke Finding | Nutrition Concern |
|---|---|
| Facial droop or one-sided weakness | Pocketing food on weak side |
| Slurred speech or weak cough | Possible impaired swallow/airway protection |
| Decreased level of consciousness | Unsafe oral intake |
| Coughing or wet voice after swallow | Aspiration risk |
| Poor coordination | Needs slow feeding and supervision |
| Neglect/inattention | May ignore food on one side of tray or mouth |

### Stroke Meal Setup

| Action | Why |
|---|---|
| Position upright | Reduces aspiration |
| Place food on stronger side | Helps with chewing and oral control |
| Use small bites | Lowers choking risk |
| Check mouth before more food/liquid | Prevents pocketing and aspiration |
| Reduce distractions | Helps focus on swallowing |
| Keep suction available when indicated | Safety if aspiration occurs |

<h3 id="other-neuro">Other Neurologic Conditions Affecting Swallowing</h3>

Dysphagia isn't unique to stroke and Parkinson's — several other neurologic conditions affect swallowing through different mechanisms.

| Condition | How It Affects Swallowing |
|---|---|
| **Myasthenia gravis** | Muscle weakness *worsens with repeated use* — chewing/swallowing may become harder as a meal goes on; eating earlier in the medication dosing cycle (when strength is best) and choosing smaller, less fatiguing meals can help |
| **ALS (amyotrophic lateral sclerosis)** | Progressive muscle weakness, including swallowing muscles — dysphagia typically worsens over time and may eventually require enteral nutrition |
| **Traumatic brain injury** | Increases metabolic rate (hypermetabolic state) in addition to any swallowing impairment from the injury itself |

### Myasthenia Gravis Meal Timing

Myasthenia gravis causes muscle weakness that worsens with repeated use. The safest meal timing is often **earlier in the medication dosing cycle**, when strength is best. Smaller, more frequent meals can reduce fatigue because chewing and swallowing become harder as the meal continues.

### ALS Nutrition Pattern

ALS causes progressive muscle weakness, including the muscles used for chewing and swallowing. As dysphagia worsens, nutrition may shift toward modified textures and eventually enteral nutrition if oral intake is no longer safe or adequate.

### Traumatic Brain Injury Nutrition Pattern

Traumatic brain injury can create a hypermetabolic state. This means the client may need increased calories and protein even before considering any swallowing impairment from the injury itself.

<h3 id="metabolic-demand">Increased Metabolic Demand</h3>

Several conditions across this page share one mechanism: the body is working harder than usual, so it burns more calories than usual, even without any change in diet.

Increased metabolic demand shows up with: infection, fever, burns, trauma, surgery, cancer, COPD, and conditions causing constant muscle activity (Parkinson's rigidity, ALS, TBI). If intake doesn't rise to match, expect weight loss, muscle wasting, poor wound healing, and fatigue.

### Increased Metabolic Demand: What to Feed

| Need | Nutrition Focus |
|---|---|
| Higher calories | Nutrient-dense meals/snacks |
| Higher protein | Tissue repair, muscle maintenance, immune support |
| Poor intake | Oral supplements or nutrition support if needed |
| Weight loss | Monitor trends and intervene early |
| Poor wound healing | Protein, vitamin C, zinc, adequate calories |

### Conditions That Raise Calorie/Protein Needs

| Condition | Why Needs Rise |
|---|---|
| COPD | Work of breathing increases energy use |
| Parkinson's disease | Tremor/rigidity increase metabolic demand |
| ALS | Muscle wasting and progressive weakness increase nutrition risk |
| TBI | Hypermetabolic state |
| Infection/fever | Higher metabolic rate |
| Burns/trauma/surgery | Tissue repair and stress response |
| Cancer | Inflammation, treatment effects, and cachexia risk |

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="food-sources">Food Sources Quick Reference</h2>

#### High-Calorie, High-Protein Choices (COPD, Parkinson's, Increased Metabolic Demand)

| Nutrient-Dense Options |
|---|
| Eggs, cottage cheese, Greek yogurt, fortified shakes/oral nutrition supplements, peanut butter (if safe to swallow), cheese, lean meats, beans/lentils if tolerated |

#### Easier-to-Swallow Food Textures (Dysphagia)

| Better Tolerated | Use Caution With |
|---|---|
| Pureed, minced/moist, or soft bite-sized foods per IDDSI level prescribed; thickened liquids per order | Thin liquids if thickened liquids are ordered; dry, crumbly, or stringy foods; mixed-texture foods (e.g., soup with chunks in thin broth) |

#### Salivation-Stimulating Foods (Dry Mouth / Choking Prevention)

| Foods |
|---|
| Lemon slices, dill pickles, or other sour/tart foods — used to stimulate saliva, not as a meal component |

#### COPD Meal Ideas

| Goal | Examples |
|---|---|
| High-calorie/high-protein snack | Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, eggs, cheese, peanut butter if safe |
| Less fatigue while eating | Small frequent meals, oral supplements, easy-prep foods |
| Less early fullness | Fluids between meals, not with meals if fullness is a problem |

#### Dysphagia Foods to Avoid Unless Approved

| Food Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Thin liquids if restricted | Water, coffee, tea, juice without thickener |
| Dry/crumbly foods | Crackers, dry rice, crumbly cornbread |
| Stringy foods | Celery, pineapple, tough meats |
| Mixed textures | Soup with thin broth and chunks, cereal with milk |

| Modern dysphagia diet terminology | IDDSI levels 0–7, replacing older "nectar/honey-thick" language |
| Stroke + new dysphagia concern | Confirm swallow safety before any oral intake — may require NPO status |
| Muscle weakness that worsens with repeated chewing | Myasthenia gravis — eat earlier in the medication cycle, smaller meals |
| Progressive neuromuscular weakness + worsening swallow over time | ALS — may eventually require enteral nutrition |
| Infection, burns, trauma, surgery, cancer, or constant muscle activity | Increased metabolic demand — calorie/protein needs rise |
| Full stomach worsens dyspnea | Small frequent meals |

| Wet/gurgly voice after swallowing | Possible aspiration |
| Recurrent pneumonia | Consider silent aspiration |
| Myasthenia gravis meal fatigue | Schedule meals when medication effect is strongest |
