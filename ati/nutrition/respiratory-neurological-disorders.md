<h2 class="section-divider" id="respiratory-and-neurologic-nutrition">Respiratory &amp; Neurologic Nutrition</h2>

<div class="memory">
For this section, ask two different questions depending on the condition: <strong>(1) is the work of breathing or moving burning more calories than usual?</strong> and <strong>(2) can this client safely swallow?</strong> Most respiratory questions are about #1; most neurologic questions are about #2.
</div>

---

<h3 id="big-picture">Big Picture: Two Different Problems</h3>

| Condition | Core Nutrition Problem | Nursing Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **COPD** | Increased work of breathing → increased calorie needs | High-calorie, high-protein nutrition; reduce fatigue with meals |
| **Parkinson's disease** | Muscle rigidity/tremor → increased calorie needs + chewing/swallowing difficulty | High-calorie intake **and** safe swallowing |
| **Dysphagia (any cause)** | Impaired swallowing → aspiration risk | Positioning, food/liquid texture, slow feeding |
| **Stroke** | Variable — weakness, impaired coordination, possible dysphagia | Confirm swallow safety **before** oral intake |

---

<h3 id="copd">COPD Nutrition</h3>

Breathing itself uses energy. In COPD, the increased work of breathing can raise calorie needs well above what the client is eating — at the same time, a full stomach pushes on the diaphragm and makes breathing feel harder, creating a cycle of poor intake and weight loss.

<div class="danger">
A client with COPD who is losing weight or becoming weak may need <strong>more</strong> calories and protein, not fewer — poor nutrition further weakens the respiratory muscles that are already working overtime.
</div>

<div class="teaching">
Core teaching: small, frequent meals (a full stomach worsens dyspnea); rest before meals to conserve energy; choose nutrient-dense, high-calorie, high-protein foods; use a bronchodilator before meals if prescribed; drink fluids between meals rather than with them if early fullness is a problem.
</div>

---

<h3 id="pulmonary-formula">Pulmonary Nutrition Formulas</h3>

Some enteral formulas are designed specifically for chronic respiratory disease.

<div class="patho">
Carbohydrate metabolism produces more <strong>CO₂</strong> per calorie than fat metabolism does. For a client who already struggles to clear CO₂, a formula that's <strong>high-calorie, high-protein, lower-to-moderate carbohydrate, and higher fat</strong> can help reduce the CO₂ load the lungs have to clear.
</div>

---

<h3 id="parkinsons">Parkinson's Disease Nutrition</h3>

Constant muscle rigidity and tremor burn calories continuously, even at rest — on top of that, chewing, swallowing, and self-feeding all become physically harder as the disease progresses.

<div class="nclex">
Muscular rigidity in Parkinson's disease increases metabolic rate, so the nurse should anticipate the need for <strong>increased calorie intake</strong> — this is a frequently tested mechanism.
</div>

<div class="teaching">
Encourage high-calorie foods if weight loss is occurring; allow extra time for meals; offer foods that are easy to chew and swallow; consider adaptive utensils; monitor closely for dysphagia as the disease progresses; address constipation (common from slowed GI motility, reduced activity, and medications) with gradual fiber increases plus more fluids.
</div>

<h3 id="dysphagia">Dysphagia &amp; Aspiration Precautions</h3>

Dysphagia means difficulty swallowing safely. The danger isn't just discomfort — food, fluid, or saliva can enter the airway (aspiration), risking pneumonia or airway obstruction.

| Aspiration Risk Sign | What It Suggests |
|---|---|
| Coughing or choking during meals | Material may be entering the airway |
| Wet, gurgly voice after swallowing | Liquid sitting at/near the vocal cords |
| Drooling or pocketing food in the cheek | Poor oral control, often on the weaker side |
| Recurrent pneumonia | Possible chronic silent aspiration |

<div class="danger">
A wet voice, coughing with meals, or pocketing food are <strong>stop-and-reassess</strong> signs — don't continue feeding until swallowing safety is addressed.
</div>

<div class="nclex">
Position the client <strong>upright at 90 degrees</strong> for meals (not reclined at 30 degrees — that increases aspiration risk). A slight forward head tilt (<strong>chin tuck</strong>) helps protect the airway during the swallow.
</div>

<div class="teaching">
If one side of the mouth is weaker, place food on the <strong>stronger/unaffected side</strong>. Check for pocketed food before offering more. Have the client fully swallow and clear the mouth of food <strong>before</strong> giving liquids — don't use a sip of liquid to "wash down" remaining food, since uncoordinated swallowing of mixed textures raises aspiration risk.
</div>

<div class="ati-tip">
A less obvious dry-mouth/choking-prevention strategy: if dry mouth during meals is contributing to difficulty managing food in the mouth, having the client think of (or taste) a sour, salivation-triggering food — like a lemon slice or a dill pickle — can stimulate saliva production and make swallowing safer.
</div>

<h3 id="iddsi">The IDDSI Framework</h3>

Older dysphagia diets used inconsistent descriptive terms ("nectar-thick," "honey-thick," "pudding-thick") that varied between facilities and even between staff. Current practice uses the **International Dysphagia Diet Standardisation Initiative (IDDSI)** — a standardized numbered scale from 0–7 covering both liquids and foods.

| IDDSI Level | General Category |
|---|---|
| **0** | Thin liquids (regular water-like consistency) |
| **1–3** | Progressively thicker liquids (slightly → mildly → moderately thick) |
| **4** | Pureed / extremely thick — no chewing required |
| **5** | Minced &amp; moist |
| **6** | Soft &amp; bite-sized |
| **7** | Regular, easy-to-chew texture |

<div class="note">
You may still see older terms like "nectar-thick" or "honey-thick" in some ATI material or older facility documentation — these map roughly onto IDDSI's middle liquid levels but are being phased out in favor of the standardized numbered system. Know both, but expect current practice (and increasingly, current exam content) to use IDDSI terminology.
</div>

<div class="danger">
Never offer a thinner liquid consistency than what's prescribed — if thickened liquids are ordered, thin liquids (including plain water) are <strong>not</strong> safe to substitute, even "just this once."
</div>

<h3 id="stroke">Stroke Nutrition</h3>

Stroke can cause weakness, impaired coordination, altered level of consciousness, and dysphagia — any combination of which threatens safe oral intake.

<div class="nclex">
Before any oral intake after a stroke, swallowing ability must be confirmed — often via speech-language pathology evaluation. The client may remain <strong>NPO</strong> until swallow safety is established, even if they appear awake and alert.
</div>

<div class="teaching">
During feeding: sit upright, feed slowly with small bites, place food on the stronger side, check for pocketing, avoid distractions, and keep suction available if aspiration risk is significant. After feeding: check the mouth for retained food, keep the client upright as prescribed, and monitor for delayed coughing or respiratory changes.
</div>

<h3 id="other-neuro">Other Neurologic Conditions Affecting Swallowing</h3>

Dysphagia isn't unique to stroke and Parkinson's — several other neurologic conditions affect swallowing through different mechanisms.

| Condition | How It Affects Swallowing |
|---|---|
| **Myasthenia gravis** | Muscle weakness *worsens with repeated use* — chewing/swallowing may become harder as a meal goes on; eating earlier in the medication dosing cycle (when strength is best) and choosing smaller, less fatiguing meals can help |
| **ALS (amyotrophic lateral sclerosis)** | Progressive muscle weakness, including swallowing muscles — dysphagia typically worsens over time and may eventually require enteral nutrition |
| **Traumatic brain injury** | Increases metabolic rate (hypermetabolic state) in addition to any swallowing impairment from the injury itself |

---

<h3 id="metabolic-demand">Increased Metabolic Demand</h3>

Several conditions across this page share one mechanism: the body is working harder than usual, so it burns more calories than usual, even without any change in diet.

<div class="memory">
Increased metabolic demand shows up with: infection, fever, burns, trauma, surgery, cancer, COPD, and conditions causing constant muscle activity (Parkinson's rigidity, ALS, TBI). If intake doesn't rise to match, expect weight loss, muscle wasting, poor wound healing, and fatigue.
</div>

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="food-sources">Food Sources Quick Reference</h2>

#### High-Calorie, High-Protein Choices (COPD, Parkinson's, Increased Metabolic Demand)

| Nutrient-Dense Options |
|---|
| Eggs, cottage cheese, Greek yogurt, fortified shakes/oral nutrition supplements, peanut butter (if safe to swallow), cheese, lean meats, beans/lentils if tolerated |

#### Easier-to-Swallow Food Textures (Dysphagia)

| Better Tolerated | Use Caution With |
|---|---|
| Pureed, minced/moist, or soft bite-sized foods per IDDSI level prescribed; thickened liquids per order | Thin liquids if thickened liquids are ordered; dry, crumbly, or stringy foods; mixed-texture foods (e.g., soup with chunks in thin broth) |

#### Salivation-Stimulating Foods (Dry Mouth / Choking Prevention)

| Foods |
|---|
| Lemon slices, dill pickles, or other sour/tart foods — used to stimulate saliva, not as a meal component |

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="section-summary">Section Summary &amp; Test-Taking Tips</h2>

| If the Question Says... | Think... |
|---|---|
| COPD + weight loss/fatigue with eating | High-calorie, high-protein foods; small frequent meals |
| Pulmonary formula for chronic respiratory disease | High-calorie, high-protein, lower-to-moderate carb, higher fat — reduces CO₂ load |
| Parkinson's disease + rigidity/tremor | Increased metabolic rate → increased calorie needs |
| Coughing, choking, wet voice, or pocketing food during meals | Aspiration risk — stop and reassess |
| Aspiration precaution positioning | Upright 90 degrees; chin tuck if recommended — not reclined at 30 degrees |
| Food placement with one-sided weakness | Place on the stronger/unaffected side |
| Before giving liquids | Make sure the mouth is clear of food first |
| Thickened liquids are ordered | Never substitute thin liquids, even briefly |
| Dry mouth contributing to choking risk | A sour food (lemon, pickle) can stimulate saliva |
| Modern dysphagia diet terminology | IDDSI levels 0–7, replacing older "nectar/honey-thick" language |
| Stroke + new dysphagia concern | Confirm swallow safety before any oral intake — may require NPO status |
| Muscle weakness that worsens with repeated chewing | Myasthenia gravis — eat earlier in the medication cycle, smaller meals |
| Progressive neuromuscular weakness + worsening swallow over time | ALS — may eventually require enteral nutrition |
| Infection, burns, trauma, surgery, cancer, or constant muscle activity | Increased metabolic demand — calorie/protein needs rise |
