<h2 class="section-divider" id="cancer-immunity-and-wound-healing-nutrition">Cancer, Immunity &amp; Wound Healing Nutrition</h2>

<div class="memory">
The theme across this whole section: <strong>increased need + decreased intake.</strong> Cancer, infection, and wounds all raise calorie/protein demand at the exact same time something else (nausea, pain, mouth sores, fatigue) is making it harder to eat.
</div>

---

<h3 id="big-picture">Big Picture: Increased Need, Decreased Intake</h3>

| Problem | Why Nutrition Matters |
|---|---|
| **Cancer prevention** | Healthy weight, activity, and diet quality can reduce risk for some cancers |
| **Cancer treatment** | Increases metabolic demand while simultaneously reducing appetite/intake |
| **Wounds/pressure injuries** | Require protein, calories, vitamin C, zinc, vitamin A, and iron to heal |
| **Infection** | Increases metabolic rate and nutrient needs |
| **Immunocompromised state** (neutropenia, autoimmune disease on immunosuppressants, HIV/AIDS, transplant) | Doesn't change nutrient needs, but makes food safety a priority |

---

<h3 id="prevention">Cancer Prevention</h3>

<div class="ati-tip">
At least <strong>150 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise per week</strong> and limiting high-calorie foods/beverages are both tied to reduced obesity-related cancer risk — excess adipose tissue affects hormones and inflammation in ways that can promote cancer cell growth.
</div>

<div class="note">
ATI favors realistic, sustainable lifestyle teaching over crash diets or eliminating entire food groups for cancer prevention.
</div>

---

<h3 id="treatment">Cancer Nutrition During Treatment</h3>

Cancer and its treatments can increase metabolic demand while simultaneously causing nausea, vomiting, fatigue, mucositis, altered taste, and early satiety — a client can need *more* nutrition while being *less* able to eat.

<div class="danger">
Cancer clients can lose weight quickly because the body may need significantly more calories while intake is dropping at the same time — both directions working against the client at once.
</div>

<div class="teaching">
Core strategy: small, frequent, nutrient-dense meals; high-calorie/high-protein foods and drinks; eat when appetite is strongest (often earlier in the day); manage nausea proactively before meals if prescribed; avoid filling up on low-calorie fluids when intake is already a struggle.
</div>

<div class="danger">
<strong>Counterintuitive but well-documented:</strong> high-dose antioxidant supplements (vitamin C, vitamin E, beta-carotene, selenium) are generally <strong>discouraged during active chemotherapy or radiation</strong>. Both treatments work partly by causing oxidative damage to cancer cells — antioxidants can blunt that effect and may protect tumor cells along with healthy ones. Getting antioxidants from food is different and still encouraged; it's the concentrated supplement form during active treatment that's the concern.
</div>

---

<h3 id="cachexia">Cancer Cachexia vs. Simple Starvation</h3>

<div class="patho">
Cancer cachexia isn't the same mechanism as simple starvation, and that distinction matters for expectations: in simple starvation, calorie supplementation reliably reverses weight/muscle loss. In cancer cachexia, tumor-driven inflammatory cytokines cause ongoing muscle wasting that <strong>doesn't fully reverse with calories alone</strong> — nutrition support still matters (it slows decline and supports comfort/function), but it isn't expected to "fix" cachexia the way refeeding fixes simple starvation.
</div>

<div class="note">
Don't expect a test answer that promises calorie supplementation will fully reverse cachexia-related muscle wasting — the realistic goal is slowing decline and maximizing comfort and function, not full reversal.
</div>

<h3 id="mucositis">Mucositis &amp; Stomatitis</h3>

Mucositis (inflammation of mucous membranes — can affect the mouth specifically, where it's called **stomatitis**, or extend through the GI tract) is one of the most heavily tested topics in this whole content area, so it's worth knowing in real depth, not just the food list.

#### Causes

<div class="patho">
Most often caused by <strong>chemotherapy</strong> (especially 5-fluorouracil and methotrexate) or <strong>radiation therapy to the head/neck</strong>. Both damage the rapidly dividing cells of the mucosal lining the same way they damage cancer cells. It can also occur with neutropenia/infection, immunosuppression, poorly fitting dentures, or poor oral hygiene.
</div>

#### Grading and Why It Matters for Diet

ATI-style questions sometimes describe a severity level without naming "mucositis" directly — recognizing the grade tells you what diet is even possible.

| WHO Grade | Presentation | Diet Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **0** | No mucositis | Regular diet |
| **1** | Soreness/redness, no ulcers | Regular diet, modify as needed for comfort |
| **2** | Erythema + ulcers, **solid food still tolerated** | Soft/modified diet |
| **3** | Ulcers, **liquid diet only** | Oral nutrition supplements, high-calorie/high-protein liquids |
| **4** | Ulcers so severe **oral intake isn't possible** | Enteral or parenteral nutrition likely needed |

<div class="danger">
A client at Grade 3–4 mucositis isn't a "encourage more food" situation — they may not be physically able to eat orally at all. Recognize when the answer shifts from <strong>diet teaching</strong> to <strong>escalating the route of nutrition support</strong>.
</div>

#### Prevention: Oral Cryotherapy

<div class="ati-tip">
For clients receiving <strong>bolus 5-FU or melphalan</strong> specifically, holding <strong>ice chips in the mouth</strong> starting just before the infusion and continuing through it (commonly ~30 minutes) causes localized vasoconstriction that reduces drug exposure to the oral mucosa — a real, evidence-based, low-cost nursing intervention to <em>prevent</em> mucositis, not just manage it after it develops.
</div>

#### Nutrition &amp; Comfort Teaching

| Better Tolerated | Avoid / Limit |
|---|---|
| Cold or room-temperature foods, soft/moist/smooth textures, high-calorie shakes, popsicles | Spicy, salty, acidic/citrus, tomato-based, rough/crunchy, very hot foods or drinks, alcohol-based mouthwash |

<div class="danger">
Citrus is a contradiction worth knowing precisely: it's generally <strong>avoided</strong> with mucositis because it irritates open sores, but citrus can still be an appropriate recommendation for a client whose main problem is <strong>dry mouth</strong> without active mucositis — the two symptoms call for different (sometimes opposite) food choices.
</div>

<div class="teaching">
Oral care: soft-bristled toothbrush; rinse with 0.9% sodium chloride or a baking soda/water mixture; avoid alcohol-based mouthwash and <strong>glycerin swabs</strong> (these dry and further irritate tissue). A straw can help liquids bypass painful areas of the mouth. Frozen foods (popsicles, frozen banana, ice cream) can numb the mouth and ease pain while still providing calories.
</div>

#### Complications to Recognize

Mucositis isn't just painful — broken mucosal integrity is also a portal of entry for infection, which matters enormously in a client who may already be immunocompromised from treatment.

| Finding | Possible Complication |
|---|---|
| White patches/plaques on the oral mucosa | **Candidiasis (oral thrush)** — common secondary infection, especially with immunosuppression; often treated with nystatin oral suspension |
| Painful grouped vesicles/ulcers, possible fever | **HSV reactivation** — herpes simplex virus can reactivate with immunosuppression and increase metabolic demand (see Infection section above) |
| Fever in a client with both mucositis and neutropenia | Broken mucosa + low neutrophils = increased risk the infection becomes systemic (bacteremia) — report promptly |

<div class="nclex">
Don't assume every oral finding during cancer treatment is "just mucositis." White patches, new ulcer patterns, or fever in this population deserve assessment for a <strong>secondary infection</strong>, not just more comfort measures.
</div>

<h3 id="altered-taste">Altered Taste &amp; Poor Appetite</h3>

<div class="ati-tip">
Chemotherapy can cause a <strong>metallic taste</strong>. Using <strong>plastic utensils</strong> instead of metal can reduce this effect. Sucking on mints can also help override the metallic taste.
</div>

<div class="teaching">
For poor appetite generally: small frequent meals, eat the largest meal when appetite is best, keep easy snacks on hand, use oral nutrition supplements if prescribed, and don't fill up on low-calorie beverages before eating.
</div>

<h3 id="neutropenia">Immunocompromised Clients &amp; Food Safety</h3>

Neutropenia is the classic example ATI uses, but it's not the only reason a client needs this teaching. **Any client with reduced immune function is at the same elevated risk from unsafe food** — the food safety teaching below applies just as much to these other populations:

| Population | Why They're at Risk |
|---|---|
| **Neutropenia** (chemo, radiation, bone marrow suppression) | Low neutrophil count directly impairs the ability to fight infection |
| **Autoimmune disease on immunosuppressive therapy** (e.g., lupus, rheumatoid arthritis on corticosteroids, biologics, or other DMARDs) | The medication suppresses immune response as a side effect of treating the autoimmune disease |
| **HIV/AIDS** | Progressive loss of immune function (specifically CD4 T-cells) |
| **Transplant recipients** | Lifelong immunosuppressive therapy to prevent organ rejection also suppresses infection-fighting ability |

<div class="danger">
Don't mentally file food safety teaching under "cancer only." If the question describes <strong>any</strong> client with reduced immune function — autoimmune disease on immunosuppressants, HIV/AIDS, post-transplant, or neutropenia from any cause — the same food safety principles apply.
</div>

Neutropenia specifically can be quantified by lab value, which is why it's the example most often used to teach the concept:

| Severity | Absolute Neutrophil Count (ANC) |
|---|---|
| Normal | ~1,500–7,000 cells/µL (varies by lab) |
| Mild | 1,000–1,500 cells/µL |
| Moderate | 500–1,000 cells/µL |
| **Severe** | Less than **500 cells/µL** |

<div class="danger">
Neutropenic precautions are typically started when ANC is <strong>below 1,000</strong>, with stricter precautions often used below <strong>500</strong> (severe neutropenia) — the lower the ANC, the higher the infection risk.
</div>

<div class="teaching">
Food safety teaching — for <strong>any</strong> immunocompromised client, regardless of cause: wash hands before food prep/eating; cook foods thoroughly (avoid raw or undercooked meat, seafood, poultry, eggs); avoid unpasteurized dairy/juice; use a <strong>separate cutting board</strong> for raw poultry/meat to prevent cross-contamination; avoid moldy or expired foods.
</div>

| Food Safety Point | ATI Teaching |
|---|---|
| Leftovers | Use within **24 hr** |
| Home-canned goods | Use within 1 year; boil for **10 min** before eating |
| Hot, cooked food | Keep above **60°C (140°F)** |
| Frozen foods | Thaw in the **refrigerator**, not on the counter |

<h3 id="infection">Infection &amp; Increased Nutrition Needs</h3>

<div class="nclex">
A secondary infection (for example, herpes simplex virus) triggers an inflammatory response that <strong>increases metabolic rate</strong> — this is a reason to increase nutritional intake even if the infection seems minor on the surface.
</div>

<h3 id="wound-healing">Wound Healing Nutrition</h3>

| Nutrient | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Protein** | Builds and repairs tissue; supports collagen formation |
| **Calories** | Provide the energy needed for the healing process itself |
| **Vitamin A** | Supports epithelial tissue repair and immune function |
| **Vitamin C** | Required for collagen synthesis |
| **Zinc** | Supports tissue repair and immune function |
| **Iron** | Supports oxygen delivery to healing tissue via hemoglobin |
| **Fluids** | Support tissue perfusion and hydration |

<div class="danger">
Poor wound healing is not only a skin problem — it's frequently a <strong>nutrition</strong> problem. A client with a non-healing wound and poor intake should prompt a nutrition assessment, not just a wound-care change.
</div>

<h3 id="burns">Burns: The Most Extreme Case of Increased Metabolic Demand</h3>

Burns push the "increased need, decreased intake" theme to its most extreme version. A major burn can roughly **double** resting metabolic rate, while protein is lost both through urine and directly through wound exudate — protein catabolism can exceed 150 g/day (close to half a pound of muscle), independent of how much the client eats.

| Concept | ATI-Relevant Detail |
|---|---|
| **Calorie needs** | Markedly increased — often well above standard formulas, individualized to burn size |
| **Protein needs** | Substantially higher than standard wound healing — commonly **1.5–2 g/kg/day** |
| **Preferred macronutrient profile** | High-carbohydrate, lower-fat enteral feeding is generally preferred — this combination supports positive nitrogen balance better than high-fat feeding and is associated with lower pneumonia rates |
| **Glucose control** | The stress response itself can cause hyperglycemia, even in clients without diabetes — may require insulin and temporary carbohydrate moderation |
| **Weight monitoring** | Unreliable early on due to significant fluid shifts/edema — don't trust early post-burn weights for nutrition status |

<div class="danger">
Burn clients need significantly more <strong>protein and calories</strong> than almost any other patient population on this page — malnutrition in this group directly impairs wound healing, immune function, and survival, so nutrition support is started early and aggressively (enteral preferred over parenteral when the gut can be used).
</div>

<div class="note">
The same wound-healing micronutrients matter here too — <strong>vitamin C, zinc, and vitamin A</strong> — but the calorie and protein targets are higher than for routine wound healing because of the degree of hypermetabolism involved.
</div>

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="food-sources">Food Sources Quick Reference</h2>

#### Better Tolerated With Mucositis

| Better Choices | Avoid/Limit |
|---|---|
| Cold or room-temperature foods, popsicles, frozen banana, ice cream, high-calorie/high-protein shakes | Spicy foods, salty foods, citrus, tomato juice, rough/crunchy foods, very hot foods/drinks |

<div class="note">
Remember: the food list only applies when oral intake is actually possible (WHO Grade 0–3). At Grade 4, the focus shifts entirely to enteral/parenteral nutrition support.
</div>

#### Wound Healing Nutrients

| Nutrient | Food Sources |
|---|---|
| Protein | Eggs, meat, poultry, fish, milk, yogurt, cottage cheese, beans |
| Vitamin A | Carrots, sweet potatoes, spinach, kale, liver |
| Vitamin C | Citrus fruits, strawberries, bell peppers, broccoli, tomatoes |
| Zinc | Meat, shellfish, legumes, seeds, nuts |
| Iron | Red meat, fortified grains, beans, lentils, leafy greens |

#### High-Calorie, High-Protein Choices (Cancer Treatment, Wound Healing, Burns)

| Nutrient-Dense Options |
|---|
| Eggs, cottage cheese, Greek yogurt, fortified shakes/oral nutrition supplements, peanut butter (if tolerated), cheese, lean meats, beans/lentils |

<div class="note">
For burns specifically, these same foods apply, but the <strong>quantity</strong> needed is significantly higher (protein target 1.5–2 g/kg/day) — enteral nutrition support is often started early rather than relying on oral intake alone to meet such elevated needs.
</div>

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="section-summary">Section Summary &amp; Test-Taking Tips</h2>

| If the Question Says... | Think... |
|---|---|
| Cancer prevention teaching | 150 min/week moderate exercise; limit high-calorie foods/beverages |
| Cancer treatment + poor intake/weight loss | High-calorie, high-protein intake is usually needed |
| Cachexia + calorie supplementation | Slows decline; does **not** fully reverse the wasting the way refeeding reverses starvation |
| Mouth sores after chemo/radiation | Mucositis — cold/room-temp, soft, bland foods; avoid spicy/salty/acidic |
| Mucositis + ulcers but solid food tolerated | WHO Grade 2 |
| Mucositis + liquid diet only | WHO Grade 3 |
| Mucositis + oral intake not possible | WHO Grade 4 — needs enteral/parenteral nutrition, not just diet teaching |
| Client receiving bolus 5-FU or melphalan | Oral cryotherapy (ice chips during infusion) helps **prevent** mucositis |
| Citrus + mucositis vs. citrus + dry mouth | Avoid with active mucositis; can be appropriate for dry mouth alone |
| Mucositis oral care | Soft toothbrush; saline or baking soda rinse; avoid glycerin swabs |
| White patches in the mouth during cancer treatment | Consider oral candidiasis (thrush), not just mucositis |
| Fever + mucositis + neutropenia | Risk for systemic infection — report promptly |
| Metallic taste during chemo | Use plastic utensils |
| ANC below 1,000 (especially below 500) | Neutropenic precautions; food safety is priority |
| Autoimmune disease + immunosuppressive therapy, HIV/AIDS, or transplant recipient | Same food safety precautions as neutropenia apply — not just a "cancer" teaching point |
| Neutropenic + leftovers | Use within 24 hr |
| Neutropenic + home-canned food | Use within 1 year; boil 10 min |
| Neutropenic + hot food | Keep above 140°F (60°C) |
| Neutropenic + frozen food | Thaw in the refrigerator |
| Secondary infection (e.g., HSV) | Increases metabolic rate — increase nutrition intake |
| Poor wound healing | Think protein, vitamin A, vitamin C, zinc, iron, fluids — a nutrition problem, not just a skin problem |
| Burn injury | Protein 1.5–2 g/kg/day; markedly increased calorie needs; high-carb/lower-fat enteral feeding preferred; expect stress hyperglycemia |
| Burn client's weight shortly after injury | Unreliable for nutrition status — fluid shifts/edema distort it |
| Antioxidant supplements (vitamin C/E, beta-carotene, selenium) during active chemo/radiation | Generally discouraged — may protect tumor cells from treatment along with healthy cells |
