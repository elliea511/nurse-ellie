<h2 class="section-divider" id="medication-and-food-interactions">Medication &amp; Food Interactions</h2>

---

<h3 id="big-picture">Big Picture: Types of Interactions</h3>

Medication-food interactions fall into a few repeating patterns:

- **Food/nutrient increases the drug's effect** — can cause toxicity
- **Food/nutrient decreases the drug's effect** — drug doesn't work as well
- **The drug changes nutrient levels in the body** — may need monitoring or replacement
- **The drug requires consistent intake of a nutrient** — sudden changes make dosing unpredictable
- **The drug needs to be taken with or without food** for absorption or GI tolerance
- **The drug and a specific food must never be combined** — a genuine safety risk, not just a minor effect

<h3 id="levothyroxine">Levothyroxine</h3>

Food and several minerals reduce how much levothyroxine actually gets absorbed.

- Take on an **empty stomach**, typically **30–60 minutes before breakfast**, with plain water.
- **Separate from calcium, iron, and antacids** by at least a few hours — all three can bind the medication and significantly reduce absorption.
- Coffee taken too close to the dose can also reduce absorption.

---

<h3 id="bisphosphonates">Bisphosphonates</h3>

Used for osteoporosis (e.g., alendronate). Two separate concerns here: absorption and esophageal irritation.

- Take with **plain water only** — first thing in the morning, on an empty stomach.
- **Stay upright (sitting or standing) for at least 30–60 minutes** after the dose and don't lie back down — this isn't about absorption, it's to prevent the medication from sitting against the esophageal lining and causing irritation or esophagitis.
- Wait the full recommended time before eating or drinking anything else.

---

<h3 id="tetracyclines">Tetracyclines &amp; Fluoroquinolones</h3>

Both antibiotic classes bind to certain minerals in the gut, which blocks absorption of the drug itself.

- **Separate from dairy, calcium, iron, magnesium, and antacids** — these minerals chelate (bind) the antibiotic, reducing how much is actually absorbed.
- This means a glass of milk taken with the dose can meaningfully reduce the antibiotic's effectiveness, not just cause mild GI upset.

---

<h3 id="lithium">Lithium</h3>

Lithium has a narrow safety margin, and the kidneys handle it similarly to sodium — which is exactly why sodium and fluid status matter so much.

**Dehydration or low sodium intake increases lithium toxicity risk** — the kidneys reabsorb more lithium along with sodium when sodium is low, raising blood lithium levels. Teaching: keep sodium and fluid intake **consistent** day to day; don't start a low-sodium diet or significantly increase fluid loss (vomiting, diarrhea, heavy sweating, diuretics) without medical guidance, since either direction can swing lithium levels.

---

<h3 id="diuretics">Diuretics</h3>

| Diuretic Type | Effect on Potassium | Teaching |
|---|---|---|
| **Loop diuretics** (e.g., furosemide) | Causes potassium **loss** | May need potassium-rich foods unless contraindicated |
| **Thiazide diuretics** (e.g., hydrochlorothiazide) | Causes potassium **loss** | Same as above |
| **Potassium-sparing diuretics** (e.g., spironolactone) | Causes potassium **retention** | Avoid excess potassium intake and potassium-containing salt substitutes |

---

<h3 id="digoxin">Digoxin</h3>

**Low potassium increases digoxin toxicity risk** — even a "normal-ish" potassium that's drifted low can be enough to push digoxin levels into a toxic range.

<div class="danger">
Report nausea, visual changes (halos, blurred or yellow-tinged vision), or new dysrhythmias promptly — these are classic digoxin toxicity signs, and checking potassium is one of the first things that should happen.
</div>

---

<h3 id="corticosteroids">Corticosteroids</h3>

Long-term corticosteroid use affects glucose, sodium/fluid balance, and bone health all at once.

- Can **increase blood glucose** — monitor more closely, even in clients without diabetes.
- Cause **sodium and fluid retention** — limit sodium if hypertension or edema is a concern.
- Contribute to **bone loss** with long-term use — calcium and vitamin D intake become more important.

---

<h3 id="isoniazid">Isoniazid</h3>

Used to treat tuberculosis. **Isoniazid depletes vitamin B6 (pyridoxine)**, which can cause peripheral neuropathy if untreated. Pyridoxine supplementation is commonly prescribed alongside isoniazid specifically to prevent this.

---

<h3 id="phenytoin">Phenytoin</h3>

An anticonvulsant with two separate nutrition connections.

- Can **reduce folate and vitamin D levels** over time with long-term use — monitor and supplement as needed.
- **Continuous enteral tube feeding can reduce phenytoin absorption** — the two compete in the gut. The usual fix is to **hold the tube feeding for a period before and after the dose** (per facility protocol) rather than giving them simultaneously.

---

<h3 id="metformin-contrast">Metformin &amp; Contrast Dye</h3>

This isn't a food interaction, but it's a medication-administration-timing issue ATI tests right alongside the others.

**Metformin is typically held around procedures using IV iodinated contrast dye**, especially in clients with reduced kidney function. Contrast can transiently impair kidney function; since metformin is cleared by the kidneys, impaired clearance allows it to accumulate.

<div class="danger">
Metformin accumulation can cause <strong>lactic acidosis</strong> — rare, but serious, with a high mortality rate when it occurs. The typical pattern: hold metformin at the time of the contrast study, and don't resume until kidney function is confirmed stable (commonly reassessed at about 48 hours).
</div>

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="quick-reference">Quick Reference: What to Separate From What</h2>

| Medication | Separate From | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Levothyroxine | Calcium, iron, antacids, coffee | These reduce absorption |
| Bisphosphonates | All food and drink except plain water | Reduces absorption; also irritates esophagus if taken lying down |
| Tetracyclines/fluoroquinolones | Dairy, calcium, iron, magnesium, antacids | These minerals chelate and block absorption |
| Phenytoin | Continuous tube feeding | Tube feeding reduces absorption — hold feeding around dose |

#### Medication → Nutrient It Depletes or Requires Monitoring

| Medication | Nutrient Effect |
|---|---|
| Isoniazid | Depletes vitamin B6 — often given with pyridoxine |
| Phenytoin (long-term) | Depletes folate and vitamin D |
| Corticosteroids (long-term) | Raises glucose, sodium/fluid retention, bone loss (calcium/vitamin D needs increase) |
| Loop/thiazide diuretics | Lowers potassium |
| Potassium-sparing diuretics | Raises potassium |
| Lithium | Levels rise with dehydration/low sodium, fall with high sodium/fluid intake |
| Digoxin | Toxicity risk rises with low potassium |

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="section-summary">Section Summary &amp; Test-Taking Tips</h2>

| If the Question Says... | Think... |
|---|---|
| Levothyroxine + breakfast/calcium/iron | Take on empty stomach; separate from minerals |
| Bisphosphonate + lying down after the dose | Stay upright — esophageal irritation risk |
| Antibiotic (tetracycline/fluoroquinolone) + dairy at the same time | Reduced absorption — separate doses |
| Client on lithium + dehydration or new low-sodium diet | Risk for lithium toxicity — keep sodium/fluid consistent |
| Furosemide or hydrochlorothiazide | Potassium loss |
| Spironolactone | Potassium retention — avoid excess potassium/salt substitutes |
| Digoxin + nausea, visual changes, new dysrhythmia | Suspect toxicity — check potassium |
| Long-term corticosteroid use | Monitor glucose, limit sodium, support calcium/vitamin D |
| Isoniazid | Give with vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) to prevent neuropathy |
| Phenytoin + continuous tube feeding | Reduced absorption — hold feeding around dose |
| Phenytoin, long-term | Monitor folate and vitamin D |
| Metformin + upcoming IV contrast study | Hold metformin — lactic acidosis risk if kidney function drops |
