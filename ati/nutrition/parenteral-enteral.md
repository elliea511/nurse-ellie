# Enteral & Parenteral Nutrition

This section covers nutrition support when a client cannot meet needs by regular oral intake. The main idea is simple: **enteral nutrition uses the GI tract**, while **parenteral nutrition bypasses the GI tract and goes straight into the bloodstream**.

<h2 class="section-divider" id="big-picture">Big Picture: GI Tract vs. Bloodstream</h2>

The route determines the risk. **Enteral nutrition** is used when the GI tract works, but the client cannot or should not eat enough by mouth. **Parenteral nutrition** is used when the GI tract cannot be used or cannot absorb enough nutrition.

| Type | Route | Used When | Main Complication Pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Enteral nutrition** | Feeding tube into the GI tract | GI tract works, but the client can't/shouldn't eat enough by mouth | GI problems: diarrhea, aspiration, intolerance |
| **Parenteral nutrition** | IV line into the bloodstream | GI tract can't be used or can't absorb enough | Metabolic and line-related problems: glucose swings, electrolyte shifts, infection |
| **PPN** | Peripheral IV | Short-term, less concentrated nutrition support | Peripheral vein irritation, limited calories |
| **TPN** | Central venous access | Provides most/all nutrition needs; hypertonic | Infection, hyperglycemia, hypoglycemia if stopped abruptly, electrolyte shifts |

If the GI tract works, **enteral nutrition is generally preferred** over parenteral nutrition because it maintains gut function and carries fewer serious complications than IV nutrition.

**Remember:** enteral = gut problems. Parenteral = glucose, electrolytes, and central line problems.

<h2 class="section-divider" id="enteral">Enteral Nutrition</h2>

Enteral nutrition provides formula through a feeding tube into the stomach or small intestine. It is used when the client cannot safely or adequately eat by mouth, but the GI tract can still digest or absorb nutrition.

Common reasons a client may need enteral nutrition include impaired swallowing, decreased level of consciousness, major illness, poor oral intake, some neurologic conditions, or increased nutritional needs that cannot be met orally.

### Feeding Methods

| Feeding Method | What It Means | ATI Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Continuous** | Formula runs slowly over many hours | Often used for poor tolerance or high aspiration risk |
| **Intermittent** | Given several times per day on a schedule | More like scheduled meals; monitor tolerance since volume is larger at once |
| **Bolus** | Larger amount given quickly | Highest risk for intolerance if given too fast |

Continuous feedings are usually easier to tolerate because the formula enters the GI tract slowly. Bolus feedings are more convenient, but they can cause cramping, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, or aspiration if given too quickly.

<h2 class="section-divider" id="tube-types">Types of Feeding Tubes</h2>

The tube location matters because it changes aspiration risk, feeding method, residual checks, and placement verification.

| Tube | Route | Typical Use | Key Point |
|---|---|---|---|
| **NG tube** | Nose → stomach | Short-term, generally under 4–6 weeks | Gastric tube; aspiration precautions apply |
| **OG tube** | Mouth → stomach | Used when nasal route is unsafe | Used instead of NG with suspected basilar skull fracture or facial/nasal trauma |
| **NJ tube** | Nose → jejunum | Short-term, when gastric feeding is not tolerated or aspiration risk is high | Small bowel tube; usually continuous feeding |
| **PEG / G-tube** | Abdominal wall → stomach | Long-term, generally beyond 4–6 weeks | Gastric tube; residual and aspiration precautions may apply |
| **PEJ / J-tube** | Abdominal wall → jejunum | Long-term, when gastric feeding is contraindicated or poorly tolerated | Small bowel tube; continuous feeding, not bolus |

### Gastric vs. Jejunal Tubes

| Tube Ending Location | Examples | Main Nursing Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Stomach** | NG, OG, PEG/G-tube | Higher aspiration risk; residual checks may be used; keep HOB elevated |
| **Jejunum** | NJ, PEJ/J-tube | Bypasses stomach; lower aspiration risk; continuous feeding; no traditional gastric residual checks |

**NG tubes are contraindicated with a suspected basilar skull fracture or significant facial/nasal trauma** because the tube could pass through a fracture into the cranial vault. In this situation, an **OG tube** is used instead.

Jejunal feedings are almost always given **continuously** because the small intestine has very little reservoir capacity. Do **not** give bolus feedings through a jejunal tube.

If the question describes severe GERD, delayed gastric emptying, or recurrent aspiration with gastric feedings, a jejunal tube may be the safer option because it bypasses the stomach.

<h2 class="section-divider" id="placement">Tube Placement & Positioning</h2>

### Initial Tube Placement

Before the first feeding, initial tube placement must be verified by **X-ray**. This confirms that the tube is in the correct location before anything is infused.

X-ray does **not** have to be repeated before every feeding unless there is concern that the tube moved.

Reasons to suspect displacement include:

- Coughing
- Choking
- Respiratory distress
- Tube movement
- Change in external tube length
- New difficulty tolerating the feeding

### Ongoing Placement Checks

After initial X-ray confirmation, bedside placement checks usually include aspirating a small amount of gastric contents and checking the **pH**.

| Finding | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **pH 5.5 or lower** | Supports gastric placement |
| **Higher pH** | May suggest migration or may be affected by medications/feedings; reassess placement |
| **Respiratory symptoms with unclear pH** | Stop and reassess; X-ray may be needed if there is real doubt |

Acid-reducing medications such as PPIs and H2 blockers can raise gastric pH. Ongoing tube feedings can also raise the pH. This means pH testing is helpful, but not perfect.

The old **“whoosh test”** — injecting air through the tube while listening over the stomach — is no longer considered reliable and should not be used to confirm placement.

### Positioning During Feedings

Keep the head of the bed elevated **30–45 degrees during the feeding and for about 1 hr after** to reduce aspiration risk.

This applies especially to gastric feedings because formula can reflux from the stomach into the esophagus and airway.

### Aspiration Prevention and Response

| Prevention | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Verify placement before use | Prevents formula from entering the airway |
| Keep HOB 30–45 degrees | Reduces reflux and aspiration risk |
| Monitor for coughing, choking, wet respirations, or respiratory distress | These can indicate aspiration or tube displacement |
| Check tolerance before increasing rate | Poor tolerance increases aspiration risk |

If aspiration is suspected, stop the feeding, keep the client upright, assess respiratory status, and notify the provider according to facility policy.

<h2 class="section-divider" id="residuals">Gastric Residuals & Feeding Tolerance</h2>

A large gastric residual suggests **delayed gastric emptying**, which increases the risk for regurgitation and aspiration.

Signs of feeding intolerance include:

- Large gastric residuals
- Nausea
- Vomiting
- Abdominal distention
- Bloating
- Cramping
- Diarrhea
- Discomfort during feeding

Do not ignore nausea, vomiting, abdominal distention, or a large residual and just continue the feeding as if nothing changed. These findings require follow-up.

Traditional gastric residual checks do **not** apply the same way to jejunal tubes because the tube bypasses the stomach.

<h2 class="section-divider" id="diarrhea">Diarrhea With Enteral Feedings</h2>

The most common testable cause of diarrhea during enteral feeding is that the feeding rate is **too fast** for the client to tolerate.

When formula moves through the small intestine faster than it can be absorbed, undigested formula pulls water into the bowel osmotically. This causes watery stool, similar to the mechanism of dumping syndrome.

The usual nursing action is to **decrease the feeding rate** according to the order or protocol.

### Other Causes of Tube-Feeding Diarrhea

Before assuming the rate is the only problem, also consider contamination and medications.

| Possible Cause | What to Check |
|---|---|
| **Rate too fast** | Feeding rate; recent increases in rate or volume |
| **Contaminated formula** | Hang time, expiration, handling, tubing changes |
| **Medication-induced diarrhea** | Sorbitol-based liquid medications, antibiotics, magnesium-containing meds |
| **Formula intolerance** | Ongoing symptoms despite correct rate and safe handling |

Formula should not hang longer than facility policy allows. Hang-time limits are usually measured in hours, not a full day.

<h2 class="section-divider" id="gi-complications">Other GI Complications: Symptom → Mechanism → Fix</h2>

ATI often gives a symptom and asks for the best action. The safest way to answer is to match the symptom to the mechanism.

| Complication | Why It Happens | Nursing Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Diarrhea** | Formula moving faster than the gut can absorb it; osmotic fluid pull into the bowel | Decrease the feeding rate |
| **Diarrhea not improving with slower rate** | Contaminated/expired formula, sorbitol-based liquid meds, or antibiotics | Check formula hang time and review medication list |
| **Abdominal distention/bloating** | Fat slows gastric emptying, so formula sits longer and increases fullness | Lower-fat formula |
| **Nausea/vomiting** | Often reflects temporary lactose intolerance from illness-related GI mucosa changes, or delayed emptying | Lactose-free formula |
| **Constipation** | Tube-feeding formulas are calorie-dense but relatively low in free water compared to oral diets | Increase free water given with or between feedings |
| **Cramping** | Formula is too cold or infused too quickly | Warm formula to room temperature; consider rate |
| **Dehydration / high specific gravity** | High renal solute load from protein without enough water | Add more free water; consider lower-protein formula |

**Simple memory line:** too fast → diarrhea → slow it down. Too much fat → distention → lower the fat. Lactose → nausea → remove lactose. Not enough free water → constipation → add water.

<h2 class="section-divider" id="dehydration">Dehydration With Tube Feeding</h2>

Tube-fed clients can become dehydrated because formula is calorie-dense and may not provide enough free water unless water flushes are ordered and given consistently.

An elevated urine **specific gravity** in a tube-fed client suggests dehydration.

| Finding | What It Suggests | Action |
|---|---|---|
| High urine specific gravity | Concentrated urine/dehydration | Add more free water to or between feedings |
| Constipation | Not enough free water | Increase free water |
| Dry mucous membranes, decreased urine output, tachycardia | Fluid volume deficit | Assess hydration and notify provider as needed |

Do not slow the feeding rate for dehydration unless the client also has diarrhea or intolerance. The fix for dehydration is usually **more free water**, not less formula.

<h2 class="section-divider" id="flushing">Tube Flushing & Medications</h2>

Flushes help maintain tube patency and prevent medication/formula interactions inside the tube.

| Situation | Flush Teaching |
|---|---|
| Before medication administration | Flush with about **30–50 mL water** |
| After medication administration | Flush with about **30–50 mL water** |
| Between multiple medications | Flush between each medication |
| Unused feeding tube | Use roughly **1.5 times** the usual flush volume |

Do not mix medications together before putting them through the tube. Give each medication separately, flushing between medications.

Never crush **enteric-coated** or **extended-release** medications for tube administration. Use a liquid form when available, especially for infants and children.

### Clogged Tube Prevention

| Prevention | Why |
|---|---|
| Flush before and after meds | Prevents medication from sticking in the tube |
| Flush between meds | Prevents drug interactions in the tube |
| Use liquid meds when available | Easier to pass through the tube |
| Crush only medications that are safe to crush | Prevents altered absorption or toxicity |
| Do not add medications directly to formula | Prevents clogging and interactions |

<h2 class="section-divider" id="transition-oral">Transitioning From Enteral to Oral Intake</h2>

When a client begins eating again, tube feedings are reduced gradually. The goal is to encourage oral intake while still preventing underfeeding.

A common stepwise approach is:

1. Hold the enteral feeding for about **1 hr before a meal** to encourage appetite.
2. Gradually increase the number of oral meals as tolerance improves.
3. Move toward **6 small meals/day** if needed.
4. Once oral intake reaches about **500–750 kcal/day**, continuous tube feeding may be shifted to **nighttime only**.
5. Continue calorie counts to make sure oral intake is sustained.

The core safety principle is: **overlap, don’t swap.** Reduce the old route gradually as the new route proves itself.

Before the feeding tube is removed, the client should be reliably and consistently meeting nutrition needs orally. This should be confirmed with calorie counts over more than one day, and swallowing safety should already be established.

Do not remove tube access based on one good meal.

<h2 class="section-divider" id="refeeding">Refeeding Syndrome</h2>

Refeeding syndrome is a **potentially fatal** complication that can occur when nutrition is restarted too aggressively in a client who has been severely malnourished or in a prolonged starvation state.

During starvation, the body shifts to fat and protein breakdown. Electrolyte stores become depleted even if serum levels look normal. When feeding restarts, especially with carbohydrates, insulin increases and drives glucose, phosphate, potassium, and magnesium into the cells.

This causes sudden drops in serum electrolytes.

### Refeeding Syndrome Mechanism

| Step | What Happens |
|---|---|
| Starvation/prolonged poor intake | Body uses fat and protein for energy; electrolyte stores become depleted |
| Carbohydrates are restarted | Insulin surges |
| Insulin shifts electrolytes into cells | Serum phosphate, potassium, and magnesium drop |
| Fluid shifts occur | Cardiac, respiratory, and neurologic complications can occur |

### Hallmark Lab Finding

Severe **hypophosphatemia** is the hallmark finding and the most dangerous part of refeeding syndrome. It can cause cardiac arrhythmias, respiratory muscle failure, and rhabdomyolysis.

This intracellular electrolyte shift is similar to what happens during DKA treatment, except the insulin surge here is the body’s own response to carbohydrate refeeding rather than administered insulin.

### Risk Factors and Prevention

| Who Is at Risk | Prevention |
|---|---|
| Prolonged poor intake/starvation for 5 days or more | Start nutrition slowly |
| Anorexia nervosa | Advance calories/volume gradually over days |
| Chronic alcoholism | Monitor phosphate, potassium, and magnesium closely |
| Significant unintentional weight loss | Supplement thiamine |
| Malnourished cancer clients | Correct electrolytes proactively |
| Malnourished older adults | Monitor especially closely during the first week |

For a malnourished client starting tube feeding, begin with a limited initial volume rather than immediately running the full prescribed rate. This reflects the broader principle: **start low, go slow**.

### Refeeding Syndrome Electrolyte Triad

| Electrolyte | Direction | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Phosphate** | Drops sharply | Most dangerous; cardiac and respiratory muscle failure risk |
| **Potassium** | Drops | Cardiac arrhythmia risk |
| **Magnesium** | Drops | Worsens hypokalemia; neuromuscular symptoms |

<h2 class="section-divider" id="parenteral">Parenteral Nutrition</h2>

Parenteral nutrition is IV nutrition. It is used when the GI tract cannot be used or cannot absorb enough nutrition.

| Type | Meaning | Route |
|---|---|---|
| **PPN** | Partial parenteral nutrition; less concentrated | Peripheral IV |
| **TPN** | Total parenteral nutrition; provides most or all nutrition needs; hypertonic | Central venous access |

TPN is used when the GI tract genuinely cannot meet nutrition needs, such as severe malabsorption, bowel obstruction, short bowel syndrome, severe IBD, or major GI surgery.

TPN is **not** used simply because a client dislikes hospital food or has temporarily reduced appetite.

<h2 class="section-divider" id="central-line-safety">Central Line Catheter Safety</h2>

TPN requires central venous access because it is hypertonic. The central line itself creates risks separate from the TPN solution.

### Infection Prevention

Central lines bypass the skin’s normal barrier, so strict aseptic technique is required with every access, dressing change, and tubing change.

Monitor for catheter-related bloodstream infection, also called **CLABSI**.

| CLABSI Clues | Nursing Meaning |
|---|---|
| Fever or chills | Possible bloodstream infection |
| Redness at insertion site | Local infection/inflammation |
| Swelling or warmth | Possible infection or irritation |
| Drainage at insertion site | Report promptly |

Report signs of central line infection promptly instead of waiting to see if they resolve.

Dressing changes generally follow a schedule:

| Dressing Type/Situation | General Timing |
|---|---|
| Gauze dressing | Commonly every 1–2 days |
| Transparent semipermeable dressing | Up to about 1 week |
| Damp, loose, or visibly soiled dressing | Change right away |
| Between scheduled changes | Assess the site daily |

### Air Embolism

Air embolism is a central line-specific risk. It is most likely during insertion, tubing changes, or catheter removal — anytime the line is open to air.

| Signs of Air Embolism |
|---|
| Sudden shortness of breath |
| Chest pain |
| Cough |
| Hypoxia |
| Sense of impending doom |

Prevention includes keeping all connections secure, clamping the catheter when not in use, priming/clearing air from tubing, and using the **Valsalva maneuver** during line insertion, tubing changes, or removal if the client is able.

After catheter removal, apply an **occlusive dressing** and keep the client flat/supine for about **30 minutes**, while monitoring for symptoms of air embolism.

<h2 class="section-divider" id="tpn-admin">TPN Administration Safety</h2>

TPN must be administered carefully because it is hypertonic and high in dextrose.

| Safety Step | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Use an **infusion pump**, never gravity | Prevents rate fluctuations and glucose swings |
| Confirm central line placement by radiology before starting | Prevents complications from malposition |
| If refrigerated, administer within about **30 minutes** of removing it | Avoids leaving solution at room temperature too long and lowering infection risk |
| Change TPN bag and tubing every **24 hr** | Limits microorganism growth |
| Inspect the solution before hanging | Oily layer or “cracked” appearance suggests calcium/phosphorus incompatibility |
| Follow facility policy for meds through the line | Some medications, such as heparin, may be added directly into the TPN port per order/protocol |

If a TPN solution appears oily, separated, or cracked, do not hang it.

### Dedicated Line/Lumen Rule

TPN is usually infused through a dedicated lumen. Avoid using the TPN lumen for routine blood draws, medications, or other IV fluids unless facility policy and the provider order allow it.

This reduces infection risk, incompatibility risk, and interruption of the high-dextrose infusion.

<h2 class="section-divider" id="tpn-glucose">TPN Glucose Complications</h2>

TPN contains a high concentration of dextrose, so glucose monitoring is a major nursing priority.

| Situation | Risk | Nursing Action |
|---|---|---|
| TPN infusing normally | Hyperglycemia | Monitor glucose regularly; administer insulin as prescribed |
| TPN stopped abruptly | Hypoglycemia | Hang prescribed dextrose-containing IV solution if the next bag is not ready |
| Confusion, cool/clammy skin, erratic behavior, shakiness after stopping TPN | Hypoglycemia | Check glucose and treat per protocol |

If TPN must be interrupted and the next bag is not ready, the typical action is to hang a prescribed dextrose-containing IV solution, such as **D10W**, to prevent hypoglycemia.

Do not simply leave the line without glucose support.

---

<h2 class="section-divider" id="tpn-electrolytes">TPN Electrolyte Complications</h2>

TPN can cause electrolyte shifts as nutrition is metabolized. This is similar to the broad mechanism of refeeding syndrome, especially in malnourished clients.

Monitor:

- Magnesium
- Potassium
- Phosphorus
- Calcium
- Glucose

TPN can cause **low magnesium** as electrolytes shift into cells during nutrient metabolism.

Electrolyte imbalances can trigger cardiac rhythm changes, neuromuscular symptoms, weakness, and worsening overall instability.

---

<h2 class="section-divider" id="lipid-emulsions">TPN Lipid Emulsions</h2>

Lipid emulsions may be part of TPN or given separately. They are often made from soybean or safflower oil with **egg phospholipid** as an emulsifier.

Always check for **egg allergy** before administration and report it to the provider if present.

| Lipid Emulsion Concern | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Egg allergy | Egg phospholipid may be used as emulsifier |
| Oily or cracked appearance | Possible incompatibility; do not use |
| Separate lipid tubing or timing per facility policy | Reduces infection and compatibility problems |

---

<h2 class="section-divider" id="transition-tpn">Transitioning Off TPN</h2>

TPN is tapered down gradually as oral or enteral intake increases. It is not switched off all at once because the body has adapted to a continuous high-dextrose infusion, and abrupt stopping can cause rebound hypoglycemia.

TPN may be discontinued once the client is taking in more than **60% of estimated calorie needs orally or enterally**, but that intake should be sustained rather than reached only once.

During the taper:

- Continue checking blood glucose as the rate decreases.
- Track oral or enteral intake with calorie counts.
- Confirm the 60% threshold is sustained.
- Monitor weight.
- Monitor relevant labs, including electrolytes and glucose.
- Keep central line access until nutrition is reliably established through the new route and the line is no longer needed for any other purpose.

The same transition rule applies here: **overlap, don’t swap**.

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="quick-reference">Quick Reference</h2>

### Matching Enteral GI Symptom to Mechanism and Fix

| Symptom | Mechanism | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Diarrhea | Rate too fast; osmotic fluid pull into bowel | Decrease feeding rate |
| Diarrhea not improving with slower rate | Contaminated/expired formula, sorbitol-based liquid meds, antibiotics | Check hang time; review medication list |
| Abdominal distention/bloating | Fat slows gastric emptying | Lower-fat formula |
| Nausea/vomiting | Temporary lactose intolerance or delayed emptying | Lactose-free formula |
| Constipation | Formula is calorie-dense but low in free water | Increase free water |
| Cramping | Formula too cold or infused too fast | Warm to room temperature; consider rate |
| Dehydration/high specific gravity | High renal solute load from protein without enough water | More free water; consider lower-protein formula |

### TPN Safety Checklist

| Step | Why |
|---|---|
| Infusion pump, never gravity | Prevents glucose swings from rate fluctuation |
| Confirm central line via radiology first | Prevents complications from malposition |
| Use within ~30 min of refrigeration | Limits infection risk |
| Change bag/tubing every 24 hr | Limits microorganism growth |
| Check for cracked/oily appearance | Calcium/phosphorus incompatibility — do not use |
| Check egg allergy before lipid emulsion | Emulsifier contains egg phospholipid |
| Use dedicated lumen when possible | Reduces infection, incompatibility, and interruption risks |
| Monitor glucose | TPN can cause hyperglycemia; abrupt stop can cause hypoglycemia |
| Monitor electrolytes | Refeeding-like shifts can lower phosphorus, potassium, and magnesium |

### Route Transition Rules

| Transition | Main Rule |
|---|---|
| Enteral → oral | Hold feeding before meals, increase oral intake gradually, then shift feeds to nighttime if intake improves |
| Tube removal | Only after oral intake is reliable and swallowing safety is confirmed |
| TPN → enteral/oral | Taper gradually once intake is sustained at more than 60% of needs |
| Any nutrition transition | Overlap, don’t swap |
