<h2 class="section-divider" id="endocrine-and-diabetes-nutrition">Endocrine &amp; Diabetes Nutrition</h2>

Diabetes is one of the highest-yield endocrine topics on the ATI Nutrition exam because nutrition, medication timing, illness, activity, and glucose monitoring all intersect in nearly every question.

<div class="memory">
Insulin's job is simple: move glucose from the <strong>bloodstream</strong> into <strong>cells</strong>. When insulin is missing, ineffective, or mistimed, glucose stays in the blood instead of fueling the body — that single idea explains almost every diabetes complication on this page.
</div>

---

<h3 id="big-picture">Big Picture: Glucose and Insulin</h3>

| Concept | What It Means | ATI Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Glucose** | The body's main fuel | Too much or too little becomes dangerous quickly |
| **Insulin** | Moves glucose from blood into cells | Low or ineffective insulin → hyperglycemia |
| **Carbohydrates** | The nutrient with the most direct effect on glucose | Consistency and choice matter more than total elimination |

<div class="nclex">
ATI nutrition teaching for diabetes is not "avoid sugar." The safer teaching is <strong>consistent carbohydrate intake, balanced meals, glucose monitoring, and early symptom recognition</strong>.
</div>

---

<h3 id="type1-type2">Type 1 vs. Type 2 Diabetes</h3>

| Feature | Type 1 | Type 2 |
|---|---|---|
| **Underlying problem** | Little to no insulin production | Insulin resistance (pancreas may still produce insulin) |
| **Insulin requirement** | Always required | May or may not require insulin, depending on management |
| **Onset** | Often younger, more abrupt | Often gradual, linked to insulin resistance |
| **Higher risk for** | DKA | HHS |
| **Nutrition focus** | Balance meals, insulin, and activity carefully | Nutrition and activity can directly improve insulin sensitivity |

<div class="danger">
A client with type 1 diabetes who has hyperglycemia, ketones, fruity breath, nausea/vomiting, or deep rapid (Kussmaul) respirations may be developing <strong>DKA</strong>.
</div>

---

<h3 id="carbs-diabetes">Carbohydrates and Diabetes</h3>

Carbohydrates affect blood glucose more directly than any other nutrient because they break down into glucose. The goal isn't elimination — it's **consistency**.

| Concept | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Consistent carbohydrate intake** | Eating a similar amount of carbs at similar times each day prevents large glucose swings and makes insulin/medication dosing predictable |
| **Glycemic index** | Ranks how quickly and how much a carbohydrate-containing food raises blood glucose — even foods that don't taste sweet can rank high |

<div class="ati-tip">
A high-glycemic-index food isn't always an "obviously sugary" food. A <strong>baked potato</strong> is a classic ATI example of a high-glycemic starch (~85–90 on the index) — it can raise glucose significantly even though it doesn't taste sweet.
</div>

<div class="teaching">
Practical teaching: don't skip meals, eat at consistent times, pair carbohydrates with protein/fat to slow absorption, read labels, and watch portion sizes — not "avoid all carbs."
</div>

---

<h3 id="plate-method">The Diabetes Plate Method</h3>

A simple visual tool used for client teaching that doesn't require carb counting math.

| Portion of the Plate | What Goes There |
|---|---|
| **½ of the plate** | Non-starchy vegetables |
| **¼ of the plate** | Lean protein |
| **¼ of the plate** | Carbohydrate foods (grains, starchy vegetables, fruit, dairy) |

<div class="note">
This method is popular for client teaching because it's visual and doesn't require math — useful for clients who struggle with carbohydrate counting or have low health literacy.
</div>

<h3 id="monitoring">Blood Glucose Monitoring &amp; HbA1c</h3>

<div class="nclex">
If a client with diabetes develops symptoms of hypo- or hyperglycemia, the nurse's <strong>first</strong> action is to check the blood glucose level — this follows the nursing process (assess before intervening).
</div>

| Lab | What It Reflects | ATI Goal Range |
|---|---|---|
| **Point-of-care glucose** | Glucose right now | Varies by facility/provider order |
| **HbA1c** | Average glucose over the past **2–3 months** | Common goal: **6.5%–7%** for many clients with diabetes |

<div class="danger">
An HbA1c above the goal range (for example, <strong>8.7%</strong>) indicates poor long-term glucose control even if a recent fingerstick happened to be normal — HbA1c shows the pattern, not the moment.
</div>

<h3 id="hypoglycemia">Hypoglycemia</h3>

Hypoglycemia (low blood glucose) is an emergency because the brain depends on a steady glucose supply.

| Cause | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Too much insulin/medication | Moves too much glucose out of the blood |
| Skipped or delayed meal | Not enough glucose intake to match medication |
| Increased exercise | Muscles use more glucose |
| Alcohol without food | Interferes with glucose regulation (see Alcohol section below) |

<div class="assessment">
Classic hypoglycemia presentation: <strong>shaky, sweaty, cool/clammy skin, confused, irritable or erratic behavior, hungry, tachycardic.</strong> Confusion and erratic behavior are neurologic effects of the brain not getting enough glucose.
</div>

#### Treating Hypoglycemia: The 15-15 Rule

<div class="memory">
If the client is awake and able to swallow: give <strong>15 grams of a fast-acting carbohydrate</strong>, wait <strong>15 minutes</strong>, then recheck glucose. Repeat if still below target. Once stable, follow with a snack or meal containing protein if the next meal isn't soon.
</div>

<div class="danger">
Never give oral food or fluid to a client who is <strong>unconscious or unable to swallow</strong> — aspiration risk. These clients need glucagon or IV dextrose per protocol/order instead.
</div>

---

<h3 id="hyperglycemia">Hyperglycemia</h3>

| Cause | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Missed insulin/medication | Not enough glucose moves into cells |
| Infection or illness | Stress hormones raise glucose and blunt insulin effectiveness |
| Excess carbohydrate intake | More glucose enters the bloodstream than can be used |
| Decreased activity | Less glucose used by muscles |

<div class="patho">
When glucose is very high, it spills into the urine. Water follows the glucose (<strong>osmotic diuresis</strong>), causing polyuria, thirst, dry skin, and — if severe — dehydration and hypotension.
</div>

<div class="memory">
Quick contrast: hypoglycemia often looks <strong>cool, clammy, shaky, confused</strong>. Hyperglycemia often looks <strong>dry, thirsty, fatigued, urinating frequently</strong>.
</div>

<h3 id="dka">Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)</h3>

DKA is a serious complication most associated with **type 1 diabetes**, occurring when there isn't enough insulin for cells to use glucose. The body responds by breaking down fat for energy, producing acidic ketones.

| Step | What's Happening |
|---|---|
| 1 | Insulin is absent or very low |
| 2 | Glucose stays in the bloodstream; cells can't use it |
| 3 | Body breaks down fat for energy → ketones accumulate |
| 4 | Metabolic acidosis develops |
| 5 | Osmotic diuresis causes dehydration |

<div class="danger">
DKA assessment pattern: <strong>hyperglycemia + ketones + acidosis + dehydration.</strong> Look for fruity breath, Kussmaul respirations (deep, rapid breathing that compensates for acidosis), nausea/vomiting, abdominal pain, and altered mental status.
</div>

<div class="lab">
<strong>Potassium paradox during DKA treatment:</strong> serum potassium often looks normal or even high on admission because acidosis and insulin deficiency push potassium <em>out</em> of cells — but total body potassium is actually depleted. Once insulin therapy starts, potassium shifts rapidly back <em>into</em> cells, and serum potassium can fall fast. Potassium must be monitored closely during treatment, and severely low potassium may need correction <strong>before</strong> insulin is started to avoid life-threatening cardiac dysrhythmias.
</div>

<h3 id="hhs">Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS)</h3>

HHS is most associated with **type 2 diabetes**. Blood glucose becomes extremely high with severe dehydration, but because the client usually has *some* insulin, major fat breakdown (and ketone production) doesn't occur the way it does in DKA.

| Feature | What's Happening |
|---|---|
| Very high glucose | Severe hyperglycemia |
| Severe dehydration | Osmotic diuresis pulls large amounts of fluid into urine |
| Little or no ketones | Some insulin is present, preventing major fat breakdown |
| Altered mental status | Common, especially as dehydration becomes severe |

<div class="danger">
Think <strong>type 2 diabetes + very high glucose + severe dehydration + altered mental status</strong>, usually <strong>without</strong> significant ketones or acidosis.
</div>

---

<h3 id="dka-vs-hhs">DKA vs. HHS</h3>

| Feature | DKA | HHS |
|---|---|---|
| Most associated with | Type 1 | Type 2 |
| Main problem | No insulin → ketones + acidosis | Severe hyperglycemia + dehydration |
| Glucose | High | Very high |
| Ketones | Present | Minimal/absent |
| Acidosis | Present | Usually absent/mild |
| Respirations | Kussmaul possible | Usually not Kussmaul |
| Onset | Can be rapid (hours) | Often slower (days) |

<h3 id="somogyi-dawn">Somogyi vs. Dawn Phenomenon</h3>

Both cause **morning hyperglycemia**, but the underlying cause — and what happened overnight — is different.

| Feature | Somogyi Phenomenon | Dawn Phenomenon |
|---|---|---|
| **Cause** | Rebound hyperglycemia after **nighttime hypoglycemia** | Early-morning hormone release raises glucose directly |
| **Overnight glucose** | Drops low around 2–3 AM, then rebounds high | Normal or rising — no preceding low |
| **Key clue** | Night sweats, nightmares, morning headache, restless sleep | No nighttime hypoglycemia symptoms |

<div class="nclex">
The best way to tell them apart: check a <strong>2–3 AM blood glucose</strong> if prescribed. Low at 2–3 AM → Somogyi. Normal/high at 2–3 AM → Dawn phenomenon.
</div>

---

<h3 id="sick-day">Diabetes During Illness</h3>

Illness and stress raise blood glucose because stress hormones increase glucose production and reduce insulin's effectiveness — even if the client isn't eating normally.

<div class="teaching">
Sick-day teaching: monitor glucose more frequently; continue diabetes medications unless told otherwise by the provider; maintain fluid intake; try to consume some carbohydrate even with reduced appetite; report persistent vomiting, high glucose, or moderate/large ketones.
</div>

<div class="danger">
Illness increases the risk for hyperglycemia, dehydration, DKA, and HHS — clients shouldn't assume "I'm not eating much, so I don't need my usual medication."
</div>

---

<h3 id="alcohol">Alcohol and Diabetes</h3>

Alcohol can cause **delayed hypoglycemia** — sometimes hours after drinking, including overnight — because the liver prioritizes metabolizing alcohol over releasing stored glucose (glycogenolysis).

<div class="danger">
A client with diabetes who drinks alcohol without food is at risk for hypoglycemia that can be <strong>delayed</strong> and occur well after drinking has stopped, including while asleep.
</div>

<div class="teaching">
Teaching: never drink alcohol on an empty stomach; pair alcohol with food containing carbohydrates; monitor glucose more closely after drinking, including before bed.
</div>

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="food-sources">Food Sources Quick Reference</h2>

#### Fast-Acting Carbohydrates for Treating Hypoglycemia (15 g examples)

| Food | Approximate Amount for 15 g Carb |
|---|---|
| Glucose tablets | 3–4 tablets (check product label) |
| Fruit juice or regular soda | About ½ cup (4 oz) |
| Regular (non-diet) soft drink | About ½ cup (4 oz) |
| Hard candy | Check label for exact count |
| Honey or table sugar | About 1 tablespoon |

<div class="note">
Avoid using high-fat or high-fiber foods (chocolate, ice cream, beans) to treat hypoglycemia — fat and fiber slow absorption, delaying the glucose rise when speed matters most.
</div>

#### High- vs. Lower-Glycemic-Index Foods

| Higher Glycemic Index (raises glucose faster) | Lower Glycemic Index (raises glucose more gradually) |
|---|---|
| Baked potato, white bread, sweetened cereal, white rice | Whole grains, legumes, most fruits, non-starchy vegetables |

#### Consistent-Carbohydrate Meal Building

| Carbohydrate Group | Examples |
|---|---|
| Starches/grains | Whole grains, rice, pasta, bread |
| Fruit | Fresh, frozen, or canned without added sugar |
| Milk/dairy | Milk, yogurt |
| Starchy vegetables | Potatoes, corn, peas |

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="section-summary">Section Summary &amp; Test-Taking Tips</h2>

| If the Question Says... | Think... |
|---|---|
| Little/no insulin production | Type 1 diabetes — insulin required, higher DKA risk |
| Insulin resistance | Type 2 diabetes — nutrition/activity improve control |
| Baked potato | High glycemic index food |
| HbA1c reflects what time frame? | Average glucose over 2–3 months |
| HbA1c goal | 6.5%–7% |
| Symptoms of hypo- or hyperglycemia present | Check blood glucose first |
| Shaky, sweaty, cool/clammy, confused | Hypoglycemia |
| Treating hypoglycemia in an awake client | 15 g fast-acting carb, recheck in 15 min |
| Client is unconscious/can't swallow | No oral intake — glucagon/IV dextrose per protocol |
| Dry, thirsty, frequent urination, fatigue | Hyperglycemia — osmotic diuresis risk |
| Type 1 + ketones + fruity breath + Kussmaul respirations | DKA |
| DKA potassium on admission looks normal/high | Still expect it to **drop** once insulin therapy starts — monitor closely |
| Type 2 + very high glucose + severe dehydration, minimal ketones | HHS |
| Morning hyperglycemia + nighttime hypoglycemia | Somogyi phenomenon |
| Morning hyperglycemia, no preceding low | Dawn phenomenon |
| Illness in a client with diabetes | Continue medications, monitor glucose closely, watch for DKA/HHS |
| Alcohol + diabetes | Risk for delayed hypoglycemia, even hours later — never drink without food |
