<h2 class="section-divider" id="older-adult-and-special-population-nutrition">Older Adult Nutrition</h2>

<div class="memory">
Don't assume older adults need <strong>less</strong> nutrition across the board. They usually need <strong>fewer calories</strong> (lower metabolism, less lean mass) — but the same or <strong>higher</strong> protein, plus extra attention to vitamin D, calcium, B12, and fluids.
</div>

---

<h3 id="big-picture">Big Picture: Fewer Calories, Not Fewer Needs</h3>

| Issue | Why It Happens | Nursing Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Decreased calorie needs** | Lower metabolism, less muscle mass, often less activity | Choose nutrient-dense foods over empty calories |
| **Unchanged/increased protein needs** | Aging muscle (sarcopenia) and illness both demand more, not less | Don't under-prescribe protein just because calorie needs are lower |
| **Hydration risk** | Blunted thirst, declining body water, renal changes | Offer fluids proactively, don't wait for the client to ask |
| **Absorption changes** | Atrophic gastritis reduces stomach acid | Watch for B12, iron, and calcium issues |
| **Dementia** | Forgetting to eat, distraction, self-feeding difficulty | Simplify the environment and the food itself |

---

<h3 id="protein">Protein Needs</h3>

| Older Adult Status | Protein Target |
|---|---|
| Healthy | **1–1.2 g/kg/day** |
| Acute or chronic illness | **1.2–1.5 g/kg/day** |

<div class="ati-tip">
Protein needs in older adults are usually <strong>equal to or higher than</strong> younger healthy adults — illness, wounds, and muscle maintenance all push the number up, not down. The major exception remains kidney disease, where protein may need individualized restriction depending on stage/dialysis status.
</div>

<h3 id="sarcopenia">Sarcopenia</h3>

Sarcopenia is the progressive, age-related loss of muscle mass and strength — distinct from cachexia (which is driven by disease/inflammation) and distinct from simple malnutrition, though all three can overlap.

<div class="patho">
Older muscle has <strong>anabolic resistance</strong> — it doesn't respond to the same protein intake as efficiently as younger muscle does to build/maintain mass. This is part of why standard adult protein RDAs (set for younger populations) tend to undershoot what older adults actually need.
</div>

<div class="teaching">
The two interventions that work together, not separately: <strong>adequate protein intake</strong> (especially leucine-rich sources) <strong>and resistance/strength exercise</strong>. Protein alone, without the muscle-loading stimulus of exercise, is a weaker strategy than the combination.
</div>

<h3 id="post-surgery">After Surgery</h3>

<div class="danger">
Older adults after surgery need adequate calories to support healing — often at least <strong>1,500 kcal/day</strong>, individualized to need. An intake around <strong>1,000 kcal below estimated needs</strong> is not adequate and raises risk for poor wound healing and malnutrition.
</div>

---

<h3 id="calcium-vitamin-d">Calcium &amp; Vitamin D</h3>

| Nutrient | Why It Matters | Key Number |
|---|---|---|
| **Vitamin D** | Needed for calcium absorption; skin becomes less efficient at synthesizing it from sunlight with age | Often **1,000–2,000 IU/day** if supplementation is recommended |
| **Calcium** | Bone health, falls/fracture prevention | Calcium **carbonate** should be taken **with meals** to improve absorption |

<h3 id="b12">Vitamin B12 and Atrophic Gastritis</h3>

This is a mechanism worth understanding, not just memorizing — it explains why B12 deficiency is so common in this age group.

<div class="patho">
B12 absorption from <strong>food</strong> requires stomach acid to release it from food proteins, plus <strong>intrinsic factor</strong> (made by stomach cells) to carry it to the small intestine for absorption. Atrophic gastritis — common with aging — reduces both stomach acid and intrinsic factor production, impairing food-bound B12 absorption.
</div>

<div class="ati-tip">
Here's the testable twist: absorption of <strong>crystalline B12</strong> (the form in supplements and fortified foods) does <strong>not</strong> require stomach acid the same way and stays largely intact even with atrophic gastritis. This is why older adults with low B12 are often told to rely on <strong>fortified foods or supplements</strong> rather than just "eat more meat."
</div>

<div class="note">
Long-term use of <strong>proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)</strong> or other acid-reducing medications can compound this problem by further suppressing the stomach acid needed to release B12 from food — another reason older adults on long-term PPI therapy are at higher risk for B12 deficiency.
</div>

<h3 id="hydration">Hydration Risk</h3>

<div class="assessment">
Dehydration in older adults often presents <strong>atypically</strong> — confusion, weakness, dizziness, or a fall, rather than the thirst complaint you'd expect. Don't wait for "I'm thirsty" before assessing fluid status in this population.
</div>

<div class="teaching">
Contributing factors: blunted thirst sensation, fear of incontinence (leading to self-restriction), difficulty accessing fluids independently, diuretics, and reduced total body water with age. Offer fluids proactively and on a schedule rather than waiting to be asked.
</div>

<h3 id="sensory-mechanical">Sensory and Mechanical Changes</h3>

| Change | Nutrition Impact |
|---|---|
| **Decreased taste/smell** | Reduced appetite; may oversalt or oversweeten food to compensate |
| **Dental issues/missing teeth** | Avoidance of meat, fresh fruits/vegetables — common hidden cause of inadequate protein and fiber |
| **Presbyphagia** (age-related swallowing changes) | Slower, less coordinated swallow even without a specific neurologic diagnosis — a baseline risk factor that can tip into true dysphagia with illness or fatigue |

<div class="note">
If an older client is quietly avoiding meat and fresh produce, ask about <strong>dental fit and chewing ability</strong> before assuming it's a preference or appetite issue.
</div>

---

<h3 id="dementia">Dementia and Nutrition</h3>

Clients with dementia may forget to eat, become distracted mid-meal, have trouble recognizing food, or struggle with utensils — on top of possibly wandering, which increases calorie needs.

<div class="teaching">
Nursing care: calm meal environment with minimal distractions; offer finger foods if utensils are difficult; small frequent meals/snacks; use familiar foods; provide supervision or assistance; monitor weight and hydration; watch for swallowing difficulty.
</div>

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="food-sources">Food Sources Quick Reference</h2>

#### High-Protein, Nutrient-Dense Choices (Sarcopenia, Illness, Post-Surgery)

| Nutrient-Dense Options |
|---|
| Eggs, cottage cheese, Greek yogurt, fortified shakes/oral nutrition supplements, lean meats, fish, beans/lentils |

#### Vitamin B12 (Crystalline Form — Bypasses the Atrophic Gastritis Problem)

| Sources |
|---|
| Fortified breakfast cereals, fortified nutritional yeast, B12 supplements |

#### Calcium &amp; Vitamin D

| Calcium Sources | Vitamin D Sources |
|---|---|
| Milk, yogurt, cheese, fortified foods, leafy greens, almonds | Fortified milk, fatty fish, supplements |

#### Easier-to-Chew Choices (Dental Issues)

| Better Tolerated |
|---|
| Ground or shredded meat, soft-cooked vegetables, soups, smoothies with protein added, soft fruits |

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="section-summary">Section Summary &amp; Test-Taking Tips</h2>

| If the Question Says... | Think... |
|---|---|
| Older adult, general calorie needs | Decreased — but choose nutrient-dense foods |
| Healthy older adult protein target | 1–1.2 g/kg/day |
| Older adult with acute/chronic illness | 1.2–1.5 g/kg/day protein |
| Sarcopenia | Combine adequate protein **with** resistance exercise — not protein alone |
| Older adult after surgery | At least 1,500 kcal/day; 1,000 kcal below needs is inadequate |
| Vitamin D supplementation in older adults | 1,000–2,000 IU/day if recommended |
| Calcium carbonate | Take with meals |
| Low B12 + atrophic gastritis | Food-bound B12 absorption is impaired; crystalline B12 (supplements/fortified foods) still absorbs well |
| Long-term PPI use in an older adult | Added risk factor for B12 deficiency |
| Older adult with confusion, weakness, or a new fall | Consider dehydration first |
| Older adult avoiding meat/fresh produce | Assess dental fit/chewing ability before assuming poor appetite |
| Dementia + poor intake | Reduce distractions, offer finger foods, supervise/assist with meals |
