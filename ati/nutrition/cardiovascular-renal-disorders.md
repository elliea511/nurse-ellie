<h2 class="section-divider" id="cardiovascular-and-renal-nutrition">Cardiovascular & Renal Nutrition</h2>

For this section, the core question is almost always: **control sodium, protect the heart, protect the kidneys, watch potassium.**

---

<h3 id="big-picture">Big Picture: Heart and Kidneys Together</h3>

The heart pumps blood; the kidneys regulate fluid volume, sodium, potassium, blood pressure, and waste removal. When nutrition isn't managed well, the result is usually worsening hypertension, fluid overload, electrolyte imbalance, or increased cardiovascular risk.

| Topic | Why Nutrition Matters |
|---|---|
| **Hypertension** | Sodium and fluid retention raise blood pressure |
| **Cardiovascular disease** | Saturated fat, excess sodium, and excess calories increase risk |
| **CKD** | Kidneys may not regulate fluid, potassium, sodium, or phosphorus well |
| **Kidney stones** | Fluid intake and specific dietary factors affect stone formation |

### ATI Addition: Main Nutrition Priorities by System

| System | Main Nutrition Focus | What ATI Usually Tests |
|---|---|---|
| **Cardiovascular** | Sodium, fat type, cholesterol, weight, activity | Can the client reduce BP/cardiac workload and lower CVD risk? |
| **Renal** | Sodium, potassium, phosphorus, protein, fluids | Can the client avoid fluid overload, hyperkalemia, bone/mineral problems, and waste buildup? |
| **Both** | Fluid balance and electrolytes | Can the nurse recognize when diet changes could worsen heart rhythm, BP, edema, or kidney function? |

<h3 id="sodium-bp">Sodium and Blood Pressure</h3>

Sodium causes the body to retain water (see <a href="{{ '/nutrition-basics.html#minerals' | relative_url }}">Nutrition Basics → Minerals</a> for the full extracellular fluid mechanism). More retained fluid means more circulating volume, which raises blood pressure and cardiac workload.

The greatest blood pressure reductions are seen with sodium intake around **1,500 mg/day or less**.

Core teaching: avoid adding salt at the table, read nutrition labels (sodium hides in packaged/processed foods), limit canned soups and processed/deli meats, choose fresh foods, and use herbs/spices for flavor instead of salt.

### Label Reading for Sodium

| Label Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Sodium-free** | Very little sodium per serving |
| **Very low sodium** | Less sodium than standard foods, but still check serving size |
| **Low sodium** | Helpful for BP/heart/renal diets, but not unlimited |
| **Reduced sodium** | Less sodium than the original product, but it may still be high |
| **No salt added** | No salt added during processing, but the food may still contain natural sodium |

### ATI Addition: Sodium Teaching That Sounds “Right” but Can Be Wrong

| Client Statement | Nursing Response |
|---|---|
| “I only use sea salt now.” | Sea salt is still sodium and still affects blood pressure. |
| “I bought reduced-sodium soup, so I can eat more of it.” | Reduced sodium does not always mean low sodium. Check the label and serving size. |
| “I stopped using table salt, so I’m good.” | Most sodium comes from packaged, canned, restaurant, and processed foods, not only the salt shaker. |

<h3 id="salt-substitutes">Salt Substitutes</h3>

Many salt substitutes replace sodium with **potassium** — they are not sodium-free and can contain roughly half as much sodium as table salt while adding meaningful potassium. This is risky for clients with CKD, hyperkalemia risk, or those on ACE inhibitors, ARBs, or potassium-sparing diuretics.

### Salt Substitute Safety

| Higher Risk Client | Why Salt Substitutes Can Be Unsafe |
|---|---|
| **CKD** | Kidneys may not clear potassium well |
| **ACE inhibitor use** | ACE inhibitors can retain potassium |
| **ARB use** | ARBs can retain potassium |
| **Potassium-sparing diuretic use** | These medications already conserve potassium |
| **History of hyperkalemia** | Added potassium can worsen cardiac rhythm risk |

<h3 id="cholesterol-fat">Cholesterol and Fat Teaching</h3>

The goal isn't "no fat" — it's choosing the right type of fat in the right amount.

| Fat Type | Effect | Teaching |
|---|---|---|
| **Saturated fat** | Raises LDL ("bad") cholesterol | Limit; choose leaner cuts and lower-fat dairy |
| **Trans fat** | Raises LDL, lowers HDL | Avoid as much as possible; check labels for "partially hydrogenated oils" |
| **Unsaturated fat** | Generally heart-protective | Preferred fat source, in appropriate portions |

Cooking method matters as much as fat type: baked, grilled, or broiled instead of fried; remove poultry skin (it holds much of the fat); choose liquid oils (e.g., canola) over solid fats like margarine or butter; use nonfat or low-fat milk instead of whole milk.

Fatty fish (such as salmon) eaten at least **twice a week** is a recommended source of omega-3 fatty acids for cardiovascular health, even on a low-cholesterol diet — fish isn't excluded the way fatty red meat is.

### ATI Addition: Lipid Panel Basics

| Lab | What It Means | Desired Direction |
|---|---|---|
| **LDL** | “Bad” cholesterol; contributes to plaque buildup | Lower is better (less than 100) |
| **HDL** | “Good” cholesterol; helps remove cholesterol | Higher is better (less than 50) |
| **Triglycerides** | Blood fat often affected by excess calories, sugar, alcohol, and diabetes control | Lower is better (less than 150 |
| **Total cholesterol** | Overall cholesterol number, but less specific than LDL/HDL | Lower is generally better (less than 200) |

### ATI Addition: Heart-Healthy Diet Patterns

| Diet Pattern | Main Features |
|---|---|
| **DASH diet** | High in fruits, vegetables, low-fat dairy, whole grains; limits sodium, saturated fat, and added sugar |
| **Mediterranean-style eating** | Emphasizes fruits, vegetables, whole grains, beans, nuts, olive oil, and fish |
| **Low-saturated-fat diet** | Limits fatty meats, full-fat dairy, butter, fried foods, and trans fats |

---

<h3 id="plant-protein">Plant-Based Protein and Heart Health</h3>

Replacing high-fat animal protein with plant-based protein (beans, lentils, peas, nuts, seeds, soy) can help **lower cholesterol** and reduce the heart's workload, while still supporting muscle mass for weight management.

### Plant-Based Protein Examples

| Protein Source | Why It Helps |
|---|---|
| **Beans/lentils/peas** | Provide protein and fiber with less saturated fat than fatty meats |
| **Soy foods** | Plant protein option that can replace higher-fat animal proteins |
| **Nuts/seeds** | Heart-healthy fats, but portion control matters because they are calorie-dense |

---

<h3 id="niacin">Niacin</h3>

Niacin (a B vitamin) can be used to help lower **LDL cholesterol and triglycerides**.

### ATI Addition: Niacin Teaching

| Teaching Point | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Take as prescribed | High doses are medication-like, not just a routine vitamin |
| Flushing can occur | Warmth/flushing is a common adverse effect |
| Report liver-related symptoms | High-dose niacin can affect the liver |
| Avoid self-prescribing mega-doses | More is not automatically safer |

---

<h3 id="exercise">Exercise and Cardiovascular Risk</h3>

At least **150 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise per week** supports cardiovascular health, blood pressure control, glucose control, and lipid improvement.

### Exercise Teaching

| Teaching | ATI Focus |
|---|---|
| Start gradually if sedentary | Prevents injury and supports adherence |
| Include regular aerobic activity | Supports BP, glucose, and lipid control |
| Stop and report chest pain, severe shortness of breath, dizziness, or fainting | Possible cardiac warning signs |
| Pair exercise with diet changes | Lifestyle changes work best together |

---

<h3 id="supplements">Vitamin Supplements and CVD</h3>

Multivitamin supplements do **not** decrease or prevent cardiovascular disease when the diet is otherwise well balanced. Don't choose "start a multivitamin" as a cardiovascular risk-reduction answer.

### Supplement Safety

| Supplement Issue | ATI Point |
|---|---|
| **Multivitamin for CVD prevention** | Not the best answer if the diet is already balanced |
| **Potassium supplements** | Use caution with CKD, ACE inhibitors, ARBs, and potassium-sparing diuretics |
| **Vitamin K supplements** | Can affect warfarin therapy |
| **High-dose vitamin C** | Can increase oxalate stone risk in susceptible clients |

<h3 id="medication-food">Medication-Food Interactions</h3>

#### Warfarin and Vitamin K

Keep vitamin K intake **consistent**, not eliminated — sudden changes in either direction affect INR and bleeding/clotting risk.

#### ACE Inhibitors and Potassium

ACE inhibitors (e.g., captopril, enalapril, lisinopril) can cause potassium **retention**, risking hyperkalemia, which affects cardiac rhythm.

#### MAOIs and Tyramine

MAOIs plus high-tyramine foods (aged cheeses, cured/processed meats, fermented or pickled foods) can trigger a **hypertensive crisis**.

#### Statins and Grapefruit

Grapefruit interactions are statin-specific, based on which liver enzyme metabolizes the drug.

| Interacts With Grapefruit (avoid) | Does Not Significantly Interact |
|---|---|
| Atorvastatin, simvastatin, lovastatin | Pravastatin, rosuvastatin, fluvastatin, pitavastatin |

ATI may test that a client on **pravastatin** can safely continue grapefruit juice, while a client on **simvastatin or atorvastatin** should avoid it — the difference comes down to which liver enzyme breaks down that specific statin.

### Medication-Food Interaction Quick Chart

| Medication/Class | Food/Nutrient Concern | Teaching |
|---|---|---|
| **Warfarin** | Vitamin K | Keep intake consistent |
| **ACE inhibitors** | Potassium | Avoid sudden increases in high-potassium foods or salt substitutes unless approved |
| **ARBs** | Potassium | Same potassium caution as ACE inhibitors |
| **Potassium-sparing diuretics** | Potassium | Avoid potassium salt substitutes unless approved |
| **MAOIs** | Tyramine | Avoid aged, cured, fermented, and pickled foods |
| **Simvastatin/atorvastatin/lovastatin** | Grapefruit | Avoid grapefruit/grapefruit juice |
| **Pravastatin/rosuvastatin/fluvastatin/pitavastatin** | Grapefruit | Does not significantly interact |

<h3 id="ckd">Chronic Kidney Disease Nutrition</h3>

Never assume every CKD client needs the same diet. The plan depends on **potassium, phosphorus, sodium, fluid status, and dialysis status** — look for those specific clues in the question.

| Nutrient | CKD Consideration |
|---|---|
| **Sodium** | Often restricted to help control BP and fluid retention |
| **Potassium** | Restricted if serum potassium is high; kidneys may not clear it well |
| **Phosphorus** | Restricted if serum phosphorus is high — ties to bone/mineral balance |
| **Protein** | Depends on dialysis status (see below) |
| **Fluid** | Depends on urine output, edema, and dialysis status |
| **Carbohydrates** | A good calorie source that helps spare protein from being used for energy |

#### Protein and Dialysis Status

| Situation | Protein Teaching |
|---|---|
| **CKD, not yet on dialysis** | May be **restricted** if prescribed — helps preserve remaining kidney function |
| **On dialysis** | Protein needs are usually **higher** — dialysis itself removes some protein |

Rapid weight gain, edema, and crackles in a CKD client suggest **fluid overload** — a priority finding.

Many clients with CKD also need calcium, vitamin D, and iron supplementation, and may receive **erythropoietin** for anemia related to decreased kidney production of erythropoietin. Iron stores should be adequate for erythropoietin therapy to work effectively — a client who is iron-deficient may not respond well to erythropoietin alone.

### ATI Addition: CKD Electrolyte Clues

| Finding | Think |
|---|---|
| **High potassium** | Dysrhythmia risk; restrict high-potassium foods if prescribed |
| **High phosphorus** | Bone/mineral problems; restrict phosphorus and use phosphate binders |
| **Low calcium** | Often connected to phosphorus imbalance and vitamin D activation problems |
| **Fluid overload** | Rapid weight gain, edema, crackles, hypertension, shortness of breath |
| **Anemia** | Decreased erythropoietin production by kidneys |

### ATI Addition: Common CKD Food Teaching

| Nutrient Concern | Limit/Watch | Better Choices Often Used |
|---|---|---|
| **Potassium** | Bananas, oranges/orange juice, potatoes, tomatoes, spinach, dried fruit | Apples, berries, grapes, rice, pasta, green beans, lettuce |
| **Phosphorus** | Dairy, nuts, beans, colas, processed foods with phosphate additives | Follow prescribed binder/diet plan; choose lower-phosphorus alternatives |
| **Sodium** | Canned/processed foods, deli meats, salty snacks | Fresh foods, herbs/spices, low-sodium options |
| **Fluid** | Fluids may be restricted if urine output is low or fluid overload occurs | Track intake and daily weights if prescribed |

### ATI Addition: Dialysis Notes

| Type | Nutrition Point |
|---|---|
| **Hemodialysis** | Protein needs are usually higher because dialysis removes some protein |
| **Peritoneal dialysis** | Dialysate contains glucose, so calories from dialysate may matter |
| **Both** | Continue monitoring potassium, phosphorus, sodium, fluid, weight, and anemia labs |

---

<h3 id="phosphate-binders">Phosphate Binders</h3>

Phosphate binders must be taken **with meals and snacks** — they only work if they're in the gut at the same time as the phosphorus-containing food, binding it before it can be absorbed.

### Phosphate Binder Teaching

| Teaching | Why |
|---|---|
| Take with meals/snacks | Binds phosphorus from food in the gut |
| Do not take only at bedtime unless prescribed | It will not bind meal phosphorus if no food is present |
| Continue diet restrictions if prescribed | Binders help, but do not erase phosphorus intake |
| Report constipation or GI upset | Common binder-related concerns |

<h3 id="kidney-stones">Kidney Stones (Nephrolithiasis)</h3>

There are five major stone types. Diet teaching — and how much diet even helps — depends on which one the client has.

| Stone Type | How Common | Key Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Calcium oxalate** | Most common (~80% of stones) | Oxalate binds calcium in the urine |
| **Calcium phosphate** | Less common | Linked to alkaline urine and certain conditions (e.g., renal tubular acidosis, hyperparathyroidism) more than diet alone |
| **Uric acid** | Common | High purine intake → high uric acid → acidic urine favors crystal formation |
| **Struvite** | Less common | Forms with **urinary tract infections** caused by urease-producing bacteria (e.g., *Proteus*) — an infection stone, not primarily a diet stone |
| **Cystine** | Rare (genetic — cystinuria) | Inherited defect causes excess cystine excretion in urine |

A general rule across **every** stone type: increase fluid intake significantly (often 3–4 L/day if not contraindicated) to dilute the urine. This single intervention helps regardless of stone type — know it even if you forget the specific dietary nuance for each stone.

#### Calcium Oxalate Stones

This is a common ATI trap: clients prone to calcium oxalate stones should **not** avoid all calcium. Adequate **dietary** calcium (the RDA, from food) actually binds oxalate in the gut, reducing how much is absorbed and excreted in urine. What should be limited instead is **high-dose vitamin C supplements** and high-oxalate foods.

#### Calcium Phosphate Stones

Management overlaps with calcium oxalate stones (adequate dietary calcium, sodium restriction), but calcium phosphate stones are more strongly tied to urine pH and underlying medical conditions or medications than to diet alone — diet modification has a smaller effect on this stone type.

#### Uric Acid Stones

Limit high-purine foods (organ meats, red meat, shellfish, sardines, anchovies); limit alcohol (impairs uric acid excretion); maintain good hydration. Urine may also be alkalinized with medication to help dissolve existing uric acid stones — unlike most other stone types.

**Allopurinol** is the medication most associated with uric acid stones and gout — it inhibits xanthine oxidase, reducing how much uric acid the body produces in the first place. Diet lowers the *intake* of purines; allopurinol lowers the body's *production* of uric acid — they work by different mechanisms and are often used together.

##### Allopurinol nursing/nutrition points

- Take **with food** to reduce GI upset.
- Increase fluid intake (often 2–3 L/day if not contraindicated) — when therapy starts, uric acid crystals can mobilize and temporarily raise stone risk before levels stabilize.
- Avoid alcohol — it raises uric acid production and can work against the medication.
- Not started during an **acute gout flare** — starting it then can worsen the attack; it's used for longer-term prevention.
- Watch for severe hypersensitivity reactions (rare but serious, e.g., Stevens-Johnson syndrome) — report rash promptly.

#### Struvite Stones

Struvite stones are fundamentally an **infection** problem, not a diet problem — they form because urease-producing bacteria make urine alkaline. The nursing priority is **preventing and promptly treating UTIs**, not dietary restriction. Stone removal is often needed because the infection persists as long as the stone is present.

#### Cystine Stones

Cystinuria is genetic, so diet doesn't cure it, but supportive teaching includes: **high fluid intake is the single most important intervention** (often even higher targets than other stone types); limit sodium; limit animal protein (cystine comes from methionine metabolism, found in animal protein); urine alkalinization may be used to increase cystine solubility.

### ATI Addition: Kidney Stone Comparison

| Stone Type | Priority Teaching |
|---|---|
| **Calcium oxalate** | Increase fluids; limit high-oxalate foods and high-dose vitamin C; maintain dietary calcium |
| **Calcium phosphate** | Increase fluids; manage underlying urine pH/medical causes; sodium restriction may help |
| **Uric acid** | Increase fluids; limit purines and alcohol; allopurinol may be prescribed |
| **Struvite** | Treat/prevent UTI; diet is not the main fix |
| **Cystine** | Very high fluid intake; limit sodium and animal protein |

### ATI Addition: Urine pH Clues

| Stone Type | Urine Pattern |
|---|---|
| **Uric acid stones** | More likely in acidic urine |
| **Calcium phosphate stones** | More likely in alkaline urine |
| **Struvite stones** | Alkaline urine from urease-producing bacteria |
| **Cystine stones** | Solubility improves with alkalinization |

<h3 id="alcohol">Alcohol Teaching</h3>

General moderation teaching: up to **1 drink/day for women**, up to **2 drinks/day for men**, if consumed at all.

### Alcohol and Cardiorenal Nutrition

| Concern | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Hypertension** | Alcohol can worsen BP control |
| **Triglycerides** | Alcohol can increase triglycerides |
| **Uric acid stones/gout** | Alcohol impairs uric acid excretion |
| **Medication safety** | Alcohol can interact with many cardiac, lipid, and renal-related medications |

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="food-sources">Food Sources Quick Reference</h2>

#### Heart-Healthy Substitutions

| Instead of This | Choose This |
|---|---|
| High-fat cheese | Cheese with 3 g fat or less per serving |
| Margarine high in saturated fat | Margarine with 2 g saturated fat or less per tablespoon |
| Fatty ground beef | Ground beef at least 90% lean |
| Refried beans | Canned pinto beans |
| Pasta with white (cream) sauce | Pasta with red (tomato) sauce |
| Sausage | Canadian bacon or another lower-fat meat |
| Sweetened fruit yogurt | Plain fat-free yogurt with fresh fruit |
| Whole milk | Nonfat or low-fat milk |
| Butter/margarine (solid fat) | Liquid oils such as canola or olive oil |

#### High-Tyramine Foods (Avoid With MAOIs)

| Foods to Avoid |
|---|
| Aged cheeses, cured/processed/smoked meats, fermented or pickled foods |

#### High-Potassium Foods (Watch With ACE Inhibitors / CKD)

| Foods |
|---|
| Bananas, cantaloupe, oranges/orange juice, potatoes, tomatoes, spinach, dried fruit |

#### Lower-Potassium Options

| Foods |
|---|
| Apples, berries, grapes, rice, pasta, green beans, lettuce |

#### High-Phosphorus Foods (Watch With CKD)

| Foods |
|---|
| Dairy products, nuts, beans, colas, processed foods with phosphate additives |

#### High-Vitamin K Foods (Keep Consistent With Warfarin)

| Foods |
|---|
| Kale, spinach, collard greens, broccoli, Brussels sprouts |

#### Uric Acid Stone Prevention: Purine Content

| Higher-Purine (Limit) | Lower-Purine (Better Choices) |
|---|---|
| Organ meats, red meat, shellfish, sardines, anchovies, alcohol | Poultry, low-fat dairy, eggs, most fruits/vegetables, legumes |

#### Calcium Oxalate & Calcium Phosphate Stone Prevention

| Maintain | Limit |
|---|---|
| Dietary calcium (RDA, from food); adequate fluids | High-oxalate foods (spinach, rhubarb, beets, nuts, chocolate, tea); high-dose vitamin C supplements; excess sodium |

#### Cystine Stone Prevention

| Maintain | Limit |
|---|---|
| Very high fluid intake | Animal protein (source of methionine → cystine); sodium |

#### Struvite Stones

No specific food list — this stone type is driven by infection, not diet. Focus is UTI prevention/treatment.
