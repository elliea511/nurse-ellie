<h2 class="section-divider" id="cardiovascular-and-renal-nutrition">Cardiovascular &amp; Renal Nutrition</h2>

<div class="memory">
For this section, the core question is almost always: <strong>control sodium, protect the heart, protect the kidneys, watch potassium.</strong>
</div>

---

<h3 id="big-picture">Big Picture: Heart and Kidneys Together</h3>

The heart pumps blood; the kidneys regulate fluid volume, sodium, potassium, blood pressure, and waste removal. When nutrition isn't managed well, the result is usually worsening hypertension, fluid overload, electrolyte imbalance, or increased cardiovascular risk.

| Topic | Why Nutrition Matters |
|---|---|
| **Hypertension** | Sodium and fluid retention raise blood pressure |
| **Cardiovascular disease** | Saturated fat, excess sodium, and excess calories increase risk |
| **CKD** | Kidneys may not regulate fluid, potassium, sodium, or phosphorus well |
| **Kidney stones** | Fluid intake and specific dietary factors affect stone formation |

---

<h3 id="sodium-bp">Sodium and Blood Pressure</h3>

Sodium causes the body to retain water (see <a href="{{ '/nutrition-basics.html#minerals' | relative_url }}">Nutrition Basics → Minerals</a> for the full extracellular fluid mechanism). More retained fluid means more circulating volume, which raises blood pressure and cardiac workload.

<div class="ati-tip">
The greatest blood pressure reductions are seen with sodium intake around <strong>1,500 mg/day or less</strong>.
</div>

<div class="teaching">
Core teaching: avoid adding salt at the table, read nutrition labels (sodium hides in packaged/processed foods), limit canned soups and processed/deli meats, choose fresh foods, and use herbs/spices for flavor instead of salt.
</div>

---

<h3 id="salt-substitutes">Salt Substitutes</h3>

<div class="danger">
Many salt substitutes replace sodium with <strong>potassium</strong> — they are not sodium-free and can contain roughly half as much sodium as table salt while adding meaningful potassium. This is risky for clients with CKD, hyperkalemia risk, or those on ACE inhibitors, ARBs, or potassium-sparing diuretics.
</div>

---

<h3 id="cholesterol-fat">Cholesterol and Fat Teaching</h3>

The goal isn't "no fat" — it's choosing the right type of fat in the right amount.

| Fat Type | Effect | Teaching |
|---|---|---|
| **Saturated fat** | Raises LDL ("bad") cholesterol | Limit; choose leaner cuts and lower-fat dairy |
| **Trans fat** | Raises LDL, lowers HDL | Avoid as much as possible; check labels for "partially hydrogenated oils" |
| **Unsaturated fat** | Generally heart-protective | Preferred fat source, in appropriate portions |

<div class="teaching">
Cooking method matters as much as fat type: baked, grilled, or broiled instead of fried; remove poultry skin (it holds much of the fat); choose liquid oils (e.g., canola) over solid fats like margarine or butter; use nonfat or low-fat milk instead of whole milk.
</div>

<div class="ati-tip">
Fatty fish (such as salmon) eaten at least <strong>twice a week</strong> is a recommended source of omega-3 fatty acids for cardiovascular health, even on a low-cholesterol diet — fish isn't excluded the way fatty red meat is.
</div>

---

<h3 id="plant-protein">Plant-Based Protein and Heart Health</h3>

<div class="nclex">
Replacing high-fat animal protein with plant-based protein (beans, lentils, peas, nuts, seeds, soy) can help <strong>lower cholesterol</strong> and reduce the heart's workload, while still supporting muscle mass for weight management.
</div>

---

<h3 id="niacin">Niacin</h3>

<div class="med">
Niacin (a B vitamin) can be used to help lower <strong>LDL cholesterol and triglycerides</strong>.
</div>

---

<h3 id="exercise">Exercise and Cardiovascular Risk</h3>

<div class="ati-tip">
At least <strong>150 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise per week</strong> supports cardiovascular health, blood pressure control, glucose control, and lipid improvement.
</div>

---

<h3 id="supplements">Vitamin Supplements and CVD</h3>

<div class="note">
Multivitamin supplements do <strong>not</strong> decrease or prevent cardiovascular disease when the diet is otherwise well balanced. Don't choose "start a multivitamin" as a cardiovascular risk-reduction answer.
</div>

---

<h3 id="medication-food">Medication-Food Interactions</h3>

#### Warfarin and Vitamin K

<div class="med">
Keep vitamin K intake <strong>consistent</strong>, not eliminated — sudden changes in either direction affect INR and bleeding/clotting risk.
</div>

#### ACE Inhibitors and Potassium

<div class="danger">
ACE inhibitors (e.g., captopril, enalapril, lisinopril) can cause potassium <strong>retention</strong>, risking hyperkalemia, which affects cardiac rhythm.
</div>

#### MAOIs and Tyramine

<div class="danger">
MAOIs plus high-tyramine foods (aged cheeses, cured/processed meats, fermented or pickled foods) can trigger a <strong>hypertensive crisis</strong>.
</div>

#### Statins and Grapefruit

Grapefruit interactions are statin-specific, based on which liver enzyme metabolizes the drug.

| Interacts With Grapefruit (avoid) | Does Not Significantly Interact |
|---|---|
| Atorvastatin, simvastatin, lovastatin | Pravastatin, rosuvastatin, fluvastatin, pitavastatin |

<div class="ati-tip">
ATI may test that a client on <strong>pravastatin</strong> can safely continue grapefruit juice, while a client on <strong>simvastatin or atorvastatin</strong> should avoid it — the difference comes down to which liver enzyme breaks down that specific statin.
</div>

<h3 id="ckd">Chronic Kidney Disease Nutrition</h3>

<div class="nclex">
Never assume every CKD client needs the same diet. The plan depends on <strong>potassium, phosphorus, sodium, fluid status, and dialysis status</strong> — look for those specific clues in the question.
</div>

| Nutrient | CKD Consideration |
|---|---|
| **Sodium** | Often restricted to help control BP and fluid retention |
| **Potassium** | Restricted if serum potassium is high; kidneys may not clear it well |
| **Phosphorus** | Restricted if serum phosphorus is high — ties to bone/mineral balance |
| **Protein** | Depends on dialysis status (see below) |
| **Fluid** | Depends on urine output, edema, and dialysis status |
| **Carbohydrates** | A good calorie source that helps spare protein from being used for energy |

#### Protein and Dialysis Status

| Situation | Protein Teaching |
|---|---|
| **CKD, not yet on dialysis** | May be **restricted** if prescribed — helps preserve remaining kidney function |
| **On dialysis** | Protein needs are usually **higher** — dialysis itself removes some protein |

<div class="danger">
Rapid weight gain, edema, and crackles in a CKD client suggest <strong>fluid overload</strong> — a priority finding.
</div>

<div class="note">
Many clients with CKD also need calcium, vitamin D, and iron supplementation, and may receive <strong>erythropoietin</strong> for anemia related to decreased kidney production of erythropoietin. Iron stores should be adequate for erythropoietin therapy to work effectively — a client who is iron-deficient may not respond well to erythropoietin alone.
</div>

---

<h3 id="phosphate-binders">Phosphate Binders</h3>

<div class="med">
Phosphate binders must be taken <strong>with meals and snacks</strong> — they only work if they're in the gut at the same time as the phosphorus-containing food, binding it before it can be absorbed.
</div>

---

<h3 id="kidney-stones">Kidney Stones (Nephrolithiasis)</h3>

There are five major stone types. Diet teaching — and how much diet even helps — depends on which one the client has.

| Stone Type | How Common | Key Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Calcium oxalate** | Most common (~80% of stones) | Oxalate binds calcium in the urine |
| **Calcium phosphate** | Less common | Linked to alkaline urine and certain conditions (e.g., renal tubular acidosis, hyperparathyroidism) more than diet alone |
| **Uric acid** | Common | High purine intake → high uric acid → acidic urine favors crystal formation |
| **Struvite** | Less common | Forms with **urinary tract infections** caused by urease-producing bacteria (e.g., *Proteus*) — an infection stone, not primarily a diet stone |
| **Cystine** | Rare (genetic — cystinuria) | Inherited defect causes excess cystine excretion in urine |

<div class="ati-tip">
A general rule across <strong>every</strong> stone type: increase fluid intake significantly (often 3–4 L/day if not contraindicated) to dilute the urine. This single intervention helps regardless of stone type — know it even if you forget the specific dietary nuance for each stone.
</div>

#### Calcium Oxalate Stones

<div class="danger">
This is a common ATI trap: clients prone to calcium oxalate stones should <strong>not</strong> avoid all calcium. Adequate <strong>dietary</strong> calcium (the RDA, from food) actually binds oxalate in the gut, reducing how much is absorbed and excreted in urine. What should be limited instead is <strong>high-dose vitamin C supplements</strong> and high-oxalate foods.
</div>

#### Calcium Phosphate Stones

<div class="note">
Management overlaps with calcium oxalate stones (adequate dietary calcium, sodium restriction), but calcium phosphate stones are more strongly tied to urine pH and underlying medical conditions or medications than to diet alone — diet modification has a smaller effect on this stone type.
</div>

#### Uric Acid Stones

<div class="teaching">
Limit high-purine foods (organ meats, red meat, shellfish, sardines, anchovies); limit alcohol (impairs uric acid excretion); maintain good hydration. Urine may also be alkalinized with medication to help dissolve existing uric acid stones — unlike most other stone types.
</div>

<div class="med">
<strong>Allopurinol</strong> is the medication most associated with uric acid stones and gout — it inhibits xanthine oxidase, reducing how much uric acid the body produces in the first place. Diet lowers the *intake* of purines; allopurinol lowers the body's *production* of uric acid — they work by different mechanisms and are often used together.
</div>

<details class="study-dropdown orange" markdown="1">
<summary><strong>Dig Deeper: Allopurinol nursing/nutrition points</strong></summary>

- Take **with food** to reduce GI upset.
- Increase fluid intake (often 2–3 L/day if not contraindicated) — when therapy starts, uric acid crystals can mobilize and temporarily raise stone risk before levels stabilize.
- Avoid alcohol — it raises uric acid production and can work against the medication.
- Not started during an **acute gout flare** — starting it then can worsen the attack; it's used for longer-term prevention.
- Watch for severe hypersensitivity reactions (rare but serious, e.g., Stevens-Johnson syndrome) — report rash promptly.

</details>

#### Struvite Stones

<div class="danger">
Struvite stones are fundamentally an <strong>infection</strong> problem, not a diet problem — they form because urease-producing bacteria make urine alkaline. The nursing priority is <strong>preventing and promptly treating UTIs</strong>, not dietary restriction. Stone removal is often needed because the infection persists as long as the stone is present.
</div>

#### Cystine Stones

<div class="teaching">
Cystinuria is genetic, so diet doesn't cure it, but supportive teaching includes: <strong>high fluid intake is the single most important intervention</strong> (often even higher targets than other stone types); limit sodium; limit animal protein (cystine comes from methionine metabolism, found in animal protein); urine alkalinization may be used to increase cystine solubility.
</div>

---

<h3 id="alcohol">Alcohol Teaching</h3>

<div class="note">
General moderation teaching: up to <strong>1 drink/day for women</strong>, up to <strong>2 drinks/day for men</strong>, if consumed at all.
</div>

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="food-sources">Food Sources Quick Reference</h2>

#### Heart-Healthy Substitutions

| Instead of This | Choose This |
|---|---|
| High-fat cheese | Cheese with 3 g fat or less per serving |
| Margarine high in saturated fat | Margarine with 2 g saturated fat or less per tablespoon |
| Fatty ground beef | Ground beef at least 90% lean |
| Refried beans | Canned pinto beans |
| Pasta with white (cream) sauce | Pasta with red (tomato) sauce |
| Sausage | Canadian bacon or another lower-fat meat |
| Sweetened fruit yogurt | Plain fat-free yogurt with fresh fruit |
| Whole milk | Nonfat or low-fat milk |
| Butter/margarine (solid fat) | Liquid oils such as canola or olive oil |

#### High-Tyramine Foods (Avoid With MAOIs)

| Foods to Avoid |
|---|
| Aged cheeses, cured/processed/smoked meats, fermented or pickled foods |

#### High-Potassium Foods (Watch With ACE Inhibitors / CKD)

| Foods |
|---|
| Bananas, cantaloupe, oranges/orange juice, potatoes, tomatoes, spinach, dried fruit |

#### High-Vitamin K Foods (Keep Consistent With Warfarin)

| Foods |
|---|
| Kale, spinach, collard greens, broccoli, Brussels sprouts |

#### Uric Acid Stone Prevention: Purine Content

| Higher-Purine (Limit) | Lower-Purine (Better Choices) |
|---|---|
| Organ meats, red meat, shellfish, sardines, anchovies, alcohol | Poultry, low-fat dairy, eggs, most fruits/vegetables, legumes |

#### Calcium Oxalate &amp; Calcium Phosphate Stone Prevention

| Maintain | Limit |
|---|---|
| Dietary calcium (RDA, from food); adequate fluids | High-oxalate foods (spinach, rhubarb, beets, nuts, chocolate, tea); high-dose vitamin C supplements; excess sodium |

#### Cystine Stone Prevention

| Maintain | Limit |
|---|---|
| Very high fluid intake | Animal protein (source of methionine → cystine); sodium |

#### Struvite Stones

<div class="note">
No specific food list — this stone type is driven by infection, not diet. Focus is UTI prevention/treatment.
</div>

<h2 class="section-divider small" id="section-summary">Section Summary &amp; Test-Taking Tips</h2>

| If the Question Says... | Think... |
|---|---|
| Sodium target for BP reduction | ~1,500 mg/day or less |
| "Low-sodium" salt substitute | May still contain meaningful sodium and added potassium — caution with CKD/ACE inhibitors |
| Plant-based protein | Lowers cholesterol, reduces heart workload |
| Niacin | Lowers LDL and triglycerides |
| Multivitamin + cardiovascular disease | Does not prevent CVD if diet is balanced |
| Warfarin | Consistent vitamin K, not elimination |
| ACE inhibitor + high-potassium food | Hyperkalemia risk |
| MAOI + aged cheese/cured meat | Hypertensive crisis risk (tyramine) |
| Pravastatin/rosuvastatin + grapefruit | Generally safe — these aren't CYP3A4-metabolized |
| Simvastatin/atorvastatin/lovastatin + grapefruit | Avoid — significant interaction |
| CKD diet plan | Depends on potassium, phosphorus, sodium, fluid status, dialysis status |
| CKD not on dialysis | Protein may be restricted |
| CKD on dialysis | Protein needs are usually higher |
| Phosphate binder timing | With meals and snacks |
| CKD + anemia | May need erythropoietin; adequate iron supports its effectiveness |
| Calcium oxalate / calcium phosphate stones | Maintain RDA calcium from food; limit oxalate foods and high-dose vitamin C |
| Uric acid stones | Limit purine-rich foods (organ meats, shellfish, red meat); limit alcohol |
| Allopurinol | Reduces uric acid production (not intake); take with food; increase fluids; avoid alcohol; don't start during acute gout flare |
| Struvite stones | Infection stone — focus on UTI prevention/treatment, not diet |
| Cystine stones | Genetic — very high fluid intake is most important; limit sodium and animal protein |
| Any kidney stone type | Increase fluid intake significantly unless contraindicated |
| Rapid weight gain + edema + crackles in CKD | Fluid overload — priority finding |
