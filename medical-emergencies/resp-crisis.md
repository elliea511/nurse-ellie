# Pulmonary Embolism — PE

## What It Is

A pulmonary embolism is a blockage in the pulmonary artery or one of its branches.

It is usually caused by a blood clot that formed somewhere else, often in the deep veins of the legs, and traveled to the lungs.

## Main Problem

A clot blocks blood flow in the lungs.

This causes impaired gas exchange and decreased oxygenation.

## Simple Explanation

The client may be able to breathe air in, but blood cannot flow properly through part of the lung to pick up oxygen.

This makes PE both a respiratory emergency and a perfusion emergency.

---

## Risk Factors

* Immobility
* Bed rest
* Recent surgery
* Trauma
* Pregnancy
* Postpartum period
* Heart failure
* Hypercoagulable states
* Cancer
* Obesity
* Smoking
* Oral contraceptive use
* Long travel
* History of DVT or PE

---

## Signs and Symptoms

A PE usually has a sudden onset.

Key findings:

* Sudden shortness of breath
* Chest pain
* Chest pain worse with breathing
* Tachypnea
* Tachycardia
* Low oxygen saturation
* Anxiety
* Restlessness
* Cough
* Hemoptysis
* Syncope
* Feeling of impending doom

## NCLEX Clue

Sudden shortness of breath after surgery, immobility, pregnancy, or DVT symptoms should make you think pulmonary embolism.

---

## Priority Nursing Action

The priority is respiratory assessment and support.

First actions:

1. Assess airway and breathing.
2. Check oxygen saturation.
3. Raise the head of the bed.
4. Apply oxygen.
5. Stay with the client.
6. Notify the provider or rapid response team.
7. Prepare for diagnostic testing and anticoagulation.

## Why Respiratory Support Comes First

The clot is blocking blood flow in the lungs.

The immediate danger is impaired oxygenation.

---

## Diagnostics

| Test                | Purpose                                           |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| D-dimer             | Helps rule out inappropriate clot formation       |
| CT angiography      | Common test to confirm PE                         |
| V/Q scan            | Measures airflow and blood flow through lungs     |
| Pulmonary angiogram | Uses contrast dye to check for PE                 |
| ABGs                | May show hypoxemia or respiratory alkalosis early |
| Chest x-ray         | May rule out other causes                         |
| Leg ultrasound      | Looks for DVT source                              |

## D-Dimer

A normal D-dimer is usually less than or equal to 500 ng/mL.

An elevated D-dimer does not automatically mean PE, but it tells us clot breakdown may be occurring somewhere in the body.

---

## Treatment

| Treatment                       | Why It Is Used                                                          |
| ------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Oxygen                          | Improves oxygenation                                                    |
| Heparin drip                    | Prevents clot from getting bigger                                       |
| Warfarin or oral anticoagulants | Long-term clot prevention                                               |
| Thrombolytics                   | Break down severe or life-threatening clots                             |
| Surgical embolectomy            | Removes clot if needed                                                  |
| Vena cava filter                | Prevents clots from traveling to lungs if anticoagulants cannot be used |

## Important Medication Point

Anticoagulants do not dissolve the clot.

They prevent the clot from getting bigger and help prevent new clots.

Thrombolytics dissolve clots.

---

# Heparin for PE

## Why Heparin Is Common

Heparin is commonly used in the hospital for PE.

It works quickly and is usually given IV.

## Lab to Monitor

Monitor PTT/aPTT.

Memory trick:

**Two T’s = Heparin**

## Bleeding Precautions

* Monitor for bruising.
* Monitor gums.
* Monitor urine and stool.
* Avoid IM injections if possible.
* Use soft toothbrush.
* Use electric razor.
* Hold pressure longer after blood draws.
* Report black stools.
* Report blood in urine.
* Report severe headache or neuro changes.

---

# Thrombolytics for PE

Thrombolytics may be used when the PE is severe or life-threatening.

They break down clots but increase the risk for bleeding.

## Monitor For

* Bleeding gums
* Nosebleeds
* Blood in urine
* Blood in stool
* Sudden headache
* Decreased level of consciousness
* Hypotension
* Signs of internal bleeding

---

# PE Complications

* Respiratory failure
* Shock
* Pulmonary hypertension
* Right-sided heart strain
* Cardiac arrest
* Death

---

# PE Prevention

Prevention is important for hospitalized or post-op clients.

Interventions:

* Early ambulation
* Leg exercises
* Sequential compression devices
* Anticoagulants as prescribed
* Hydration if allowed
* Avoid crossing legs
* Stop smoking
* Change positions during long travel

# Anaphylaxis

## What It Is

Anaphylaxis is a severe, life-threatening allergic reaction.

It can affect the airway, breathing, circulation, and skin.

## Main Problem

Massive allergic response causes:

* Airway swelling
* Bronchoconstriction
* Vasodilation
* Capillary leaking
* Hypotension
* Shock

## Simple Explanation

The body overreacts to an allergen.

The airway may swell, the lungs may tighten, and the blood pressure may drop quickly.

---

## Common Triggers

* Foods, especially peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, eggs, milk
* Medications, especially antibiotics
* Latex
* Bee or insect stings
* Contrast dye
* Vaccines, rarely
* Blood products, rarely

---

## Signs and Symptoms

### Airway/Breathing

* Throat tightness
* Tongue swelling
* Lip swelling
* Hoarseness
* Stridor
* Wheezing
* Shortness of breath
* Cough
* Trouble swallowing

### Circulation

* Hypotension
* Tachycardia
* Weak pulse
* Dizziness
* Syncope
* Shock

### Skin/GI

* Hives
* Itching
* Flushing
* Swelling
* Nausea
* Vomiting
* Abdominal cramping
* Diarrhea

## NCLEX Tip

Do not wait for hives to diagnose anaphylaxis.

Airway swelling, wheezing, and hypotension after allergen exposure are enough to treat immediately.

---

## Priority Nursing Action

1. Stop exposure to the trigger.
2. Call for help.
3. Assess airway, breathing, circulation.
4. Give IM epinephrine as prescribed.
5. Apply oxygen.
6. Place client supine with legs elevated if hypotensive, unless breathing is worse.
7. Establish IV access.
8. Give IV fluids if hypotensive.
9. Prepare for airway support.
10. Monitor closely for recurrence.

---

# Epinephrine

## Why It Is First

Epinephrine is the priority medication for anaphylaxis.

It helps:

* Relax bronchial smooth muscle
* Decrease airway swelling
* Increase blood pressure
* Improve cardiac output

## Route

Epinephrine is usually given IM in the outer thigh for emergency anaphylaxis.

## NCLEX Tip

If the question asks what medication comes first for anaphylaxis, choose epinephrine.

Antihistamines and steroids may help later, but they do not fix airway swelling and shock fast enough.

---

## Other Treatments

| Treatment       | Purpose                                  |
| --------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| Oxygen          | Improves oxygenation                     |
| IV fluids       | Treats hypotension/shock                 |
| Albuterol       | Helps bronchospasm/wheezing              |
| Antihistamines  | Helps hives/itching after stabilization  |
| Corticosteroids | Helps decrease prolonged inflammation    |
| Vasopressors    | May be needed if hypotension continues   |
| Intubation      | May be needed if airway swelling worsens |

---

## Patient Teaching

* Avoid known triggers.
* Wear medical alert identification.
* Carry epinephrine auto-injector if prescribed.
* Know how to use the auto-injector.
* Seek emergency care after using epinephrine.
* Check expiration date.
* Teach family/friends how to help.
* Read food and medication labels carefully.

# Pneumothorax

## What It Is

A pneumothorax occurs when air enters the pleural space.

The pleural space is the space between the lung and chest wall.

Air in this space can cause the lung to collapse.

## Main Problem

Air in pleural space → lung cannot fully expand → impaired ventilation.

---

## Types

| Type                     | Meaning                                                |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| Spontaneous pneumothorax | Occurs without trauma                                  |
| Traumatic pneumothorax   | Caused by chest trauma                                 |
| Iatrogenic pneumothorax  | Caused by medical procedure                            |
| Open pneumothorax        | Chest wall opening allows air into pleural space       |
| Tension pneumothorax     | Air enters and cannot escape, causing pressure buildup |

---

## Risk Factors

* Chest trauma
* Rib fracture
* Mechanical ventilation
* Central line placement
* Lung disease
* Tall, thin body build
* Smoking
* Previous pneumothorax

---

## Signs and Symptoms

* Sudden sharp chest pain
* Shortness of breath
* Tachypnea
* Tachycardia
* Decreased or absent breath sounds on affected side
* Unequal chest expansion
* Anxiety
* Low oxygen saturation
* Hyperresonance on affected side

---

# Tension Pneumothorax

## What It Is

Tension pneumothorax is the most dangerous type.

Air enters the pleural space but cannot escape.

Pressure builds up and pushes the lung, heart, and great vessels.

## Main Problem

Pressure in the chest increases so much that it decreases venous return and cardiac output.

This can cause obstructive shock.

---

## Signs and Symptoms

* Severe respiratory distress
* Severe shortness of breath
* Chest pain
* Absent breath sounds on affected side
* Hypotension
* Tachycardia
* Jugular vein distention
* Tracheal deviation away from affected side
* Cyanosis
* Restlessness
* Anxiety
* Decreased level of consciousness

## Late Sign

Tracheal deviation is usually a late sign.

Do not wait for tracheal deviation to act if the client is unstable.

---

## Priority Nursing Action

Tension pneumothorax is an emergency.

1. Call for emergency help.
2. Apply oxygen.
3. Prepare for immediate needle decompression.
4. Prepare for chest tube placement.
5. Monitor respiratory status and vital signs.

## NCLEX Tip

If tension pneumothorax is suspected and the client is unstable, treatment should not wait for chest x-ray.

---

# Open Pneumothorax

## What It Is

An open pneumothorax happens when there is an opening in the chest wall.

Air can move through the chest wound into the pleural space.

## Signs

* Open chest wound
* Sucking sound
* Frothy blood from wound
* Respiratory distress
* Decreased breath sounds

## Nursing Care

* Apply a sterile occlusive dressing.
* Tape the dressing on three sides if ordered/taught by protocol.
* This allows air to escape but helps prevent more air from entering.
* Apply oxygen.
* Notify provider/rapid response.
* Prepare for chest tube.

---

# Chest Tube Basics

## Why It Is Used

A chest tube removes air or fluid from the pleural space.

This allows the lung to re-expand.

## Nursing Care

* Keep drainage system below chest level.
* Do not clamp unless prescribed or facility protocol.
* Assess breath sounds.
* Monitor respiratory status.
* Assess insertion site.
* Check for tidaling in water seal chamber.
* Monitor drainage amount and color.
* Report sudden increase in bright red drainage.
* Report new respiratory distress.
* Ensure connections are secure.

## If Chest Tube Comes Out

* Cover site with sterile occlusive dressing.
* Notify provider immediately.
* Monitor respiratory status.

## If Tubing Disconnects

* Place tube end in sterile water if available.
* Notify provider.
* Reconnect using sterile technique if directed by facility protocol.

# Airway Obstruction

## What It Is

An airway obstruction happens when something blocks air movement.

This can be caused by:

* Food
* Foreign object
* Secretions
* Swelling
* Vomit
* Tongue obstruction in an unconscious client

---

## Mild Airway Obstruction

The client can still move air.

Signs:

* Forceful cough
* Able to speak
* Able to breathe
* Wheezing between coughs

## Nursing Action

Encourage coughing.

Do not interfere if the client is coughing effectively.

---

## Severe Airway Obstruction

The client cannot move air effectively.

Signs:

* Cannot speak
* Cannot cough effectively
* Cannot breathe
* Silent cough
* Cyanosis
* Clutching throat
* Panic
* Loss of consciousness if untreated

## Nursing Action for Conscious Adult or Child

* Call for help.
* Perform abdominal thrusts.
* Continue until object is expelled or client becomes unconscious.

## If Pregnant or Obese

Use chest thrusts instead of abdominal thrusts.

---

# Infant Airway Obstruction

## Severe Obstruction in Conscious Infant

Use:

* Back blows
* Chest thrusts

Do not use abdominal thrusts on infants.

## Important

Do not do blind finger sweeps.

Only remove an object if you can clearly see it.

---

# Unconscious Airway Obstruction

If the client becomes unconscious:

1. Lower the client to the floor.
2. Call for help/code.
3. Start CPR.
4. Open airway before breaths.
5. Look for visible object.
6. Remove object only if seen.
7. Do not perform blind finger sweep.

---

# Aspiration Risk

## What It Is

Aspiration occurs when food, fluid, vomit, or secretions enter the airway.

## Risk Factors

* Stroke
* Dysphagia
* Decreased level of consciousness
* Sedation
* Vomiting
* Tube feedings
* Weak cough
* Neuromuscular disorders

## Signs

* Coughing while eating
* Choking
* Wet/gurgly voice
* Drooling
* Wheezing
* Crackles
* Respiratory distress
* Fever later if aspiration pneumonia develops

## Nursing Care

* Keep NPO if swallowing is unsafe.
* Perform swallow screen as ordered.
* Sit upright for meals.
* Use thickened liquids if ordered.
* Keep suction available.
* Turn unconscious vomiting client to side.
* Maintain HOB elevation for tube feedings.
* Monitor lung sounds and oxygen saturation.

---

# Oxygen and Positioning Priorities

| Situation                    | Best Position                                      |
| ---------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| General respiratory distress | High Fowler’s                                      |
| Pulmonary edema              | High Fowler’s                                      |
| PE                           | High Fowler’s                                      |
| Anaphylaxis with hypotension | Supine with legs elevated unless breathing worsens |
| Unconscious vomiting client  | Side-lying                                         |
| Pneumothorax                 | Position of comfort, often high Fowler’s           |
| Post chest tube              | Usually semi-Fowler’s or ordered position          |

---

# Most Important “What Do I Do First?” Points

## Pulmonary Embolism

Respiratory assessment/support, oxygen, high Fowler’s, notify provider.

## Anaphylaxis

Give epinephrine first.

## Pneumothorax

Assess respiratory status, give oxygen, prepare for chest tube if needed.

## Tension Pneumothorax

Prepare for immediate needle decompression.

## Airway Obstruction

If coughing effectively, encourage cough.

If unable to talk/cough/breathe, perform obstruction maneuvers.

## Aspiration Risk

Keep NPO until swallow safety is confirmed.
