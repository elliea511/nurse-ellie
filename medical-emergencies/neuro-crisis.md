# Increased ICP and Hydrocephalus

------

# Increased Intracranial Pressure

## What It Is

Increased intracranial pressure, or increased ICP, means there is too much pressure inside the skull.

Think of the skull as a **rigid box**. It does not stretch like skin.

Inside the skull are:

- Brain tissue
- Blood
- Cerebrospinal fluid, or CSF

If any of these increase, pressure inside the skull rises.

## Causes

Increased ICP can occur from:

- Brain swelling
- Head injury
- Bleeding in the brain
- Tumor
- Stroke
- Infection
- Hydrocephalus
- Excess CSF

------

# Why Increased ICP Is Dangerous

Increased pressure can decrease blood flow and oxygen to the brain.

This can lead to:

- Brain tissue damage
- Seizures
- Herniation
- Respiratory changes
- Death

------

# Normal ICP

| ICP Value                                  | Meaning                    |
| ------------------------------------------ | -------------------------- |
| 0–15 mm Hg                                 | Normal ICP                 |
| 20 mm Hg or higher for more than 5 minutes | Life-threatening emergency |

------

# Recognize Increased ICP

## Earliest Sign

The earliest and most important sign is a **change in level of consciousness**.

This may look like:

- Restlessness
- Confusion
- Drowsiness
- Irritability
- Decreased responsiveness

------

## Other Signs and Symptoms

- Headache
- Nausea and vomiting
- Pupillary changes
- Weakness
- Slurred speech
- Seizures
- Papilledema
- Abnormal posturing

## Late Signs

Late signs include:

- Bradycardia
- Hypertension
- Widened pulse pressure
- Bradypnea
- Cheyne-Stokes respirations
- Severe headache
- Nausea and vomiting
- Fixed and dilated pupils

------

# Cushing’s Triad

Cushing’s triad is a **late sign** of increased ICP.

## Findings

| Finding                | What Happens                                        |
| ---------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Systolic hypertension  | Systolic BP increases                               |
| Widened pulse pressure | Difference between systolic and diastolic BP widens |
| Bradycardia            | Pulse decreases                                     |
| Respiratory depression | Respirations decrease or become abnormal            |

------

# Increased ICP vs Shock

| Finding          | Increased ICP                | Shock            |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------- | ---------------- |
| Blood pressure   | Systolic BP increases        | BP decreases     |
| Pulse            | Decreases                    | Increases        |
| Respirations     | Decrease or become irregular | Increase         |
| Priority concern | Brain pressure/perfusion     | Tissue perfusion |

------

# Level of Consciousness Terms

| Term               | Meaning                                            |
| ------------------ | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Full consciousness | Awake, alert, oriented, interacts with environment |
| Confused           | Lacks ability to think clearly and rapidly         |
| Disoriented        | Does not recognize place or person                 |
| Lethargic          | Awakens easily but has limited responsiveness      |
| Obtunded           | Sleeps and, once aroused, has limited interaction  |
| Stupor             | Requires stimulation to arouse                     |
| Coma               | Vigorous stimulation produces no response          |

------

# Glasgow Coma Scale Basics

You may not need to calculate the full score, but know what the ranges mean.

| GCS Score | Severity             |
| --------- | -------------------- |
| 13–15     | Mild head injury     |
| 9–12      | Moderate head injury |
| 3–8       | Severe head injury   |

------

## Blood and Urine Tests

- ABGs
- CBC
- Coagulation profile
- Electrolytes
- Serum osmolality
- Urinalysis
- Urine osmolality

## Imaging and Other Tests

- CT scan of the head
- MRI
- EEG

# Priority Assessment for Increased ICP

Assess:

- Level of consciousness
- Pupils
- Motor strength
- Speech
- Vital signs
- Respiratory pattern
- Headache
- Vomiting
- Seizure activity
- Intake and output
- Drainage from ears or nose
- Bilateral neuro assessment

## Report Immediately

- Change in LOC
- New confusion
- Unequal pupils
- Fixed or dilated pupils
- New weakness
- Seizure
- Cushing’s triad
- Projectile vomiting
- Worsening headache
- Abnormal posturing
- Fever
- Drainage from ears or nose

------

# Nursing Interventions for Increased ICP

## Positioning

- Place client in Semi-Fowler’s position.
- Keep head in neutral midline position.
- Avoid neck flexion.
- Avoid neck rotation.
- Avoid extreme hip flexion.

### Why

This helps promote venous drainage from the brain.

------

## Safety and Neuro Protection

- Monitor LOC closely.
- Compare assessments bilaterally.
- Monitor vital signs carefully.
- Report changes immediately.
- Treat as spinal injury until ruled out.
- Monitor for halo sign if fluid leaks from ears or nose.
- Keep environment calm.
- Limit unnecessary stimulation.
- Allow rest periods.

------

## Respiratory Care

- Monitor respiratory status.
- Monitor ABGs.
- Maintain oxygenation.
- Keep PaO₂ above 80 mm Hg as ordered to help prevent secondary brain injury.
- Prepare for advanced oxygen therapy if needed.

------

## Fever

Fever should be reported immediately.

Fever may be caused by:

- Infection
- Injury to the hypothalamus

------

# What To Avoid With Increased ICP

| Avoid                 | Why                        |
| --------------------- | -------------------------- |
| Coughing              | Increases pressure         |
| Straining             | Increases pressure         |
| Valsalva maneuver     | Increases pressure         |
| Excessive suctioning  | Can increase ICP           |
| Overstimulation       | Can worsen ICP             |
| Hip flexion           | Reduces venous drainage    |
| Neck flexion/rotation | Reduces venous drainage    |
| Tight collars or ties | Can restrict venous return |

------

# Medications and Treatments for Increased ICP

## Steroids

### Purpose

May decrease inflammation and swelling depending on cause.

------

## Anticonvulsants

Examples:

- Phenytoin, or Dilantin
- Phenobarbital

### Purpose

Used to prevent or treat seizures.

------

## Mannitol

Mannitol is an osmotic diuretic.

### Purpose

Pulls fluid from the brain into the bloodstream so the kidneys can remove it.

### Monitor

- Urine output
- Lung sounds
- Blood pressure
- Electrolytes
- Kidney function
- Signs of dehydration

------

## Furosemide

Furosemide, or Lasix, may be used as a diuretic.

### Monitor

- Potassium
- Blood pressure
- Urine output
- Hydration status

------

## Hypertonic Saline

Hypertonic saline may be used to reduce ICP.

### Monitor

- Sodium level
- Neuro status
- Fluid status
- Lung sounds
- IV site

------

## Opioids

Fentanyl may be used for ventilated clients.

### Purpose

Helps control pain and decrease agitation.

------

## Barbiturate Coma

A barbiturate coma may be used when other interventions are unsuccessful.

# Hydrocephalus

## What It Is

Hydrocephalus is a buildup of cerebrospinal fluid, or CSF, in the brain.

This causes increased pressure on brain tissue.

## Basic CSF Flow

CSF normally flows through the ventricles and around the brain and spinal cord.

The brain has ventricles, including:

- Two lateral ventricles
- Third ventricle
- Fourth ventricle

CSF is made in the lateral ventricles and flows through connected passages. It eventually bathes the brain and spinal cord and is reabsorbed.

## What Goes Wrong

Hydrocephalus can occur when CSF flow is blocked, CSF is not absorbed properly, or too much CSF builds up.

A common cause is narrowing or blockage of the aqueduct of Sylvius.

------

# Hydrocephalus in Infants

## Recognize

Infants may show:

- Enlarged head circumference
- Bulging fontanel
- Tense fontanel
- High-pitched or shrill cry
- Poor feeding
- Vomiting
- Irritability
- Sleepiness
- Seizures
- Sunsetting eyes
- Developmental delay

------

# Hydrocephalus in Older Children and Adults

## Recognize

Older children and adults may have:

- Headache
- Impaired vision
- Cognitive difficulties
- Loss of coordination
- Incontinence
- Nausea and vomiting
- Confusion
- Decreased LOC
- Seizures

------

# Hydrocephalus Priority Assessment

Assess:

- Level of consciousness
- Head circumference in infants
- Fontanels in infants
- Feeding tolerance
- Vomiting
- Eye movement
- Pupils
- Seizure activity
- Motor function
- Coordination
- Vital signs

## Report Immediately

- Bulging fontanel
- Rapid increase in head size
- High-pitched cry
- Sunsetting eyes
- Vomiting
- Seizure
- Decreased LOC
- Signs of shunt infection or malfunction

------

# Hydrocephalus Treatment

## Goal

Remove excess fluid from the brain and decrease pressure.

Treatment often includes a surgically placed shunt.

------

# VP Shunt

## What It Is

A ventriculoperitoneal shunt drains CSF from a ventricle in the brain into the peritoneal cavity.

### Pathway

Brain ventricle → tubing under skin → peritoneal cavity

------

# VA Shunt

## What It Is

A ventriculoatrial shunt drains CSF from the brain into the vascular system.

### Pathway

Brain ventricle → tubing under skin → vascular system/heart area

------

# Nursing Care After Shunt Placement

## Priority Care

- Monitor vital signs.
- Monitor neurologic status.
- Assess incision sites.
- Monitor for infection.
- Monitor for pain.
- Administer antibiotics as ordered.
- Administer pain medication as ordered.
- Measure head circumference in infants.
- Assess fontanels in infants.
- Teach family dressing care as ordered.
- Teach family signs of shunt problems.

------

# Shunt Positioning Safety

## Do Not

- Do not place pressure on the shunt side.
- Do not position the infant directly on the side of the shunt unless ordered.

### Why

Pressure can cause:

- Skin breakdown
- Compression of the shunt apparatus
- Shunt problems

------

# Signs of Shunt Malfunction

Recognize:

- High-pitched cry
- Irritability
- Vomiting
- Poor feeding
- Sleepiness
- Bulging fontanel
- Increased head circumference
- Headache
- Seizures
- Decreased LOC
- Return of hydrocephalus symptoms

------

# Signs of Shunt Infection

Recognize:

- Fever
- Redness at incision
- Swelling
- Drainage
- Irritability
- Neck stiffness
- Abdominal pain
- Poor feeding

# TIA — Transient Ischemic Attack

## What It Is

A TIA is a **sudden, temporary episode of neurological dysfunction** caused by decreased blood flow to the brain.

Symptoms usually resolve, often in less than an hour.

## Simple Explanation

A TIA is like a temporary warning stroke.

The symptoms go away, but it is still serious because it can be a warning sign of a future stroke.

## Main Problem

Temporary decreased cerebral blood flow.

The brain needs constant blood flow because it cannot store oxygen or glucose.

---

## Signs and Symptoms of TIA

Symptoms are sudden and may include:

* Visual changes
* Sudden weakness
* Sudden numbness
* Trouble speaking
* Trouble understanding speech
* Dizziness
* Loss of balance
* Sudden sensory changes
* Sudden motor changes
* Facial drooping

## NCLEX Tip

Even if symptoms go away, a TIA is not “nothing.”

It should be treated as a warning sign that the client is at risk for stroke.

---

## Nursing Care for TIA

* Assess neurological status.
* Ask when symptoms started.
* Ask when symptoms resolved.
* Notify provider.
* Monitor vital signs.
* Assess blood glucose.
* Monitor for return or worsening of symptoms.
* Teach stroke warning signs.
* Teach risk factor control.

---

## TIA Patient Teaching

Teach the client to control stroke risk factors:

* Take medications as prescribed.
* Control blood pressure.
* Control blood glucose if diabetic.
* Stop smoking.
* Follow low-fat/heart-healthy diet if prescribed.
* Exercise as allowed.
* Keep follow-up appointments.
* Seek emergency care if symptoms return.

---

# Stroke — CVA / Brain Attack

## What It Is

A stroke is a change in the normal blood supply to the brain.

It is also called a **brain attack**.

## Main Problem

The brain tissue does not receive enough oxygen-rich blood, or there is bleeding in or around the brain.

The brain cannot store oxygen or glucose, so it depends on constant blood flow to function.

---

## Types of Stroke

| Type               | Cause                                               |
| ------------------ | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Ischemic stroke    | Blockage or interruption of blood flow to the brain |
| Hemorrhagic stroke | Bleeding within or around the brain                 |

---

# Ischemic Stroke

## What It Is

An ischemic stroke occurs when blood flow to part of the brain is blocked.

This may happen from partial or complete occlusion of an artery.

Most strokes are ischemic.

## Types of Ischemic Stroke

| Type              | Cause                                               |
| ----------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Thrombotic stroke | Clot forms in an artery, often from atherosclerosis |
| Embolic stroke    | Clot travels from somewhere else to the brain       |

## Embolic Stroke Sources

An embolic stroke can be associated with:

* Atrial fibrillation
* MI
* Blood stasis in the heart
* Cardiac dysrhythmias

---

# Hemorrhagic Stroke

## What It Is

A hemorrhagic stroke occurs when there is bleeding in or around the brain.

## Causes/Risk Factors

* Severe hypertension
* Aneurysm
* Cranial hemorrhage
* Anticoagulant use
* Head trauma
* Vascular malformation

## Why It Is Dangerous

Blood collects in the skull and increases pressure on brain tissue.

This can decrease perfusion and cause brain damage.

---

# Stroke Risk Factors

* Hypertension
* Obesity
* Smoking
* Cocaine use
* Hyperlipidemia
* Diabetes mellitus
* Peripheral vascular disease
* Atrial fibrillation
* Previous TIA or stroke
* Aneurysm
* Cranial hemorrhage
* Carotid artery disease
* Older age

## Biggest Risk Factor to Remember

Hypertension is a major stroke risk factor.

It is especially important with hemorrhagic stroke.

---

# Stroke Manifestations

## Hallmark Finding

The hallmark of stroke is **sudden onset of focal neurological symptoms**.

This means the symptoms start suddenly and affect a specific function or part of the body.

---

## FAST

Use FAST to recognize stroke:

| Letter | Meaning                     |
| ------ | --------------------------- |
| F      | Face drooping               |
| A      | Arm weakness                |
| S      | Speech difficulty           |
| T      | Time to call emergency help |

Other possible symptoms:

* Sudden confusion
* Sudden numbness or weakness
* Vision changes
* Severe headache
* Trouble walking
* Dizziness
* Loss of balance
* Trouble speaking
* Trouble understanding speech

---

## Opposite Side Rule

Physical symptoms usually occur on the **opposite side** of the brain damage.

| Brain Damage       | Expected Finding     |
| ------------------ | -------------------- |
| Left brain damage  | Right-sided weakness |
| Right brain damage | Left-sided weakness  |

---

# Left-Sided vs Right-Sided Stroke

| Left Brain Stroke               | Right Brain Stroke          |
| ------------------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Right-sided weakness            | Left-sided weakness         |
| Aphasia/language problems       | Spatial-perceptual problems |
| Slow, cautious behavior         | Impulsivity                 |
| Depression/frustration          | Poor judgment               |
| Difficulty with speech/language | Left-sided neglect          |

---

# Speech and Communication Problems

## Aphasia

Aphasia is difficulty with language.

The client may have trouble:

* Speaking
* Understanding speech
* Reading
* Writing

## Nursing Care for Aphasia

* Use simple words.
* Ask yes/no questions.
* Give the client time to respond.
* Do not rush.
* Use communication boards if needed.
* Reduce distractions.

---

## Dysarthria

Dysarthria is difficulty speaking clearly due to muscle weakness.

## Nursing Care for Dysarthria

* Encourage slow speech.
* Listen patiently.
* Do not pretend to understand.
* Ask the client to repeat if needed.
* Use alternative communication methods.

---

# Priority Action for Suspected Stroke

## Most Important Question

**When was the client last known well?**

This is not always the same as when symptoms were noticed.

Example:

If the client woke up with symptoms, the last known well time may be when they went to bed.

## Why It Matters

Thrombolytic therapy has a time limit.

Your notes emphasize:

**Time is brain destruction.**

Treatment should be initiated within 3 hours of symptom onset for this review. Some facilities may use up to 4.5 hours for select clients, but remember the 3-hour window for this exam.

---

# Emergency Stroke Care

## Immediate Priorities

1. Assess airway.
2. Assess breathing.
3. Assess circulation.
4. Determine last known well time.
5. Perform neuro assessment.
6. Check blood glucose.
7. Keep NPO until swallow screen.
8. Prepare for CT scan.
9. Establish IV access.
10. Monitor vital signs.

## Airway Comes First

Airway is a primary concern because stroke can affect:

* Level of consciousness
* Swallowing
* Gag reflex
* Cough
* Breathing pattern

---

# CT Scan Before Thrombolytics

A CT scan is needed to determine whether the stroke is ischemic or hemorrhagic.

Thrombolytics may be used for ischemic stroke.

Thrombolytics are **not** used for hemorrhagic stroke.

## NCLEX Tip

Do not give thrombolytics until bleeding has been ruled out.

---

# Thrombolytic Therapy for Ischemic Stroke

## What It Does

Thrombolytic therapy breaks down an existing clot.

It helps:

* Re-establish blood flow
* Prevent cerebral infarction
* Limit secondary brain damage

## Example

* rtPA/alteplase

## Route

Given IV.

## Timing

Remember treatment within **3 hours** of symptom onset.

Some studies support up to 4.5 hours in select clients, but most facilities want it started within 3 hours.

---

## Before Giving Thrombolytics

Assess:

* Last known well time
* Eligibility criteria
* Consent
* Weight, because dose is weight-based
* Vital signs
* Signs of hemorrhage
* Signs of intracranial bleeding
* Baseline PT/INR
* Baseline aPTT
* Blood glucose
* Recent surgery or trauma history
* Current anticoagulant use

---

## Monitor for Bleeding

After thrombolytics, monitor for:

* Severe headache
* Sudden worsening neuro status
* Vomiting
* Decreased level of consciousness
* Bleeding gums
* Blood in urine
* Blood in stool
* Bruising
* Hypotension
* Signs of intracranial bleeding

---

# If the Client Is Not Eligible for Thrombolytics

Care focuses on close monitoring and preventing complications.

Nursing care includes:

* Frequent neuro assessments
* Respiratory assessments
* Cardiovascular assessments
* Vital signs
* Blood pressure control
* Monitoring for increased ICP
* Seizure precautions if needed
* Preparing for surgery if large clot or hemorrhage is present

---

# Early Stroke Care

Early care priorities include:

* Aspiration precautions due to dysphagia
* Monitor vital signs
* Obtain baseline PT/INR and aPTT
* Start two IV lines with normal saline
* Monitor for increased ICP
* Hyperoxygenate before suctioning
* Keep HOB at or greater than 30°
* Decrease environmental stimulation
* Avoid clustering procedures because too much activity can increase ICP

---

# Swallowing Safety

## Why It Matters

Stroke can cause dysphagia.

Dysphagia increases the risk for aspiration.

## Nursing Care

* Keep the client NPO until swallow screen is completed.
* Do not give PO meds until swallowing is cleared.
* Watch for coughing, choking, drooling, or wet voice.
* Keep suction available if needed.
* Sit the client upright when eating is allowed.
* Use thickened liquids if ordered.

## Signs of Aspiration Risk

* Coughing with food or fluids
* Wet/gurgly voice
* Drooling
* Pocketing food
* Choking
* Difficulty swallowing
* Recurrent pneumonia

---

# Increased ICP Risk After Stroke

Stroke can lead to swelling and increased intracranial pressure.

## Watch For

* Decreased level of consciousness
* Worsening headache
* Vomiting
* Pupil changes
* Worsening weakness
* Seizures
* Abnormal posturing
* Changes in vital signs

## Nursing Care to Reduce ICP Risk

* Keep HOB at least 30° unless contraindicated.
* Keep head midline.
* Avoid excessive stimulation.
* Avoid clustering care.
* Hyperoxygenate before suctioning.
* Avoid prolonged suctioning.
* Monitor neuro status frequently.
* Report worsening changes quickly.

---

# Later Stroke Care

Once the client is more stable, care focuses on function, safety, and preventing complications.

Interventions include:

* Thickened liquids if ordered.
* Range-of-motion exercises.
* Monitor for DVT.
* Promote independence.
* Promote effective communication.
* Promote continence.
* Place objects within the client’s field of vision.
* Teach the client to touch and use both sides of the body.

---

# Safety Concerns After Stroke

## Fall Risk

Stroke may cause:

* Weakness
* Impulsivity
* Poor balance
* Neglect
* Vision changes

Nursing care:

* Keep bed low.
* Use call light.
* Use assistive devices.
* Assist with ambulation.
* Provide non-skid footwear.

## Skin Breakdown

Weakness and immobility increase skin risk.

Nursing care:

* Reposition.
* Keep skin clean and dry.
* Use pressure-relieving surfaces.
* Encourage mobility as allowed.

## DVT Risk

Immobility increases clot risk.

Nursing care:

* ROM exercises.
* Sequential compression devices if ordered.
* Anticoagulants if prescribed.
* Early mobility when safe.

---

# Patient Teaching After Stroke/TIA

Teach the client and family:

* Call emergency services for stroke symptoms.
* Do not wait to see if symptoms improve.
* Know the FAST signs.
* Take medications as prescribed.
* Control blood pressure.
* Control blood glucose if diabetic.
* Stop smoking.
* Follow diet recommendations.
* Attend rehab therapy.
* Keep follow-up appointments.
* Use safety precautions at home.

# Why Increased ICP Is Dangerous

Increased pressure can decrease blood flow and oxygen to the brain.

This can lead to:

* Brain tissue damage
* Seizures
* Herniation
* Respiratory changes
* Death

---

# Normal ICP

| ICP Value                                  | Meaning                    |
| ------------------------------------------ | -------------------------- |
| 0–15 mm Hg                                 | Normal ICP                 |
| 20 mm Hg or higher for more than 5 minutes | Life-threatening emergency |

---

# Recognize Increased ICP

## Earliest Sign

The earliest and most important sign is a **change in level of consciousness**.

This may look like:

* Restlessness
* Confusion
* Drowsiness
* Irritability
* Decreased responsiveness

---

## Other Signs and Symptoms

* Headache
* Nausea and vomiting
* Pupillary changes
* Weakness
* Slurred speech
* Seizures
* Papilledema
* Abnormal posturing

## Late Signs

Late signs include:

* Bradycardia
* Hypertension
* Widened pulse pressure
* Bradypnea
* Cheyne-Stokes respirations
* Severe headache
* Nausea and vomiting
* Fixed and dilated pupils

---

# Cushing’s Triad

Cushing’s triad is a **late sign** of increased ICP.

## Findings

| Finding                | What Happens                                        |
| ---------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Systolic hypertension  | Systolic BP increases                               |
| Widened pulse pressure | Difference between systolic and diastolic BP widens |
| Bradycardia            | Pulse decreases                                     |
| Respiratory depression | Respirations decrease or become abnormal            |

---

# Increased ICP vs Shock

| Finding          | Increased ICP                | Shock            |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------- | ---------------- |
| Blood pressure   | Systolic BP increases        | BP decreases     |
| Pulse            | Decreases                    | Increases        |
| Respirations     | Decrease or become irregular | Increase         |
| Priority concern | Brain pressure/perfusion     | Tissue perfusion |

---

# Level of Consciousness Terms

| Term               | Meaning                                            |
| ------------------ | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Full consciousness | Awake, alert, oriented, interacts with environment |
| Confused           | Lacks ability to think clearly and rapidly         |
| Disoriented        | Does not recognize place or person                 |
| Lethargic          | Awakens easily but has limited responsiveness      |
| Obtunded           | Sleeps and, once aroused, has limited interaction  |
| Stupor             | Requires stimulation to arouse                     |
| Coma               | Vigorous stimulation produces no response          |

# Priority Assessment for Increased ICP

Assess:

* Level of consciousness
* Pupils
* Motor strength
* Speech
* Vital signs
* Respiratory pattern
* Headache
* Vomiting
* Seizure activity
* Intake and output
* Drainage from ears or nose
* Bilateral neuro assessment

## Report Immediately

* Change in LOC
* New confusion
* Unequal pupils
* Fixed or dilated pupils
* New weakness
* Seizure
* Cushing’s triad
* Projectile vomiting
* Worsening headache
* Abnormal posturing
* Fever
* Drainage from ears or nose

---

# Nursing Interventions for Increased ICP

## Positioning

* Place client in Semi-Fowler’s position.
* Keep head in neutral midline position.
* Avoid neck flexion.
* Avoid neck rotation.
* Avoid extreme hip flexion.

### Why

This helps promote venous drainage from the brain.

---

## Safety and Neuro Protection

* Monitor LOC closely.
* Compare assessments bilaterally.
* Monitor vital signs carefully.
* Report changes immediately.
* Treat as spinal injury until ruled out.
* Monitor for halo sign if fluid leaks from ears or nose.
* Keep environment calm.
* Limit unnecessary stimulation.
* Allow rest periods.

---

## Respiratory Care

* Monitor respiratory status.
* Monitor ABGs.
* Maintain oxygenation.
* Keep PaO₂ above 80 mm Hg as ordered to help prevent secondary brain injury.
* Prepare for advanced oxygen therapy if needed.

---

## Fever

Fever should be reported immediately.

Fever may be caused by:

* Infection
* Injury to the hypothalamus

---

# What To Avoid With Increased ICP

| Avoid                 | Why                        |
| --------------------- | -------------------------- |
| Coughing              | Increases pressure         |
| Straining             | Increases pressure         |
| Valsalva maneuver     | Increases pressure         |
| Excessive suctioning  | Can increase ICP           |
| Overstimulation       | Can worsen ICP             |
| Hip flexion           | Reduces venous drainage    |
| Neck flexion/rotation | Reduces venous drainage    |
| Tight collars or ties | Can restrict venous return |

# Medications and Treatments for Increased ICP

## Steroids

### Purpose

May decrease inflammation and swelling depending on cause.

---

## Anticonvulsants

Examples:

* Phenytoin, or Dilantin
* Phenobarbital

### Purpose

Used to prevent or treat seizures.

---

## Mannitol

Mannitol is an osmotic diuretic.

### Purpose

Pulls fluid from the brain into the bloodstream so the kidneys can remove it.

### Monitor

* Urine output
* Lung sounds
* Blood pressure
* Electrolytes
* Kidney function
* Signs of dehydration

---

## Furosemide

Furosemide, or Lasix, may be used as a diuretic.

### Monitor

* Potassium
* Blood pressure
* Urine output
* Hydration status

---

## Hypertonic Saline

Hypertonic saline may be used to reduce ICP.

### Monitor

* Sodium level
* Neuro status
* Fluid status
* Lung sounds
* IV site

---

## Opioids

Fentanyl may be used for ventilated clients.

### Purpose

Helps control pain and decrease agitation.

---

## Barbiturate Coma

A barbiturate coma may be used when other interventions are unsuccessful.

# Hydrocephalus

## What It Is

Hydrocephalus is a buildup of cerebrospinal fluid, or CSF, in the brain.

This causes increased pressure on brain tissue.

## Basic CSF Flow

CSF normally flows through the ventricles and around the brain and spinal cord.

The brain has ventricles, including:

* Two lateral ventricles
* Third ventricle
* Fourth ventricle

CSF is made in the lateral ventricles and flows through connected passages. It eventually bathes the brain and spinal cord and is reabsorbed.

## What Goes Wrong

Hydrocephalus can occur when CSF flow is blocked, CSF is not absorbed properly, or too much CSF builds up.

A common cause is narrowing or blockage of the aqueduct of Sylvius.

---

# Hydrocephalus in Infants

## Recognize

Infants may show:

* Enlarged head circumference
* Bulging fontanel
* Tense fontanel
* High-pitched or shrill cry
* Poor feeding
* Vomiting
* Irritability
* Sleepiness
* Seizures
* Sunsetting eyes
* Developmental delay

---

# Hydrocephalus in Older Children and Adults

## Recognize

Older children and adults may have:

* Headache
* Impaired vision
* Cognitive difficulties
* Loss of coordination
* Incontinence
* Nausea and vomiting
* Confusion
* Decreased LOC
* Seizures

---

# Hydrocephalus Priority Assessment

Assess:

* Level of consciousness
* Head circumference in infants
* Fontanels in infants
* Feeding tolerance
* Vomiting
* Eye movement
* Pupils
* Seizure activity
* Motor function
* Coordination
* Vital signs

## Report Immediately

* Bulging fontanel
* Rapid increase in head size
* High-pitched cry
* Sunsetting eyes
* Vomiting
* Seizure
* Decreased LOC
* Signs of shunt infection or malfunction

---

# Hydrocephalus Treatment

## Goal

Remove excess fluid from the brain and decrease pressure.

Treatment often includes a surgically placed shunt.

---

# VP Shunt

## What It Is

A ventriculoperitoneal shunt drains CSF from a ventricle in the brain into the peritoneal cavity.

### Pathway

Brain ventricle → tubing under skin → peritoneal cavity

---

# VA Shunt

## What It Is

A ventriculoatrial shunt drains CSF from the brain into the vascular system.

### Pathway

Brain ventricle → tubing under skin → vascular system/heart area

---

# Nursing Care After Shunt Placement

## Priority Care

* Monitor vital signs.
* Monitor neurologic status.
* Assess incision sites.
* Monitor for infection.
* Monitor for pain.
* Administer antibiotics as ordered.
* Administer pain medication as ordered.
* Measure head circumference in infants.
* Assess fontanels in infants.
* Teach family dressing care as ordered.
* Teach family signs of shunt problems.

---

# Shunt Positioning Safety

## Do Not

* Do not place pressure on the shunt side.
* Do not position the infant directly on the side of the shunt unless ordered.

### Why

Pressure can cause:

* Skin breakdown
* Compression of the shunt apparatus
* Shunt problems

---

# Signs of Shunt Malfunction

Recognize:

* High-pitched cry
* Irritability
* Vomiting
* Poor feeding
* Sleepiness
* Bulging fontanel
* Increased head circumference
* Headache
* Seizures
* Decreased LOC
* Return of hydrocephalus symptoms

---

# Signs of Shunt Infection

Recognize:

* Fever
* Redness at incision
* Swelling
* Drainage
* Irritability
* Neck stiffness
* Abdominal pain
* Poor feeding
