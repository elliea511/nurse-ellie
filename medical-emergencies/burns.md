# Burns

<div class="note">
Burns are injuries that cause a loss of tissue integrity. Severity depends on the depth of the burn, total body surface area burned, location, cause, age of the client, and complications.
</div>


## Big Picture

Burns are dangerous because the skin normally protects the body from:

- Fluid loss
- Infection
- Temperature changes
- Injury
- Electrolyte imbalance

When skin and tissue integrity are lost, the client is at risk for:

- Airway problems
  - from the burn itself or smoke inhalation
- Fluid and electrolyte shifts
- Shock
- Infection
- Pain
- Impaired wound healing
- Decreased ability to perform ADLs
  - skin tightens from tissue damage/growth. 
- Psychosocial concerns related to appearance, pain, trauma, and long recovery

<div class="priority">
<strong>Priority:</strong> With burns, always think <strong>airway first</strong>, then circulation/fluid replacement, then infection prevention and wound healing.
</div>


---

# Types of Burns

## Thermal Burns

Thermal burns are caused by contact with heat.

Examples:

- Flames
- Hot liquids
- Steam
- Hot substances
- Explosions

### Dry Heat Injuries

Dry heat injuries are caused by open flames or explosions.

These may cause **flash burns**.

### Moist Heat Injuries

Moist heat injuries are caused by hot liquids or steam.

These are also called **scalds** and are often seen in age extremes, such as very young children and older adults.

---

## Contact Burns

Contact burns occur from touching hot substances.

Examples:

- Hot metal
- Tar
- Grease

<div class="warning">
<strong>Important:</strong> Contact burns can lead to full-thickness injury because the hot substance may stay on the skin longer.
</div>


---

## Chemical Burns

Chemical burns are caused by strong acids, alkalis, or other chemicals.

Examples:

- Cleaning products
- Industrial chemicals
- Battery acid

### Nursing Care

- Remove contaminated clothing.
- Brush off dry chemicals first.
- Flush the area with large amounts of water.
- Wear gloves and PPE.
- Remove jewelry because swelling can occur quickly.

<div class="warning">
<strong>Do not trap the chemical against the skin.</strong> Remove contaminated clothing and flush as ordered.
</div>


---

## Electrical Burns

Electrical burns are caused by electrical current passing through the body.

These can look small on the outside but cause major internal damage.

### Major Concerns

- Dysrhythmias
- Deep tissue injury
- Muscle damage
- Kidney injury from myoglobin
- Entry and exit wounds

<div class="priority">
<strong>Priority:</strong> Electrical burns require cardiac monitoring because they can cause dysrhythmias.
</div>


---

## Radiation Burns

Radiation burns are caused by radiation exposure.

Examples:

- Prolonged sun exposure
- Radiation treatments
- X-rays

Radiation burns may develop gradually and can cause redness, peeling, irritation, and skin breakdown.

---

## Smoke-Related Burns

Smoke-related burns are caused by inhalation of smoke and hot gases.

<div class="priority">
<strong>Think:</strong> AIRWAY, AIRWAY, AIRWAY.
</div>


Smoke inhalation can cause swelling, airway obstruction, and carbon monoxide poisoning.

---

# Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

Carbon monoxide is a colorless, odorless, tasteless gas.

It is a major cause of death in fire-related injuries because it binds to hemoglobin instead of oxygen. This prevents oxygen from being delivered to tissues.

## What Happens

Carbon monoxide:

- Binds to hemoglobin in place of oxygen
- Decreases oxygen delivery to tissues
- Can cause vasodilation
- May cause a cherry-red appearance, although this is not always seen

## Symptoms Can Include

- Headache
- Dizziness
- Confusion
- Weakness
- Nausea
- Shortness of breath
- Altered mental status

<div class="priority">
<strong>Priority:</strong> If carbon monoxide poisoning is suspected or confirmed, administer high-flow oxygen for at least 6 hours.
</div>

# Burn Depth

## Superficial Burn

Also called a **1st degree burn**.

Affects only the epidermis.

### Assessment Findings

- Red
- Dry
- Painful
- No blisters
- Skin remains intact

Example: basic sunburn. 

**if you have skin you can get a sunburn**

### Healing

Usually heals quickly without scarring.

<div class="note">
<strong>Class Note:</strong> Even superficial burns can be a concern for babies and older adults because they are still at risk for dehydration.
</div>


---

## Superficial Partial-Thickness Burn

Also called a **2nd degree burn**.

Affects the epidermis and upper dermis.

### Assessment Findings

- Red or pink
- Moist or wet-looking
- Blisters
- Very painful
- Blanches with pressure
- Nerve endings are exposed, which causes pain

<div class="ati-box">
<strong>Recognition Tip:</strong> If the burn looks wet, moist, blistered, and very painful, think partial-thickness.		
</div>

### Healing

Uncomplicated healing occurs in 10-21 days


---



## Deep Partial-Thickness Burn

Also called a **2nd degree burn**. 

Affects deeper layers of the dermis. 

### Assessment Findings

- Red, white, or waxy
- Less moist than superficial partial-thickness
- Blisters may be present but can be less obvious
- Less blanching
- Pain may be decreased compared to superficial partial-thickness burns

### Pain level

Pain level depends on if the nerves are exposed or damaged. Exposed = **very painful**. 

### Healing

Can take weeks (2-6 weeks) and **may** require grafting.

---

## Full-Thickness Burn

Also called a **3rd degree burn**.

Destroys the epidermis and dermis and may extend into subcutaneous tissue.

### Assessment Findings

- White, brown, black, yellow, or charred
- Dry
- Tight
- Leathery
- No blisters
- No bleeding
- Little or no pain in the center of the burn
- Eschar may be present

<div class="warning">
<strong>Important:</strong> Full-thickness burns may not hurt at the center because nerve endings are destroyed. The surrounding areas can still be very painful. 
</div>


### Healing

Full-thickness burns do not heal normally on their own and usually require grafting. Patient usually requires lots of rehab to regain functionality of effected area. Pain increases as nerve endings repair themselves. 

---

## Fourth Degree Burn

Fourth degree burns extend beyond the skin into deeper structures.

### Tissue Involved

May include:

- Muscle
- Tendon
- Bone

### Assessment Findings

- Blackened or charred tissue
- Deep tissue destruction
- Visible muscle or bone may be present
- No sensation in the destroyed tissue

### Treatment Concern

Skin grafts usually do not work because there may be nothing healthy to graft onto.

<div class="priority">
<strong>Important:</strong> Fourth degree burns often require specialized care and may require amputation depending on the location and severity.
</div>


---

# Burn Depth Quick Chart

| Burn Depth                                 | Skin Involved                 | Appearance                       | Pain                           | Healing                                    |
| ------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------- | -------------------------------- | ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------ |
| Superficial / 1st degree                   | Epidermis                     | Red, dry, no blisters            | Painful                        | Heals quickly                              |
| Superficial partial-thickness / 2nd degree | Epidermis + upper dermis      | Red/pink, moist, blisters        | Very painful                   | Re-epithelialization                       |
| Deep partial-thickness / 2nd degree        | Deeper dermis                 | Less moist, waxy, less blanching | Some pain, may be decreased    | May need grafting                          |
| Full-thickness / 3rd degree                | Entire dermis destroyed       | Dry, leathery, white/brown/black | Little/no pain in center       | Requires grafting                          |
| Fourth degree                              | Extends to muscle/tendon/bone | Black, charred, deep destruction | No sensation in destroyed area | Grafting may not work; possible amputation |

# Estimating Burn Size

Burn size is estimated using **total body surface area**, or **TBSA**.

TBSA helps determine:

- Severity of injury
- Fluid replacement needs
- Risk for complications
- Need for burn center transfer

---

# Rule of Nines: Adults Only

The Rule of Nines divides the adult body into areas worth 9% or multiples of 9%. 

<div class="warning">
<strong>Important:</strong> The Rule of Nines is for adults. Children have different body proportions, so a pediatric burn chart is more accurate.
</div>

## Adult Rule of Nines

| Body Area       | Percent |
| --------------- | ------: |
| Head and neck   |      9% |
| Each arm        |      9% |
| Anterior trunk  |     18% |
| Posterior trunk |     18% |
| Each leg        |     18% |
| Perineum        |      1% |

<div class="ati-box">
<strong>Memory Tip:</strong> There are 11 areas of 9%. 11 × 9 = 99%, and the perineum is 1%.
</div>


---

![rule of nines, burns, nursing, calculation. practice problems, quiz](https://www.registerednursern.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/rule-of-nines-burns.png)

## Rule of Nines Practice Example

A 28-year-old female has full-thickness burns to the:

- Anterior and posterior head and neck
- Front of the left leg
- Perineum

Calculate the TBSA burned.

| Area Burned                          | Percent |
| ------------------------------------ | ------: |
| Anterior and posterior head and neck |      9% |
| Front of left leg                    |      9% |
| Perineum                             |      1% |
| Total                                |     19% |

<div class="ati-box">
<strong>Answer:</strong> 19%
</div>


---

# Pediatric Burns

Children have different body proportions than adults.

Children have:

- Larger head percentage
- Smaller leg percentage

<div class="note">
For children, use a pediatric burn chart such as the Lund and Browder chart because the adult Rule of Nines is less accurate.
</div>

# Burn Severity

Burn severity depends on:

- Depth of burn
- Percent of body burned
- Location of burn
- Age of the client
- Cause of burn
- Other injuries or health conditions

## High-Risk Burn Locations

Burns are more serious when they involve the:

- Face
- Neck
- Hands
- Feet
- Genitals
- Perineum
- Major joints
- Circumferential areas

<div class="priority">
<strong>Priority:</strong> Face and neck burns are concerning because of possible airway edema.
</div>


---

# Circumferential Burns

A circumferential burn goes all the way around an extremity, chest, or body part.

## Why This Is Dangerous

As swelling increases, burned skin may not stretch. This can decrease circulation or restrict breathing.

## Assessment Findings

- Increasing pain
- Numbness or tingling
- Cool extremity
- Weak or absent pulses
- Delayed capillary refill
- Tight, firm skin
- Difficulty breathing if around the chest

<div class="warning">
  <strong>Report immediately:</strong> Weak pulses, numbness, worsening pain, or difficulty breathing may indicate impaired circulation or restricted chest expansion. (similar to Compartment Syndrome)
</div>


---

# Burn Phases

## Emergent Phase

The emergent phase begins at the time of injury and continues through the beginning of treatment.

Your notes describe this as the beginning phase, usually the first **24 to 48 hours**, where the injury is evaluated and the plan of care is decided.

### Main Priorities

- Airway
- Breathing
- Circulation
- Stop the burning process
- Prevent shock
- Begin fluid replacement
- Pain control

<div class="priority">
<strong>First Priority:</strong> Airway always comes first, especially with facial burns, smoke inhalation, or burns in an enclosed space.
</div>


---

## Acute Phase

The acute phase begins after the initial emergent phase and continues while wounds are treated and fluid shifts begin to resolve.

Usually begins around **36 to 48 hours**, when fluid shift should begin resolving.

### Main Priorities

- Wound care
- Infection prevention
- Pain management
- Nutrition
- Mobility
- Skin grafting if needed

---

## Rehabilitation Phase

The rehabilitation phase starts with wound closure and continues until the client reaches the highest possible level of function.

### Main Priorities

- Mobility

- Scar management

- Body image

- Coping

- Long-term function

- Physical therapy

- Occupational therapy

- ADL support

  - Encourage Independence as much as possible!

  <div class="warning">
  <strong>Important:</strong> TPN may be required if patient develops paralytic ileus.
  </div>

---

# Initial Nursing Priorities

## Stop the Burning Process

- Remove the client from the source.
- Remove burned clothing if it is not stuck to the skin.
- Remove jewelry because swelling can occur quickly.
- Cool small burns with cool water.

<div class="warning">
<strong>Do not use ice.</strong> Ice can cause vasoconstriction, worsen tissue damage, and increase risk for hypothermia.
</div>


---

## Airway Assessment

Airway injury is a major concern with burns.

### Red Flags for Inhalation Injury

- Burns to face or neck
- Singed nasal hairs
- Soot around mouth or nose
- Hoarse voice
- Difficulty swallowing
- Coughing
- Carbonaceous sputum
- Burns in an enclosed space
- Stridor
- Any weird or worsening respiratory symptoms

<div class="priority">
<strong>NCLEX Priority:</strong> Hoarseness, stridor, facial burns, or soot after a fire can mean airway swelling. Prepare for early intubation before the airway closes.
</div>


---

## Breathing

Assess:

- Respiratory rate
- Oxygen saturation
- Lung sounds
- Work of breathing
- Chest expansion
- Presence of soot or sputum

### Nursing Care

- Apply oxygen as ordered.
- Continuously monitor oxygenation.
- Prepare for intubation if airway swelling worsens.

<div class="warning">
<strong>Important:</strong> If the airway becomes too swollen, intubation may not be possible and the client may need a tracheostomy.
</div>


---

## Circulation

Burns can cause massive fluid shifts from the bloodstream into the tissues.

This can lead to:

- Hypovolemia
- Decreased cardiac output
- Shock
- Decreased kidney perfusion
- Electrolyte imbalance

### Findings of Burn Shock

- Tachycardia
- Hypotension
- Cool clammy skin
- Decreased urine output
- Weak pulses
- Altered mental status

---

# Fluid Resuscitation

Large burns require IV fluid replacement because fluid shifts out of the vascular space.

## Common Fluid Used

Lactated Ringer’s solution is commonly used for burn resuscitation.

## Goal of Fluids

The goal is to maintain:

- Circulation
- Blood pressure
- Tissue perfusion
- Kidney perfusion
- Urine output

<div class="ati-box">
<strong>ATI/NCLEX Tip:</strong> Urine output is one of the best indicators that fluid resuscitation is effective.
</div>


---

# Shock Phase

The shock phase usually occurs during the first **24 to 48 hours**.

## What Happens

Fluid shifts from the plasma/intravascular space into the interstitial space.

Think: **fluid leaves the bloodstream and moves into the tissues.**

## Expected Findings

- Decreased blood pressure
- Decreased urine output
- Increased hematocrit
- Increased potassium
- Acidosis
- Risk for shock

## Nursing Priorities

- Check vital signs.
- Monitor blood pressure.
- Monitor urine output.
- Watch for shock.
- Give IV fluids as ordered. 
- Monitor electrolytes and acid-base balance.
- Patient will need to be on telemetry so you will still need to stick pads to patient regardless of condiion. 
- Possible nuetropenic precautions. 

<div class="priority">
<strong>Priority:</strong> During the shock phase, monitor urine output closely. Urine output usually decreases because the body is trying to retain fluid.
</div>


---

# Diuretic Phase

The diuretic phase usually occurs around **48 to 72 hours**.

## What Happens

Fluid shifts from the interstitial space back into the plasma/intravascular space.

Think: **fluid comes back into the bloodstream.**

## Expected Findings

- Increased urine output
- Decreased hematocrit
- Decreased potassium
- Increased central venous pressure
- Risk for fluid volume overload
- Risk for pulmonary edema

## Nursing Priorities

- Check vital signs.
- Monitor urine output.
  - should increase
- Monitor lung sounds.
- Watch for pulmonary edema.
- Monitor electrolytes.
- Assess for fluid overload.

<div class="warning">
<strong>Important:</strong> During the diuretic phase, watch for pulmonary edema, especially in clients with heart failure. Furosemide may be used if prescribed.
</div>


---

# Shock Phase vs Diuretic Phase

| Phase          | Time Frame | Fluid Shift            | Labs/Findings                                                | Major Risk        |
| -------------- | ---------- | ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | ----------------- |
| Shock phase    | 24–48 hr   | Plasma to interstitial | Increased Hct, increased K+, acidosis, decreased BP, decreased UOP | Hypovolemic shock |
| Diuretic phase | 48–72 hr   | Interstitial to plasma | Decreased Hct, decreased K+, increased CVP, increased UOP    | Pulmonary edema   |

---

# Expected Urine Output

| Client          |              Minimum Urine Output |
| --------------- | --------------------------------: |
| Adult           |                          30 mL/hr |
| Child           |                        1 mL/kg/hr |
| Electrical burn | Higher urine output may be needed |

# Burn Interventions

Burn care focuses on:

- Airway maintenance
- Pain control
- Infection control
- Wound healing

------

## Airway Maintenance

The airway can swell quickly after smoke inhalation or facial burns.

### Nursing Care

- Assess respiratory status frequently.
- Monitor for hoarseness, stridor, soot, and respiratory distress.
- Apply oxygen as ordered.
- Prepare for intubation if airway swelling is suspected.

------

## Pain Control

Burn pain can be severe.

### Nursing Care

- Assess pain frequently.
- Give pain medication before wound care.
- Use IV pain medication for severe burns.
- Reposition carefully.
- Use nonpharmacologic comfort measures.

------

## Infection Control

Burn clients are at high risk for infection because the skin barrier is damaged.

### Nursing Care

- Use strict hand hygiene.
- Use sterile technique for wound care.
- Monitor temperature.
- Assess drainage and odor.
- Limit exposure of wounds.
- Change dressings as prescribed.
- Monitor WBC trends.

------

## Wound Healing

Burn wound healing may include:

- Cleaning
- Debridement
- Topical antimicrobials
- Dressings
- Skin grafting
- Nutrition support

------

# Burn Medications

## Gentamicin

Gentamicin is an antibiotic that may be used for infection control.

### Major Concern

Gentamicin is **nephrotoxic**.

### Monitor

- BUN
- Serum creatinine
- Urine output

------

## Sulfamylon

Sulfamylon is a sterile burn ointment.

### Application

- Apply with sterile technique.
- Use a sterile gloved hand.
- May be used with an open method of treatment.
  - No dressing may be used depending on the order.

### Major Concern

As Sulfamylon is metabolized, it can cause a heavy acid load. 

### Monitor For

- Metabolic acidosis (ABG's)
- Acid-base imbalance

------

## Silver Nitrate

Silver nitrate is used with dressings.

### Key Point

Dressings must stay moist for silver nitrate to be effective.

Silver Nitrate turns EVERYTHING it touches black; does not mean the wound is necrotic. 

### Major Concern

Silver nitrate can affect fluids and electrolytes.

It can pull sodium and potassium chloride into the dressing, which may contribute to electrolyte dilution.

### Monitor

- Fluid balance
- Sodium
- Potassium
- Other electrolytes as ordered

------

## Santyl

Santyl is a debridement ointment. Seen a lot with Aquacel silver dressing. 

### Purpose

Used to remove necrotic tissue, eschar, or sloughing tissue.

### When To Stop

Santyl should be stopped when debridement of necrotic tissue (eschar or slough) is complete. Will continue to destroy healthy tissue. 

------

## Silvadene / Silver Sulfadiazine

Silvadene is frequently used often for burn wounds.

### Application

- Apply cream with a sterile gloved hand.
- Cover with petroleum jelly dressing.
- Hold dressing in place with Kerlix wrap, if ordered.

### Side Effects

- Rash
- Leukopenia

### Monitor

- WBC count
- Signs of infection
- Allergy history

------

# Skin Grafting

Skin grafting may be needed for deep partial-thickness or full-thickness burns.

## Nursing Care After Grafting

- Protect the graft site.
  - DO NOT pack. Kills graft. 
- Immobilize the area as prescribed.
- Avoid pressure or shearing.
- Monitor for infection.
- Assess color, drainage, and adherence.
- Elevate the extremity if prescribed.

------

# Types of Grafts

## Autograft

An autograft uses the client’s own skin.

### Key Point

This is usually the preferred graft because it comes from the same person.

Example: skin taken from the client’s healthy skin and placed over the burn.

------

## Syngeneic Graft

A syngeneic graft comes from an identical twin.

### Key Point

This is also called an isograft.

------

## Allograft

An allograft comes from another human who is not genetically identical.

### Key Point

This is also called a homograft.

It can temporarily cover the wound but is not usually permanent.

------

## Xenograft

A xenograft comes from another species.

Examples:

- Pig skin
- Fish skin

### Key Point

This can temporarily maintain skin integrity until another treatment is ready.

------

# Donor Site Care

A donor site is where healthy skin is removed from the client for grafting.

## Important Points

- Donor sites can be very painful.
- Donor sites may be more painful than the graft site.
- Do not cover donor sites with sheets if ordered to leave open to air.
- Avoid friction.
- Monitor for bleeding and infection.

------

# Mesh Grafts

A mesh graft is skin that has been expanded to cover a larger wound area.

### Why It Is Used

- Allows a smaller piece of skin to cover a larger wound
- Helps drainage escape
- Covers larger burn areas

------

# Nutrition and Burns

Burns increase metabolic demand.

The body needs extra calories and protein for wound healing.

## Nutrition Priorities

- High protein
- High calorie
- Vitamin C
- Zinc
- Adequate fluids
- Enteral feeding if needed

------

# Complications of Burns

## Airway Edema

Most concerning with face, neck, or inhalation burns.

### Red Flags

- Hoarseness
- Stridor
- Drooling
- Difficulty swallowing
- Increased work of breathing

------

## Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

Most concerning with smoke inhalation or burns in an enclosed space.

### Red Flags

- Headache
- Confusion
- Weakness
- Dizziness
- Altered mental status
- Possible cherry-red skin

------

## Hypovolemic Shock

Caused by fluid shifting out of the bloodstream.

### Red Flags

- Low blood pressure
- High heart rate
- Decreased urine output
- Weak pulses
- Confusion

------

## Pulmonary Edema

Can occur when fluid shifts back into the vascular space during the diuretic phase.

### Red Flags

- Shortness of breath
- Crackles
- Increased work of breathing
- Increased CVP
- Decreased oxygen saturation

------

## Infection and Sepsis

Caused by loss of skin barrier.

### Red Flags

- Fever or hypothermia
- Increased wound drainage
- Foul odor
- Increased pain
- Confusion
- Hypotension
- Tachycardia

------

## Contractures

Can occur when scar tissue limits movement.

### Prevention

- Range-of-motion exercises
- Splints
- Proper positioning
- Physical therapy
- Occupational therapy

------

# What Not To Do

| Do Not                                                 | Why                                        |
| ------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------ |
| Do not apply ice                                       | Worsens tissue injury and hypothermia risk |
| Do not break blisters                                  | Increases infection risk                   |
| Do not remove clothing stuck to the burn               | Can worsen tissue damage                   |
| Do not apply butter or home remedies                   | Traps heat and increases infection risk    |
| Do not delay airway management                         | Airway edema can progress quickly          |
| Do not underestimate electrical burns                  | Internal damage can be severe              |
| Do not pack graft sites                                | Can damage or kill the graft               |
| Do not keep Santyl going after necrotic tissue is gone | Can affect healthy tissue                  |

------

# Priority Cues to Recognize

## Airway Problem

Think:

- Facial burns
- Soot
- Hoarseness
- Stridor
- Burns in an enclosed space
- Smoke inhalation

Action:

- Assess airway.
- Give oxygen.
- Prepare for early intubation.

------

## Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

Think:

- Fire exposure
- Enclosed-space fire
- Headache
- Confusion
- Possible cherry-red appearance

Action:

- Administer high-flow oxygen as ordered.

------

## Fluid Volume Deficit

Think:

- Tachycardia
- Hypotension
- Low urine output
- Increased hematocrit
- Shock phase

Action:

- IV fluids.
- Monitor urine output.
- Assess for shock.

------

## Fluid Volume Overload

Think:

- Diuretic phase
- Increased CVP
- Increased urine output
- Crackles
- Pulmonary edema

Action:

- Monitor lung sounds.
- Monitor oxygenation.
- Notify provider if pulmonary edema signs occur.

------

## Infection

Think:

- Wound drainage
- Odor
- Fever or hypothermia
- Increased pain
- Sepsis signs

Action:

- Wound care.
- Cultures if prescribed.
- Antibiotics if prescribed.
- Monitor WBC.

------

## Circulation Problem

Think:

- Circumferential burn
- Weak pulse
- Numbness
- Cool limb
- Delayed capillary refill

Action:

- Assess neurovascular status.
- Notify provider.

------
