<h2 class="section-divider" id="big-picture">Big Picture: How Nurses Think About Burns</h2>

Burns are not just skin damage — they are a **systemic injury** that affects fluid balance, circulation, immune defense, airway, metabolism, and psychology. The NCLEX and ATI will test you across the entire recovery timeline, not just the first hour.

<div class="memory">
Think in three phases: <strong>Emergent (survive) → Acute (heal) → Rehabilitative (recover).</strong> Your priorities, fluid management, and wound care goals shift at each phase.
</div>

---

<h2 class="section-divider" id="burn-classification">Burn Classification</h2>

<h3 id="depth">Depth of Burn</h3>

| Depth | Old Name | Layers Involved | Appearance | Pain | Healing |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Superficial** | First degree | Epidermis only | Red, dry, no blisters | Painful | 3–5 days, no scarring |
| **Superficial partial-thickness** | Second degree (superficial) | Epidermis + superficial dermis | Moist, blistered, pink/red | Very painful | 7–21 days, minimal scarring |
| **Deep partial-thickness** | Second degree (deep) | Epidermis + deep dermis | Pale, mottled, wet or waxy | Less painful (nerve damage) | 21–35 days, likely scarring |
| **Full-thickness** | Third degree | All skin layers | Leathery, dry, white/brown/black | Painless (nerves destroyed) | Requires grafting |
| **Full-thickness + subdermal** | Fourth degree | Skin + muscle/bone/tendon | Charred, black | Painless | Amputation may be needed |

<div class="ati-tip">
Superficial burns (sunburn) are <strong>not included</strong> in total body surface area (TBSA) calculations because they do not require fluid resuscitation. Only partial-thickness and full-thickness burns count.
</div>

<h3 id="size">Size: Total Body Surface Area (TBSA)</h3>

TBSA tells you how much of the body is burned — this drives fluid resuscitation, hospital admission decisions, and mortality risk.

<div class="note">
TBSA is estimated using the <strong>Rule of Nines</strong> (adults) or the <strong>Lund-Browder chart</strong> (children, whose head and legs are proportionally different).
</div>

---

<h2 class="section-divider" id="rule-of-nines">Rule of Nines</h2>

| Body Area | % TBSA (Adult) |
|---|---|
| Head and neck | 9% |
| Each arm (entire) | 9% |
| Chest (anterior trunk) | 18% |
| Back (posterior trunk) | 18% |
| Each leg (entire) | 18% |
| Perineum/genitalia | 1% |
| **Total** | **100%** |

<div class="memory">
The rule: <strong>9s everywhere except the trunk (18s) and perineum (1%).</strong> Head = 9, each arm = 9, each leg = 18, front trunk = 18, back trunk = 18, genitals = 1. Adds up to 100.
</div>

<div class="ati-tip">
For irregular or scattered burns, use the <strong>patient's palm</strong> (including fingers) as approximately <strong>1% TBSA</strong> — helpful for estimating patchy areas.
</div>

<h2 class="section-divider" id="inhalation-injury">Inhalation Injury</h2>

Inhalation injury is the leading cause of death in burn patients — more so than the burn itself in many cases. Suspect it with any burn in an enclosed space.

<h3>Signs of Inhalation Injury</h3>

| Finding | Significance |
|---|---|
| Singed nasal hairs or eyebrows | Direct heat exposure to upper airway |
| Soot in mouth, nose, or sputum | Smoke inhalation |
| Hoarseness, stridor, brassy cough | Airway edema — impending obstruction |
| Facial burns | High risk for airway involvement |
| Carbonaceous (black) sputum | Carbon particles from smoke |
| History of enclosed-space fire | Always assume inhalation injury |

<div class="danger">
Airway edema after inhalation injury can develop over hours. A patient who is talking and breathing normally on arrival may obstruct within 12–24 hours. <strong>Early intubation is prioritized over "wait and see."</strong>
</div>

<h3>Carbon Monoxide Poisoning</h3>

Carbon monoxide (CO) binds hemoglobin with 200x greater affinity than oxygen, causing tissue hypoxia even when oxygen saturation looks normal on pulse oximetry.

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classic sign** | Cherry-red skin (often absent or late) |
| **Symptoms** | Headache, confusion, dizziness, loss of consciousness |
| **Pulse ox** | Falsely normal — cannot distinguish COHb from OxyHb |
| **Diagnosis** | Co-oximetry (arterial blood gas) |
| **Treatment** | 100% O₂ via non-rebreather mask; hyperbaric O₂ if severe |

<div class="priority">
Do NOT rely on pulse oximetry to rule out CO poisoning. A patient with cherry-red skin, confusion, and a history of enclosed-space exposure needs 100% O₂ immediately.
</div>

<h2 class="section-divider" id="emergent-phase">Emergent Phase: First 24–48 Hours</h2>

The emergent phase begins at the time of injury and lasts until capillary integrity is restored (usually 24–48 hours). The primary threat is **hypovolemic shock** from massive fluid shift.

<h3>Pathophysiology of the Fluid Shift</h3>

<div class="patho">
Burn injury causes massive release of inflammatory mediators → capillary permeability increases dramatically → fluid, protein, and electrolytes leak from the vascular space into the interstitium (third-spacing) → circulating blood volume drops rapidly → decreased cardiac output → shock. This is why even patients with moderate burns need aggressive IV fluid resuscitation.
</div>

<h3>Assessment Priorities in the Emergent Phase</h3>

| System | What to Assess | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Airway** | Hoarseness, stridor, facial burns, singed hairs | Inhalation injury → intubate early |
| **Breathing** | RR, SpO₂, breath sounds | CO poisoning, circumferential chest burns |
| **Circulation** | BP, HR, urine output, cap refill | Hypovolemic shock from fluid shift |
| **Neuro** | LOC, orientation | CO poisoning, shock |
| **Wound** | Depth, extent (TBSA), location | Drives resuscitation and care plan |
| **Extremities** | Pulses, sensation, cap refill distal to burn | Compartment syndrome risk |

<div class="assessment">
<strong>Urine output is the best indicator of adequate fluid resuscitation</strong> in burn patients. Target: <strong>0.5–1 mL/kg/hr in adults</strong> (30–50 mL/hr); <strong>1 mL/kg/hr in children</strong>.
</div>

<h3>Circumferential Burns and Escharotomy</h3>

Full-thickness circumferential burns form rigid eschar that does not expand — as underlying tissue swells, compartment syndrome develops.

<div class="danger">
Circumferential burns of the chest can restrict breathing. Circumferential burns of extremities can cut off distal circulation. An <strong>escharotomy</strong> (surgical incision through the eschar) relieves the pressure. Check distal pulses hourly with a Doppler.
</div>

<h2 class="section-divider" id="parkland-formula">Fluid Resuscitation: Parkland Formula</h2>

<div class="note">
The Parkland formula calculates the amount of Lactated Ringer's (LR) needed in the first 24 hours after a burn injury. It applies to burns ≥ 20% TBSA.
</div>

**Formula:**

> **4 mL × weight (kg) × % TBSA burned = total mL of LR in first 24 hours**

**Delivery schedule:**
- First **½** of the total — given over the **first 8 hours** (from time of injury, not from time of arrival)
- Second **½** — given over the **next 16 hours**

<div class="memory">
<strong>4-2-16 rule:</strong> 4 mL/kg/%, split in half — first half in 8 hours, second half in 16 hours. The clock starts at time of injury.
</div>

<div class="ati-tip">
Use <strong>Lactated Ringer's</strong> — not normal saline, not D5W. LR most closely matches the electrolyte composition of fluid being lost and reduces the risk of hyperchloremic acidosis. Colloids (albumin) are not added until after the first 24 hours when capillary integrity has been restored.
</div>

**Example:**
- 70 kg patient with 40% TBSA burn
- Total LR = 4 × 70 × 40 = **11,200 mL**
- First 8 hours: 5,600 mL
- Next 16 hours: 5,600 mL

<div class="assessment">
Titrate IV fluids to maintain urine output of <strong>0.5–1 mL/kg/hr</strong>. The formula gives a starting point — the actual rate is adjusted based on the patient's response.
</div>

<h2 class="section-divider" id="acute-phase">Acute Phase: Day 3 Through Wound Closure</h2>

Capillary integrity begins to restore around 48–72 hours. Fluid that was third-spaced starts to reabsorb — this is called **fluid remobilization**.

<div class="danger">
During fluid remobilization, circulating volume increases rapidly. Watch for <strong>fluid overload</strong>: pulmonary edema, elevated CVP, hypertension. Patients who needed aggressive resuscitation can develop overload as the fluid shifts back.
</div>

<h3>Acute Phase Priorities</h3>

| Priority | Details |
|---|---|
| **Wound care** | Daily debridement, dressing changes, topical antimicrobials |
| **Infection surveillance** | Burn wounds are not sterile — infection is the leading cause of death in the acute phase |
| **Pain control** | IV opioids before dressing changes; non-pharmacologic adjuncts |
| **Nutrition** | Hypermetabolic state — high protein, high calorie needs |
| **PT/OT** | Early mobilization, splinting, positioning to prevent contractures |
| **Psychological support** | Body image, PTSD, depression are common |

---

<h2 class="section-divider" id="rehab-phase">Rehabilitative Phase</h2>

Begins when wounds are closed (grafted or healed) and continues for months to years.

| Goal | Intervention |
|---|---|
| **Prevent contractures** | Splinting, range of motion exercises, positioning |
| **Manage scarring** | Pressure garments worn 23 hrs/day for up to 2 years |
| **Restore function** | PT/OT, occupational retraining if needed |
| **Psychological recovery** | Counseling, support groups, body image work |
| **Sun protection** | Healed burn skin has no melanin protection — sunscreen lifelong |

<h2 class="section-divider" id="wound-care">Wound Care Priorities</h2>

<h3>Topical Antimicrobials</h3>

| Agent | Coverage | Key Nursing Point |
|---|---|---|
| **Silver sulfadiazine (Silvadene)** | Broad spectrum (gram+, gram-, some fungi) | Do NOT use on face or in sulfa allergy; can cause leukopenia |
| **Mafenide acetate (Sulfamylon)** | Broad spectrum; penetrates eschar | Can cause metabolic acidosis; painful on application |
| **Silver nitrate** | Broad spectrum | Stains everything black/gray; causes electrolyte loss |
| **Bacitracin / petrolatum gauze** | Mild; gram+ focus | Used for facial burns and superficial areas |

<div class="ati-tip">
Silver sulfadiazine is the most commonly tested topical agent. Know that it is contraindicated in sulfa allergy and is not used on the face.
</div>

<h3>Biological and Synthetic Dressings</h3>

| Type | Examples | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Autograft** | Patient's own skin | Permanent coverage; gold standard |
| **Allograft** | Cadaver skin | Temporary biological cover while waiting for autograft |
| **Xenograft** | Pig skin (porcine) | Temporary; acts as biological dressing |
| **Synthetic** | Biobrane, Integra | Temporary; promotes healing of partial-thickness wounds |

<h2 class="section-divider" id="infection">Infection and Sepsis Risk</h2>

Burn wounds destroy the skin barrier entirely. The burn eschar is avascular — antibiotics cannot reach it through the bloodstream, which is why **topical** agents are essential.

<h3>Signs of Burn Wound Infection vs. Normal Healing</h3>

| Finding | Infection | Normal |
|---|---|---|
| Wound color change | Green, black, brown (new) | Pink, red |
| Odor | Foul, unusual | Mild |
| Eschar separation | Rapid, premature | Gradual |
| Surrounding skin | Erythema spreading beyond wound | Stable |
| Systemic signs | Fever >38.5°C or hypothermia, tachycardia, hypotension | Absent |

<div class="danger">
Burn patients can develop <strong>sepsis without fever</strong> because their thermoregulation is impaired. Hypothermia, increased glucose levels, and deteriorating mental status may be the first signs.
</div>

<div class="intervention">
Infection control measures: strict hand hygiene, contact precautions, sterile technique for dressing changes, wound cultures if infection suspected, and daily wound assessment. Do not use prophylactic systemic antibiotics — this promotes resistant organisms.
</div>

<h2 class="section-divider" id="pain">Pain Management</h2>

Burns cause some of the most severe pain in nursing — both background (constant) pain and procedural pain during dressing changes.

| Pain Type | Management |
|---|---|
| **Background pain** | Scheduled oral or IV opioids; adjuncts (NSAIDs, gabapentin) |
| **Procedural pain** | IV opioid 15–30 min before dressing change; anxiolytics; ketamine for large dressing changes |
| **Non-pharmacologic** | Positioning, distraction, music therapy, relaxation techniques |
| **Breakthrough pain** | PRN opioid doses available |

<div class="teaching">
Warn patients that deep partial-thickness and full-thickness burns may actually have <strong>less pain</strong> initially because nerve endings are destroyed — but as healing occurs and nerves regenerate, pain increases significantly.
</div>

<div class="priority">
Premedicate patients <strong>before</strong> dressing changes. Do not wait until the patient is in the middle of a painful procedure to administer analgesia.
</div>

<h2 class="section-divider" id="nutrition">Nutritional Needs</h2>

Burns create a profoundly hypermetabolic state. Metabolic rate can increase 2–3× normal. Nutritional support is a critical nursing priority.

| Nutrient | Goal | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Calories** | Up to 2× basal metabolic rate | Hypermetabolic state, tissue repair |
| **Protein** | 1.5–3 g/kg/day | Wound healing, prevent catabolism |
| **Carbohydrates** | Primary energy source | Protein sparing |
| **Vitamin C** | High dose | Collagen synthesis, wound healing |
| **Zinc** | Supplemented | Wound healing, immune function |
| **Iron** | Monitor | Anemia from blood loss/hemolysis |

<div class="note">
Enteral nutrition (tube feeding) is preferred over parenteral (TPN) and should be started within 6 hours of injury when possible. Early enteral feeding reduces infection risk, maintains gut integrity, and decreases hospital length of stay.
</div>

<div class="assessment">
Monitor weight daily, albumin and prealbumin weekly, and nitrogen balance. Patients who are not meeting caloric goals orally should be considered for tube feeds.
</div>

<h2 class="section-divider" id="priority-actions">Priority Nursing Actions by Phase</h2>

<h3>Emergent Phase (0–48 hours)</h3>

<div class="intervention">

1. Establish and protect the airway — intubate early if inhalation injury suspected
2. Administer 100% O₂ via non-rebreather if CO poisoning possible
3. Insert two large-bore IVs through unburned skin if possible
4. Begin Parkland formula fluid resuscitation with Lactated Ringer's
5. Insert Foley catheter — monitor urine output every hour (goal: 0.5–1 mL/kg/hr)
6. Remove all jewelry and clothing from burned areas
7. Cover burns with clean dry dressings (no ice, no wet towels — causes hypothermia)
8. Check distal pulses hourly on circumferential burns — prepare for possible escharotomy
9. Obtain weight, height, TBSA estimate, and history (including tetanus status)
10. Initiate pain management
</div>

<h3>Acute Phase (48 hours–wound closure)</h3>

<div class="intervention">

1. Daily wound assessment and dressing changes using sterile technique
2. Apply topical antimicrobial agents as ordered
3. Monitor for infection signs (wound, systemic)
4. Titrate fluids to urine output during fluid remobilization — watch for overload
5. Ensure adequate nutritional intake — consult dietitian
6. Collaborate with PT/OT for early mobilization and splinting
7. Manage background and procedural pain proactively
8. Monitor labs: CBC, electrolytes, albumin, glucose, wound cultures
9. Provide psychological support and body image counseling
</div>

<h3>Rehabilitative Phase</h3>

<div class="teaching">

- Wear pressure garments 23 hours per day as prescribed
- Perform range of motion exercises daily
- Protect healed skin from sun exposure — apply SPF 50+ sunscreen lifelong
- Moisturize healed skin regularly — new skin lacks normal gland function
- Report any wound reopening, blistering, or signs of infection
- Follow up with burn clinic, PT/OT, and counseling as scheduled
</div>
