# Medical Emergencies: Cardiac Emergencies

<div class="note">
Cardiac emergencies are situations where the heart is not getting enough oxygen, cannot pump effectively, or is under dangerous pressure. For NCLEX, focus on recognizing the emergency, assessing oxygenation and circulation, and knowing what action comes first.
</div>

---

# Cardiac Emergency Quick Chart

| Emergency                                    | Main Problem                                             | Biggest Clue                                                          | Priority                                                      |
| -------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Stable angina                                | Temporary decreased blood flow to heart muscle           | Chest pain with activity/stress, relieved by rest or nitro            | Stop activity, rest, assess, give nitro as prescribed         |
| Unstable angina                              | Worsening or unpredictable ischemia                      | Chest pain at rest or not relieved by nitro                           | Treat like possible MI                                        |
| Myocardial infarction                        | Blocked coronary blood flow causing heart muscle damage  | Chest pain not relieved by rest/nitro, diaphoresis, elevated troponin | ECG, oxygenation, cardiac monitor, emergency meds/reperfusion |
| Acute CHF/pulmonary edema                    | Heart cannot pump effectively; fluid backs up into lungs | Severe SOB, crackles, pink frothy sputum                              | High Fowler’s, oxygen, diuretics as prescribed                |
| Hypertensive crisis                          | Dangerously elevated BP                                  | BP often around 180 systolic or 120 diastolic                         | Assess for organ damage                                       |
| Hypertensive emergency                       | Severe BP with organ damage                              | Chest pain, neuro changes, SOB, kidney injury                         | IV BP meds, close monitoring                                  |
| Hypertensive urgency/severe asymptomatic HTN | Severe BP without organ damage                           | Very high BP but no acute organ damage                                | Oral meds/adjustments and close follow-up, not rapid IV drop  |

# Angina

## What It Is

Angina is **temporary chest pain caused by decreased blood flow to the heart muscle**.

The heart muscle is not getting enough oxygen for the amount of work it is doing.

This is called **myocardial ischemia**.

## Simple Explanation

The coronary artery is narrowed, but not completely blocked.

When the heart needs more oxygen, the narrowed vessel cannot supply enough blood. This causes chest pain.

---

## Stable Angina

Stable angina is predictable.

It usually happens with:

* Activity
* Exercise
* Stress
* Cold weather
* Heavy meals

It usually improves with:

* Rest
* Nitroglycerin

## Key Point

Stable angina is usually a chronic pattern.

The client may say, “I get chest pain when I walk too fast, but it goes away when I rest.”

---

## Unstable Angina

Unstable angina is an emergency.

It may occur:

* At rest
* With minimal activity
* More often than usual
* With more severe pain
* Without relief from nitroglycerin

## Why It Matters

Unstable angina can be a warning sign that the client may be developing an MI.

For exams, chest pain at rest is always more concerning than predictable chest pain with activity.

---

## Angina Triggers

Your notes use the **E words**.

| Trigger     | Why It Causes Angina                                |
| ----------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Exertion    | Heart works harder and needs more oxygen            |
| Excitement  | Stress increases sympathetic stimulation            |
| Environment | Very hot or cold weather can stress the heart       |
| Eating      | Large meals increase blood flow needs for digestion |

Other important trigger:

* Nicotine, because it causes vasoconstriction

---

## Signs and Symptoms of Angina

* Chest pressure
* Chest tightness
* Chest pain
* Pain radiating to the arm, jaw, neck, shoulder, or back
* Shortness of breath
* Nausea
* Sweating
* Fatigue
* Anxiety

## Important NCLEX Point

Radiating pain can happen with both angina and MI.

Radiation does **not** automatically mean MI.

What is more concerning is:

* Pain at rest
* Pain that is new
* Pain that is worsening
* Pain not relieved by nitroglycerin

---

# Nursing Care for Angina

## Priority During an Episode

1. Stop activity.
2. Have the client rest.
3. Place in semi-Fowler’s or high Fowler’s.
4. Assess chest pain.
5. Check vital signs.
6. Check oxygen saturation.
7. Apply oxygen if hypoxemic or in respiratory distress.
8. Monitor ECG.
9. Give nitroglycerin as prescribed.
10. Notify provider if pain is new, worsening, or not relieved.

## Why Fowler’s Position Helps

Semi-Fowler’s or high Fowler’s decreases venous return.

Less venous return means the heart has less blood to pump.

This decreases cardiac workload.

---

# Nitroglycerin

## What It Does

Nitroglycerin is a nitrate.

It causes vasodilation.

It dilates coronary arteries, which helps increase blood flow to the heart muscle.

## Why It Helps Angina

More blood flow to the myocardium means more oxygen delivery.

This can relieve chest pain caused by ischemia.

---

## Side Effects

Nitroglycerin does not only dilate coronary arteries.

It can also dilate other blood vessels.

| Effect                     | Result                           |
| -------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Cerebral vessel dilation   | Headache                         |
| Peripheral vessel dilation | Hypotension, dizziness, fainting |

---

## Nursing Considerations

Before giving nitroglycerin:

* Check blood pressure.
* Assess chest pain.
* Have the client sit or lie down.
* Monitor for dizziness.
* Monitor for hypotension.

## Major Safety Warning

Do **not** give nitroglycerin with erectile dysfunction medications such as sildenafil/Viagra.

Both are vasodilators and can cause severe hypotension.

---

## Sublingual Nitroglycerin Teaching

Teach the client:

* Place tablet under the tongue.
* Do not chew.
* Do not swallow.
* Let it dissolve.
* Sit or lie down before taking it.
* Take one dose every 5 minutes as prescribed.
* Maximum is usually 3 doses.
* If pain is not relieved, seek emergency care.

## NCLEX Tip

If nitroglycerin does not relieve the pain, think:

* Possible MI
* Medication may be expired
* Client needs emergency evaluation

---

## Nitroglycerin Storage

Nitroglycerin is sensitive to heat, light, and moisture.

Teach the client:

* Keep it in the original dark glass container.
* Keep it away from heat.
* Keep it away from light.
* Keep it away from humidity.
* Do not store it in the refrigerator.
* Replace it every 6 to 12 months or when expired.

---

## Nitroglycerin Patch or Paste

Nitroglycerin can also come as:

* Patch
* Paste
* Spray
* IV form

### Patch/Paste Teaching

* Apply to a hairless area.
* Clip hair if needed; do not shave.
* Rotate sites.
* Remove old patch or paste before applying a new one.
* Wear gloves when handling nitroglycerin paste.
* IV nitroglycerin should be protected from light.

---

# Calcium Channel Blockers for Angina

## Examples

* Nifedipine
* Verapamil

## Why They Are Used

Calcium channel blockers can be used for:

* Angina
* Hypertension
* Some supraventricular dysrhythmias

## Action

They decrease myocardial oxygen demand by:

* Dilating blood vessels
* Decreasing blood pressure
* Decreasing myocardial contractility

## Side Effects

* Hypotension
* Headache
* Dizziness
* Bradycardia or pulse changes
* AV block
* Edema
* Constipation, especially with verapamil

## Nursing Considerations

* Monitor blood pressure.
* Monitor heart rate.
* Assess for dizziness.
* Assess for edema.
* Teach client not to stop medication abruptly.

# Myocardial Infarction — MI

## What It Is

A myocardial infarction is a heart attack.

It happens when blood flow through a coronary artery is severely blocked or completely blocked.

This causes heart muscle damage.

## Main Problem

The myocardium is not getting enough oxygen.

If oxygen is not restored quickly, the heart muscle begins to die.

---

## Angina vs MI

| Angina                            | MI                                  |
| --------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Temporary ischemia                | Heart muscle damage                 |
| Usually relieved by rest or nitro | Not fully relieved by rest or nitro |
| Troponin usually normal           | Troponin elevated                   |
| Predictable if stable             | Often severe or prolonged           |
| Chronic pattern if stable         | Emergency                           |

---

## Causes of MI

An MI may happen when:

* Plaque builds up inside coronary arteries.
* Plaque ruptures.
* A blood clot forms.
* A clot travels and blocks a narrowed artery.
* The coronary artery becomes fully or almost fully blocked.

---

## Risk Factors

* Coronary artery disease
* Hypertension
* Hyperlipidemia
* Smoking
* Diabetes mellitus
* Obesity
* Sedentary lifestyle
* Family history
* Older age
* Stress
* High-fat diet

---

## Signs and Symptoms

Classic symptoms:

* Chest pain or pressure
* Chest tightness
* Pain radiating to left arm, jaw, neck, shoulder, or back
* Shortness of breath
* Diaphoresis
* Nausea or vomiting
* Anxiety
* Feeling of impending doom
* Dizziness
* Fatigue

## Atypical Symptoms

Women, older adults, and clients with diabetes may have less classic symptoms.

They may report:

* Indigestion
* Unusual fatigue
* Nausea
* Back pain
* Jaw pain
* Shortness of breath without obvious chest pain

---

# Priority Nursing Care for Suspected MI

## First Actions

1. Assess chest pain.
2. Assess airway and breathing.
3. Check oxygen saturation.
4. Apply oxygen if needed.
5. Obtain vital signs.
6. Place on cardiac monitor.
7. Obtain ECG.
8. Establish IV access.
9. Administer prescribed medications.
10. Prepare for reperfusion therapy if ordered.

## NCLEX Priority

The client with chest pain needs rapid assessment because heart muscle damage worsens over time.

---

# Diagnostics for MI

| Test                    | Purpose                                                                 |
| ----------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| ECG                     | Looks for ST elevation, ST depression, T-wave changes, and dysrhythmias |
| Troponin                | Most specific lab for myocardial injury                                 |
| CK-MB                   | Can show heart muscle damage                                            |
| Chest x-ray             | Helps rule out other causes                                             |
| Cardiac catheterization | Locates coronary artery blockage                                        |
| Echocardiogram          | Shows heart function and wall motion                                    |

## Troponin

Troponin rises when heart muscle is damaged.

For NCLEX, troponin is the key cardiac marker for MI.

---

# Emergency Treatment for MI

The goals are to:

* Improve oxygenation
* Decrease cardiac workload
* Prevent clot from getting worse
* Restore blood flow
* Relieve pain and anxiety

| Treatment        | Why It Is Used                                             |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| Oxygen           | Improves oxygenation if O2 is low or client is in distress |
| Aspirin          | Antiplatelet; helps prevent clot from worsening            |
| Nitroglycerin    | Vasodilates and decreases cardiac workload                 |
| Morphine         | Relieves pain and anxiety if ordered                       |
| Anticoagulants   | Prevent clot extension                                     |
| Beta blockers    | Decrease heart rate and cardiac workload                   |
| Statins          | Lower cholesterol and stabilize plaque                     |
| PCI/cardiac cath | Opens blocked coronary artery                              |

## MONA

MONA is a memory tool:

* Morphine
* Oxygen
* Nitroglycerin
* Aspirin

But remember: MONA is not always the exact order.

Oxygen is usually given if the client is hypoxemic or in respiratory distress.

---

# Post-MI Complications

Watch for:

* Dysrhythmias
* Heart failure
* Cardiogenic shock
* Recurrent chest pain
* Hypotension
* Pulmonary edema
* Decreased urine output
* Cardiac arrest

---

# Post-MI Nursing Care

* Maintain cardiac monitoring.
* Monitor vital signs.
* Monitor oxygen saturation.
* Assess chest pain.
* Assess lung sounds.
* Monitor intake and output.
* Monitor labs.
* Promote rest.
* Reduce anxiety.
* Avoid overexertion.
* Teach activity restrictions.
* Prepare for cardiac rehab.

# Valsalva Maneuver After MI

## What It Is

Valsalva means bearing down against a closed glottis.

This commonly happens when a client strains to have a bowel movement.

## Why It Is Dangerous

Straining stimulates the vagus nerve.

This can cause:

* Bradycardia
* PVCs
* Dysrhythmias
* Decreased cardiac output

This is especially dangerous after an MI.

## Nursing Interventions

* Administer stool softeners as prescribed.
* Prevent constipation.
* Encourage fluids if allowed.
* Encourage fiber if allowed.
* Teach the client not to strain.
* Teach the client not to hold their breath during activity.

---

# Cardiac Procedures

## Balloon Angioplasty

A balloon-tipped catheter is inserted into a narrowed artery.

The balloon is inflated to open the vessel.

## Key Points

* Opens the vessel quickly.
* May not be permanent.
* The artery can narrow again.
* Lifestyle changes are still needed.
* There is a risk of plaque or clot material breaking loose.

---

## Stent

A stent may be placed during angioplasty.

It helps keep the artery open.

## Nursing Teaching

* Take antiplatelet medications as prescribed.
* Do not stop antiplatelet medication unless directed.
* Report chest pain.
* Follow cardiac rehab instructions.
* Make lifestyle changes to reduce restenosis risk.

---

## CABG — Coronary Artery Bypass Graft

CABG creates a new route around blocked coronary arteries.

The graft may come from:

* A vein in the leg
* An artery in the chest

## Key Points

* This is major surgery.
* The sternum is cut and wired closed.
* The client may be on a heart-lung machine during surgery.
* Recovery includes sternal precautions.
* Lifestyle changes are still necessary.

## CABG Teaching

* Splint the chest when coughing.
* Report signs of infection.
* Follow activity restrictions.
* Avoid lifting heavy objects until cleared.
* Attend cardiac rehab.
* Take medications as prescribed.
* Follow heart-healthy diet.

---

# Acute CHF / Pulmonary Edema

## What It Is

Congestive heart failure means the heart cannot pump effectively enough to meet the body’s needs.

Fluid can back up into the lungs or the body.

When fluid backs up into the lungs, it can become an emergency called **pulmonary edema**.

---

## Left-Sided Heart Failure

Left-sided heart failure causes lung symptoms.

Memory trick:

**Left = Lungs**

### Why

The left ventricle cannot pump blood forward effectively.

Blood backs up into:

1. Left ventricle
2. Left atrium
3. Pulmonary veins
4. Lungs

### Signs and Symptoms

* Dyspnea
* Crackles/rales
* Cough
* Orthopnea
* Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea
* Low oxygen saturation
* Restlessness
* Fatigue
* Pink frothy sputum if severe

---

## Right-Sided Heart Failure

Right-sided heart failure causes venous congestion in the body.

Memory trick:

**Right = Rest of body**

### Signs and Symptoms

* Jugular vein distention
* Peripheral edema
* Pitting edema
* Weight gain
* Ascites
* Hepatomegaly
* Enlarged abdomen
* Nausea

---

# Acute Pulmonary Edema

## What It Is

Pulmonary edema occurs when fluid builds up in the lungs.

This is a medical emergency because gas exchange is impaired.

## Signs and Symptoms

* Severe shortness of breath
* Crackles
* Frothy sputum
* Pink frothy sputum
* Anxiety
* Restlessness
* Tachycardia
* Low oxygen saturation
* Sitting upright/tripod positioning
* Cool, clammy skin

## Priority Nursing Action

Improve oxygenation.

---

## Nursing Care for Acute CHF/Pulmonary Edema

1. Place in high Fowler’s.
2. Apply oxygen.
3. Assess lung sounds.
4. Monitor oxygen saturation.
5. Notify provider or rapid response if severe.
6. Administer diuretics as prescribed.
7. Monitor vital signs.
8. Monitor intake and output.
9. Monitor urine output.
10. Monitor potassium and renal labs.
11. Reduce anxiety.
12. Prepare for additional respiratory support if needed.

---

# CHF Treatment: Pump It and Pee It

CHF treatment often focuses on:

| Goal                | Medication Example | Why                             |
| ------------------- | ------------------ | ------------------------------- |
| Help the heart pump | Digoxin            | Slows and strengthens heartbeat |
| Remove extra fluid  | Furosemide/Lasix   | Decreases fluid overload        |

---

# Digoxin

## What It Does

Digoxin slows and strengthens the heartbeat.

It acts on the SA node and AV node.

This means it can:

* Slow heart rate
* Strengthen contraction
* Improve cardiac output

## Uses

* Heart failure
* Atrial fibrillation
* Atrial flutter
* Some supraventricular dysrhythmias

---

## Before Giving Digoxin

Check the apical pulse for 1 full minute.

Hold digoxin if:

* Adult pulse is below 60
* Infant pulse is below 100
* Toddler pulse is around below 90
* Signs of toxicity are present
* Digoxin level is too high

---

## Digoxin Levels

| Level                  | Meaning                  |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------ |
| 0.8–2.0 ng/mL          | Therapeutic              |
| Greater than 2.0 ng/mL | Hold and notify provider |
| Around 2.5 ng/mL       | Toxicity concern         |

## Antidote

Digibind

---

## Digoxin Toxicity

Watch for:

* Nausea
* Vomiting
* Anorexia
* Bradycardia
* Dysrhythmias
* Green/yellow halos
* Vision changes
* Confusion
* Dizziness

## NCLEX Clue

If the client says they see green or yellow halos, think digoxin toxicity.

---

## Potassium and Digoxin

Low potassium increases the risk of digoxin toxicity.

This is important because clients may take digoxin with furosemide/Lasix.

Lasix can lower potassium.

Low potassium + digoxin = higher toxicity risk.

---

# Diuretics in CHF

## Potassium-Wasting Diuretics

| Type               | Examples                    | Main Concerns                        |
| ------------------ | --------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Thiazide diuretics | Hydrochlorothiazide, Diuril | Hypokalemia, orthostatic hypotension |
| Loop diuretics     | Furosemide/Lasix            | Hypokalemia, hyponatremia            |

## Potassium-Sparing Diuretics

| Medication               | Main Concern |
| ------------------------ | ------------ |
| Spironolactone/Aldactone | Hyperkalemia |

## Spironolactone Action

Spironolactone blocks aldosterone.

Aldosterone tells the kidneys to hold sodium and water and get rid of potassium.

If aldosterone is blocked:

* Sodium and water are excreted
* Potassium is retained

---

## High-Potassium Foods

Know these for clients on potassium-wasting diuretics:

* Bananas
* Oranges
* Melons
* Apricots
* Prunes
* Baked potatoes
* Beans
* Squash
* Meats

| Test           | What It Shows                                 |
| -------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| BNP            | Elevated in heart failure                     |
| Echocardiogram | Heart structure, valves, ejection fraction    |

| ---------------- | --------------- |

# Hypertensive Crisis

## What It Is

A hypertensive crisis means blood pressure is dangerously high.

It is often around:

* Systolic BP 180 or higher
* Diastolic BP 120 or higher

The most important question is not just “How high is the BP?”

The most important question is:

**Is there acute target-organ damage?**

---

# Hypertensive Emergency vs Hypertensive Urgency

| Type                                                  | Meaning                                               | Treatment                                 |
| ----------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Hypertensive emergency                                | Severe BP elevation with acute target-organ damage    | IV antihypertensives and close monitoring |
| Hypertensive urgency/severe asymptomatic hypertension | Severe BP elevation without acute target-organ damage | Oral meds/adjustment and close follow-up  |

## NCLEX Tip

The number matters, but symptoms and organ damage matter more.

A BP of 190/120 with chest pain, neuro changes, or shortness of breath is more urgent than a high BP with no symptoms.

---

# Target Organs

Hypertensive emergency can damage:

* Brain
* Heart
* Kidneys
* Eyes
* Blood vessels

---

# Signs of Target-Organ Damage

## Brain

* Severe headache
* Confusion
* Altered mental status
* Seizures
* Vision changes
* Stroke symptoms
* Weakness
* Trouble speaking

## Heart

* Chest pain
* Shortness of breath
* Pulmonary edema
* MI symptoms
* Dysrhythmias

## Kidneys

* Decreased urine output
* Elevated creatinine
* Blood or protein in urine

## Eyes

* Blurred vision
* Retinal changes
* Vision loss

---

# Causes / Risk Factors

* Chronic hypertension
* Missed BP medications
* Medication nonadherence
* Kidney disease
* Pregnancy complications
* Cocaine or stimulant use
* Severe pain or anxiety
* Certain medication interactions
* Endocrine disorders

---

# Assessment for Hypertensive Crisis

## First Assess

* Blood pressure in both arms if appropriate
* Heart rate
* Respiratory status
* Oxygen saturation
* Chest pain
* Neuro status
* Level of consciousness
* Vision changes
* Headache
* Urine output
* Medication history
* Last dose of BP medications

## Ask About

* Chest pain
* Shortness of breath
* Severe headache
* Confusion
* Weakness
* Vision changes
* Decreased urination
* Pregnancy/postpartum status
* Cocaine/stimulant use
* Missed antihypertensive medications

---

# Diagnostics

| Test           | Why                                         |
| -------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| ECG            | Checks for MI, ischemia, dysrhythmias       |
| Troponin       | Checks for cardiac injury                   |
| BMP/CMP        | Checks kidney function and electrolytes     |
| Creatinine/BUN | Checks kidney injury                        |
| Urinalysis     | Checks protein or blood in urine            |
| Chest x-ray    | Checks pulmonary edema or heart enlargement |
| CT head        | If neuro symptoms are present               |
| Eye exam       | May show retinal damage                     |

# Treatment of Hypertensive Emergency

## Main Goal

Lower blood pressure safely and prevent further organ damage.

## Important

Do not drop BP too fast unless there is a specific reason.

A sudden large BP drop can decrease perfusion to the brain, heart, or kidneys.

## Treatment

* IV antihypertensives
* Continuous or frequent BP monitoring
* Cardiac monitoring
* Treat the affected organ system
* Monitor kidney function
* Monitor neuro status
* Monitor urine output

---

## IV Medications That May Be Used

Examples include:

* Labetalol
* Nicardipine
* Clevidipine
* Esmolol
* Hydralazine
* Nitroglycerin
* Nitroprusside

The specific medication depends on the client’s condition.

---

# Treatment of Hypertensive Urgency / Severe Asymptomatic Hypertension

If there is no acute organ damage, treatment is usually less aggressive.

Treatment may include:

* Oral antihypertensive medication
* Restarting missed medications
* Adjusting home medications
* Monitoring BP
* Close follow-up

## NCLEX Tip

Do not rapidly drop BP in an asymptomatic client unless ordered for a specific reason.

---

# Nursing Priorities for Hypertensive Crisis

1. Recheck BP correctly.
2. Assess for symptoms of organ damage.
3. Assess neuro status.
4. Assess chest pain and respiratory status.
5. Place on cardiac monitor if emergency symptoms are present.
6. Establish IV access if hypertensive emergency is suspected.
7. Administer medications as prescribed.
8. Monitor BP frequently.
9. Monitor urine output.
10. Implement safety precautions.

---

# Patient Teaching for Hypertension Prevention

Teach the client:

* Take BP medications exactly as prescribed.
* Do not stop medications suddenly.
* Check BP at home.
* Follow low-sodium diet if prescribed.
* Avoid smoking.
* Limit alcohol.
* Exercise as allowed.
* Maintain healthy weight.
* Manage stress.
* Keep follow-up appointments.
* Report severe headache, chest pain, SOB, weakness, or vision changes immediately.

---

# When to Seek Emergency Help

The client should seek emergency care for high BP with:

* Chest pain
* Shortness of breath
* Severe headache
* Confusion
* Weakness
* Trouble speaking
* Vision changes
* Seizure
* Decreased level of consciousness
* Decreased urine output
* Stroke symptoms

# Hemophilia Bleeding Emergency

## What It Is

Hemophilia is a clotting disorder where the blood does not clot properly.

The client is missing a clotting factor, so bleeding can last longer than normal.

## Main Problem

Missing clotting factor → prolonged bleeding

## Types

| Type | Missing Factor |
|---|---|
| Hemophilia A | Factor VIII |
| Hemophilia B | Factor IX |

## Why It Is an Emergency

The biggest concern is uncontrolled bleeding.

Bleeding can happen:

- After injury
- After surgery
- After dental work
- Into joints
- Into muscles
- Internally
- In the brain after head injury

## Classic Hemophilia

Classic hemophilia is usually X-linked recessive.

Males are more likely to have the disease.

Females are more likely to be carriers.

## Signs and Symptoms

- Easy bruising
- Prolonged bleeding
- Nosebleeds
- Blood in urine
- Blood in stool
- Swollen painful joints
- Limited range of motion
- Hematomas
- Excessive bleeding after injury or procedure

## Hemarthrosis

Hemarthrosis means bleeding into a joint.

This is common in hemophilia.

Signs include:

- Joint swelling
- Severe joint pain
- Warmth
- Decreased movement

Common joints:

- Knees
- Ankles
- Elbows

## Priority Concern

Bleeding.

Any head injury in a client with hemophilia is an emergency because of the risk for intracranial bleeding.

## Nursing Care During a Bleeding Episode

Use RICE:

| Intervention | Why |
|---|---|
| Rest | Prevents worsening bleeding |
| Ice | Decreases swelling and bleeding |
| Compression | Helps control bleeding |
| Elevation | Decreases swelling |

Also:

- Administer prescribed clotting factor.
- Assess pain.
- Monitor vital signs.
- Monitor for signs of shock.
- Avoid unnecessary invasive procedures.

## Joint Bleeding Nursing Care

If the client has a swollen painful joint:

- Rest the joint.
- Avoid range-of-motion exercises during active bleeding.
- Use pain control as prescribed.
- Use a bed cradle if needed to keep sheets off the painful joint.
- Give factor replacement as prescribed.

## Treatment

| Treatment | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Factor VIII | Replaces missing factor in Hemophilia A |
| Factor IX | Replaces missing factor in Hemophilia B |
| Cryoprecipitate | May be used for factor replacement |
| Desmopressin | May be used for mild Hemophilia A |
| Acetaminophen | Preferred for pain/fever |

## Avoid

- Aspirin
- NSAIDs like ibuprofen
- IM injections
- Rectal temperatures
- Contact sports
- Rough toothbrushes
- Unnecessary procedures

## Safe Pain/Fever Medication

Acetaminophen is usually preferred.

Avoid aspirin and NSAIDs because they increase bleeding risk.

## Activity Teaching

The child should not be kept inside all the time, but activities should be safe.

Safer activities:

- Swimming
- Walking
- Non-contact activities
- Chess/math team
- Scorekeeper role

Avoid:

- Football
- Soccer
- Hockey
- Wrestling
- Rough contact sports

## Parent/Client Teaching

- Wear medical alert identification.
- Keep factor replacement available if prescribed.
- Use a soft toothbrush.
- Use an electric razor.
- Apply pressure after blood draws.
- Go to regular dental checkups.
- Avoid contact sports.
- Report head injury immediately.
- Report joint pain or swelling early.
- Do not give aspirin or ibuprofen unless specifically prescribed.

## NCLEX Tips

- Head injury + hemophilia = emergency.
- Joint swelling and pain = possible bleeding into the joint.
- Do not perform ROM during active joint bleeding.
- Avoid aspirin and NSAIDs.
- Give the missing clotting factor.
- Do not overprotect the child completely; promote safe activity.

## Cardiac & Perfusion Emergencies Review Chart

| Emergency                                          | Main Problem                                                       | Biggest Clues                                                                                                           | Priority Nursing Action                                                            | Key Treatment/Medications                                                                              | NCLEX Tip                                                           |
| -------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Stable Angina**                                  | Temporary decreased blood flow to the heart muscle                 | Chest pain with exertion, stress, cold weather, large meals, or smoking; relieved by rest/nitro                         | Stop activity, have client rest, assess pain and VS                                | Nitroglycerin, oxygen if needed, calcium channel blockers, beta blockers as prescribed                 | Stable angina is predictable and relieved by rest or nitro          |
| **Unstable Angina**                                | Worsening cardiac ischemia that may lead to MI                     | Chest pain at rest, new chest pain, worsening pattern, not relieved by nitro                                            | Treat like possible MI; assess oxygenation, VS, ECG, notify provider               | Nitro, aspirin, oxygen if needed, anticoagulants/antiplatelets as prescribed                           | Chest pain at rest is more concerning than chest pain with activity |
| **Myocardial Infarction**                          | Blocked coronary blood flow causing heart muscle damage            | Chest pressure/pain not relieved by rest or nitro, diaphoresis, SOB, nausea, anxiety, radiating pain, elevated troponin | Assess chest pain and oxygenation, obtain ECG, place on cardiac monitor, IV access | Aspirin, nitro, oxygen if hypoxemic, morphine if ordered, anticoagulants, PCI/cath                     | Troponin is the key lab for MI                                      |
| **Post-MI Valsalva Risk**                          | Straining can stimulate vagus nerve and trigger dysrhythmias       | Constipation, bearing down, holding breath with movement                                                                | Prevent straining                                                                  | Stool softeners, fluids/fiber if allowed                                                               | Post-MI clients should not strain during bowel movements            |
| **Acute CHF / Pulmonary Edema**                    | Heart cannot pump effectively; fluid backs up into lungs           | Severe SOB, crackles/rales, orthopnea, low O2, anxiety, pink frothy sputum                                              | High Fowler’s, oxygen, assess lung sounds, notify provider if severe               | Furosemide/Lasix, oxygen, possible morphine/nitrates depending on order                                | Left-sided failure = lung symptoms                                  |
| **Right-Sided Heart Failure**                      | Blood backs up into venous system/body                             | JVD, peripheral edema, pitting edema, weight gain, hepatomegaly, ascites                                                | Assess edema, daily weights, I&O, monitor fluid overload                           | Diuretics, sodium/fluid restriction if ordered                                                         | Right-sided failure = rest of body swelling                         |
| **Digoxin Toxicity**                               | Too much digoxin or increased sensitivity, often worse with low K+ | N/V, anorexia, bradycardia, dysrhythmias, green/yellow halos                                                            | Hold digoxin and notify provider                                                   | Check digoxin level and potassium; antidote is Digibind                                                | Low potassium increases risk for digoxin toxicity                   |
| **Hypertensive Crisis**                            | Dangerously high BP, usually around 180 systolic or 120 diastolic  | Severe BP elevation; may or may not have symptoms                                                                       | Assess for target-organ damage                                                     | Depends on whether emergency or urgency                                                                | The BP number matters, but organ damage matters more                |
| **Hypertensive Emergency**                         | Severe BP elevation with acute organ damage                        | Chest pain, SOB, neuro changes, severe headache, confusion, vision changes, decreased urine output                      | IV access, frequent BP monitoring, cardiac/neuro assessment                        | IV antihypertensives such as labetalol, nicardipine, nitroglycerin, hydralazine depending on condition | Do not drop BP too fast unless specifically ordered                 |
| **Hypertensive Urgency / Severe Asymptomatic HTN** | Severe BP elevation without acute organ damage                     | Very high BP but no chest pain, neuro changes, SOB, or kidney injury symptoms                                           | Recheck BP correctly, assess symptoms, notify provider                             | Oral meds or home med adjustment, close follow-up                                                      | No organ damage = usually not rapid IV BP drop                      |
| **Hemophilia Bleeding Emergency**                  | Missing clotting factor causes prolonged bleeding                  | Easy bruising, prolonged bleeding, swollen painful joint, hematoma, nosebleeds, blood in urine/stool                    | Control bleeding and give prescribed clotting factor                               | Factor VIII for Hemophilia A, Factor IX for Hemophilia B, cryoprecipitate, desmopressin for mild A     | Head injury in hemophilia is an emergency                           |
| **Hemarthrosis**                                   | Bleeding into a joint                                              | Swollen, painful, warm joint with decreased movement                                                                    | Rest joint; avoid ROM during active bleeding                                       | RICE, factor replacement, pain control, bed cradle if needed                                           | Do not exercise a joint that is actively bleeding                   |
| **Bleeding Precautions**                           | Prevent injury and worsening bleeding                              | Hemophilia, low platelets, anticoagulants, bleeding disorders                                                           | Avoid trauma and invasive procedures when possible                                 | Soft toothbrush, electric razor, pressure after blood draws                                            | Avoid aspirin, NSAIDs, IM injections, and contact sports            |

## Super Quick Priority Cues

| Clue                                       | Think                       | First/Priority Action                          |
| ------------------------------------------ | --------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Chest pain with activity, relieved by rest | Stable angina               | Stop activity and rest                         |
| Chest pain while watching TV               | Unstable angina             | Treat like possible MI                         |
| Chest pain not relieved by nitro           | MI                          | ECG, oxygenation assessment, cardiac monitor   |
| Elevated troponin                          | MI                          | Continue emergency cardiac care                |
| Crackles + severe SOB                      | Pulmonary edema/acute CHF   | High Fowler’s and oxygen                       |
| Pink frothy sputum                         | Pulmonary edema             | Oxygen and rapid provider notification         |
| Green/yellow halos                         | Digoxin toxicity            | Hold digoxin and notify provider               |
| Low K+ with digoxin                        | Increased toxicity risk     | Monitor potassium and digoxin level            |
| BP 180/120 with neuro changes              | Hypertensive emergency      | Assess neuro status and prepare IV BP meds     |
| BP 180/120 but no symptoms                 | Hypertensive urgency        | Recheck BP, assess, oral med adjustment likely |

| Hemophilia + swollen painful knee          | Hemarthrosis                | Rest, ice, factor replacement, no ROM          |

# Most Important “What Do I Do First?” Points

## Angina

Stop activity and rest.

## Unstable Angina

Treat as possible MI.

## MI

Assess chest pain, oxygenation, get ECG, and place on cardiac monitor.

## Acute CHF/Pulmonary Edema

High Fowler’s and oxygen.

## Hypertensive Crisis

Assess for organ damage.

## Hypertensive Emergency

IV BP medications and close monitoring.

## Post-MI Valsalva Risk

Give stool softeners and prevent straining.
