# Medical Emergencies: Preparedness and Crisis Intervention

<div class="note" markdown="1">

This section focuses on emergency department basics, triage, disaster preparedness, human trafficking recognition, and crisis intervention.

Big idea: **know triage, know your role, follow the disaster plan, identify human trafficking, and believe the patient’s report.**

</div>

## Big Picture

Emergency care is fast-paced and can change quickly.

Patients may come to the emergency department for:

- Chest pain
- Abdominal pain
- Difficulty breathing
- Injury
- Headache
- Fever
- Generalized pain
- Mental health crisis
- Trauma
- Abuse or unsafe home situation

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

Emergency nursing is about quickly identifying who is unstable, who can wait, and who needs protection or further screening.

</div>

---

# Emergency Department Basics

Emergency departments care for patients across the life span with many different problems.

The ED team may include:

| Team Member | Role |
|---|---|
| Physician | Diagnoses and orders treatment |
| Nurse | Assessment, triage, interventions, monitoring, education |
| PA / CRNP | Provider care, orders, procedures |
| Lab | Bloodwork and diagnostic testing |
| Respiratory therapy | Oxygen, breathing treatments, airway support |
| Techs | Vitals, transport, assistance |
| Medics | Prehospital care and handoff |
| Case manager | Discharge planning/resources |
| Social worker | Safety concerns, placement, abuse, homelessness, resources |

## ED Nurse Training

ED nurses often need specialized training, such as:

- ACLS
- PALS
- Longer ED orientation
- Trauma training
- Some may be trained as SANE nurses

<div class="memory" markdown="1">

SANE = Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner.

</div>

# Emergency Department Patients

ED patients vary constantly.

The ED may be calm one minute and overwhelmed the next.

## Vulnerable Populations in the ED

Vulnerable patients may include:

- Older adults
- Uninsured patients
- Underinsured patients
- Economically disadvantaged patients
- Homeless patients
- Patients with mental health concerns
- Patients experiencing abuse, violence, or trafficking

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

Older adults who come to the ED are more likely to be admitted to the hospital.

</div>

---

# Emergency Department Barriers

## Communication Barriers

Communication may be difficult if the patient has:

- Memory loss
- Acute delirium
- Language barrier
- Missing hearing aids
- Missing glasses
- Fear of the person with them
- Altered mental status
- Severe pain or anxiety

### Nursing Actions

- Use an authorized translator.
- Speak clearly and calmly.
- Face the patient when speaking.
- Provide glasses or hearing aids if available.
- Ask simple, direct questions.
- Get the patient alone if abuse or trafficking is suspected.

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

Do not use family members or visitors as translators when safety, abuse, trafficking, or consent may be involved.

</div>

## Violence as a Barrier

Emergency departments can have heightened emotions because patients and families may be scared, angry, intoxicated, in pain, or grieving.

Nursing priorities:

- Keep yourself safe.
- Know facility safety codes.
- Keep an exit available.
- Call for help early.
- Do not argue with an escalating patient.
- Follow facility policy.

## Space as a Barrier

ED space is limited.

Patients need to be:

- Treated
- Discharged
- Admitted
- Transferred

This matters because overcrowding can delay care for new emergencies.

# Trauma Center Levels

Emergency departments may be ranked by trauma level.

| Trauma Level | Description |
|---|---|
| Level 1 | Regional resource facility; provides total care from prevention through rehabilitation; often large teaching hospitals |
| Level 2 | Community hospital that can care for most injured patients |
| Level 3 | Smaller/rural hospital; focuses on initial stabilization and transfer if needed |
| Level 4 | Rural/remote setting; provides ACLS and transfers after stabilization |

<div class="memory" markdown="1">

Level 1 = highest level of trauma resources.

</div>

# ED Triage

Triage means sorting patients based on illness or injury severity.

In the ED, triage helps decide who needs care first.

The three basic categories are:

- Emergent
- Urgent
- Non-urgent

---

## Emergent

Emergent patients have the **highest priority**.

These patients have an immediate threat to life or limb.

### Examples

| Emergent Finding | Why It Is Priority |
|---|---|
| Respiratory distress | Airway/breathing problem |
| Chest pain with diaphoresis | Possible MI |
| Active hemorrhage | Circulation problem |
| Unstable vital signs | Patient may be deteriorating |
| Severe trauma | Possible life-threatening injury |
| Altered LOC with unstable findings | Neuro/oxygenation concern |

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

Emergent = life-threatening or limb-threatening.

</div>

---

## Urgent

Urgent patients have a serious health problem and need quick treatment, but there is no immediate threat to life.

### Examples

| Urgent Finding | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Severe abdominal pain | Could be serious but may be stable |
| Renal colic | Severe pain but usually not immediate death risk |
| Displaced fracture | Needs treatment but can usually wait short-term |
| Multiple fractures with stable vitals | Serious but not immediately unstable |
| Complex soft tissue injury | Needs repair/evaluation |
| New-onset respiratory infection | Could worsen |
| Pneumonia in older adult | Higher risk for complications |

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

Pneumonia in an older adult is more concerning than a mild cold because older adults can decline quickly.

</div>

---

## Non-Urgent

Non-urgent patients have episodic illness or minor injury.

They can usually wait several hours without fear of becoming unstable.

### Examples

| Non-Urgent Finding | Why It Can Wait |
|---|---|
| Skin rash | Usually not immediately life-threatening |
| Strain or sprain | Stable injury |
| Cold symptoms | Usually minor |
| Simple fracture | Needs care, but not usually immediate life threat |

---

# Triage Quick Chart

| Category | Priority | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Emergent | Highest | Immediate threat to life/limb | Respiratory distress, chest pain with sweating, active bleeding, unstable vitals |
| Urgent | Middle | Serious, needs treatment soon | Severe abdominal pain, renal colic, displaced fracture, pneumonia in older adult |
| Non-urgent | Lowest | Can wait several hours | Rash, sprain, cold, simple fracture |

<div class="memory" markdown="1">

Triage question: **Who is most likely to die or lose a limb if not treated now?**

</div>

# Primary Survey

The primary survey is the first rapid assessment of the injured or unstable patient.

It should be completed quickly, usually within **1 to 2 minutes**, and interventions may happen at the same time.

## ABCDE

| Letter | Meaning | What Nurse Assesses |
|---|---|---|
| A | Airway/cervical spine | Is airway open? Protect C-spine if trauma suspected |
| B | Breathing | Respiratory effort, breath sounds, oxygenation |
| C | Circulation | Pulse, bleeding, perfusion, skin, BP |
| D | Disability | Neuro status, LOC, pupils, movement |
| E | Exposure | Fully assess for injuries while preventing hypothermia |

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

If trauma is suspected, protect the cervical spine while assessing airway.

</div>

## ABC vs CAB

Usually use:

- Airway
- Breathing
- Circulation

Exception:

- If there is **excessive bleeding**, use **CAB**.

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

Massive bleeding can kill quickly. Control severe bleeding first.

</div>

---

# Secondary Survey

The secondary survey is done after immediate life threats are addressed.

It includes:

- Head-to-toe assessment
- Baseline vital signs
- Health history
- Posterior inspection
- Imaging
- Labs
- Specialty consults

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

Primary survey = quick life-threat check.  
Secondary survey = more complete assessment after stabilization.

</div>

# Prevention Levels

## Primary Prevention

Primary prevention means preventing the event from happening.

| Example | Why |
|---|---|
| Driving safety classes | Prevent crashes |
| Speed limit enforcement | Reduce accidents |
| Campaigns against drinking and driving | Prevent impaired driving injuries |

## Secondary Prevention

Secondary prevention means minimizing injury when an event happens.

| Example | Why |
|---|---|
| Seat belt use | Reduces injury in crash |
| Airbags | Decreases trauma severity |
| Car seat use | Protects children |
| Helmet use | Reduces head injury |

## Tertiary Prevention

Tertiary prevention means maximizing recovery after injury occurs.

| Example | Why |
|---|---|
| Emergency response systems | Rapid treatment |
| Medical and surgical care | Treat injuries |
| Rehabilitation services | Restore function |

<div class="memory" markdown="1">

Primary = prevent it.  
Secondary = reduce injury.  
Tertiary = recover after.

</div>

# Human Trafficking

Human trafficking is a rapidly growing criminal activity.

It may involve:

- Forced sex
- Forced labor
- Both forced sex and forced labor

## Possible ED Clues

A victim may present with:

- Older boyfriend
- Travel partner
- Person who answers for them
- History of chronic running away
- Homelessness
- Self-mutilation
- Fearful behavior
- Agitation
- Cowering
- Deferring to the person with them

## Physical and Emotional Findings

| Finding | Possible Concern |
|---|---|
| Special marks or tattoos | Branding/control |
| Poor dentition | Neglect |
| Multiple injuries in different stages | Ongoing abuse |
| Abdominal pain | Physical/sexual trauma or stress |
| Dizziness/fatigue/headaches | Poor health, injury, stress |
| Rashes or sores | Neglect, infection, abuse |
| Addiction | Control mechanism or coping |
| Panic attacks | Trauma response |
| Impulsivity/hostility | Crisis or trauma response |
| Suicidal tendencies | Severe distress/safety risk |

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

Human trafficking victims may not identify themselves as victims. They may be afraid to tell the truth while the controlling person is present.

</div>

## Nursing Action if Trafficking Is Suspected

The nurse should try to get the patient alone **safely**.

Do not confront the suspected trafficker directly.

### Questions to Ask

- “Is there anyone who makes you feel unsafe?”
- “Do you control your own finances?”
- “Are you able to come and go as you please?”
- “Who is the person accompanying you?”
- “Has anyone forced you to do something you did not want to do?”
- “Do you have access to your identification documents?”

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

If abuse or trafficking is suspected, the nurse should separate the patient from the accompanying person in a safe and private way.

</div>

## Human Trafficking Resource

The National Human Trafficking Hotline can be used as a resource.

<div class="memory" markdown="1">

Human trafficking NCLEX clue: controlling companion + fearful patient + inconsistent injuries + cannot speak alone.

</div>

# Emergency Care in a Disaster

A disaster is an event that overwhelms normal resources and requires a coordinated emergency response.

Disasters may be:

- Internal
- External

---

## Internal Disaster

An internal disaster occurs **inside** the healthcare facility or campus and could endanger patients or staff.

### Examples

- Loss of power
- Fire
- Active shooter
- Flooding inside facility
- Utility failure
- Hazardous exposure inside facility

Possible actions:

- Evacuate
- Relocate patients
- Shelter in place
- Activate facility emergency codes
- Follow emergency management plan

---

## External Disaster

An external disaster occurs **outside** the healthcare facility but requires the facility to activate the Emergency Management Plan.

### Examples

| Type | Example |
|---|---|
| Man-made accidental | Manufacturing plant explosion |
| Man-made intentional | Terrorist attack |
| Natural disaster | Tornado |

External disasters may bring many patients to the ED at once.

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

Disaster care focuses on doing the greatest good for the greatest number of people.

</div>

---

# Emergency Management Plan

All facilities should have an Emergency Management Plan, also called an EMP.

The EMP should be:

- Reviewed
- Practiced
- Revised as needed

The plan prepares the facility for credible threats to patient, staff, and community safety.

## EMP May Include

- Staff roles
- Communication plan
- Evacuation plan
- Shelter-in-place plan
- Patient relocation plan
- Supply management
- Security response
- Disaster triage process
- Backup power plans
- Hazardous materials response
- Media/public communication rules

<div class="memory" markdown="1">

Know your role before the disaster happens.

</div>

---

# Emergency Disaster Planning and Response

Important parts of disaster planning include:

| Item | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Joint Commission preparedness standards | Facilities must be prepared |
| Emergency Operations Plan | Guides response |
| Disaster drills | Practice before real event |
| FEMA | Disaster response/support |
| American Red Cross | Shelter, supplies, community support |
| HazMat teams | Hazardous material response |
| PPE | Protect staff and patients |
| Outcome review | Improve future response |
| Therapy/help hotlines | Emotional recovery support |

---

# Hazardous Materials and PPE

HazMat situations may involve chemicals, radiation, biological agents, or unknown substances.

Nursing priorities:

- Do not rush into an unsafe area.
- Use correct PPE.
- Follow decontamination procedures.
- Prevent spread of contamination.
- Remove contaminated clothing if instructed.
- Protect airway, breathing, and circulation.

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

Do not become another victim. Scene safety and PPE come first.

</div>

---

# Disaster Triage

Disaster triage is different from regular ED triage.

In a disaster, resources may be limited.

The goal is:

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

Save the greatest number of people possible.

</div>

## Disaster Triage Color Categories

| Color | Category | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Red | Immediate | Life-threatening but likely to survive with quick care |
| Yellow | Delayed | Serious injury but can wait short-term |
| Green | Minor | Walking wounded |
| Black | Expectant/deceased | Dead or unlikely to survive with available resources |

## Disaster Triage Examples

| Patient | Category |
|---|---|
| Severe bleeding but breathing and treatable | Red |
| Respiratory distress | Red |
| Open fracture with stable vitals | Yellow |
| Burns without airway involvement and stable vitals | Yellow |
| Walking with minor cuts | Green |
| Sprain/minor wound | Green |
| No breathing after airway opened | Black |
| Massive fatal injury | Black |

<div class="memory" markdown="1">

Green patients can walk. Red patients need help now. Black patients are deceased or expected to die with available resources.

</div>

# Crisis Intervention

A crisis happens when a person cannot cope with a stressful or dangerous situation using their normal coping skills.

Crisis situations may include:

- Suicide risk
- Violence risk
- Abuse
- Injury to staff or other patients
- Someone else harming the patient
- Human trafficking
- Assault
- Death or traumatic event
- Disaster response

## Crisis Intervention Priorities

| Priority | Nursing Action |
|---|---|
| Assess risk | Suicide, violence, harm from others |
| Remove threats | Visitors, weapons, unsafe objects |
| Protect patient identity if needed | John/Jane Doe |
| Use 1:1 sitter if needed | Prevent self-harm |
| Stay calm | Helps reduce escalation |
| Follow facility policy | Use safety codes and chain of command |

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

Safety is always first in a crisis.

</div>

---

# Suicide Risk

Assess directly for suicide risk.

Ask:

- “Are you thinking about hurting yourself?”
- “Do you have a plan?”
- “Do you have access to weapons or medications?”
- “Have you tried to hurt yourself before?”
- “Do you feel safe being alone?”

## High-Risk Findings

| Finding | Why It Is Serious |
|---|---|
| Specific plan | Higher risk |
| Access to means | Patient can act on plan |
| Previous attempt | Strong risk factor |
| Giving away belongings | Warning sign |
| Sudden calm after distress | May indicate decision to act |
| Substance use | Decreases impulse control |
| No support system | Less protection |

## Suicide Precautions

- Do not leave actively suicidal patient alone.
- Remove dangerous items.
- Initiate 1:1 observation as ordered.
- Notify provider/crisis team.
- Use safe room if available.
- Document exact patient statements.
- Follow facility policy.

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

Asking about suicide does not put the idea in the patient’s head. It helps identify risk.

</div>

---

# Violence Risk

Assess for:

- Threats
- Yelling
- Pacing
- Clenched fists
- Agitation
- Intoxication
- Paranoia
- Weapon access
- History of violence

## De-escalation

| Do | Avoid |
|---|---|
| Stay calm | Arguing |
| Keep safe distance | Standing too close |
| Keep exit accessible | Blocking doorway |
| Use simple words | Long explanations |
| Offer choices | Power struggles |
| Call for help early | Managing alone |
| Follow facility codes | Ignoring warning signs |

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

If the patient becomes unsafe, call for help and follow facility policy.

</div>

---

# Therapeutic Communication in Crisis

Use calm, simple statements.

Helpful responses:

- “I am here with you.”
- “You are safe right now.”
- “Tell me what happened.”
- “What feels most overwhelming?”
- “Who can we call for support?”
- “Let’s focus on the next step.”

Avoid:

- “Calm down.”
- “Everything happens for a reason.”
- “I know exactly how you feel.”
- “At least it was not worse.”
- “You should be over this.”
- False reassurance

<div class="nclex" markdown="1">

The best response usually acknowledges feelings and encourages the patient to talk.

</div>

---

# Believe the Patient’s Report

<div class="danger" markdown="1">

Believe the patient’s report.

</div>

This is important with:

- Abuse
- Sexual assault
- Human trafficking
- Domestic violence
- Suicidal thoughts
- Threats from others
- Feeling unsafe

Nursing care should be supportive, calm, and nonjudgmental.

Say:

- “I believe you.”
- “You did the right thing telling someone.”
- “You are not in trouble.”
- “We are going to help keep you safe.”

Avoid:

- Blaming questions
- Doubting the patient
- Asking why they did not leave
- Confronting the suspected abuser in front of the patient

# Quick NCLEX Priority Chart

| Situation | First Nursing Action |
|---|---|
| Patient arrives in respiratory distress | Emergent triage; assess airway/breathing |
| Chest pain with diaphoresis | Emergent triage |
| Active hemorrhage | Control bleeding; CAB priority |
| Severe abdominal pain, stable vitals | Urgent |
| Skin rash, stable patient | Non-urgent |
| Trauma patient arrives | Primary survey ABCDE |
| Excessive bleeding present | CAB instead of ABC |
| Suspected trafficking | Safely separate patient from companion |
| Patient says they feel unsafe | Believe report and assess safety |
| Suicidal patient | Ask directly about plan/intent and keep safe |
| Violent patient | Keep distance, call for help, follow policy |
| Internal disaster | Follow facility emergency plan |
| External disaster | Activate EMP and prepare for surge |
| HazMat exposure | PPE/decontamination before treatment |
| Disaster triage | Greatest good for greatest number |
