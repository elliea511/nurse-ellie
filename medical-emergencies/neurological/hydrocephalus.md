# Increased ICP and Hydrocephalus

---

# Increased Intracranial Pressure

## What It Is

Increased intracranial pressure, or increased ICP, means there is too much pressure inside the skull.

Think of the skull as a **rigid box**. It does not stretch like skin.

Inside the skull are:

* Brain tissue
* Blood
* Cerebrospinal fluid, or CSF

If any of these increase, pressure inside the skull rises.

## Causes

Increased ICP can occur from:

* Brain swelling
* Head injury
* Bleeding in the brain
* Tumor
* Stroke
* Infection
* Hydrocephalus
* Excess CSF

---

# Why Increased ICP Is Dangerous

Increased pressure can decrease blood flow and oxygen to the brain.

This can lead to:

* Brain tissue damage
* Seizures
* Herniation
* Respiratory changes
* Death

---

# Normal ICP

| ICP Value                                  | Meaning                    |
| ------------------------------------------ | -------------------------- |
| 0–15 mm Hg                                 | Normal ICP                 |
| 20 mm Hg or higher for more than 5 minutes | Life-threatening emergency |

---

# Recognize Increased ICP

## Earliest Sign

The earliest and most important sign is a **change in level of consciousness**.

This may look like:

* Restlessness
* Confusion
* Drowsiness
* Irritability
* Decreased responsiveness

---

## Other Signs and Symptoms

* Headache
* Nausea and vomiting
* Pupillary changes
* Weakness
* Slurred speech
* Seizures
* Papilledema
* Abnormal posturing

## Late Signs

Late signs include:

* Bradycardia
* Hypertension
* Widened pulse pressure
* Bradypnea
* Cheyne-Stokes respirations
* Severe headache
* Nausea and vomiting
* Fixed and dilated pupils

---

# Cushing’s Triad

Cushing’s triad is a **late sign** of increased ICP.

## Findings

| Finding                | What Happens                                        |
| ---------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Systolic hypertension  | Systolic BP increases                               |
| Widened pulse pressure | Difference between systolic and diastolic BP widens |
| Bradycardia            | Pulse decreases                                     |
| Respiratory depression | Respirations decrease or become abnormal            |

---

# Increased ICP vs Shock

| Finding          | Increased ICP                | Shock            |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------- | ---------------- |
| Blood pressure   | Systolic BP increases        | BP decreases     |
| Pulse            | Decreases                    | Increases        |
| Respirations     | Decrease or become irregular | Increase         |
| Priority concern | Brain pressure/perfusion     | Tissue perfusion |

---

# Level of Consciousness Terms

| Term               | Meaning                                            |
| ------------------ | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Full consciousness | Awake, alert, oriented, interacts with environment |
| Confused           | Lacks ability to think clearly and rapidly         |
| Disoriented        | Does not recognize place or person                 |
| Lethargic          | Awakens easily but has limited responsiveness      |
| Obtunded           | Sleeps and, once aroused, has limited interaction  |
| Stupor             | Requires stimulation to arouse                     |
| Coma               | Vigorous stimulation produces no response          |

---

# Glasgow Coma Scale Basics

You may not need to calculate the full score, but know what the ranges mean.

| GCS Score | Severity             |
| --------- | -------------------- |
| 13–15     | Mild head injury     |
| 9–12      | Moderate head injury |
| 3–8       | Severe head injury   |

# Diagnostic Tests for Increased ICP

## Blood and Urine Tests

* ABGs
* CBC
* Coagulation profile
* Electrolytes
* Serum osmolality
* Urinalysis
* Urine osmolality

## Imaging and Other Tests

* CT scan of the head
* MRI
* EEG

---

# Priority Assessment for Increased ICP

Assess:

* Level of consciousness
* Pupils
* Motor strength
* Speech
* Vital signs
* Respiratory pattern
* Headache
* Vomiting
* Seizure activity
* Intake and output
* Drainage from ears or nose
* Bilateral neuro assessment

## Report Immediately

* Change in LOC
* New confusion
* Unequal pupils
* Fixed or dilated pupils
* New weakness
* Seizure
* Cushing’s triad
* Projectile vomiting
* Worsening headache
* Abnormal posturing
* Fever
* Drainage from ears or nose

---

# Nursing Interventions for Increased ICP

## Positioning

* Place client in Semi-Fowler’s position.
* Keep head in neutral midline position.
* Avoid neck flexion.
* Avoid neck rotation.
* Avoid extreme hip flexion.

### Why

This helps promote venous drainage from the brain.

---

## Safety and Neuro Protection

* Monitor LOC closely.
* Compare assessments bilaterally.
* Monitor vital signs carefully.
* Report changes immediately.
* Treat as spinal injury until ruled out.
* Monitor for halo sign if fluid leaks from ears or nose.
* Keep environment calm.
* Limit unnecessary stimulation.
* Allow rest periods.

---

## Respiratory Care

* Monitor respiratory status.
* Monitor ABGs.
* Maintain oxygenation.
* Keep PaO₂ above 80 mm Hg as ordered to help prevent secondary brain injury.
* Prepare for advanced oxygen therapy if needed.

---

## Fever

Fever should be reported immediately.

Fever may be caused by:

* Infection
* Injury to the hypothalamus

---

# What To Avoid With Increased ICP

| Avoid                 | Why                        |
| --------------------- | -------------------------- |
| Coughing              | Increases pressure         |
| Straining             | Increases pressure         |
| Valsalva maneuver     | Increases pressure         |
| Excessive suctioning  | Can increase ICP           |
| Overstimulation       | Can worsen ICP             |
| Hip flexion           | Reduces venous drainage    |
| Neck flexion/rotation | Reduces venous drainage    |
| Tight collars or ties | Can restrict venous return |

---

# Medications and Treatments for Increased ICP

## Steroids

### Purpose

May decrease inflammation and swelling depending on cause.

---

## Anticonvulsants

Examples:

* Phenytoin, or Dilantin
* Phenobarbital

### Purpose

Used to prevent or treat seizures.

---

## Mannitol

Mannitol is an osmotic diuretic.

### Purpose

Pulls fluid from the brain into the bloodstream so the kidneys can remove it.

### Monitor

* Urine output
* Lung sounds
* Blood pressure
* Electrolytes
* Kidney function
* Signs of dehydration

---

## Furosemide

Furosemide, or Lasix, may be used as a diuretic.

### Monitor

* Potassium
* Blood pressure
* Urine output
* Hydration status

---

## Hypertonic Saline

Hypertonic saline may be used to reduce ICP.

### Monitor

* Sodium level
* Neuro status
* Fluid status
* Lung sounds
* IV site

---

## Opioids

Fentanyl may be used for ventilated clients.

### Purpose

Helps control pain and decrease agitation.

---

## Barbiturate Coma

A barbiturate coma may be used when other interventions are unsuccessful.

---

# Measuring ICP

## Devices

| Device                          | Basic Point                                 |
| ------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Intraventricular catheter       | Placed in ventricle; highest infection risk |
| Subarachnoid bolt or screw      | Measures pressure in one spot only          |
| Epidural sensor/transducer      | Less accurate because it is indirect        |
| Parenchymal fiberoptic catheter | Very accurate and reliable but expensive    |

# Hydrocephalus

## What It Is

Hydrocephalus is a buildup of cerebrospinal fluid, or CSF, in the brain.

This causes increased pressure on brain tissue.

## Basic CSF Flow

CSF normally flows through the ventricles and around the brain and spinal cord.

The brain has ventricles, including:

* Two lateral ventricles
* Third ventricle
* Fourth ventricle

CSF is made in the lateral ventricles and flows through connected passages. It eventually bathes the brain and spinal cord and is reabsorbed.

## What Goes Wrong

Hydrocephalus can occur when CSF flow is blocked, CSF is not absorbed properly, or too much CSF builds up.

A common cause is narrowing or blockage of the aqueduct of Sylvius.

---

# Hydrocephalus in Infants

## Recognize

Infants may show:

* Enlarged head circumference
* Bulging fontanel
* Tense fontanel
* High-pitched or shrill cry
* Poor feeding
* Vomiting
* Irritability
* Sleepiness
* Seizures
* Sunsetting eyes
* Developmental delay

---

# Hydrocephalus in Older Children and Adults

## Recognize

Older children and adults may have:

* Headache
* Impaired vision
* Cognitive difficulties
* Loss of coordination
* Incontinence
* Nausea and vomiting
* Confusion
* Decreased LOC
* Seizures

---

# Hydrocephalus Priority Assessment

Assess:

* Level of consciousness
* Head circumference in infants
* Fontanels in infants
* Feeding tolerance
* Vomiting
* Eye movement
* Pupils
* Seizure activity
* Motor function
* Coordination
* Vital signs

## Report Immediately

* Bulging fontanel
* Rapid increase in head size
* High-pitched cry
* Sunsetting eyes
* Vomiting
* Seizure
* Decreased LOC
* Signs of shunt infection or malfunction

---

# Hydrocephalus Treatment

## Goal

Remove excess fluid from the brain and decrease pressure.

Treatment often includes a surgically placed shunt.

---

# VP Shunt

## What It Is

A ventriculoperitoneal shunt drains CSF from a ventricle in the brain into the peritoneal cavity.

### Pathway

Brain ventricle → tubing under skin → peritoneal cavity

---

# VA Shunt

## What It Is

A ventriculoatrial shunt drains CSF from the brain into the vascular system.

### Pathway

Brain ventricle → tubing under skin → vascular system/heart area

---

# Nursing Care After Shunt Placement

## Priority Care

* Monitor vital signs.
* Monitor neurologic status.
* Assess incision sites.
* Monitor for infection.
* Monitor for pain.
* Administer antibiotics as ordered.
* Administer pain medication as ordered.
* Measure head circumference in infants.
* Assess fontanels in infants.
* Teach family dressing care as ordered.
* Teach family signs of shunt problems.

---

# Shunt Positioning Safety

## Do Not

* Do not place pressure on the shunt side.
* Do not position the infant directly on the side of the shunt unless ordered.

### Why

Pressure can cause:

* Skin breakdown
* Compression of the shunt apparatus
* Shunt problems

---

# Signs of Shunt Malfunction

Recognize:

* High-pitched cry
* Irritability
* Vomiting
* Poor feeding
* Sleepiness
* Bulging fontanel
* Increased head circumference
* Headache
* Seizures
* Decreased LOC
* Return of hydrocephalus symptoms

---

# Signs of Shunt Infection

Recognize:

* Fever
* Redness at incision
* Swelling
* Drainage
* Irritability
* Neck stiffness
* Abdominal pain
* Poor feeding
