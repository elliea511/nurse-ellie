# Poisoning and Ingestions
<div class="note">
Poisoning and ingestion emergencies occur when a harmful substance is swallowed, inhaled, injected, absorbed, or splashed onto the skin or eyes. Nursing care focuses on airway, breathing, circulation, identifying the substance, preventing further absorption, and giving the correct treatment or antidote.
</div>
---
# Big Picture
## Why This Matters
Poisoning is especially common in children.
- About half of all poisonings happen in children **6 years old or younger**
- More than **90%** happen in the home
- Common accidental medication ingestions include **acetaminophen** and **salicylates**
## Priority Nursing Focus
For any poisoning or ingestion, think:
1. **Airway**
2. **Breathing**
3. **Circulation**
4. **Level of consciousness**
5. **Identify the substance**
6. **Prevent further absorption**
7. **Prepare for antidote or supportive care**
<div class="priority">
<strong>NCLEX Priority:</strong> Do not focus on the poison first if the client has airway, breathing, circulation, or neurologic problems. Stabilize the client first.
</div>
---
# General Poisoning Assessment
## Recognize
Poisoning or ingestion may be suspected with:
- Altered mental status
- Nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain
- Respiratory depression
- Respiratory distress
- Seizures
- Burns around the mouth
- Drooling
- Unusual breath odor
- Empty medication bottles
- Chemical containers nearby
- Sudden behavior changes
- Unknown substance exposure
---
## Priority Assessment
Assess:
- Airway patency
- Respiratory rate and effort
- Oxygen saturation
- Heart rate and blood pressure
- Temperature
- Pupils
- Level of consciousness
- Skin findings
- Vomiting or drooling
- Seizure activity
- Time of ingestion
- Amount ingested
- Substance involved, if known
<div class="ati-tip">
<strong>ATI/NCLEX Tip:</strong> If the client is unconscious or has a decreased gag reflex, do not give anything by mouth.
</div>
---
# General Nursing Interventions
## Immediate Actions
- Maintain airway.
- Give oxygen as prescribed.
- Place client on cardiac monitor if indicated.
- Establish IV access.
- Assess vital signs frequently.
- Check blood glucose if altered mental status is present.
- Remove contaminated clothing if skin exposure occurred.
- Save containers, pills, plants, batteries, or substances for identification.
- Contact poison control or provider as directed.
- Prepare for labs and toxicology screening.
<div class="priority">
<strong>Priority:</strong> Bring the container, bottle, battery package, or substance to the emergency department if possible. This helps identify the poison and guide treatment.
</div>
---
# What Not To Do
| Do Not | Why |
| --- | --- |
| Do not induce vomiting unless specifically directed | Can worsen injury or cause aspiration |
| Do not give oral fluids to an unconscious client | Aspiration risk |
| Do not give activated charcoal to a client with decreased LOC unless airway is protected | Aspiration risk |
| Do not neutralize acids with bases or bases with acids | Can create heat and worsen tissue injury |
| Do not delay care while trying to identify the exact substance | Stabilization comes first |
| Do not assume symptoms happen immediately | Some toxins have delayed effects |
---
# Activated Charcoal
## Purpose
Activated charcoal binds some toxins in the GI tract and decreases absorption.
## Priority Assessment Before Giving
Assess:
- Level of consciousness
- Gag reflex
- Airway protection
- Risk for vomiting
- Risk for aspiration
- Type of substance ingested
## Nursing Interventions
- Give only if prescribed or recommended.
- Do not give if the client cannot protect their airway.
- Monitor for vomiting.
- Monitor bowel sounds and elimination.
<div class="danger">
<strong>Important:</strong> Activated charcoal does not work for every ingestion.
</div>
## Not Effective For
Activated charcoal is generally not effective for:
- Alcohols
- Iron
- Lithium
- Heavy metals
- Caustic acids or alkalis
- Hydrocarbons

# Acetaminophen Poisoning
## Source
Acetaminophen is found in many OTC medications.
Examples:
- Tylenol
- Cold and flu medications
- Combination pain relievers
## Recognize
Acetaminophen can be toxic and may cause **hepatotoxicity**.
Early findings may be mild:
- Nausea
- Vomiting
- Sweating
- Malaise
- Abdominal discomfort
Later findings may include:
- Right upper quadrant pain
- Jaundice
- Elevated liver enzymes
- Increased PT/INR
- Confusion
- Signs of liver failure
## Priority Assessment
Assess:
- Time of ingestion
- Amount ingested
- Liver function tests
- Acetaminophen level
- PT/INR
- Mental status
## Treatment / Antidote
- **N-acetylcysteine**
- Also called **Mucomyst**
- IV fluids
- Activated charcoal may be used if appropriate
## Nursing Interventions
- Maintain airway if mental status is decreased.
- Obtain acetaminophen level as ordered.
- Monitor AST and ALT.
- Monitor PT/INR.
- Monitor for signs of liver failure.
- Administer N-acetylcysteine as prescribed.
- Provide IV fluids as prescribed.
<div class="ati-tip">
<strong>ATI/NCLEX Tip:</strong> Acetaminophen overdose can look mild at first but can progress to serious liver damage.
</div>

# Salicylate Poisoning
## Source
Salicylates are found in:
- Aspirin
- Oil of wintergreen
- Adult Pepto-Bismol
- Other OTC medications
## Recognize
Salicylates can cause **respiratory alkalosis** and electrolyte imbalances.
Findings may include:
- Tinnitus
- Nausea and vomiting
- Sweating
- Fever
- Hyperventilation
- Dizziness
- Confusion
- Respiratory alkalosis early
- Metabolic acidosis later
## Priority Assessment
Assess:
- Respiratory rate
- Temperature
- Mental status
- Salicylate level
- ABGs
- Electrolytes
- Kidney function
- Clotting status if bleeding is present
## Treatment
Treatment may include:
- Activated charcoal
- IV fluids
- Sodium bicarbonate
- Vitamin K if clotting issues occur
- Cooling measures if hyperthermic
- Hemodialysis for severe toxicity, if prescribed
## Nursing Interventions
- Monitor respiratory status.
- Monitor ABGs.
- Monitor electrolytes.
- Monitor temperature.
- Monitor bleeding or clotting issues.
- Institute seizure precautions if needed.
- Administer sodium bicarbonate as prescribed.
<div class="ati-tip">
<strong>Recognition Tip:</strong> Tinnitus after aspirin ingestion is a major clue for salicylate toxicity.
</div>
---
# Corrosive Ingestion
## Source
Corrosives are extremely caustic substances.
Examples:
- Drain cleaners
- Toilet cleaners
- Bleach
- Ammonia
- Strong acids
- Strong alkalis
## Recognize
Corrosives can cause:
- Chemical burns
- Splash burns
- Oral burns
- Drooling
- Difficulty swallowing
- Chest pain
- Abdominal pain
- Vomiting
- Airway swelling
- Future gastric problems
<div class="priority">
<strong>Think:</strong> AIRWAY, AIRWAY, AIRWAY.
</div>
## Priority Assessment
Assess:
- Airway swelling
- Respiratory status
- Ability to swallow
- Oral burns
- Drooling
- Stridor
- Pain
- Signs of perforation
## Treatment
Treatment may include:
- IV fluids
- Pain medication
- Steroids
- Antibiotics
- Tube feedings
- Endoscopy if ordered
## Nursing Interventions
- Maintain airway.
- Keep client NPO.
- Do not induce vomiting.
- Do not give activated charcoal unless specifically ordered.
- Do not attempt to neutralize the substance.
- Give IV fluids as prescribed.
- Give pain medication as prescribed.
- Prepare for nutritional support if swallowing is unsafe.
<div class="priority">
<strong>Priority:</strong> Airway swelling can occur with corrosive ingestion. Watch for drooling, stridor, hoarseness, or difficulty swallowing.
</div>
---
# Hydrocarbon Ingestion
## Source
Hydrocarbons are oily substances that can easily enter the lungs if vomited or aspirated.
Examples:
- Gasoline
- Paint thinners
- Lighter fluid
- Turpentine
- Furniture polish
## Recognize
Hydrocarbons can cause **chemical pneumonitis from aspiration**.
Findings may include:
- Vomiting
- Coughing
- Choking
- Gagging
- CNS depression
- Respiratory distress
- Wheezing
- Chemical odor
Severe complications may include:
- Pneumonia
- Necrotizing disease within 24 hours
<div class="priority">
<strong>Think:</strong> AIRWAY, AIRWAY, AIRWAY.
</div>
## Priority Assessment
Assess:
- Airway and breathing
- Lung sounds
- Oxygen saturation
- Coughing or choking
- Vomiting
- Level of consciousness
- Risk for aspiration
## Treatment
Treatment may include:
- Oxygen
- IV fluids
- Respiratory support
- Chest x-ray if ordered
## Nursing Interventions
- Prevent vomiting.
- Do not induce vomiting.
- Keep client calm.
- Monitor respiratory status.
- Give oxygen as prescribed.
- Start IV fluids as prescribed.
- Prepare for chest x-ray if ordered.
<div class="danger">
<strong>Important:</strong> The major danger with hydrocarbons is aspiration and chemical pneumonitis.
</div>

# Lead Poisoning
## Source
Lead can be found in:
- Older home paint
- Painted surfaces
- Dust
- Soil
- Old plumbing
- Some imported toys or ceramics
## Lab Value
Normal serum lead is less than **5 mcg/dL**.
**15 mcg/dL is considered harmful**.
## Recognize
Lead can be found in:
- Blood
- Bone
- Soft tissue
Lead toxicity can affect the:
- CNS
- Gastrointestinal system
- Skeletal system
- Kidneys
Possible findings:
- Developmental delay
- Learning problems
- Irritability
- Abdominal pain
- Constipation
- Fatigue
- Anemia
- Neurologic changes
- Seizures in severe cases
- Renal problems
## Priority Assessment
Assess:
- Blood lead level
- Developmental status
- Neurologic status
- GI symptoms
- Renal function
- Seizure activity
- Home/environmental risk factors
## Treatment / Antidote
Treatment may include chelating agents.
**Chemet**, also called **succimer**, is a chelating agent that binds to lead in the blood and allows it to pass out in the urine.
## Nursing Interventions
- Hydrate the client as prescribed.
- Monitor renal function.
- Monitor urine output.
- Provide vitamins as prescribed.
- Administer anticonvulsants if prescribed for seizures.
- Administer chelating agents as prescribed.
- Remove the source of lead exposure.
- Teach family to check older homes for lead paint.
<div class="ati-tip">
<strong>ATI/NCLEX Tip:</strong> With lead poisoning, think neurologic changes, GI symptoms, renal effects, and chelation therapy.
</div>

# Lithium Button Battery Ingestion
## Source
Lithium button batteries may be found in:
- Toys
- Watches
- Remote controls
- Key fobs
- Hearing aids
- Thermometers
- Small electronics
- Decorations or light-up items
## Why It Is Dangerous
Lithium button batteries can cause tissue necrosis within hours of ingestion.
If the battery gets stuck in the esophagus, it can burn through tissue and cause life-threatening injury.
<div class="priority">
<strong>Priority:</strong> Everything happens fast with button batteries. Treat this as an emergency.
</div>
## Recognize
The client may have:
- Drooling
- Coughing
- Gagging
- Vomiting
- Chest discomfort
- Difficulty swallowing
- Refusal to eat or drink
- Respiratory symptoms
- Abdominal pain
The client may also be asymptomatic at first.
## Priority Assessment
Assess:
- Airway
- Breathing
- Drooling
- Ability to swallow
- Respiratory distress
- Time of ingestion
- Type and size of battery if known
- Whether the battery may be stuck in the esophagus
## Treatment
Treatment may include:
- Immediate emergency evaluation
- X-ray to locate the battery
- Removal if lodged in the esophagus
- Honey in certain cases before arrival to the hospital
<div class="danger">
<strong>Honey Note:</strong> Honey may be recommended in intervals while going to the hospital, but not for children under 12 months old and not if the child cannot swallow, is vomiting, or has decreased level of consciousness. The child still needs emergency evaluation immediately.
</div>
## Nursing Interventions
- Maintain airway.
- Keep client NPO unless honey is specifically appropriate before arrival.
- Do not induce vomiting.
- Prepare for imaging.
- Prepare for urgent removal if battery is in the esophagus.
- Monitor for respiratory distress.
- Monitor for signs of perforation or tissue injury.
<div class="priority">
<strong>NCLEX Priority:</strong> A swallowed button battery is not a "wait and see" situation. The child needs emergency care fast.
</div>

# Poisoning in Children
## Why Children Are High Risk
Children are at higher risk because they:
- Explore with their mouths
- May mistake medications for candy
- Have smaller body size
- Depend on adults for safe storage
- Can access items quickly even when caregivers are careful
## Common Household Risks
- Medications
- OTC medications
- Cleaning products
- Garage chemicals
- Lead paint
- Batteries
- Small electronics
- Toys with unsecured battery compartments
## Nursing Interventions
- Assess airway and breathing first.
- Identify the substance and amount.
- Ask when ingestion occurred.
- Assess for symptoms.
- Contact poison control or provider as directed.
- Teach safe storage and prevention.
---
# Home Safety Teaching
## Medication Safety
Teach caregivers to:
- Keep medications locked up.
- Keep medications up and away from small children.
- Use child-resistant containers.
- Store medications in original containers.
- Avoid calling medicine "candy."
- Keep purses and bags out of reach.
- Dispose of unused medications safely.
## Chemical Safety
Teach caregivers to:
- Lock up cleaning products.
- Keep chemicals in original containers.
- Never mix cleaning chemicals.
- Store chemicals away from food.
- Use child safety locks.
- Keep garage liquids out of reach and locked up.
## Battery Safety
Teach caregivers to:
- Avoid toys with button batteries when possible.
- Make sure battery compartments are secured.
- Store loose batteries out of reach.
- Do not leave batteries on tables, counters, or floors.
- Check remotes, key fobs, thermometers, and toys.
- Seek emergency care immediately if a battery is swallowed.
## Lead Safety
Teach caregivers to:
- Check older homes for lead paint.
- Avoid sanding or scraping old paint without proper safety precautions.
- Report peeling paint in older homes.
- Follow public health recommendations for testing if exposure is suspected.
---
# Poison Control
## When To Contact
Contact poison control or provider for:
- Unknown substance
- Medication overdose
- Chemical ingestion
- Plant ingestion
- Household product ingestion
- Inhaled toxin exposure
- Battery ingestion
## Information To Have Ready
- Client age and weight
- Substance name
- Amount taken
- Time of exposure
- Symptoms
- Medical history
- Current medications
<div class="priority">
<strong>Important:</strong> Do not wait for severe symptoms before seeking help after a suspected poisoning. If the client collapses, has a seizure, has trouble breathing, or cannot be awakened, call emergency services immediately. Poison Control can be reached in the U.S. at 1-800-222-1222.
</div>

# Priority Cues to Recognize
## Airway Problem
Think:
- Corrosive ingestion
- Hydrocarbon aspiration
- Button battery stuck in esophagus
- Drooling
- Stridor
- Respiratory distress
- Decreased level of consciousness
Action:
- Maintain airway.
- Give oxygen as ordered.
- Prepare for emergency airway support if needed.
---
## Aspiration Risk
Think:
- Hydrocarbon ingestion
- Vomiting
- Decreased LOC
- No gag reflex
Action:
- Do not induce vomiting.
- Prevent vomiting if possible.
- Protect airway.
---
## Liver Injury Risk
Think:
- Acetaminophen overdose
- Right upper quadrant pain
- Jaundice
- Increased PT/INR
- Elevated AST/ALT
Action:
- Monitor liver labs.
- Administer N-acetylcysteine as prescribed.
---
## Acid-Base / Electrolyte Problem
Think:
- Salicylate toxicity
- Respiratory alkalosis
- Electrolyte imbalances
- Hyperventilation
Action:
- Monitor ABGs.
- Monitor electrolytes.
- Administer sodium bicarbonate as prescribed.
---
## Renal Problem
Think:
- Lead poisoning
- Chelation therapy
- Decreased urine output
- Abnormal BUN/creatinine
Action:
- Hydrate as prescribed.
- Monitor kidney function.
- Monitor urine output.
