# Poisoning and Ingestions

Poisoning and ingestion emergencies occur when a harmful substance is swallowed, inhaled, injected, absorbed, or splashed onto the skin or eyes.

Nursing care focuses on:

* Airway
* Breathing
* Circulation
* Level of consciousness
* Identifying the substance
* Preventing further absorption
* Giving the correct treatment or antidote

<div class="priority">
<strong>NCLEX Priority:</strong> Stabilize ABCs first. Do not focus on the poison before airway, breathing, circulation, or neurologic problems.
</div>

---

## Big Picture

Poisoning is especially common in children.

| Key Point | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| About half of all poisonings happen in children 6 years old or younger | Children explore with their mouths |
| More than 90% happen in the home | Prevention teaching is important |
| Common accidental medication ingestions include acetaminophen and salicylates | Know the antidotes and complications |

---

## General Poisoning Assessment

### Recognize

- Altered mental status
- Nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain
- Respiratory depression or distress
- Seizures
- Burns around the mouth
- Drooling
- Unusual breath odor
- Empty medication bottles
- Chemical containers nearby
- Sudden behavior changes
- Unknown substance exposure

### Priority Assessment

| Assess | Why |
|---|---|
| Airway patency | Poisoning can cause swelling, aspiration, or decreased LOC |
| Respiratory rate/effort and O₂ sat | Detects respiratory depression or distress |
| Heart rate and blood pressure | Detects shock or dysrhythmias |
| Temperature | Some toxins cause fever or hypothermia |
| Pupils | Can help identify toxidrome |
| Level of consciousness | Determines airway protection |
| Skin findings | Chemical exposure, burns, flushing, sweating |
| Vomiting/drooling | Aspiration or corrosive concern |
| Seizure activity | Neurotoxicity |
| Time, amount, and substance | Guides treatment/antidote |

---

## General Nursing Interventions

| Nursing Action | Why |
|---|---|
| Maintain airway | First priority |
| Give oxygen as prescribed | Supports oxygenation |
| Place on cardiac monitor if indicated | Detects dysrhythmias |
| Establish IV access | Allows meds/fluids |
| Assess vital signs frequently | Detects worsening |
| Check blood glucose if altered LOC | Hypoglycemia can mimic poisoning |
| Remove contaminated clothing if skin exposure occurred | Prevents further absorption |
| Save containers, pills, plants, batteries, or substances | Helps identify the poison |
| Contact poison control/provider as directed | Guides treatment |
| Prepare for labs/toxicology screening | Confirms exposure and organ effects |

---

## What Not To Do

| Do Not | Why |
|---|---|
| Do not induce vomiting unless specifically directed | Can worsen injury or cause aspiration |
| Do not give oral fluids to an unconscious client | Aspiration risk |
| Do not give activated charcoal with decreased LOC unless airway is protected | Aspiration risk |
| Do not neutralize acids with bases or bases with acids | Can create heat and worsen tissue injury |
| Do not delay care while identifying the exact substance | Stabilization comes first |
| Do not assume symptoms happen immediately | Some toxins have delayed effects |

## Activated Charcoal

### Purpose

Activated charcoal binds some toxins in the GI tract and decreases absorption.

### Before Giving

Assess:

* Level of consciousness
* Gag reflex
* Airway protection
* Risk for vomiting
* Risk for aspiration
* Type of substance ingested

### Nursing Care

- Give only if prescribed or recommended
- Do not give if the client cannot protect their airway
- Monitor for vomiting
- Monitor bowel sounds and elimination

### Not Effective For

- Alcohols
- Iron
- Lithium
- Heavy metals
- Caustic acids or alkalis
- Hydrocarbons

## Acetaminophen Poisoning

### Source

Acetaminophen is found in many OTC medications, including Tylenol, cold/flu medications, and combination pain relievers.

### Main Problem

Acetaminophen overdose can cause hepatotoxicity.

Early symptoms may look mild, but serious liver damage can develop later.

### Recognize

| Early Findings | Later Findings |
|---|---|
| Nausea | Right upper quadrant pain |
| Vomiting | Jaundice |
| Sweating | Elevated liver enzymes |
| Malaise | Increased PT/INR |
| Abdominal discomfort | Confusion |
|  | Signs of liver failure |

### Assessment and Treatment

| Priority | What To Do |
|---|---|
| Time and amount ingested | Helps determine risk |
| Acetaminophen level | Confirms toxicity |
| AST/ALT | Monitors liver injury |
| PT/INR | Monitors liver clotting function |
| Mental status | Detects worsening liver failure |
| N-acetylcysteine / Mucomyst | Antidote |
| IV fluids | Supportive care |
| Activated charcoal | May be used if appropriate |

## Salicylate Poisoning

### Source

Salicylates are found in aspirin, oil of wintergreen, adult Pepto-Bismol, and other OTC medications.

### Main Problem

Salicylates can cause respiratory alkalosis early and metabolic acidosis later.

They can also cause electrolyte imbalances and bleeding/clotting issues.

### Recognize

- Tinnitus
- Nausea and vomiting
- Sweating
- Fever
- Hyperventilation
- Dizziness
- Confusion
- Respiratory alkalosis early
- Metabolic acidosis later

### Assessment and Treatment

| Priority | What To Do |
|---|---|
| Respiratory rate | Hyperventilation is common |
| Temperature | Fever can occur |
| Mental status | Detects worsening toxicity |
| Salicylate level | Confirms toxicity |
| ABGs | Tracks acid-base changes |
| Electrolytes | Detects imbalances |
| Kidney function | Guides clearance/safety |
| Activated charcoal | May reduce absorption |
| IV fluids | Supports perfusion/clearance |
| Sodium bicarbonate | Used as prescribed |
| Vitamin K | May be used if clotting issues occur |
| Hemodialysis | For severe toxicity if prescribed |

<div class="nclex">
<strong>Recognition Tip:</strong> Tinnitus after aspirin ingestion is a major clue for salicylate toxicity.
</div>

## Corrosive Ingestion

### Source

Corrosives are extremely caustic substances, such as drain cleaners, toilet cleaners, bleach, ammonia, strong acids, and strong alkalis.

### Main Problem

Corrosives can burn the mouth, throat, airway, esophagus, and stomach.

Airway swelling is the priority concern.

### Recognize

- Chemical burns
- Splash burns
- Oral burns
- Drooling
- Difficulty swallowing
- Chest pain
- Abdominal pain
- Vomiting
- Airway swelling
- Future gastric problems

### Assessment and Treatment

| Priority | Nursing Care |
|---|---|
| Airway swelling, drooling, stridor, hoarseness | Maintain airway and prepare for emergency airway support |
| Respiratory status | Give oxygen as prescribed |
| Ability to swallow | Keep NPO |
| Pain | Give pain medication as prescribed |
| Fluid status | Give IV fluids as prescribed |
| Tissue injury | Prepare for endoscopy if ordered |
| Nutrition needs | Prepare for tube feedings if swallowing is unsafe |
| Infection/inflammation | Steroids or antibiotics may be prescribed |

### Do Not

- Do not induce vomiting
- Do not give activated charcoal unless specifically ordered
- Do not attempt to neutralize the substance

## Hydrocarbon Ingestion

### Source

Hydrocarbons are oily substances such as gasoline, paint thinners, lighter fluid, turpentine, and furniture polish.

### Main Problem

Hydrocarbons can easily enter the lungs if the client vomits or aspirates.

The major danger is aspiration and chemical pneumonitis.

### Recognize

- Vomiting
- Coughing
- Choking
- Gagging
- CNS depression
- Respiratory distress
- Wheezing
- Chemical odor

Severe complications may include pneumonia or necrotizing disease within 24 hours.

### Assessment and Treatment

| Priority | Nursing Care |
|---|---|
| Airway and breathing | Give oxygen as prescribed |
| Lung sounds and O₂ sat | Monitor for chemical pneumonitis |
| Vomiting/choking | Prevent vomiting |
| Level of consciousness | Protect airway |
| Respiratory distress | Prepare respiratory support |
| Possible lung injury | Prepare for chest x-ray if ordered |
| Fluid support | Start IV fluids as prescribed |

### Do Not

- Do not induce vomiting
- Do not give activated charcoal unless specifically ordered

## Lead Poisoning

### Source

Lead can be found in older home paint, painted surfaces, dust, soil, old plumbing, and some imported toys or ceramics.

### Lab Value

| Lead Level | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Less than 5 mcg/dL | Normal |
| 15 mcg/dL | Harmful |

### Main Problem

Lead can affect the CNS, GI system, skeletal system, and kidneys.

### Recognize

| System | Possible Findings |
|---|---|
| CNS | Developmental delay, learning problems, irritability, neurologic changes, seizures in severe cases |
| GI | Abdominal pain, constipation |
| Blood | Anemia, fatigue |
| Renal | Renal problems |

### Treatment / Nursing Care

| Priority | Nursing Care |
|---|---|
| Blood lead level | Confirms exposure |
| Developmental/neuro status | Detects CNS effects |
| GI symptoms | Tracks toxicity |
| Renal function and urine output | Needed with chelation |
| Seizure activity | Anticonvulsants if prescribed |
| Chelation therapy | Succimer/Chemet binds lead so it can pass in urine |
| Hydration | Supports kidney clearance |
| Exposure source | Remove lead source and teach family home safety |

## Lithium Button Battery Ingestion

### Source

Lithium button batteries may be found in toys, watches, remotes, key fobs, hearing aids, thermometers, small electronics, and decorations.

### Why It Is Dangerous

Button batteries can cause tissue necrosis within hours.

If the battery gets stuck in the esophagus, it can burn through tissue and cause life-threatening injury.

### Recognize

- Drooling
- Coughing
- Gagging
- Vomiting
- Chest discomfort
- Difficulty swallowing
- Refusal to eat or drink
- Respiratory symptoms
- Abdominal pain
- May be asymptomatic at first

### Assessment and Treatment

| Priority | Nursing Care |
|---|---|
| Airway and breathing | Maintain airway |
| Drooling/dysphagia | Keep NPO unless honey is specifically appropriate before arrival |
| Time, type, and size of battery | Helps guide urgency |
| Possible esophageal lodging | Prepare for x-ray and urgent removal |
| Respiratory distress | Monitor closely |
| Perforation/tissue injury | Watch for worsening symptoms |
| Vomiting risk | Do not induce vomiting |

### Honey Note

Honey may be recommended in some cases while going to the hospital.

Do not give honey if:

* Child is under 12 months old
* Child cannot swallow
* Child is vomiting
* Child has decreased level of consciousness

The child still needs emergency evaluation immediately.

<div class="priority">
<strong>Priority:</strong> A swallowed button battery is not a “wait and see” situation. Tissue necrosis can happen within hours.
</div>

## Poisoning in Children

### Why Children Are High Risk

Children are at higher risk because they:

* Explore with their mouths
* May mistake medications for candy
* Have smaller body size
* Depend on adults for safe storage
* Can access items quickly even when caregivers are careful

### Common Household Risks

- Medications and OTC medications
- Cleaning products
- Garage chemicals
- Lead paint
- Batteries
- Small electronics
- Toys with unsecured battery compartments

### Nursing Interventions

- Assess airway and breathing first
- Identify the substance and amount
- Ask when ingestion occurred
- Assess for symptoms
- Contact poison control or provider as directed
- Teach safe storage and prevention

---

## Home Safety Teaching

| Category | Teaching |
|---|---|
| Medication safety | Lock meds up, keep up and away, use child-resistant containers, store in original containers, do not call medicine candy, keep purses/bags out of reach, dispose of unused meds safely |
| Chemical safety | Lock cleaning products, keep in original containers, never mix cleaning chemicals, store away from food, use child safety locks |
| Battery safety | Avoid toys with button batteries when possible, secure battery compartments, store loose batteries out of reach, check remotes/key fobs/thermometers/toys |
| Lead safety | Check older homes for lead paint, avoid unsafe sanding/scraping, report peeling paint, follow public health testing recommendations |

---

## Poison Control

### When To Contact

Contact poison control or provider for unknown substance, medication overdose, chemical ingestion, plant ingestion, household product ingestion, inhaled toxin exposure, or battery ingestion.

### Information To Have Ready

- Client age and weight
- Substance name
- Amount taken
- Time of exposure
- Symptoms
- Medical history
- Current medications

If the client collapses, has a seizure, has trouble breathing, or cannot be awakened, call emergency services immediately.

Poison Control in the U.S.:

```text
1-800-222-1222
```

## Antidote / Treatment Quick Chart

| Poison / Toxin | Treatment or Antidote | Key Nursing Point |
|---|---|---|
| Acetaminophen | N-acetylcysteine / Mucomyst | Monitor liver function |
| Salicylates | Activated charcoal, IV fluids, sodium bicarbonate | Watch ABGs and electrolytes |
| Salicylates with clotting issues | Vitamin K may be used | Monitor bleeding/clotting |
| Corrosives | IV fluids, pain meds, airway support, possible steroids/antibiotics/tube feedings | Do not induce vomiting or neutralize |
| Hydrocarbons | Oxygen, IV fluids, respiratory support | Prevent vomiting and aspiration |
| Lead | Succimer / Chemet | Chelates lead so it can pass in urine |
| Button battery | Emergency evaluation, x-ray, possible removal | Can cause necrosis within hours |

## Recognition Quick Chart

| Finding | Think |
|---|---|
| Mild early symptoms + later liver injury | Acetaminophen poisoning |
| Tinnitus + hyperventilation | Salicylate toxicity |
| Burns around mouth + drooling | Corrosive ingestion |
| Coughing/choking after ingestion | Hydrocarbon aspiration |
| Respiratory distress after gasoline/paint thinner ingestion | Hydrocarbon chemical pneumonitis |
| Developmental delay + GI symptoms + older home | Lead poisoning |
| Renal issues + CNS/GI findings | Lead poisoning |
| Small child swallowed battery | Button battery emergency |
| Drooling/dysphagia after battery ingestion | Possible esophageal lodging |

## Priority Cues to Recognize

| Problem | Think | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Airway problem | Corrosive ingestion, hydrocarbon aspiration, battery stuck in esophagus, drooling, stridor, respiratory distress, decreased LOC | Maintain airway, give oxygen, prepare emergency support |
| Aspiration risk | Hydrocarbon ingestion, vomiting, decreased LOC, no gag reflex | Do not induce vomiting; protect airway |
| Liver injury risk | Acetaminophen overdose, RUQ pain, jaundice, increased PT/INR, elevated AST/ALT | Monitor liver labs; give N-acetylcysteine as prescribed |
| Acid-base/electrolyte problem | Salicylate toxicity, respiratory alkalosis, hyperventilation | Monitor ABGs/electrolytes; give sodium bicarbonate as prescribed |
| Renal problem | Lead poisoning, chelation therapy, decreased UOP, abnormal BUN/creatinine | Hydrate as prescribed; monitor kidney function and UOP |

## What Do I Do First?

| Situation | First Priority |
|---|---|
| Any poisoning | ABCs first |
| Decreased LOC | Protect airway; do not give PO |
| Unknown ingestion | Save/bring container and call poison control/provider |
| Acetaminophen overdose | Monitor liver labs and prepare N-acetylcysteine |
| Salicylate toxicity | Monitor ABGs/electrolytes |
| Corrosive ingestion | Airway, NPO, do not induce vomiting |
| Hydrocarbon ingestion | Prevent vomiting/aspiration |
| Lead poisoning | Remove source and prepare chelation if ordered |
| Button battery | Emergency evaluation and x-ray/removal if lodged |
