# ATI PN Pharmacology

<p class="mh-subtitle">High-yield medication safety, toxicities, adverse effects, monitoring, patient teaching, and priority nursing actions</p>

ATI pharmacology questions focus less on memorizing every medication and more on recognizing **high-alert drugs, dangerous adverse effects, toxicities, contraindications, medication interactions, and when the nurse should hold a medication or act immediately.**

This guide is organized by medication purpose and body system so you can focus on the drugs and safety concepts most likely to appear on the ATI PN Pharmacology exam.

<div class="key-findings" markdown="1">
<h3>📋 How ATI Tests Pharmacology</h3>

* Recognize the medication from its name, class, use, or suffix.
* Identify the most dangerous adverse effect.
* Know which assessment or laboratory value must be monitored.
* Determine when to hold the medication and notify the provider.
* Recognize toxicity before it becomes life-threatening.
* Know the antidote or reversal medication when one exists.
* Identify contraindications, allergies, and dangerous interactions.
* Choose the safest nursing action using ABCs, safety, and priority frameworks.

</div>

<div class="mh-col" markdown="1">

<div class="report-now" markdown="1">
<h3>Report Immediately</h3>

* Respiratory depression or oxygen desaturation
* Severe bradycardia or hypotension
* Chest pain, dysrhythmia, or syncope
* Fever, sore throat, or signs of agranulocytosis
* New rash, facial swelling, wheezing, or anaphylaxis
* Severe abdominal pain suggesting pancreatitis or liver injury
* Confusion, ataxia, seizures, or worsening tremors
* Markedly decreased urine output
* Unexpected bleeding or black stools
* Signs of medication toxicity

</div>

<div class="nclex-tip" markdown="1">
<h3>ATI Tip</h3>

When several adverse effects are listed, choose the finding that threatens the **airway, breathing, circulation, cardiac rhythm, neurologic status, or organ function** first.

</div>

<div class="mh-table-card" markdown="1">

<h3>Four Questions to Ask for Every Medication</h3>

| Question                        | What You Need to Know                                    |
| ------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| **What does it do?**            | Main use or therapeutic effect                           |
| **What is the biggest danger?** | Priority adverse effect or toxicity                      |
| **What should I monitor?**      | Vital signs, labs, intake/output, or assessment findings |
| **When should I hold it?**      | Unsafe finding that requires nursing action              |

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">
<h3>Do Not Miss</h3>

* Do not memorize every minor adverse effect.
* Focus on the finding that could seriously harm the patient.
* Know which medications require pulse, blood pressure, glucose, electrolyte, kidney, liver, or blood-count monitoring.
* Learn common class patterns instead of treating every drug as unrelated.
* Separate expected effects from findings that require immediate action.
* Remember that the correct medication action can still be wrong if performed at the wrong time.

<div class="mh-table-card" markdown="1">

<h3>Common ATI Medication Patterns</h3>

| Medication Pattern          | Think                                       |
| --------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| **Respiratory depression**  | Opioids, sedatives, CNS depressants         |
| **Bradycardia**             | Beta blockers, digoxin                      |
| **Hyperkalemia**            | ACE inhibitors, potassium-sparing diuretics |
| **Hypokalemia**             | Loop and thiazide diuretics, amphotericin B |
| **Bleeding**                | Anticoagulants and antiplatelets            |
| **Nephrotoxicity**          | Aminoglycosides, amphotericin B             |
| **Ototoxicity**             | Aminoglycosides                             |
| **Agranulocytosis**         | Clozapine, methimazole, propylthiouracil    |
| **Pancreatitis**            | Valproic acid, exenatide                    |
| **Liver injury**            | Valproic acid, isoniazid, methotrexate      |
| **Metabolic syndrome**      | Second-generation antipsychotics            |
| **Serotonin syndrome**      | Multiple serotonergic medications           |
| **Rebound congestion**      | Prolonged topical nasal decongestant use    |
| **Orthostatic hypotension** | Nitroglycerin, antihypertensives, opioids   |

<div class="mh-columns" markdown="1">

<div class="quick-review" markdown="1">
<h3>Priority Framework</h3>

* **ABCs:** Respiratory depression and severe hypotension come first.
* **Safety:** Prevent falls, bleeding, seizures, aspiration, and medication errors.
* **Acute before chronic:** New toxicity is more urgent than an expected long-term effect.
* **Unexpected before expected:** A dangerous reaction takes priority over a common side effect.
* **Assess before acting:** Unless the client has an immediate life-threatening reaction requiring intervention.

</div>

<div class="do-not-miss" markdown="1">
<h3>Never Do This</h3>

* Never give potassium by IV push.
* Never crush enteric-coated or extended-release medications.
* Never give benzathine penicillin intravenously.
* Never leave controlled substances unattended.
* Never continue a blood transfusion when a reaction is suspected.
* Never document that an incident report was completed in the medical record.
* Never give live vaccines during pregnancy.
* Never use a maintenance medication as a rescue medication unless specifically prescribed for that purpose.

</div>
