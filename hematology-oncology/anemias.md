# Anemias

---

## Quick Overview

Anemia happens when the body does not have enough healthy RBCs or hemoglobin to carry oxygen effectively.

There are three main causes:

| Cause                    | What Happens                                        | Examples                                                                   |
| ------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Blood loss               | RBCs are lost faster than the body can replace them | Hemorrhage, GI bleed                                                       |
| Hemolytic                | RBCs are destroyed too quickly                      | Sickle cell, thalassemia, G6PD deficiency, autoimmune hemolytic anemia     |
| Manufacturing deficiency | The body does not make enough healthy RBCs          | Iron deficiency, aplastic anemia, pernicious anemia, folic acid deficiency |

---

## Normal Lab Values to Memorize

| Lab         | Normal Range        |
| ----------- | ------------------- |
| Hgb, male   | 14–18 g/dL          |
| Hgb, female | 12–16 g/dL          |
| Hct, male   | 40–50%              |
| Hct, female | 35–45%              |
| WBC         | 5,000–10,000/mm³    |
| Platelets   | 150,000–400,000/mm³ |

# Iron Deficiency Anemia

Most common type of anemia.

## What Is Happening

Iron is needed to make hemoglobin. Hemoglobin carries oxygen on RBCs.

In iron deficiency anemia:

* Iron stores are depleted first.
* Then hemoglobin production decreases.
* Less hemoglobin means less oxygen is delivered to tissues.
* This causes fatigue, weakness, pallor, shortness of breath, and tissue hypoxia.

About two-thirds of the body’s iron is stored in hemoglobin. The remaining one-third is stored in the bone marrow, spleen, liver, and muscle.

## Common Causes and Risk Factors

| Risk Factor                    | Why It Matters                                                                                   |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Female sex                     | Menstruation causes expected blood loss; the body should compensate but sometimes cannot keep up |
| Chronic blood loss             | GI bleed, especially occult bleeding, can slowly deplete iron                                    |
| Ulcerative colitis             | Body does not plan for this blood loss, so compensation may not occur                            |
| Poor diet or malnutrition      | Not enough dietary iron intake                                                                   |
| Pregnancy                      | Increased iron demand                                                                            |
| Toddlers with milk-heavy diets | Milk blocks iron absorption                                                                      |
| Post-surgical blood loss       | Blood loss can exceed replacement                                                                |

## Expected Findings

* Fatigue
* Weakness
* Pallor of skin and mucous membranes
* Cold sensitivity
* Palpitations

  * Racing
  * Fluttering
  * Skipping heartbeat
* Dizziness
* Brittle hair and nails
* Cheilosis

  * Fissures at corners of the mouth
* Tachycardia
* Tachypnea
* Shortness of breath

## Diagnostics

| Test                   | Expected Result or Purpose      |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------- |
| CBC                    | Low RBC, low Hgb, low Hct       |
| RBC indices            | Microcytic and hypochromic RBCs |
| Serum iron             | Low                             |
| Ferritin               | Low                             |
| TIBC                   | High                            |
| Stool for occult blood | Checks for hidden GI bleeding   |

Iron supplements can raise Hgb about 2 g/dL in 4 weeks.

## Nursing Care

* Assess for source of blood loss.
* Monitor Hgb and Hct.
* Promote rest.
* Encourage activity as tolerated.
* Monitor oxygen saturation.
* Administer iron as ordered.
* Use fall precautions if dizzy.
* Assess for hidden GI bleeding.

## Iron Administration

### Oral Iron

Give oral iron:

* Between meals for best absorption.
* With meals if GI upset occurs.
* With vitamin C to increase absorption.
* Through a straw if liquid.

Expected effects:

* Black or dark green stools.
* Constipation.
* Nausea.
* GI upset.
* Metallic taste.

Black stools are expected and can show that the patient is taking the medication.

### IM Iron

Use the Z-track method.

| Step                                            | Rationale                                         |
| ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Draw up iron, then add 0.25 mL air to syringe   | Air clears needle and seals drug in muscle        |
| Change needle before injection                  | Removes residue that causes stinging and staining |
| Use dorsal gluteal site                         | Manufacturer still recommends this site for iron  |
| Pull tissue to the side                         | Creates Z-track path                              |
| Inject medication, then follow with 0.25 mL air | Clears the needle of drug                         |
| Withdraw needle and release skin                | Skin and muscle channels no longer line up        |
| Do not massage                                  | Prevents medication from leaking into tissue      |
| Aspirate first                                  | Ensures needle is not in a vein                   |

Why Z-track matters:

* Prevents iron from leaking into subcutaneous tissue.
* Prevents stinging.
* Prevents skin staining.

### IV Iron

Used in severe cases.

Monitor for:

* Allergic reaction
* Anaphylaxis
* Hypotension
* Flushing
* Dizziness
* Nausea

IV iron works fastest but has the highest risk for serious reaction.

<div class="note">
<strong>NCLEX Tip:</strong> IV iron requires epinephrine and resuscitation equipment nearby because of anaphylaxis risk. A test dose may be given first, and the patient may be observed for 15–30 minutes before the full infusion.
</div>

## How Patients May Feel After Iron Administration

| Route | Speed of Relief                | Common Effects                                                | Major Concern                                  |
| ----- | ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Oral  | Slowest, usually weeks         | GI upset, constipation, black stools, metallic taste          | Poor tolerance due to GI side effects          |
| IM    | Moderate, days to weeks        | Injection site pain, soreness, possible staining              | Skin staining if Z-track is not done correctly |
| IV    | Fastest, sometimes 24–48 hours | Flushing, warmth, headache, dizziness, nausea, metallic taste | Anaphylaxis and hypotension                    |

Whether an LPN can administer IV iron depends on state scope of practice and facility policy.

## Diet Teaching

Best iron sources:

* Red meat
* Organ meat
* Egg yolks
* Green leafy vegetables

  * Spinach
  * Broccoli
* Kidney beans
* Dried beans
* Raisins
* Enriched or fortified breads and cereals

Even if something says fortified or enriched, it does not always mean it contains a large amount of iron. Other sources are usually higher in iron.

## Dietary Iron Sources

| Type              | Absorption           | Notes                                     |
| ----------------- | -------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Heme iron         | 15–35%               | Animal sources; best absorbed             |
| Non-heme iron     | 2–20%                | Plant sources; affected more by blockers  |
| Vegetarian intake | Needs about 80% more | Needed to compensate for lower absorption |

### Great Heme Sources

* Chicken or beef liver

  * Highest available source, about 12.8 mg per 3.5 oz.
* Oysters
* Clams
* Lean beef
* Dark turkey meat

### Good Plant Sources

Pair plant sources with vitamin C.

* White beans
* Lentils
* Kidney beans
* Iron-fortified cereal

  * Up to 18 mg per cup; check label
* Cooked spinach
* Tofu
* Edamame

### Okay Sources

* Egg yolk
* Raisins
* Dried apricots
* Peas
* Broccoli
* Enriched bread or pasta
* Pumpkin seeds
* Quinoa
* Blackstrap molasses
* Dark chocolate, 70% or higher

## Enhancers and Blockers

| Enhances Iron Absorption     | Blocks Iron Absorption  |
| ---------------------------- | ----------------------- |
| Vitamin C                    | Milk and dairy products |
| Empty stomach                | Antacids                |
| Acidic foods                 | Calcium supplements     |
| Heme iron with non-heme iron | Coffee and tea          |
| Cooking in cast iron         | Whole wheat bran        |

Vitamin C can boost absorption by about 7 times.

<div class="note">
<strong>NCLEX Meal Logic:</strong> Pick the meal with an iron source plus a vitamin C source and no milk. Scrambled eggs and orange juice is the classic correct answer. Milk in an answer choice is usually a red flag because it blocks iron absorption.
</div>

<details>
<summary>Fecal Occult Blood Test Notes</summary>

A fecal occult blood test checks for hidden blood in the stool. This matters in iron deficiency anemia because the patient may have a hidden GI bleed.

## How to Collect

1. Patient defecates into a specimen collection hat or bedpan.
2. Use the wooden applicator to collect stool from two different areas.
3. Smear one sample on Box A.
4. Smear the second sample on Box B.
5. Close the flap.
6. Send promptly or refrigerate if needed.
7. Apply developer solution to the back of the card.
8. Blue color means positive for occult blood.

## Avoid for 3 Days Before Collection

| Avoid                       | Why                           |
| --------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Red meat                    | False positive                |
| NSAIDs or aspirin           | GI irritation, false positive |
| Large amounts of vitamin C  | False negative                |
| Beets, turnips, horseradish | False positive                |

## Label With

* Patient full name.
* Date of birth.
* Date collected.
* Time collected.
* Medical record number.
* Collector’s name or initials.
* Ordering provider, if required.

## Key Nursing Notes

* Label the specimen container or guaiac card itself.
* Do not only label the bag.
* Put the labeled card or container into a biohazard bag.
* Put lab requisition slip in the outside pocket, not inside with the specimen.
* Do not collect from toilet bowl water.
* If patient is incontinent or has a colostomy, collect directly from stool passed.
* Do not collect from the bag or pouch lining.
* A digital rectal exam sample is not acceptable because tissue trauma can cause false positives.
* If 3 separate samples are ordered, label each card with date and sample number.
* Refrigerate if not sending immediately.
* Do not freeze.

## NCLEX Tip

If a patient is taking aspirin or NSAIDs, the result may be falsely positive.

A positive FOBT in an anemic patient should prompt a GI workup.

</details>

## Complications

* Heart failure from chronic anemia.
* Severe fatigue and decreased quality of life.
* Poor wound healing.
* Developmental delays in children if prolonged.

## Interprofessional Care

* Primary care provider or hematologist.
* Dietitian.
* GI specialist if occult bleeding is suspected.

# Pernicious Anemia

Vitamin B12 deficiency.
Megaloblastic anemia.

## What Is Happening

Intrinsic factor is produced by gastric parietal cells. Intrinsic factor is required to absorb vitamin B12 in the terminal ileum.

In pernicious anemia:

* The patient lacks intrinsic factor.
* Vitamin B12 cannot be absorbed properly.
* RBCs become fewer, larger, and abnormal.
* This causes megaloblastic anemia.
* Vitamin B12 is also needed for the nervous system.
* Neurological symptoms can develop.

Most common cause in the United States:

* Lack of intrinsic factor due to autoimmune destruction of parietal cells.

Nutritional cause is possible in strict vegans, but unless the question specifically says nutritional, assume lack of intrinsic factor.

Pernicious anemia is a type of autoimmune disorder.

## Risk Factors

* Autoimmune history.
* Gastrectomy or gastric bypass.
* Strict vegetarianism.

  * No meat, dairy, eggs, or fish.
* Older adults.
* Northern European ancestry.

## Diagnostics

| Test             | Finding or Purpose                       |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| CBC              | Decreased RBC                            |
| MCV              | Elevated, macrocytic/megaloblastic cells |
| Serum B12        | Low                                      |
| Gastric analysis | Absence of intrinsic factor              |
| Schilling test   | Determines whether B12 is absorbed       |

## Expected Findings

| System      | Findings                                                                       |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Hematologic | Pallor, fatigue, weakness, jaundice                                            |
| Neurologic  | Paresthesia in hands and feet, unsteady gait, paralysis, depression, psychosis |
| GI/Oral     | Sore mouth, beefy red smooth tongue, glossitis                                 |
| General     | Weight loss                                                                    |

<div class="note">
<strong>NCLEX Tip:</strong> Beefy red tongue, also called glossitis, is a hallmark early sign. Neurological and psychiatric symptoms can mimic psych disorders, so B12 deficiency must be considered.
</div>

## Safety Considerations

* Fall precautions due to unsteady gait and paresthesia.
* Safety education for neuropathy.
* Good oral hygiene because of mouth and tongue changes.

## Nursing Care

* Administer B12 injections as ordered.
* Monitor neurological status.
* Monitor gait.
* Promote oral hygiene.
* Assess for depression or cognitive changes.
* Monitor CBC.

## Medications

### Cyanocobalamin, Vitamin B12

* Route:

  * IM injection.
  * Not oral if caused by lack of intrinsic factor.
* Schedule:

  * Weekly injections initially.
  * Then monthly injections for life.
* Severe anemia:

  * Blood transfusion may be needed.
* Nasal spray, Calomist:

  * Alternative for maintenance.

<div class="note">
<strong>NCLEX Tip:</strong> If a patient says, “I’ll take my B12 pill every day,” this indicates a need for more teaching. Pernicious anemia caused by lack of intrinsic factor requires injections because the gut cannot absorb B12.
</div>

## Client Education

* Condition is not curable if caused by lack of intrinsic factor.
* Treatment is lifelong.
* Monthly injections are required forever.
* Family members can be taught to give injections.
* Report leg pain or worsening numbness.
* Vitamin B12 is water soluble.

  * Too much can cause radioactive green/yellow urine.
* Eat foods with iron, vitamin C, and folic acid to support RBC production.
* Dietary B12 sources:

  * Meat
  * Fish
  * Eggs
  * Dairy
  * Fortified soymilk

<details>
<summary>Schilling Test Explained</summary>

The Schilling test answers this question:

Is the patient failing to absorb B12 because they lack intrinsic factor, or for another reason?

It is a diagnostic test, not a treatment.

## Basic Logic

If B12 is absorbed normally:

* It enters the blood.
* Kidneys filter the excess.
* It appears in the urine.

If B12 is not absorbed:

* It never enters the blood.
* It passes out through stool.
* Little or none appears in the urine.

## Stage 1

| Step | What Happens                                                   | Why                                                          |
| ---- | -------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| 1    | Patient fasts overnight                                        | Clean baseline                                               |
| 2    | Patient receives radioactive B12 by mouth                      | This is what is tracked                                      |
| 3    | Patient receives large IM dose of regular B12 within 1–2 hours | Saturates B12 stores so radioactive B12 is excreted in urine |
| 4    | 24-hour urine collection begins                                | Captures absorbed radioactive B12                            |
| 5    | Urine is analyzed                                              | Determines whether B12 was absorbed                          |

## Results

| Result                                             | Meaning                                                            |
| -------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Radioactive B12 found in urine, greater than 8–10% | B12 was absorbed; likely dietary deficiency, not pernicious anemia |
| Little to no B12 in urine, less than 8%            | B12 was not absorbed; GI absorption problem                        |

## Stage 2

If Stage 1 is abnormal, the test is repeated with intrinsic factor added.

| Stage 2 Result           | Meaning                                                                                       |
| ------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| B12 now appears in urine | Confirms pernicious anemia due to lack of intrinsic factor                                    |
| B12 still absent         | Problem may be malabsorption syndrome, bacterial overgrowth, or ileal disease such as Crohn’s |

## Nursing Responsibility

The nurse’s main job is proper 24-hour urine collection.

Steps:

1. Have patient void at start time.
2. Discard first specimen.
3. Collect every void for 24 hours.
4. At the end time, have patient void.
5. Keep the final specimen.
6. Keep specimen on ice or refrigerated.
7. Label properly.
8. If any specimen is missed, restart the test.
9. Do not freeze specimen.

## NCLEX Bottom Line

First void equals discard.
Last void equals keep.
Miss one specimen equals restart.

</details>

## Complications

* Permanent neurological damage if untreated.
* Subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord.
* Gastric cancer from long-term autoimmune gastritis.
* Psychosis or dementia mimicry.

## Interprofessional Care

* Hematologist.
* Neurologist if significant neuropathy is present.
* Dietitian if nutritional component is present.
* Mental health support if psychiatric symptoms are present.

# Folic Acid Deficiency Anemia

Megaloblastic anemia.

## What Is Happening

Folic acid is essential for DNA synthesis and normal RBC production.

In folic acid deficiency:

* RBCs become large and dysfunctional.
* This causes megaloblastic anemia.
* Bone marrow changes are similar to B12 deficiency.
* The key difference is that folic acid deficiency does not cause neurological symptoms.

## Risk Factors

* Poor nutrition.

  * Most common cause.
* Alcoholism.
* Malabsorption syndromes.
* Medications:

  * Anticonvulsants, such as phenytoin.
  * Oral contraceptives.
* Pregnancy due to increased folate demand.

## Diagnostics

| Test         | Finding                                              |
| ------------ | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| CBC          | Macrocytic/megaloblastic RBCs                        |
| Serum folate | Low                                                  |
| B12 level    | Normal; used to differentiate from pernicious anemia |

## Expected Findings

* Fatigue.
* Pallor.
* Weakness.
* Glossitis possible.
* No paresthesia.
* No neurological symptoms.
* Slow, compensated onset.
* Caucasians may develop lemon-yellow skin from pallor plus mild jaundice.

Glossitis means inflammation of the tongue. It can cause swelling, bright red color, smooth glossy texture, and trouble chewing, swallowing, or speaking.

## Nursing Care

* Give folic acid supplements.
* Provide dietary counseling.
* Review medications that interfere with folate absorption.
* Monitor CBC.
* Use fall precautions if fatigued or dizzy.

## Medications

* Folic acid supplements:

  * Oral or parenteral.
* Especially important in pregnancy:

  * Helps prevent neural tube defects.
* If medication is the cause:

  * Medication may need to be adjusted.
  * Patient may need supplementation.

## Client Education

Dietary sources of folic acid:

* Green leafy vegetables.
* Spinach.
* Broccoli.
* Liver.
* Yeast.
* Dried beans.
* Nuts.
* Citrus fruits.
* Enriched cereals and breads.

## Complications

* Neural tube defects if deficient during pregnancy.
* Heart failure from severe anemia.
* Same megaloblastic complications as B12 deficiency, except without neurological complications.

## Interprofessional Care

* OB/GYN for pregnant patients.
* Dietitian.
* Pharmacist to review medication interactions.

## B12 vs. Folic Acid Quick Compare

| Feature                           | Pernicious Anemia, B12   | Folic Acid Deficiency        |
| --------------------------------- | ------------------------ | ---------------------------- |
| RBC type                          | Megaloblastic            | Megaloblastic                |
| Neurological symptoms             | Yes                      | No                           |
| Glossitis                         | Yes                      | Possible                     |
| Common cause                      | Lack of intrinsic factor | Poor nutrition or alcoholism |
| Treatment                         | IM injection, lifelong   | Oral supplement              |
| Caused by OCPs or anticonvulsants | No                       | Yes                          |

# Aplastic Anemia

## What Is Happening

Aplastic anemia is bone marrow failure.

The marrow stops producing all blood cell lines, causing pancytopenia.

| Cell Line Decreased | Result                  |
| ------------------- | ----------------------- |
| RBCs                | Anemia                  |
| WBCs                | Immunocompromised state |
| Platelets           | Bleeding risk           |

This is life-threatening.

## Risk Factors

* Long-term radiation exposure.
* Toxic chemical exposure.
* Drug toxicity.
* Medication error.
* Chemotherapy.
* Viral infections.
* Unknown cause in about 50% of cases.

  * Possibly viral.

## Diagnostics

| Test                          | Finding                             |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| CBC                           | Decreased RBCs, WBCs, and platelets |
| Bone marrow aspiration/biopsy | Definitive diagnosis                |
| Bone marrow result            | Hypocellular or fatty marrow        |

## Expected Findings

| Problem                     | Findings                                |
| --------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Anemia from low RBCs        | Fatigue, pallor, weakness               |
| Infection from low WBCs     | Fever, frequent illness                 |
| Bleeding from low platelets | Petechiae, bruising, prolonged bleeding |

## Safety Considerations

* Neutropenic or reverse isolation precautions.
* Bleeding precautions.
* Soft toothbrush.
* Electric razor.
* Avoid invasive procedures when possible.
* No NSAIDs.
* Fall precautions.
* Avoid crowds and sick contacts.
* No fresh fruits or vegetables if on neutropenic diet.

  * Institutional protocol varies.

## Nursing Care

* Monitor CBC closely.
* Assess for infection.
* Assess for bleeding.
* Implement neutropenic precautions.
* Implement bleeding precautions.
* Provide supportive care.
* Identify and remove the cause if known.

## Medications and Treatments

| Treatment                  | Purpose                                 |
| -------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Blood transfusions         | Replace RBCs and platelets as needed    |
| Immunosuppressive therapy  | Used if autoimmune component is present |
| Colony-stimulating factors | Stimulate marrow production             |
| Epoetin alfa               | Stimulates RBC production               |
| Bone marrow transplant     | Potential cure depending on cause       |

### Epoetin Alfa Notes

* Given subcutaneously.
* Administer in fat tissue.
* Stimulates RBC production.
* Very expensive.
* Must be handled carefully.
* Good alternative for Jehovah’s Witness patients.
* Document all refusal of blood products.

## Blood Transfusion Notes for Aplastic Anemia

* Type and screen required.
* Normal saline only with blood products.
* Informed consent required.

## Complications

* Severe infection or sepsis.

  * Most dangerous complication.
* Uncontrolled hemorrhage.
* Death.

## Interprofessional Care

* Hematologist.
* Infectious disease if infection develops.
* Transplant team if bone marrow transplant is indicated.
* Social work for treatment support.

<div class="note">
<strong>NCLEX Tip:</strong> Aplastic anemia equals pancytopenia. If RBCs, WBCs, and platelets are all decreased, think aplastic anemia. Priority concerns are infection, then bleeding, then anemia.
</div>

# Sickle Cell Anemia

Hemolytic anemia.

## What Is Happening

Sickle cell anemia is caused by abnormal hemoglobin.

* Normal hemoglobin is Hgb A.
* Sickle cell hemoglobin is Hgb S.
* RBCs become sickle or crescent shaped when hypoxic.
* Sickled cells do not move through blood vessels easily.
* This causes clumping, thrombosis, vaso-occlusion, and ischemia.
* RBCs break down too early, causing chronic hemolytic anemia.

Normal RBC lifespan:

* 120 days.

Sickle cell RBC lifespan:

* 1–3 weeks.

RBC breakdown causes excess bilirubin, which can lead to:

* Jaundice.
* Icterus.
* Gallstones, often by age 20.
* Enlarged liver from increased bilirubin processing.

## Genetics

Sickle cell is autosomal recessive.

When both parents are carriers:

| Outcome                       | Probability |
| ----------------------------- | ----------- |
| No sickle gene, not a carrier | 25%         |
| Carrier, sickle cell trait    | 50%         |
| Sickle cell disease           | 25%         |

Important:

* Odds reset with every pregnancy.
* Having one affected child does not change the odds for the next pregnancy.

## Most Prevalent In

* African ancestry.
* Middle Eastern ancestry.
* Mediterranean ancestry.
* Southeast Asian ancestry.
* About 10% of African Americans carry the gene.

## Pediatric Note

Babies are born with high fetal hemoglobin, which protects from sickling.

Symptoms often appear in toddler years as fetal hemoglobin decreases and demand increases.

## Crisis Triggers

| Trigger                | Why It Matters                                      |
| ---------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Dehydration            | Number one most common trigger                      |
| Infection or illness   | Fever can cause dehydration                         |
| Cold exposure          | Vasoconstriction worsens sickling                   |
| High altitude          | Low oxygen tension triggers sickling                |
| Significant blood loss | Surgery, trauma, GI bleed                           |
| Stress                 | Physical or psychological stress can trigger crisis |
| Intense exercise       | Heat, dehydration, and oxygen demand                |

## Diagnostics

| Test                         | Purpose                                                 |
| ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Hemoglobin electrophoresis   | Definitive; distinguishes trait from disease            |
| Sickledex or Dithionite test | Screening only; does not distinguish trait from disease |
| Newborn screening            | Required in all U.S. states                             |
| CBC                          | Low Hgb/Hct, elevated reticulocytes                     |
| Bilirubin                    | Elevated from hemolysis                                 |
| Chest X-ray                  | Used if acute chest syndrome is suspected               |

## Expected Findings

| System         | Findings                                                               |
| -------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Pain           | Severe bone/joint pain, abdominal pain                                 |
| Hematologic    | Pallor, fatigue, jaundice, icterus                                     |
| Vascular       | Decreased pedal pulses, leg ulcers, stroke-like symptoms               |
| Respiratory    | Dyspnea, decreased oxygen saturation                                   |
| GI             | Abdominal pain that can mimic appendicitis, enlarged liver, gallstones |
| Renal          | Kidney damage                                                          |
| Ophthalmologic | Vision changes, blindness risk                                         |
| Pediatric      | Dactylitis, sausage fingers/toes, growth delays                        |

## Safety Considerations

* Hydration is priority number one during crisis.
* Avoid high altitudes.
* Avoid cold water and cold environments.
* Avoid smoking.
* Do not give aspirin.
* Prevent infection.
* Keep vaccinations current.
* Pneumococcal vaccine is especially important.
* If dactylitis is present, elevate hands and feet to promote blood flow.

## Nursing Care

* Give IV fluids.

  * Priority number one.
* Administer oxygen.
* Manage pain with opioids as ordered.
* Monitor respiratory status.
* Watch for acute chest syndrome.
* Monitor CBC, bilirubin, and oxygen saturation.
* Give folic acid to support RBC production.
* Do not give iron supplements.

## Medications

| Medication               | Purpose                      | Notes                                  |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| IV fluids                | Reverse dehydration          | Priority in crisis                     |
| Morphine or opioids      | Pain relief and vasodilation | Preferred for crisis pain              |
| Folic acid               | Supports RBC production      | Helps with anemia                      |
| Hydroxyurea              | Increases fetal hemoglobin   | Reduces sickling and transfusion need  |
| Prophylactic antibiotics | Prevent infection            | Especially in children                 |
| Blood transfusions       | Replace sickled cells        | Used in severe anemia or crisis        |
| Vaccines                 | Prevent infection            | Pneumococcal, meningococcal, influenza |

Hydroxyurea can lower WBC, so monitor for infection.

Do not give aspirin because salicylic acid can promote acidosis, which can trigger sickling crisis.

## Client Education

* Stay hydrated.
* Hydrate more during illness, exercise, or hot weather.
* Avoid mountains and high altitudes.
* Avoid flying if possible due to decreased oxygen tension.
* Avoid cold temperatures and cold water.
* Prevent infection.
* Wash hands.
* Keep vaccinations current.
* Avoid sick contacts.
* Manage stress.
* Get adequate rest.
* Do not smoke.
* Notify provider immediately for fever.
* Report leg or joint pain early.

## Therapeutic Procedures

* Blood transfusions:

  * Used during acute crisis or severe anemia.
* Bone marrow or stem cell transplant:

  * Only potential cure.
  * Limited by donor availability and risk.
* Exchange transfusion:

  * Replaces sickled blood with donor blood.
  * Used in severe cases.

## Complications

| Complication          | Description                                                                     |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Vaso-occlusive crisis | Severe pain from tissue ischemia                                                |
| Acute chest syndrome  | Fever, chest pain, cough, low O2 sat, new infiltrates on chest X-ray            |
| Stroke                | Cerebral vaso-occlusion                                                         |
| Splenic sequestration | Blood pools in spleen, causing rapid Hgb drop and shock                         |
| Aplastic crisis       | Bone marrow temporarily stops producing RBCs, often triggered by parvovirus B19 |
| Gallstones            | From excess bilirubin                                                           |
| Renal failure         | From chronic ischemia                                                           |
| Blindness             | From retinal vessel occlusion                                                   |
| Avascular necrosis    | Especially of femoral head                                                      |

## Interprofessional Care

* Hematologist.
* Pain management specialist.
* Pulmonologist.
* Ophthalmologist.
* Nephrologist.
* Genetic counselor.
* Social work or case management.

Average life expectancy is reduced by about 22 years compared with the general population.

<div class="note">
<strong>NCLEX Tip:</strong> In sickle cell crisis, the priority is IV fluids. Fever is urgent because infection can cause dehydration and trigger crisis. Folic acid helps; iron does not.
</div>

# G6PD Deficiency

Glucose-6-Phosphate Dehydrogenase Deficiency.
Hemolytic anemia.

## What Is Happening

G6PD deficiency is X-linked recessive.

* Males are primarily affected.
* RBCs lack the G6PD enzyme.
* The enzyme normally helps protect RBCs from oxidative stress.
* When exposed to certain triggers, RBCs break down.
* This causes hemolytic anemia.

Most common type:

* Hemolysis occurs only when triggered.
* It is not continuous.

## Triggers

| Category         | Triggers                                                                                         |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Medications      | Phenacetin, nitrofurantoin, antimalarials, Pyridium, sulfonamides, aspirin, quinidine, thiazides |
| Foods/substances | Fava beans, tonic water, menthol, Chinese herbs                                                  |
| Other            | Fever, illness                                                                                   |

## Higher-Risk Populations

* African American ancestry.
* Greek ancestry.
* Italian ancestry.

## Expected Findings

* Pallor.
* Fatigue.
* Jaundice.
* Symptoms usually occur after identifiable exposure.

## Safety and Teaching

* Avoid trigger medications and foods.
* Request a list of medications to avoid.
* Ask healthcare provider before taking any new drug or herb.
* Wear a medic-alert bracelet.
* Genetic counseling may be indicated.

## Interprofessional Care

* Genetic counselor.
* Pharmacist for medication safety review.

# Thalassemia

Hemolytic anemia.

## What Is Happening

Thalassemia is an autosomal recessive disorder.

The body cannot produce enough hemoglobin.

RBCs are:

* Microcytic, meaning small.
* Hypochromic, meaning pale.
* Short-lived.

Without enough hemoglobin, RBCs cannot carry oxygen effectively.

Thalassemia becomes evident in infancy.

## More Common In

* Mediterranean ancestry.
* African ancestry.
* Middle Eastern ancestry.
* Southeast Asian ancestry.

## Expected Findings

| Timing  | Findings                                                                                    |
| ------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Early   | Fever, anemia, failure to thrive, delayed growth and development, splenomegaly              |
| Later   | Freckles, bronzed skin, headache, anorexia, abdominal pain                                  |
| Chronic | Iron deposits in heart, liver, and pancreas causing dysfunction; splenomegaly crowds organs |

Bronzed skin occurs from iron deposits that come from destroyed RBCs.

## Safety Considerations

* Monitor for iron overload from repeated transfusions.
* Chelation therapy may be needed.
* Monitor for infection risk.
* Splenomegaly and immunocompromise can increase risk.

## Therapeutic Procedures

* Frequent blood transfusions:

  * Maintain oxygen-carrying capacity.
* Iron chelation:

  * Removes iron buildup from organs.
* Genetic counseling:

  * Recommended for family planning.

## Interprofessional Care

* Hematologist.
* Genetic counselor.
* Cardiologist if iron deposits affect the heart.
* Endocrinologist if iron deposits affect the pancreas.

# Autoimmune Hemolytic Anemia

## What Is Happening

The immune system produces antibodies that attack and destroy the body’s own RBCs.

After rapid RBC destruction:

* The body accelerates RBC production.
* Newly produced RBCs are abnormal.

There are two types:

* Cold antibody anemia.
* Warm antibody anemia.

## Types

| Type          | Immunoglobulin | Temperature Trigger      | Association                          |
| ------------- | -------------- | ------------------------ | ------------------------------------ |
| Cold antibody | IgM            | Around 86°F, cool temps  | Raynaud’s phenomenon                 |
| Warm antibody | IgG excess     | Around 98.6°F, body temp | Drugs, chemicals, autoimmune disease |

## Treatment

* Corticosteroids.
* Immunotherapy.
* Chemotherapy as a last resort.
* Treat the underlying cause.

# Blood Loss Anemia

Hemorrhagic anemia.

## What Is Happening

Blood loss anemia occurs when RBC loss exceeds production.

It can happen from acute or gradual blood loss.

The body temporarily compensates by pulling fluid from tissues into the bloodstream. This helps maintain volume for a short time but dilutes the blood that remains. This is not sustainable.

## Causes

* Hemorrhage.
* GI bleed.
* Surgical blood loss.
* Placental abruption.

## Expected Findings

* Tachycardia.
* Hypotension.
* Pale, cool, clammy skin.
* Decreased Hgb.
* Decreased Hct.
* Decreased oxygen saturation.

## Nursing Care and Treatment

* Control source of bleeding.
* Give IV fluids.
* Prepare for blood transfusion if ordered.
* Monitor Hgb and Hct.
* Monitor vital signs closely.

# Blood Transfusions

## Purpose

Blood transfusions give new RBCs to improve oxygen-carrying ability.

Used for:

* Severe anemia.
* Blood loss.
* Sickle cell.
* Thalassemia.
* Aplastic anemia.
* Hemorrhage.

## Before Transfusion

* Verify provider order.
* Obtain informed consent.
* Check type and screen or crossmatch.
* Use large-bore IV if possible.
* Use normal saline only with blood.
* Get baseline vital signs.

## Two-Nurse Verification

Verify:

* Patient identity.
* Blood type and Rh.
* Unit number.
* Expiration date.
* Compatibility.

## Starting the Transfusion

* Start slowly for the first 15 minutes.
* Stay with the patient for the first 15 minutes.
* Recheck vital signs after 15 minutes.
* Start blood within 30 minutes of obtaining it.
* Complete transfusion within 4 hours.

## Monitor for Reaction

Stop the transfusion if the patient has:

* Fever or chills.
* Back or flank pain.
* Chest pain.
* Shortness of breath.
* Itching.
* Hives.
* Rash.
* Hypotension.
* Anxiety or feeling of doom.
* Dark urine.

## If a Reaction Occurs

1. Stop blood immediately.
2. Keep IV open with normal saline using new tubing.
3. Notify provider and blood bank.
4. Recheck vital signs.
5. Send blood bag and tubing back per policy.
6. Collect urine and blood samples if ordered.
7. Document reaction.

## NCLEX Reminders

* Blood tubing has a filter.
* Do not add medications to blood.
* Only compatible fluid is 0.9% normal saline.
* Watch closely during the first 15 minutes.
* Febrile reaction:

  * Fever and chills.
* Hemolytic reaction:

  * Most dangerous.
  * Think wrong blood type.
  * Findings include back pain, fever, hypotension, and dark urine.
* In sickle cell, transfusions may be used, but priority in crisis is usually hydration and pain control.

# Master NCLEX Tips

## Iron Deficiency

* Liquid iron goes through a straw.
* IM iron requires Z-track.
* Change the needle before IM injection.
* Do not massage after IM iron.
* Black stools indicate compliance.
* Best meal: iron source plus vitamin C.
* Milk blocks iron absorption.
* Vitamin C increases iron absorption.

## Pernicious Anemia

* Oral B12 is wrong if the cause is lack of intrinsic factor.
* IM B12 is needed.
* Beefy red tongue is an early hallmark sign.
* Schilling test requires 24-hour urine collection.
* Treatment is lifelong.

## Folic Acid Deficiency

* Megaloblastic anemia.
* No neurological symptoms.
* Poor nutrition and alcoholism are common causes.
* Anticonvulsants and oral contraceptives can contribute.
* Important in pregnancy to prevent neural tube defects.

## Aplastic Anemia

* Think pancytopenia.
* Low RBCs, WBCs, and platelets.
* Highest priority is infection prevention.
* Use neutropenic precautions.
* Watch for bleeding.
* Bone marrow biopsy is definitive.

## Sickle Cell

* IV fluids are priority number one in crisis.
* Fever is urgent.
* Avoid dehydration.
* Avoid high altitudes.
* Avoid cold.
* No aspirin.
* No iron.
* Folic acid helps.
* Hydroxyurea increases fetal hemoglobin.
* If both parents are carriers, each pregnancy has a 25% chance of disease.

## G6PD

* X-linked recessive.
* Males most affected.
* Avoid aspirin, sulfonamides, antimalarials, and fava beans.
* Wear medic-alert bracelet.
* Ask provider before taking new medications or herbs.

## Thalassemia

* Inherited hemolytic anemia.
* Appears in infancy.
* Frequent transfusions are needed.
* Watch for iron overload.
* Chelation may be needed.
* Genetic counseling is recommended.

## Blood Transfusions

* Use normal saline only.
* Use blood tubing with filter.
* Do not add medications to blood.
* Stay with patient for first 15 minutes.
* Start blood within 30 minutes of obtaining it.
* Complete transfusion within 4 hours.
* Stop immediately if reaction occurs.
* Hemolytic reaction is the most dangerous.
